A B S T R A C T
The rapid development of biogas production will result in increased use of biogas slurry (BS) as organic
fertilizer. However, side effects such as suppression of soilborne diseases are not yet well investigated and
understood. Therefore, the objectives of the study were to evaluate the effects of biogas slurry application
on suppression of Fusarium wilt disease of watermelon and its relationship with soil chemical and
microbiological properties. Pot and ﬁeld experiments were conducted to compare effects of biogas slurry
application on Fusarium wilt disease suppression of watermelon in soil with a moisture content of 60%
water holding capacity (WHC) or ﬂooded continuously. Fusarium wilt was signiﬁcantly suppressed in soil
from biogas slurry amended plots. Biogas slurry ﬂooding enhanced the degree of suppression in the pot
experiment. Moreover, the biogas slurry treatment also signiﬁcantly suppressed Fusarium wilt in the
ﬁeld with a disease index of 33.2% compared with 69.6% in water treatment. Biogas slurry strongly
reduced the pathogen population in rhizosphere soil. The populations were decreased by 43.1% and 95.9%
in the biogas slurry moist and ﬂooding treatments, respectively. Biolog data indicated that average well
color development (AWCD) and Shannon-weaver index were increased signiﬁcantly in biogas ﬂooding
treatment. Principal component analysis showed that Fusarium wilt was negatively correlated with
NH4
+-N, available K (AK), water-soluble carbon (DOC), water soluble nitrogen (DON) and phenolic acid
(PA) contents in soil and positively correlated to soil pH and soil redox potential (Eh). Microbial
communities, in general, did not signiﬁcantly correlate with disease suppression.

1. Introduction
Biogas is an emerging renewable energy source which is
derived from the conversion of various organic wastes into biofuels
through anaerobic fermentation. Over the last decade, the number
of biogas plants has signiﬁcantly increased in China and other parts
of the world (Ward et al., 2008). The rapid development of biogas
production will result in increased production of biogas residues.
In fact, more than 1 billion tons of biogas slurry (BS), the main by-
product of biogas, was produced from 3800 large-scale biogas
plants in China annually (Jin and Chang, 2011). These residues are
rich in plant nutrients, which can be functioned as fertilizers. It has
been shown that BS application enhanced crop yield (Ernst et al.,
2008), improved N uptake and soil structure and reduced the cost
of fertilizers for farmers (Bachmann et al., 2014; Chen et al., 2012).
Consistent with other organic amendments, it has been
reported that intensive application of BS could suppress a number
of soilborne diseases (Cao et al., 2013; Jothi et al., 2003). Soil
suppressiveness induced by organic amendments has been
associated with diverse microbial communities that have a greater
probability to contain antagonistic, competitive or parasitic
species that can contribute to direct inhibition of a pathogen or
the

system, while the second type is often associated with more than
one

amendments is assumed to be the second general suppression

Fusarium oxysporum f. sp. niveum (FON) is the causal agent of
Fusarium wilt of watermelon and is considered to be the most
important soilborne facultative parasite that causes economically
important losses in watermelon production (Ling et al., 2012).
Disease suppression of F. oxysporum is quite common, and is often
associated with non-pathogenic Fusarium sp. (Hoper and Ala-
bouvette, 1996), ﬂuorescent pseudomonads (Larkin et al., 1993)
and actinomycete populations (Castano et al., 2011), but is also
affected by soil pH, organic matter content (Senechkin et al., 2014;
van Bruggen et al., 2015) and water content (Stover, 1953) in
particular. Excess of water in soils results in gradual oxygen
depletion. As a consequence, physico-chemical properties are
severely affected, which in turn has important impact on the
activity of soil microorganisms (Unger et al., 2009). Microbial
communities shift to anaerobes and potentially toxic chemicals
such as phenolic and aromatic acids or hydrogen sulﬁdes may
accumulate to decrease the viability of pathogens (Messiha et al.,
2007; Seo and DeLaune, 2010). Thus, soil ﬂooding is an effective
measure to reduce the incidence of soilborne diseases (Hord and
Ristaino, 1992; Niem et al., 2013). Biogas slurry differs from water
in that more organic materials and mineral nutrients are added to
soil, which may have complicated effects on growth of soilborne
pathogens as well as soil chemical and microbiological properties.
As a result, better understanding of the effect of soil ﬂooding by BS
on disease suppression is necessary.
It 
has 
been 
reported 
that 
intensive 
application 
of

contributes to soil suppressiveness by enhancing soil microbial
biomass and diversity (Abubaker et al., 2013). In contrast, Makadi
et al. (2007) showed that BS application has not caused drastic
changes in soil microbiological properties. Toxicity caused by
some trace contaminants during the anaerobic fermentation,
such as phenolic compounds, volatile fatty acids and polycyclic
aromatic hydrocarbons (Cao et al., 2013), may have negative
effects on some microbial enzyme activities in soils (Elfstrand
et al., 2007), but also suppress a large number of plant pathogens,
including Fusarium spp. (Blok et al., 2008). The limited number of
studies does not allow drawing conclusions concerning the
underlying mechanism towards suppression of soilborne patho-
gens after applying BS to soils. For example, it is unknown which
biotic or abiotic factors are directly associated with root disease
suppression by BS application. It is also not known whether
application of BS in ﬂooded condition leads to greater suppression
than in soil with lower moisture content. Therefore, a more
comprehensive 
study 
on 
the 
chemical 
and 
microbiological
characteristics of soil is necessary to elucidate soil suppressiveness
induced by BS. The objectives of the study were: (1) to evaluate the
effect of BS application on suppression of watermelon Fusarium
wilt disease in the greenhouse and in the ﬁeld; (2) to determine
the impacts of BS application on soil chemical properties and
ecological characteristics of soil microorganisms through soil
dilution and plate counts and community level physiological
proﬁles (Biolog); and (3) to explore the relationship between the
incidence of Fusarium wilt of watermelon and an array of
microbiological and chemical characteristics after BS application.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Biogas slurry characteristics
The biogas slurry (mesophilic 25–30 C, 12-d retention time),
produced from pig farm wastes, was collected from the large-scale
biogas plant in Luhe Animal Science Base, Jiangsu Academy of
Agricultural Sciences, Nanjing, Jiangsu Province, China. About 100 L
of the biogas slurry was collected and used immediately in the pot
experiment. A representative sample (approximately 500 mL) of
the biogas slurry was portioned into 500-mL bottles and stored at
20 C until chemical analysis. The chemical characteristics of the
BS were: dry matter 6.2 g kg1, chemical oxygen demand 587 mg
L1, total N 661 mg L1, total P 533 mg L1, total K 147 mg L1, NH4
+-
N 378 mg L1, NO3
-N 85 mg L1, and the contents of Zn, Cu, As
were 0.5, 0.35 and 0.02 mg L1, respectively.
2.2. Soil sampling and characteristics
The soil in the experiments was collected from Luhe Animal
Test site, located 60 km north-west of Nanjing (3220N, 11880E),
which is a conventional farm with a crop rotation consisting of rice
and rapeseed. The soil was a clay Tye-Fel-Stagnic Anthrosols soil
(IUSS Working Group WRB, 2007), with the following character-
istics at the beginning of the experiment: clay 32.36%, silt 30.58%,
sand 37.06%, pH 7.4, organic matter (OM) 14.81 g kg1, total
nitrogen (TN) 1.68 g kg1, available phosphorous (AP) 77 mg kg1,
available potassium (AK) 131 mg kg1. About 200 kg wet soil were
taken from the 0–20 cm layer, immediately transported to the
laboratory and air-dried. During drying, the soil was mixed
manually every day to ensure homogeneous moisture distribution.
Soil was then sieved through a 4-mm screen and inoculated with
conidia suspension of FON and thoroughly mixed to obtain a
homogeneous blend before being used in the pot experiment. FON
spore concentration in the soil was 5 105 cfu g1 dry soil.
2.3. Preparation of pathogen inocula
F. oxysporum f. sp. niveum (FON) was isolated from infected
watermelon in the ﬁeld, and was grown in Potato Dextrose Agar
(PDA) plates at 28 C for 10 days till spores were densely produced.
A colony showing typical FON morphologies was isolated and
conﬁrmed as the responsible pathogen by carrying out Koch's
postulates in pot experiments (Fang, 1998). The plates were
ﬂooded with sterile distilled water, and the spores were scraped
and collected into a tube, and ﬁltered through four layers of sterile
gauze to remove solids. The spore number was counted by a
hemacytometer. The spore solution was thoroughly mixed and
10 mL of the spore solution was loaded in both chambers of a
hemacytometer under a coverslip and examined with a micro-
scope (Nikon Eclipse E200, Japan) at 400. Spore numbers in 5
squares (each square contained 16 smaller squares) were counted
in each chamber, and counts on both sides were averaged (N). The
number of spores per mL (4 107 cfu/mL) was calculated by the
following equation: spore concentration = N/80  400  104. The
inoculum concentration of FON in the pot experiment was
5 104 cfu/g.
2.4. Pot experiment design

ized in 2% NaClO for 3 min, rinsed three times in sterile water, and
then germinated in a standard 9-cm Petri dish ﬁlled with 7 mL
sterile water and then incubated in dark at 30 C until the seeds
germinated. The germinated seeds were then moved into 72-hole
seedling trays each containing 500 g peat. After grown for 30 days,

recorded. Normal moisture treatment (60% WHC) barrels were
reweighed twice per week; additional de-ionized water was added
as needed to maintain the proper WHC. Another half of the barrels
were randomly assigned the ﬂooding treatment (BSF, W). The
ﬂooding treatments maintained 10 cm of water above the surface
of the soil for 20 days at 28 C. Then the surface water was removed
and the soil was spread in a thin (2-cm) layer in plastic boxes and
air-dried for 3 days. The air-dried soil was then sieved and moved
to pots for the following experiment. Urea, superphosphate and
potassium sulfate were applied to each pot to get a ﬁnal rate of

each block. The plants were grown in a greenhouse. Thirty ﬁve days
after transplanting, Fusarium wilt disease incidence and disease
index were recorded. The pot experiment was carried out from
September to November in 2013.
2.5. Field experiment design
Field experiment was carried out in Luhe Animal Experiment
Site, Jiangsu Province of China (3220N, 11880E), where watermel-
on has suffered from severe Fusarium wilt, with 5-year of
continuous mono-cropping. The surface soil (0–20 cm) of the
experimental site containing 11.08 g of organic C, 0.57 g of total N,
0.69 g of total P and 7.43 g of total K per kg of dry soil, and the pH of
the soil was 7.4.
Considering the environmental issues, biogas slurry ﬂooding
treatment was not adopted in the ﬁeld, although it showed a strong
effect on Fuarium wilt suppression in the pot experiment. The ﬁeld
experiment was laid out in a randomized block design with four
replications that had three treatments: (1) CK, control; (2) T1, soil
saturated by water; (3) T2, soil saturated by biogas slurry. The plots,
each 24 m  2 m in size, had 100 plants grown in it.
A total of 2 t of biogas slurry or water was applied to one plot by
ﬂooding irrigation from August 7 to August 14, 2014 in order to
ensure that the soil surface (0–15 cm) be saturated (100% WHC).
Subsequently, a plastic cover was applied to T1 and T2 treatments
and the soil remained covered by plastic sheets for 20 days. Urea,
single superphosphate and potassium sulfate fertilizers were
added to CK and T1 treatments to obtain equivalent quantities of
total N, P and K with those of T2 treatment. Plants infected by FON
were recorded every day and the cumulative number of infected
plants was calculated from the day after transplanting (August 26,
2014) until harvest (October 24, 2014). Disease incidence was
calculated by the percentage of diseased plants in each plot. The
disease symptoms rated on each plant on ﬁve classes according to
Wu et al. (2009): 0, no yellowing of leaves; 1, 1–33% yellowing of
the leaves; 2, 34–66% yellowing of the leaves; 3, 67–100%
yellowing on the leaves; 4, dead plant. Disease severity index
was calculated as follows: Disease severity index = [P (Class 
Number of plants in that class)/(4  Total number of assessed
plants)]  100 (Wu et al., 2009). The watermelon yield and stem
length, number of leaves, shoot and root weights as well as soil Zn,
Cu, As contents were assessed.
2.6. Sampling and analytical methods
To determine how soil variables changed by the ﬂooding and/
or moisture treatment, sampling occurred at 20 days after
ﬂooding. Soil redox potential (Eh) was measured with a hand-
held ORP Depolarization Automatic Analyzer with an ORP
electrode (model: FSA-6, Nanjing Chuandi Instrument & equip-
ment Co. Ltd). Soil pH was measured with a hand-held meter
(Orion 320P-81), equipped with a soil pH electrode with an Ag/
AgCl reference cell (model: G00876, Thermo Scientiﬁc). Metal
(Cu, Zn, As) contents in the soil samples collected from the ﬁeld
experiment and in the biogas slurry were determined by
Inductively 
Couples 
Plasma 
Optical 
Emission 
Spectrometry
(ICP-OES) (Jin and Chang, 2011).
A 2.5-cm in diameter polyvinyl chloride pipe was used to
collect two soil cores from each tray. The cores from each
replicate tray were mixed together, pooled in a sterile plastic bag
and transported to the laboratory on ice. All the samples were
thoroughly homogenized, and divided into two subsamples: one
was air-dried for soil chemical characteristic analysis; another
was stored at 4 C for the measurement of microbiological
properties. Thirty-ﬁve days after transplanting, pot soil and plants
were destructively sampled. Methods for TN, DOC, DON, OM, AP,
AK followed those describe previously (Wang et al., 2014). Total
soluble polyphenolic acid content was determined using the
Folin-Ciocalteu technique generally following Unger et al. (2009).
Total organic acid was determined by a spectrophotometer (Wang
and Bettany, 1993). Fluorescein diacetate (FDA) hydrolytic activity
was measured according to Adam and Duncan (2001).
The culturable microorganisms were enumerated by the
standard 10-fold dilution method using appropriate media. Total
cell counts of soil bacteria, actinomycetes, fungi, and Fusarium
were numbered by beef extract peptone, Gause No. 1 (soluble
starch 20 g, KNO3 1 g, NaCl 0.5 g, K2HPO4 0.5 g, MgSO4 0.5 g, FeSO4
0.01 g, agar 20 g, H2O 1000 mL, pH 7.2), Martin medium (glucose
10 g, peptone 5 g, KH2PO4 1 g, MgSO4 0.5 g, rose bengal 30 mg,
streptomycin 0.03 g, tap water 1000 mL) and Fusarium-selective
medium (Komada, 1975), respectively. All measurements were
based on 3 soil samples (3 replicates).
Biolog ECO MicroPlate (Matrix Technologies Corporation),
each containing 31 individual carbon substrates plus a negative
control was used to determine the nutritional versatility of
microbial metabolic potential from the various soil treatments
(Choi and Dobbs, 1999). These 31 carbon sources were repeated
three times to provide more replicates of data. An initial 101 soil
dilution was prepared by suspending 5 g of soil in 45 mL of sterile
0.85% NaCl solution. Serial dilutions were carried out to the 103
dilution. Each well of an ECO MicroPlate was inoculated with
125 mL of the 103 dilution for a given soil sample. Plates were
incubated at 25 C, and absorbance at 590 nm at 0, 24, 48, 72, 96,
120, 144 and 168 h was recorded (Zhang et al., 2012). The value
obtained for the negative control well of each plate was
subtracted from the values for the remaining wells. The adjusted
absorbance was analyzed by principal components analysis (PCA)
based on a correlation matrix using CANOCO software (Micro-
computer Power, Ithaca, NY, USA).

3. Results
3.1. Effect of different biogas slurry applications on Fusarium wilt
disease incidence and population of FON in pot experiment
The symptoms of Fusarium wilt appeared 7 days after trans-
planting into the pots. The disease incidence for plants estimated
at 35 days after transplanting, was highest in the control (W) (90%)
and lowest in the biogas slurry ﬂooding treatments (BSF) (33.3%)
(Fig. 1A). Biogas slurry moisture treatments (BS) and water
ﬂooding treatment (WF) reduced the disease incidence by 22%
and 37% compared to W. The disease severity index showed similar
trend to the disease incidence, with lowest in BSF and highest in W,
respectively (Fig. 1A).
Soil density of FON was highest in the control (W). The FON
populations in soil in treatments of BS and BSF were signiﬁcantly
lower compared with control (W) (Fig. 1B), resulting in 43.1% and
95.9% decrease, respectively. A signiﬁcant decrease of FON
population was also observed in the treatment of WF.

from BS and W clustered together and were separated from BSF
and WF. Shannon-weaver index in BS and BSF were similar and
higher than that of W and WF, while McIntosh index in BS and BSF
were signiﬁcantly lower than that of W and WF. Simpson index was
lowest in W and no signiﬁcant difference was found among all the
treatments (Table 3).
3.4. Relationship between soil chemical and microbial characteristics
and Fusarium wilt disease incidence
The PCA analysis showed that the disease incidence was
signiﬁcantly negatively correlated to the contents of DOC, DON,
NH4
+-N, AK and PA, and positively correlated to soil pH and Eh
(Fig. 3A). PCA relating the disease incidence to the population of
soil microbes and the microbial diversity revealed that the disease
incidence correlated to FDA activity, Shannon-weaver index and
Simpson 
index 
negatively, 
and 
was 
signiﬁcantly 
positively
correlated to populations of FON and total bacteria in soil. The
total numbers of culturable fungi and actionmycete also showed
positive correlation with Fusarium wilt disease, although the
correlation was not high (Fig. 3B).

fruit yield and Fusarium wilt disease incidence of watermelon in the
ﬁeld
Cu, Zn and As were present in low quantities in the slurry, giving
rise to very low contents of metals (Cu, Zn, As) in biogas slurry
treated soil sample (34 mg Cu, 69 mg Zn and 3.3 mg As per kg soil).
The average contents of the metals (Cu, Zn, As) were below the
threshold for trace metal concentrations in soil set by the
environmental 
protection 
agency 
of 
China 
(Ministry 
of
Environmental Protection of PRC, 1995). There was no signiﬁcant
difference in contents of Cu, Zn and As between biogas slurry
treated soil sample (T2) and the control (CK).
Application of BS signiﬁcantly promoted watermelon growth
and fruit yield compared to control treatment. The stem length,
plant biomass, single fruit weight and total fruit yield in BS
treatment (T2) were increased by 17.9%, 54.4%, 40.5% and 25%
compared with the control, respectively. Water treatment in-
creased single fruit weight of watermelon, but had no signiﬁcant
effect on plant growth and total fruit yield owing to high disease
incidence in the treatment (Table 4).
The disease incidence was recorded 30 days and 45 days after
watermelon plants were transplanted (DAT) and at harvest (70
DAT). The incidence of Fusaruim wilt (0.5–5%) was quite low
among all treatments 30 days after transplantation. However, the
disease incidence increased sharply to 16%, 22.8% and 10% in the
control, water treatment (T1) and biogas slurry (T2) 45 days after
transplantation, respectively. At harvest, the disease incidence of
CK, T1 and T2 were 66%, 84% and 60%, respectively. Although no
signiﬁcant difference was observed between CK and T2 in disease
incidence, the disease index of biogas slurry treatment was
signiﬁcantly lower than CK at harvest (Table 5).

4. Discussion
The incidence of watermelon Fusarium wilt and the FON
population in soil were slightly but signiﬁcantly reduced by the
application of biogas slurry under normal water content condition,
but the suppressive effect was more pronounced in biogas slurry
ﬂooding treatments (BSF), indicating that biogas slurry ﬂooding
had a stronger effect than in moist condition. Thus it was not
surprising that biogas slurry saturation treatment had the lowest
disease index in the ﬁeld. Biogas slurry pretreatment under
saturation condition delayed disease development and signiﬁ-
cantly increased watermelon yield in ﬁeld, demonstrating that
biogas slurry reduced Fusarium wilt before fruiting stage and also
promoted plant growth. Integrated treatment including pretreat-
ment of soil by biogas slurry should be combined with other
measures such as soil amendment with microbial inoculants
(Wu et al., 2014) or biogas slurry spraying on plant leaves after
transplantation (Yu et al., 2010) in order to obtain persist control of
Fusarium wilt disease incidence in the ﬁeld.
In our experiment the plots were dosed with ﬁxed levels of
mineral N, P, K and despite being fertilised with the same levels of
N, P, K, the biogas slurry treatment gave higher biomass yields
compared with water treatment. In the biogas slurry, besides
macronutrients such as P, K, Ca, Mg, micronutrients like Zn are also
preserved in biogas residue since they are stable at mesophilic
digestion conditions. It has been shown that Zn can affect plant
biomass, especially in areas where intensive cropping caused
micronutrient loss, the addition of slurry generally promoted
biomass production (Gavalda et al., 2005). This may be one
explanation of the higher yield generated from biogas slurry
compared with water treatments. The easy degradable carbon and
nitrogen were comparatively high and was therefore more efﬁcient
than the chemical fertilizers in terms of supplying plant-available
nitrogen and promoting the soil biological activity (Abubaker et al.,
2012).
In the vast literature on suppression of Fusarium wilts by
organic amendments (Borrero et al., 2004; Castano et al., 2011; Qiu
et al., 2012), the suppressiveness has been mainly attributed to
biotic factors and the type of organic amendment (Leoni et al.,
2013), evidenced from the elimination of disease suppressiveness
after biocidal treatments. Biogas slurry treatment increased
microbial 
diversity and 
signiﬁcantly changed the 
microbial
community structure as evidenced by Biolog proﬁle. Previous
studies 
with 
Fusarium 
oxysporum 
showed 
the 
diversity 
of
ammonia-oxidizing bacteria, instead of general microbial commu-
nity structures, contributed to soil suppressiveness by organic
amendments (Senechkin et al., 2014). Surprisingly, in this study,
there was a signiﬁcant positive correlation between Fusarium wilt
disease incidence and bacterial population, while populations of
total bacteria frequently are associated with the suppression of
Fusarium wilt. No direct correlations were found between the
disease incidence of watermelon wilt and fungal and actinomycetic
populations. This may be attributed to the method of microbial
enumeration, only culturable microorganisms can be enumerated
by plate count method. In addition, abundant anaerobes, excluded
by plate count, are present in large quantity under ﬂooded
condition. As was expected, there was signiﬁcantly positive
relationship between Fusarium wilt incidence and the population
of FON in soil. The result was similar to those obtained for Fusarium
wilt of cucumber (Qiu et al., 2012), melon (Zhao et al., 2011a,b) and

Alabouvette (1999) attributed soil suppressiveness towards
Fusarium oxysporum partly to carbon and nitrogen competition
among the pathogen and other microorganisms present in soil.
Dissolved organic carbon and nitrogen consists partially of easily
utilizable carbon sources that can be constantly utilized by fast-
growing microorganisms (Zelenev et al., 2005) that can compete
with plant pathogens in soil, thereby greatly inhibited Fusarium
wilt disease development (Ling et al., 2012; Qiu et al., 2012).
Apparently, biogas slurry amendment resulted in very high DOC
and DON contents in the BSF and BS plots, which may have been
diminished by high microbial activity in the biogas slurry-treated
plots. High DOC content was also recorded in the water-ﬂooding
treatment (WF). Signiﬁcant increase in DOC content in WF might
result from a higher mineralization of soil organic C under the ﬂood
condition than under the aerobic condition (Wang et al., 1993).
Thus, direct negative correlation was found between Fusarium wilt
disease incidence and soil DOC and DON contents.
Although an extensive literature review on root disease
suppression by organic amendments showed that microbiological
parameters are generally more indicative of disease suppression
than chemical parameters (Bonanomi et al., 2010), indirect effects
of various abiotic variables, such as soil organic matter content,
texture, pH and Eh, on microbial populations and availability of
mineral nutrients that contribute to suppressiveness are also
possible. Mineral nutrient can affect disease defense development
in plants. In this study, signiﬁcantly negative correlation between
Fusarium wilt disease incidence and soil NH4
+-N content was
observed, while soil NO3
-N content was positively correlated to
Fusarium wilt disease incidence. The relationship between the
nitrogen forms and the severity of plant diseases has been studied
extensively and controversial results were reported (Datnoff et al.,
2007). The controversial results on the effects of nitrogen
application on plant diseases might be due to nitrogen application
method used in different plant-pathogen systems. Under moist
conditions (W and BS), soil NO3
-N remained high because soil was
aerobic and nitriﬁcation was the dominate process in well-aerated
soils. Under ﬂooded conditions, NO3
-N was generally low
whereas 
the 
concentration 
of 
NH4
+-N 
remained 
high. 
The
transformation of ammonium into ammonia under alkaline
condition, is the mechanism most often implicated in killing
pathogens in soil treated with nitrogenous amendments (Tenuta
and Lazarovits, 2002). The negative correlation with NH4
+-N
content and positive correlation with pH observed in the study
might be attributed to complicated correlation of soil pH with soil
available nutrient contents, not only soil N, but also available P,
available K, Ca, Mg, Zn, Cu, etc (Zhao et al., 2011a,b).
Potassium amendments have been reported to be suppressive
to root rot diseases caused by Fusarium oxysporum in many crops
(Chi and Hanson, 1961; Sharma and Kolte, 1994; Srihuttagum and
Sivasithamparam, 1991). Improved K nutrition in plant tissue was
associated with phenolics which inhibited conidial germination
and sporulation of some pathogens and induction of plant
resistance (Datnoff et al., 2007). Thus, the increase of soil available
K concentration by biogas slurry ﬂooding was associated with the
reduction of watermelon Fusarium wilt disease incidence. No
direct correlation was found between Ca, Mg, Fe and Na contents
and Fusarium wilt incidence, although it is reported less available
Fe in soil can favour the competition between Fusarium and
Pseudomonas type bacteria for the nutrient in suppressive areas
(Domínguez et al., 2003). In the current study, soil total soluble
polyphenolic concentration and organic acid increased under ﬂood
treatments but not under moist condition, had negative correlation
with the wilt disease incidence. Once in the soil solution, the
phenolic compounds and organic acids produced under ﬂooded
condition may be more microbial toxic. Tenuta and Lazarovits
(2002) reported that 30 mM acetic acid reduced germination of
microsclerotia of V. dahliae by 95%.
Biogas treatment under moist condition signiﬁcantly increased
NH4
+-N, available K, DOC and DON contents, which exert a positive
inﬂuence on suppression of FON abundance and watermelon
Fusarium wilt. A greater control of Fusaiurm wilt under ﬂooded
condition than moist condition is consistent with the larger levels
of NH4
+-N, available K, DOC and DON observed in ﬂooded samples
than moist samples. Moreover, biogas slurry ﬂooding treatment
caused a decrease of soil Eh and pH, which exert a positive
inﬂuence on the release of polyphenolic compounds and organic
acids to soil solution and may probably changed the environmental
conditions (decreased pH and Eh) for soil microorganisms, in
which speciﬁc beneﬁcial bacterial groups maybe enriched, while
the abundance of FON was decreased.
In the current study, a single application of biogas slurry did not
have any effect on heavy metal (Cu, Zn and As) content in soil. In
comparison with the literature (Jin and Chang, 2011; Marcato et al.,
2008), the metal content in the slurry was very low and did not
exceed the limits laid down by regulations governing the spreading
of biogas slurry (Gavalda et al., 2005). Although the metal content
was low in soil over the growth season, it must be underlined that
soil heavy metal stock would increase after long term application
of slurry. Monitoring of biogas slurry-amended land must be
carried on, but also that of soil water, rivers and sediments.
5. Conclusion
Anaerobically digested residues from biogas production in-
duced signiﬁcant changes in the soil nutrient contents, microbial
community and function structure. Besides enhancing plant
growth, ﬂooding by biogas slurry in Fusarium oxysporum infested
soils as a soil disinfestation treatment seems strongly decrease soil
infestation levels of Fusarium oxysporum and will become an
important element in a sustainable, practical and effective
management strategy for watermelon Fusarium wilt. Further
studies are needed to conﬁrm the validity of these results on other
crop diseases caused by soilborne pathogens under ﬁeld con-
ditions.
