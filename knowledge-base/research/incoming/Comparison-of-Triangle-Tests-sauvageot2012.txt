Fifteen groups of participants in nine laboratories performed triangle tests with two pairs of soft drinks.
Groups differed in practice level with triangle tests: eight groups of 60 consumers who were not used to
triangle test, three groups of qualiﬁed assessors who have already performed a few triangle tests, and
four groups of trained assessors with a more extensive practice of triangle tests; qualiﬁed and trained
groups included 9 or 18 assessors. The soft drinks were made from syrups at two levels of dilution in
order to achieve about 55% of correct responses to test for difference and about 40% of correct responses
to test for similarity. Participants performed three replicated tests with each pair of drinks, except the
groups of 9 assessors who performed six replicated tests. When testing for difference, large inter-groups
differences were observed. One consumer group and one trained group from two different laboratories
failed by far to reach the critical number of correct responses leading to demonstrate a signiﬁcant differ-
ence between products. For similarity test, all consumer groups demonstrated a signiﬁcant similarity
whereas two qualiﬁed groups and one trained group did not. This is explained by a slightly higher level
of performance for qualiﬁed and trained assessors compared to consumers.

1. Introduction
Inter-laboratory comparison is essential to both validate meth-
ods and to test for laboratories proﬁciency (Feinberg, 1996). Sev-
eral
inter-laboratories
studies
were
conducted
dealing
with
descriptive analysis of food products (Sauvageot, 2006 for a re-
view). The ﬁrst one is probably that of Lundgren et al., 1986)
involving 11 laboratories and describing pectin gels with three
descriptors: ﬁrmness, ﬂavor, and taste. The most ambitious study
is undoubtedly that carried out within the European Sensory Net-
work (ESN, 1996) involving 11 laboratories from 9 countries
describing 16 coffees with 14 to 56 descriptors according to
laboratories.
In the ﬁeld of discriminative tests, inter-laboratory comparisons
are extremely scarce and, to the best of our knowledge, no compar-
ison has been published for triangle tests. This gap is surprising as:
(i) the triangle test is used for a long time: Amerine, Pangborn, and
Roessler (1971) imputed it to Bengtsson (1943) and even men-
tioned ‘‘for Peryam and Swartz (1950), the triangular test had been
developed in the laboratories of Joseph E. Seagram and Sons, in
1941, for quality control and research on whiskey’’; (ii) it is a
well-deﬁned procedure: an AFNOR standard was edited as early
as 1976 (1983 for the ISO standard); (iii) there is a large body of lit-
erature dealing with triangle test; (iv) it is an operative method
widely used in both research and applied works.
The purpose of this paper is to ﬁll this gap by comparing nine
laboratories, a number close to that of the ESN (1996) study on cof-
fee. Difference tests are conducted either with consumers or se-
lected and/or trained panelists. In a study where participants
performed same-different tests on vanilla ﬂavored ice cream, Ishii,
Kawaguchi, O’Mahony and Rousseau (2007) showed an adjustment
was needed to extrapolate results from selected assessors to con-
sumers because the former demonstrated higher performance le-
vel than the latter. To assess this discrepancy with triangle tests,
both selected assessors (7 groups) and consumers (8 groups) were
involved in the present study. Furthermore, as the second edition
of the 2004 ISO standard on triangle test introduced a distinction
between triangle test to demonstrate a difference and triangle test
to demonstrate a similarity between products, we considered these
two objectives with two pairs of products: grenadine syrups for
difference test and lemon syrups for similarity test, those ampli-
tudes of difference are compatible with the two objectives.

were bought in a supermarket in Rennes and dispatched in the
nine laboratories. The experiment took place within the two

For grenadine, the two drinks were prepared by adding 350 mL
vs. 385 mL of water to 50 g of syrup. For lemon, the two drinks
were prepared by adding 350 mL vs. 370 mL of water to 50 g of syr-
up. Concentrations were chosen, on the basis of preliminary tests,
to lead to 40–60% of correct responses, corresponding to the
highest sensitivity range for triangle test (Frijters, 1981). The dif-
ferences in concentrations were chosen as to get higher perfor-
mance with grenadine than lemon syrups in accordance with the
objective: testing for difference and similarity respectively.
2.2. Laboratories
Nine laboratories located in nine French cities and belonging to
seven companies (see acknowledgments) took part in the compar-
ison. They were accredited for triangle test by the French accredi-
tation society (COFRAC, 37 rue de Lyon, 75012, Paris).
2.3. Participants
Fifteen groups of participants were involved: Eight groups of
consumers including 60 participants and seven groups of selected
assessors including either 9 or 18 participants. Among the seven
groups of selected assessors, three were qualiﬁed assessors and four
were trained assessors. Groups are coded by a ﬁgure (from 1 to 9)
indicating the laboratory followed by a letter (C, Q, and T) indicat-
ing the category of participants (Consumers, Qualiﬁed assessors
and Trained assessors, respectively).
Consumers did not have any training in sensory analysis, but
were used to hedonic tests in central location facilities. Thus, they
were familiar with product evaluation in sensory booths and knew
that their results would not be communicated within the group.
Except for consumers from laboratory 5, they had never performed
triangle tests previously. Qualiﬁed assessors were participants that
had been selected on the basis of their sensory abilities (ISO 8586-
1, 1993) and had received a general training in sensory analysis,
including performing a few triangle tests. The trained assessors
were qualiﬁed assessors who had already been involved in triangle
test sessions. Qualiﬁed and trained assessors were regular asses-
sors of the laboratories.
Each laboratory managed one to three groups of participants.
Assessors performed triangle tests with both syrups excepting
groups 3C, 4C, 6C and 4T where only 21, 3, 39 and 10 assessors,
respectively tested both syrups.
2.4. Experimental conditions
Grenadine drinks were evaluated ﬁrst and lemon drinks second.
They were evaluated during the same session by nine groups (1C,
2C, 5C, 8C, 9C, 1Q, 4Q, 7Q and 1T) and during two separated ses-
sions by ﬁve groups (4C, 6C, 7Q, 4T and 8T). For the 3C group, 21
consumers carried out the tests during the same session and 39
during two separated sessions. For practical reasons, tests were
carried out in sub-groups of typically 10 to 12 assessors, within a
period of 10 days. The number of sub-sessions varied from 3 to 7
(modal value 4) for the groups of 60 consumers, from 2 to 4 (modal
value 2) for the groups of 18 assessors, and from 1 to 3 (modal va-
lue 2) for the groups of 9 assessors.
For the groups of 60 or 18 participants, assessors performed
three replicated triangle tests for each syrup. For one participant,
the odd product was the same for the three replications, but the
six triads (AAB, ABA, BAA, BBA, ABB and BAB) were counterbal-
anced over participants at each replication. For the groups of 9
participants, assessors performed six replicated tests in order to
equal the total number of responses of the other groups of selected
assessors. They received the six possible triads; the design was bal-
anced over two successive replications.
Drinks were freshly prepared for each sub-session. Separated
batches were prepared for each replication; one laboratory (labora-
tory 4) prepared separated batches for the two replicated samples
within each replication.
Tests were carried in sensory room under white light. Samples
were presented in transparent plastic glasses. According to labora-
tories, 50 to 100 mL samples were presented in 100–200 mL
glasses. Serving temperature was ﬁxed at 20 C ± 2 C. Random
controls showed that the difference among samples served at a gi-
ven sub-session was lower than 0.5 C and generally about 0.2 C.
2.5. Instructions
All laboratories provided participants with both written and
oral instructions. Written instructions included (i) a presentation
of the task; (ii) the obligation to evaluate the samples in the im-
posed order; (iii) the obligation to give a response even when un-
sure; (iv) the possibility to provide comments, for example
indicating the level of conﬁdence in the response or the nature of
the difference. Oral instructions presented the above-mentioned
aspects, indicated how to answer (checking a box, writing down
the sample code. . .), and explained that re-tasting one or several
sample(s) was allowed, but only after tasting the three samples
once. In response to questions raised by assessors, several panel
leaders indicated how to taste the samples.
2.6. Data analysis
For each group, results were ﬁrst interpreted for each replica-
tion separately and then all together. For the groups of 9 assessors,
trials 1 and 2 were gathered as well as triads 3 and 4, and triads 5
and 6. This was considered as three replications in order to get the
same number of responses as in groups of 18 assessors.
For the difference test, the null hypothesis is H0: nc = 1/3 n, with
nc = observed number of correct responses and n = total number of
responses. The alternative hypothesis is H1: nc > 1/3 n. the critical
number of correct responses to reject H0 at a = 0.05 in favor of
H1 is obtained from the binomial distribution (ISO, 2004). When
replications are analyzed all together, the same approach was fol-
lowed in accordance with Kunert and Meyners (1999) who wrote
in the abstract of their paper: ‘‘We agree that assessors will have
different probabilities of correct assessment if there are true differ-
ences. [. . .] We show that all assessments are independent and
have success probability 1/3, if the null hypothesis is true and
the experiment is properly randomized and properly carried out.
This implies that the sum of all correct assessments is binomial
with parameter p = 1/3. Therefore the usual test based on this
sum and the critical values of the binomial distribution is a level
a test for the null hypothesis of equality of the products, even if
there are replications.’’ This point of view was also followed by
Kunert (2001), Brockoff (2003) and ISO (2004).
For the similarity test, the percent correct above chance (so-
called
« percent
distinguishers »
or
« adjustment
for

which corresponds to 53.3% of correct responses (2/3 pD + 1/3;
Smith, 1981). According to Bi (2006), the null hypothesis in the
similarity test is deﬁned as: H0: nc P .533 n and the alternative
hypothesis H1: nc < .533 n. When data are analyzed by replication,
the critical number of correct responses to reject H0 at a = .05 was
obtained from the binomial distribution. When replications are
analyzed all together, the number of correct responses does not
necessarily follow, under H0, a binomial distribution. It can be

modeled by a beta-binomial (BB) distribution (Ennis & Bi, 1998;
Harries & Smith, 1982) characterized by two parameters: p, the
mean proportion of correct responses and c, the over-dispersion
factor representing the heterogeneity among assessors. We used
the Chance Corrected Beta-Binomial model (CCBB) where individ-
ual probabilities of correct responses are distributed on the range
[1/3, 1] (Morrisson, 1978; Brockoff, 2003; Meyners, 2007). Data
were analyzed with the betabinom function implemented in SensR
(Brockhoff & Christensen, 2010). As no valid method is available to
test for similarity with replicated tests, we followed the suggestion
of Meyners (2008) and computed two-sided 90% conﬁdence inter-
val of pD using the maximum likelihood estimation from the CCBB
model. In this approach, whenever the conﬁdence interval lies
completely below .30, the products are considered as similar.
For each replication, inter-groups differences were tested with
the v2 test. Inter-replications differences were tested with the
Generalized Linear Mixed Model (GLMM) with replications as ﬁxed
factor and groups as random factor and the binomial distribution
as error structure (Crawley, 2007; function lmer in lme4 package
of R, 2010).
3. Results
3.1. Testing for difference
All together, the 1818 responses led to 945 correct responses,
i.e. a proportion equal to .520. This value corresponds to a propor-
tion of distinguishers of .280.
3.1.1. Consumers
For pooled replications, i.e. 180 trials per group (Table 1a), se-
ven groups led to conclude at a signiﬁcant difference between
products at .001 a-risk, whereas one group (4C) failed to reach sig-
niﬁcance at a = .05 (pbin = .09).
The GLMM analysis showed signiﬁcant difference (p = .0007)
between replications: replication 1 led to a lower number of cor-
rect responses (pc = .435) compared to replications 2 and 3 which
are equivalent (pc = .546 and .533 respectively). As a matter of fact,
when discarding replication 1, the inter replications difference was
not signiﬁcant anymore (pGLMM = .40). This incidental training is
not surprising as consumers were not used to perform triangle
tests. The increase in performance from replication 1 to replication
2 may be due to facilitation effect linked to practice. However, all
groups did not follow this general trend. Whereas the groups were
not signiﬁcantly different at trial 1 (pv2 = .51), the difference is sig-
niﬁcant for trial 2 (pv2 = .024) and trial 3 (pv2 = .008). Actually, ﬁve
groups showed an increase in correct responses from trial 1 to trial
2 and 3, while for the three other groups the number of correct re-
sponses is stable over replications.
3.1.2. Selected assessors
For pooled replications, i.e. 54 trials per group, six groups led to
reject H0: the three groups of qualiﬁed assessors and three groups
of trained assessors (Table 1b). Only one group (group 8T) did not
lead to the same conclusion with 18 correct responses out of 54, i.e.
a proportion of correct responses equal to the expected value un-
der H0 (pbin = .56). By contrast to consumers, the inter replications
differences were not signiﬁcant (pGLMM = .50). Selected assessors
had already performed triangle tests before and thus did not show
any incidental training overall, with the exception of group 6T that
showed a large increase from trial 1 to trial 3.
3.2. Testing for similarity
When considering the 1818 responses together, the number of
correct responses was equal to 743, i.e. a proportion of .409, lead-
ing to an estimated proportion of distinguishers equal to .113. This
value is low enough to be in accordance with the idea of testing for
similarity.
3.2.1. Consumers
For pooled replications (180 responses), the Conﬁdence Interval
led to conclude to a signiﬁcant similarity for the eight groups
(Table 2a).
The GLMM analysis did not show any signiﬁcant difference be-
tween replications (p = .79). The overall number of correct responses
seems stable over replications. However, this result covers a dispar-
ity among groups: For three groups (1C, 4C, 9C), the number of cor-
rect responses increases along with replications, while for three
other groups (2C, 5C, 8C), it decreases and for the two remaining
groups (3C, 6C) it is stable. Whereas the eight groups were not signif-
icantly different at trial 1 (pv2 = .14), some groups showed an inci-
dental training when others did not and even decreased in
performance at subsequent trials, leading to a signiﬁcant difference
among groups at replications 2 (pv2 < .0001) and 3 (pv2 < .0001).
3.2.2. Selected assessors
For pooled replications (54 responses), the conﬁdence interval
led to a signiﬁcant similarity for four groups: groups 1Q, 1T, 4T
and 8T, 4T (Table 2a), but not for the three other groups.
A signiﬁcant difference was observed between groups for repli-
cation 2 (pv2 = .03), but not for replications 1 and 3 (pv2 = .10 and
.11, respectively). In addition, the inter replications differences
were not signiﬁcant (pGLMM = .70).

The ﬁrst explanation that comes to mind is a multiplicity phe-
nomenon. When a large number of measurements related to the
same population are performed, some values may be very far from
the average just by chance. When computed over the 1818 trials of
the experiment, the average frequency of correct responses is .520.
Considering this frequency of correct responses, the probability
that the smallest number of correct responses from 7 groups per-
forming 54 trials each does not exceed 18 (result from group 8T),
is .031 and the probability that the smallest number of correct re-
sponses from 8 groups performing 180 trials each does not exceed
68 (result from group 4C), is .0007. Both probabilities are low and,
even though it cannot be completely ruled out, multiplicity phe-
nomenon seems unlikely.
Another explanation could be uncontrolled or special features
in the experimental set up. A lack of control of the samples is the
major risk in inter-laboratory comparison. For instance, it has been
reported by McEvan, Hunter, van Gemert, and Lea (2002) in a pro-
ﬁciency study carried out on six tomato soups that all laboratories
could not work with the same mixer, which led to some uncon-
trolled differences in texture. To avoid this risk in our study, preli-
minary tests have been conducted by two of the eight laboratories
in order to develop preparation procedures avoiding any problem
of sample heterogeneity.
For group 4C, the low number of correct responses could still
originate from samples. For this group, the duplicated samples
came from two separate drinks preparations and not from the
same batch as for the other groups. This may have induced small
differences between duplicated samples increasing the difﬁculty
level. However, this explanation does not hold because the other
group, managed by the same laboratory (group 4T) with the same
procedure, did not show lower performance compared to the other
groups of selected assessors (Table 1b). Another explanation could
be put forward: an imperfect explanation of the triangle test meth-
od to consumers. In this group, it was introduced with three sheets
of paper, two of the same color and one of a different color. The
difference in color is obvious and may have lead assessors to
expect such a clear difference among samples which is not the case
with confusable taste samples. This explanation is supported by re-
sults collected three months later when the same laboratory repli-
cated the test except for the explanation of the test that was done
with one sample of water and two samples of 16 g/L sucrose. Re-
sults led to reject H0 for each of the three replications at p = .001
(number of correct responses: 34, 33 and 34, for replications 1, 2
and 3, respectively).
For group 8T, the panel leader suggested a potential saturation
of assessors because they carried out six trials in a row. The num-
ber of correct responses actually decreased from trial 1 to trial 6 (5,
5, 2, 2, 2, 2). However such a decrease in performance was not ob-
served for the three other groups of our study whose assessors also
carried out six trials. Moreover, data reported in the literature sug-
gest that fatigue do not occur with such a low number of replica-
tions in the case of soft drinks (Dacremont, Roger, & Sauvageot,
1998; Dacremont & Sauvageot, 1997; Dacremont, Sauvageot, &
Ha Duyen, 2000; Schlich, Dacremont, & Brockhoff, 2000). Thus,
the fatigue hypothesis does not hold.
4.2. Inter groups differences for similarity test
For pooled replications, the eight groups of consumers led to
demonstrate a signiﬁcant similarity for pd = .30 demonstrating a
good agreement among groups. For the selected assessors, only
four groups led to reject H0 and thus, to conclude to similarity. This
discrepancy can be explained by the higher proportion of correct
responses observed for selected assessors compared to consumers
(.434 vs. .402). Consequently, some groups of selected assessors ex-
ceeded the critical number of correct responses leading to reject
H0.

to the present experimental data, may originate from the nature
of tested products and, for instance, the challenging texture of ice
cream. The literature on wine and beer expertise indicates that ex-
perts are only marginally more discriminant than novices (Bende &
Nordin, 1997; Solomon, 1990; Solomon, 1997; Valentin, Chollet,
Beal, & Patris, 2007); performance at difference test would be linked
to stimuli familiarity (Chollet, Valentin, & Abdi, 2005).

partially explained by the especially low frequencies of correct re-
sponses observed at the very ﬁrst trial they performed. For most
group of consumers, the performance increased at replications 2
and 3. This ‘‘incidental training’’ was only observed for grenadine
drinks. For lemon drinks, the vast majority of assessors had already
performed triangle tests with grenadine ﬁrst. This would indicate
that participants who have never performed triangle tests before
need some practice to handle the task. This is in line with observa-
tion that warm-up is efﬁcient only for participants who had al-
ready performed triangle tests previously, even a few trials with
different products (Dacremont et al., 2000). Altogether, these re-
sults indicate that triangle test requires a learning step to handle
the task. Thus, it would be recommended to consider the very ﬁrst
trial consumers or untrained assessors perform as a dummy test
meant for training.
Qualiﬁed and trained assessors were selected assessors. The
selection process may have led to keep only assessors that demon-
strated some special skills relevant to performed triangle test, such
as large mnesic span, for instance. Dacremont and Valentin (2004)
showed that the proportion of correct responses at triangle tests is
linked to participants’ mnesic span, the larger the better. It is pos-
sible that selected assessors had better short-term memory abili-
ties than consumers. Unfortunately, memory span for tastes and/
or odors couldn’t have been measured because it was judged too
time consuming considering the main objective of the study.
4.3.2. Effect on panel heterogeneity
To explain the differences observed between novices and
trained assessors in their ice-cream experiment, Ishii, Kawaguchi,
O’Mahony, and Rousseau (2007) advocated a greater homogeneity
among trained assessors compared to novices. Our results sustain
this idea. The c-values computed for all consumers together, on
one hand, and all selected assessors who performed three replica-
tions, on the other hand, are 0.11 vs. 0.00 for grenadine drinks and
0.23 vs. 0.15 for lemon drinks. Here, only participants performing
three replications (consumers and groups of 18 selected assessors)
are considered. As a matter of fact, these participants received
three triads with the same product as odd sample. Some partici-
pants received product A as odd sample and others product B. This
is expected to slightly increase heterogeneity among assessors,
compared to the case where assessors perform the six possible tri-
ads, because Sequential Sensitivity Analysis (O’Mahony and Od-
bert, 1985) predicts a difference in performance according to the
nature of the odd sample.
Group heterogeneity reported in the present study is relatively
low. They are in accordance with over-dispersion values reported
in the literature (Angulo, Lee & O’Mahony, 2007; Liggett & Delwi-
che, 2005; Schlich et al., 2000), although values are not directly
comparable because they were not computed in the same way.
4.3.3. Outcome of the test according to objective
In the paragraph entitled ‘‘requirements’’, the ISO standard
(2004) states: ‘‘Clearly deﬁne the test objective (triangle testing for
difference or for similarity) in writing’’. This rule, used for a long
time in clinical or pharmaceutical assays, was introduced in the
ISO standard to insure an appropriate data analysis. Actually, in
some occasions, the observed number of correct responses could
lead to opposite conclusions according to the tested hypothesis.
This number is large enough to demonstrate that the two products
are different (pd is signiﬁcantly higher than 0 when testing for dif-
ference) and at the same time low enough to demonstrate that the
two products are similar (pd is lower than the critical pd0 value). In
the present experiment, when tests on lemon drinks with consum-
ers were considered as similarity tests, they lead to signiﬁcant sim-
ilarity at a = .05, but should the tests have being considered as
difference tests, a conclusion of signiﬁcant difference would have
been drawn for ﬁve groups (1C, 3C, 4C, 5C, and 9C) as the number
of correct response is equal to or higher than 71, the critical value
at a = .05. Similarly, for grenadine syrup, results of group 5C would
lead to both signiﬁcant difference and signiﬁcant similarly accord-
ing to the test performed (conﬁdence interval for pd: .09–.27).
Being able to demonstrate both signiﬁcant difference and simil-
itude is rather anecdotal as most often collected data lead to a lack
rather an exceed of power for statistical test. However, this illus-
trates the risk of data misinterpretation when the objective of
the test, testing for difference or similarity, is not well deﬁned
beforehand.
4.3.4. Objective of inter-laboratories comparisons
When several laboratories perform the same test, three opera-
tional objectives are possible: (1) testing the ability of each labora-
tory to run efﬁciently the test; (2) studying the factors that impact
the outcome of the test; (3) examining the dispersion between
laboratories.
When the objective is to test for proﬁciency, each laboratory
should perform the test under its usual conditions, providing it fol-
lows the method-speciﬁc features. For example, in the case of a tri-
angle test, assessors have to pick one sample among the three and
reporting ‘‘no difference’’ is not an option. For such an objective,
the test should be conducted in double-blind; panel leaders do
not know which sample is the odd one. Laboratories are provided
with four coded products and presentation orders. Moreover, labo-
ratories are not informed of the objective of the test (difference or
similarity) and have no cue of the expected level of correct re-
sponses. In this approach, a laboratory is either qualiﬁed or not
according to how its results are close of results of the other labora-
tories. In this case there is no need to explain why results from one
laboratory depart dramatically from the others; it is simply not
qualiﬁed.

When the objective of inter-laboratories comparison is to gain
better understanding of a method, requirements are different.
The allowed variations concerning experimental details should be
strictly controlled. For example, preparation of repeated samples
(from one batch or from two separated batches) should not be left
to the choice of each laboratory. When differences between labora-
tories are observed, it is of main relevance to identify the reasons of
this difference, as this may lead to point out a critical aspect of the
experimental design.
The present experiment is still aimed at a third objective: To ob-
serve the dispersion of the results when laboratories perform trian-
gle tests according to ISO standard. Following this objective,
experimental conditions mentioned in the standard were imposed,
such as presentation orders or the obligation for assessors to pick
up one sample, whereas others were not, such as sample prepara-
tion or instructions given to assessors. Regarding the instructions, a
large diversity was observed among laboratories concerning both
the sensory modalities to be mobilized and the wording of the
instruction. Whilst some leaders did not give any indication, others
indicated that assessors had to observe, smell and taste the sam-
ples. One panel leader indicated to rely only on in-mouth sensa-
tions and that appearance or odor should not be taken into
account; another one indicated that the differences were to be
sought from the odor and the taste, without mentioning aspect.
In addition, some discrepancy among instructions is due to the an-
swers provided by panels leaders to participants’ questions. Prac-
tice showed that a large part of these questions could and should
have been anticipated. For still unexpected questions, responses
should be carefully recorded to allow further analysis should it
be needed. Concerning the instructions, although the ISO standard
states that ‘‘Each assessor should indicate which one of the three
samples is different from the other two’’, in the present experi-
ment, this was expressed in different forms: (1) Which sample
do you perceived as different? (Quel est l’échantillon que vous perc-
evez comme différent ?) (2) identify the odd sample (Identiﬁez
l’échantillon unique) (3) identify the sample that differs from the
two others (Identiﬁez l’échantillon qui diffère des deux autres). These
wordings may have had an unexpected impact on the way partic-
ipants have handled the task. This aspect would be worth being
further investigated.
5. Conclusion
Results from this inter-laboratory study support O’Mahony
(1995) statement: ‘‘Who told you the triangle test is simple?’’.
Two major ﬁndings arise from this experiment. First, a triangle test
implemented by accredited laboratories can still lead to results lar-
gely departing from the average as showed for the difference tests
on grenadine drinks: Two groups are in large disagreement with
the thirteen others. This could not be imputed to any special fea-
ture of the experimental design and multiplicity effect seems
improbable. Second, as the number of correct responses of selected
assessors is barely higher than that of consumers, selection cannot
compensate for a lower number of trials. The number of partici-
pants should be approximately the same, regardless of partici-
pants’ qualiﬁcation.
