dence of ﬁlamentous fungi was observed in the coffee swept from ground and ﬂoating coffee samples.
The species Aspergillus ochraceus, Aspergillus sulphureus and Aspergillus sclerotiorum were ochratoxin A

1. Introduction
Ochratoxin A (OTA) is a mycotoxin considered to be nephro-
toxic, carcinogenic and, as it can cross the placenta, also embryo-
toxic and teratogenic (WHO, 2003). This toxin is a secondary
metabolite produced mainly by Penicillium verrucosum and Penicil-
lium nordicum (Larsen, Svendesen, & Smedsgaard, 2001) and by
several species of the genus Aspergillus, such as Aspergillus ochrac-
eus, Aspergillus sulphureus, Aspergillus carbonarius, Aspergillus niger
and Aspergillus sclerotiorum (Batista, Chalfoun, Prado, Schwan, &
Wheals, 2003; Frisvad, Frank, Houbraken, Kuijpers, & Samson,
2004; Pitt, 2000; Samson, Houbraken, Kuijpers, Frank, & Frisvad,
2004). OTA occurs in various foodstuffs and beverages including
a variety of cereals, beans, coffee, beer, wine, meat, cocoa, dried
fruits, spices, nuts, milk, pig blood and kidney and other tissues
of animal origin (Cast, 2003; Wilson, Mubatanhema, & Jurjevic,
2002). Similarly to other crops, coffee fruits and beans can be con-
taminated by toxigenic fungi, which, besides altering the quality of
coffee (Carvalho & Chalfoun, 1985; Krug, 1940; Krug, 1941) may
present a serious risk of OTA contamination, compromising the
safety of the product. In Brazil, the presence of OTA in coffee beans
has been vigorously evaluated with contamination levels varying
signiﬁcantly (Batista et al., 2003; Gollücke, Taniwaki, & Tavares,
2004; Moraes & Luchese, 2004; Taniwaki, Pitt, Teixeira, & Iama-
naka, 2003; Urbano, Taniwaki, Leitão, & Vicentini, 2001).
The skin (husk) of coffee beans is a signiﬁcant source of OTA,
thus cleaning and standardization methods are effective in reduc-
ing OTA levels. During the industrial process of converting coffee
beans into roasted coffee and soluble coffee, a reduction of up to
90% may occur in OTA levels (Viani, 2002).
In view of the various ecological parameters involved in the bio-
synthesis OTA and of the various types of coffee produced by dif-
ferent processing methods and localities, this study aimed to
evaluate the incidence of the ochratoxigenic fungi and OTA in cof-
fee (Coffea arabica L.) beans at different stages of maturation and
processing, which, in this study, were called fractions (product por-
tions with differentiated characteristics) in several southern Minas
Gerais locations.
Deﬁnitions – according to guidelines for the prevention of
mould formation in coffee (www.ecf-coffee.org).
Fractions: product with differentiated characteristics in the cof-
fee processing.
Floating coffee: over ripe fruit coffee separated by virtue of it
being positively buoyant in water applied to a one-pass stripping
harvest system.
Coffee cherry: fruit coffee ripe, maturation ideal point.
Cherry deshusking: cherry fruit coffee without husk (exocarp)
obtained by wet processing.
Cherry despulping: cherry fruit coffee without mucilage (meso-
carp) removed mechanical treatment obtained by wet processing.

2. Material and methods
2.1. Sampling collection
2.1.1. Sample (1) for mycological analysis
The samples consisted of approximately 5 Kg of coffee fruits
(C. arabica L), collected in up to ﬁve farms in 10 southern Minas
Gerais municipalities. The fractions (product with differentiated
characteristics) collected were ﬂoating coffee (17), cherry + non-
ripe (5), cherry deshusking (12), mixed (45), coffee swept from
ground (44) and non-ripe (4). Of the 127 samples analyzed, fruits
and their respective beans were analyzed in 104 samples and
only the beans in the remainder 23 samples, since they had been
collected already processed. The samples were randomly col-
lected from the yard, storage, coffee plantation, and bags, with
the most homogeneous samples obtained being sent to EPAMIG,
Technological Center of Southern Minas Gerais (Lavras, MG,
Brazil) for microbiological evaluation of the fruits and beans after
drying and processing.
2.1.2. Sample (2) for ochratoxin A analysis
The samples consisted of approximately 2.5 Kg of coffee (C arab-
ica L.) beans, collected from up to 5 farms from 11 southern Minas
Gerais municipalities. The fractions collected were ﬂoating coffee
(35), cherry (4), cherry + non-ripe (11), cherry deshusking (18),
cherry despulping (2), mixed (97), fruit dried on the tree (4), coffee
swept from ground (106) and non-ripe (12), totaling 289 analyzed
samples. The fractions containing fruits (cherry, cherry + non-ripe,
non-ripe, mixed) were oven dried at 40 ± 2 C until reaching a
moisture content between 11% and 12%. After processing, the sam-
ples were sent to the Laboratory of Quality Control and Food
Safety/LACQSA of the Ministry of Agriculture (Belo Horizonte,
MG, Brazil), for OTA analysis. As a result of the sampling method-
ology used by LACQSA, each sample 1 originated two sample 2,
totally 254 samples, with the remainder sample 2 (35) being
collected in another municipality and at the same locations
studied.
2.2. Isolation and identiﬁcation of fungi
For isolation of the fungi associated with the processed coffee
beans, direct plating technique was applied in DG18 culture med-
ium (glucose 10 g, peptone 5.0 g, KH2PO4 1.0 g, MgSO47H2O 0.5 g,
glycerol 178.0 g, agar 15.0 g, chloranphenycol 100.0 mg, distilled
water 1 l and dicloran 1 ml of a solution 0.2% weight/volume in
ethanol), according to Pereira, Frank, and Pfenning (2003).
2.2.1. Identiﬁcation of ﬁlamentous fungi of the Aspergillus genus
The following species were identiﬁed from pure cultures: spe-
cies of the genus Aspergillus Section Circundati, according to Chris-
tensen (1982), species of the Section Flavi according to Christensen
(1981), and species of Section Nigri, Nidulantes, Fumigati, Versicolor-
es and Usti, according to Klich (2002), with these identiﬁcations
being supported by Raper and Fennell (1965), Samson, Hoekstra,
Frisvad, and Filtenborg (2000) and Pitt and Hocking (1997).
The isolates were incubated in medium CYA – Czapek Yeast
Agar (K2HPO4 1.0 g, concentrate Czapek 10.0 ml, yeast extract
5.0 g, agar 15.0 g, distilled water 1 L. Concentrate Czapek NaNO3
30.0 g, KCl 5.0 g, MgSO4.7H2O 5.0 g, FeSO4.7H2O 0.1 g, ZnSO4.7H2O
0.1 g, CuSO45H2O 0.05 g, distilled water 100 ml) and MEA (malt
extract 20.0 g, peptone 1.0 g, glucose 30.0 g, agar 20 g, distilled
water 1 L) at 25 C and CYA at 37 C. After incubation for 7 days,
the microscopic and macroscopic characteristics described by Klich
(2002) were observed.
2.3. Determination of OTA-producing fungi by the method Plug Agar
The isolates tested were inoculated in medium YES – Yeast
Extract Sucrose Agar (yeast extract 20.0 g, sucrose 150.0 g, agar
20.0 g, MgSO47H2O 0.5 g, distilled water–1 l) with metallic solu-
tion for 7 days at 25–26 C, according to Filtenborg and Frisvad
(1980). The following was used: ochratoxin A standard (Sigma-
Aldrich), thin-layer chromatography plates (Merk-Silica Gel 60,
20  20) as mobile phase, TEF- toluene ethyl acetate and formic
Acid 90% (50:40:10). After elution, the plates were dried by air ﬂux.
Mycotoxin production was conﬁrmed in ultraviolet light with k
366 nm in chromatovisor CAMAG (UF-BETRACHTER). The isolates
considered as OTA producers presented an RF (retention factor)
and a ﬂuorescence spot similar to that of OTA standard.
2.4. Analysis of OTA in coffee beans
The OTA analyses were carried out by High Performance Liquid
Chromatography (HPLC) at the Laboratory of Quality Control and
Food Safety/LACQSA according to publication in the Ofﬁcial Union
Journal (DOU, 2000).
Extraction: was performed with methanol—sodium bicarbonate
3% (1:1) under mechanical stirring.
Puriﬁcation: Puriﬁcation was performed in immunoafﬁnity col-
umns (Ochratest-Vicam).
Quantiﬁcation by High Performance Liquid Chromatography: was
carried out by using a Liquid chromatograph (Shimadzu) in
Shimpack column C18 CLC ODS (M) 250  4.6 mm as mobile phase
acetonytril:methanol:water:acetic acid (35:35:29:1, v/v/v/v) with
ﬂux of 0.8 ml/min, ﬂuorescence detector with excitation ﬁlter in

2.5. Statistical analysis
To correlate the levels of OTA contamination with the different
coffee fractions, simple correspondence analysis was used, as de-
scribed by Greenacre (1993). This technique consists of applying
the main components such as the contingency table, in this case,
a table displaying the contamination level frequency in one line
and the coffee fractions in columns. The proportion of the coffee
sample totals corresponds to the proﬁle of the variables displayed
in the column. Analogously, the proﬁle for the variables lines is
produced. Regarding the comparison analyses of the fractions
ﬂoating coffee, mixed, and coffee swept from ground versus cof-
fee yard types (cement, ground and asphalt), conﬁdence intervals
were performed for the differences between the two ratios with
the signiﬁcance level ﬁxed at 5%. The results were presented
similarly to the Tukey test with the letter display. The software
MINITAB 13.20 was used for the statistical analyses performed
in this study.

3. Results
3.1. Incidence of Aspergillus spp in the different coffee fractions
Species of fungi belonging to the genus Aspergillus Section Cir-
cumdati and Section Nigri were found in the samples of coffee fruits
and beans at all harvest and processing stages. Aspergillus Seção
Circumdati was detected in 80% and 41% of the coffee fruit and bean
sample, respectively. However, a signiﬁcant reduction in the popu-
lation of this fungi group was observed after the beginning of the
processing. Of the 12 bean samples processed by wet method,
33% were contaminated with fungi of the genus Aspergillus Seção
Circumdati.
Species belonging to the genus Aspergillus, Section Nigri were
detected in 73% of the coffee fruits and 25% of the processed beans.
In 73% and 65% of the coffee swept from ground and ﬂoating coffee
fruit samples, respectively, the presence of these fungi was also ob-
served. Of the 12 analyzed bean samples preprocessed via wet
method, 25% presented contamination with fungi of the genus
Aspergillus Seção Nigri.
Of the 178 fungi of the genus Aspergillus Section Circumdati
identiﬁed (165 A. ochraceus, 03 A. melleus, 04 A. suphureus, 02
A. dimorphicus, 03 A. sclerotiorum e 01 A. auricomus) 85% were och-
ratoxin A producing fungi. Of the isolates of A. ochraceus, 95% were
ochratoxin A producing fungi. The other ochratoxin A-producing
species were A. sulphureus (1 in 4) and A. sclerotiorum (2 in 3). Of
the species of Section Circumdati, A. ochraceus was the most
common, accounting for around 93% of the isolate total.
Of the species of Aspergillus Section Nigri, A. niger (58) and
Aspergillus foetidus (25) none of the isolates were ochratoxin A
producer.

3.3. Analysis of the fractions ﬂoating coffee, mixed and coffee swept
from ground versus coffee yard types at different levels of OTA
contamination
The statistical analysis of the fractions coffee swept from
ground, mixed and ﬂoating coffee versus coffee yard type was con-
ducted by comparing the conﬁdence intervals for the difference of
the two ratios, considering a signiﬁcance level of 5%. As shown in
Table 5, most coffee swept from ground, mixed and ﬂoating coffee
samples were dried in an asphalt coffee yard, accounting for 51%,
74% and 80%, respectively. The ground (dusty) coffee yard was
the second most used coffee yard while the cement coffee yard
was the less used on the farms evaluated.
Practically no signiﬁcant difference among coffee yard types
was observed in the fraction coffee swept from ground, for the
samples contaminated above 5.0 lg/Kg, suggesting that the coffee
swept from ground samples had already been infected with toxi-
genic fungi or even OTA when brought onto the coffee yard, with
the likelihood of becoming aggravated if the sample is not properly
dried. A similar result was observed in the samples with contami-
nation levels between 0.1 lg/Kg and 5.0 lg/Kg, with no difference
between the ground and cement coffee yards, what is apparently
contrary to the recommendations of Good Agricultural Practices
which state that cement coffee yards favor coffee production with
a lower microorganism infection index, leading to a better quality
product.
A signiﬁcant difference was observed in the fraction mixed be-
tween the asphalt and ground coffee yards, with a larger number of
ground coffee yard samples presenting contamination at levels
from 5.1 lg/Kg to 20.0 lg/Kg and between contamination levels
20.01 and >100.00 lg/Kg, compared to asphalt coffee yard samples.
4. Discussion
Fungi of the genus Aspergillus Section Circumdati and Section
Nigri were common in the environment of the coffee plantations
sampled, although their presence was smaller in the coffee sam-
ples processed via wet method. Of the species of the Section
Circumdati, A. ochraceus was the most common, accounting for
around 93% of the isolate total. This species has been identiﬁed
in other coffee fruit and bean studies (Batista et al., 2003; Silva,
Schwan, Dias, & Wheals, 2000; Suárez-Quiroz et al., 2004; Tani-
waki et al., 2003). The remainder Aspergillus species identiﬁed were
also observed in coffee beans, such as Aspergillus melleus (Batista,
Chalfoun, & Prado, 2001), A. suphureus (Batista et al., 2001), Asper-
gillus auricomus (Batista et al., 2001), A. sclerotiorum (Batista et al.,
2001) and Aspergillus dimorphicus (Silva et al., 2000).
Among the isolates identiﬁed, such as A. niger and A. foetidus,
neither was a producer of OTA. These non-toxin producing species
were also reported in beans by Batista et al. (2003) and Silva et al.
(2000). The remainder species of Aspergillus Section Nigri identiﬁed

Meyer, Pittet, Vuataz, & Viani, 1998; Suárez-Quiroz et al., 2004),
Aspergillus ustus (Batista et al., 2001; Mislivec et al., 1983), Aspergil-

1983), Aspergillus versicolor (Batista et al., 2001; Batista et al., 2003;
Mislivec et al., 1983) and Aspergillus nidulans (Mislivec et al., 1983).
Studies on the incidence of OTA in coffee beans have shown a
great concentration range between countries as well as within
the same coffee-producing area. The percentage of contaminated
samples has varied from 22% to 65%, (Batista et al., 2003; Bucheli
et al., 1998; Moraes & Luchese, 2004; Nakajima, Tsubouchi, Miya-
be, & Ueno, 1997; Taniwaki et al., 2003). Similar variation occurred
with samples with values above 5.0 lg/Kg of coffee. According to
Batista et al. (2003), none of 40 samples analyzed presented con-
tamination above 5 lg/Kg. In one of the farms studied by Moraes
and Luchese (2004), of 14 samples analyzed, 1 (7%) presented con-
tamination above 5 lg/Kg. Taniwaki et al. (2003) detected contam-
ination above this limit in 9 of 135 samples.
The majority of coffee types cherry, cherry despulping, cherry
deshusking, non-ripe and dried on the plant showed OTA contam-
ination up to 5 lg/Kg. The coffee fractions mixed, ﬂoating coffee
and, mainly, coffee swept from ground, presented the highest
OTA contamination levels, above the 5 lg/Kg limit.
The results obtained in this study showed that the percentage of
contaminated samples as well as the levels above 5.0 lg/Kg were
inﬂuenced by the type of coffee fraction analyzed, i.e., ﬂoating cof-
fee, coffee swept from ground, cherry, and by type of processing,
i.e., cherry deshusking and cherry despulping.
In all the fractions analyzed, a varied percentage of non-
contaminated samples occurred despite the presence of OTA–
producing fungi being identiﬁed even in higher risk coffee fractions,
such as ﬂoating coffee and, mainly, coffee swept from ground. This
may have occurred due to: (1) environmental and/or microbial
population conditions not favoring the development of ochratoxi-
genic fungi and OTA production; (2) once the bean was colonized,
the conditions favorable for OTA production (water activity, tem-
perature, substrate, microbiota, and others) did not occur and if
they did occur, there was no sufﬁcient time and/or nutrients for
OTA synthesis; and (3) the chemical composition of the coffee
beans, including compounds such as caffeine and chlorogenic acid,
acted as a natural barrier, a fact extensively studied (Chalfoun,
Pereira, & Angélico, 2000; Suárez-Quiroz et al., 2004) in isolates
genetically more sensitive to this chemical composition.
Coffee swept from ground coffee is considered by researchers
and producers as a low quality coffee (Souza & Carvalho, 1997)
and it was made evident in this study that this harvest technique
also represents a safety risk due to higher fungi proliferation, com-
bined with OTA production. Due to contact with soil, which is a
natural source of toxigenic fungi, including OTA producers, the
fraction coffee swept from ground is composed of fruits displaying
a larger number of defects and, consequently, a greater risk of OTA
contamination. It was observed in this work that there was a larger
number of coffee swept from ground coffee samples contaminated
and higher OTA values. Similar results were observed by Moraes
and Luchese (2004), who found that the highest incidence of con-
taminated samples occurred in the coffee swept from ground
coffee.
Beans from the fraction ﬂoating coffee had a higher contamina-
tion risk since they originated from malformed and injured fruits
due to more exposure to climatic changes in the plantation and
to microbial contamination throughout the coffee productive and
preprocessing cycles. Bucheli, Meyer, Pittet, Vuataz, and Viani
(2000), analyzing robusta coffee, indicated that there is a greater
risk of OTA contamination in damaged cherry fruits than in healthy
cherry fruits, which normally do not show contents of OTA. The
coffee swept from ground and ﬂoating coffee groups represent a
higher risk of OTA presence above the limit of 5.0 lg/Kg of coffee.
Due to their origin, these groups have their safety compromised,
with a high risk of OTA contamination.
The coffee beans from the fraction mixed also present a food
security risk, since there are fruits in this fraction sorted out by
the hydraulic process, originating the ﬂoating coffee (a type pre-
senting a great risk for OTA occurrence). Thus, the presence of
OTA in the mixed type coffee may have been due to the presence
of fruits that would originate the fraction ﬂoating coffee.
Since the fraction cherry+non-ripe was generated only on the
farms performing hydraulic separation of the fruits, these samples
were collected on the coffee yard. Although part of the contamina-
tion level may have occurred in the ﬁeld, it can also be inferred that
type and conditions of the coffee yard where these samples were
collected may have signiﬁcantly inﬂuenced the fungi contamina-
tion and OTA concentration detected. The coffee beans collected
at the cherry+non-ripe stages, which were adequately post-harvest
processed using the wet method, fermented and dried may have
their risk of fungi contamination reduced and, consequently, at
present no or very low levels of OTA.
The presence of OTA was not detected in any of the four sam-
ples of fruits dried on the plant. All samples were collected in the
same location (Pratinha-MG, Brazil), whose normally dry coffee
harvest period and low air humidity may be the factors responsible
for the absence of OTA.
Of the four cherry fruit samples analyzed, 100% had contamina-
tion below 5.0 lg/Kg of coffee beans with one sample presenting
contamination of 0.22 lg/Kg. These results were similar to those
obtained by Taniwaki et al. (2003), according to whom, of six sam-
ples analyzed, the contamination mean was 0.2 lg/Kg with the val-
ues ranging from non-detected to 0.4 lg/Kg of coffee beans.
Bucheli et al. (2000) observed that the non-ripe and cherry coffee
samples had only traces of contamination with OTA, with an aver-
age of 0.3 lg/Kg. When working with robusta coffee, Joosten,
Goetz, Pittet, Schellenberg, and Bucheli (2001) observed that OTA
was not detected in the recently harvested fruit samples; however,
after 5 and10 days of fermentation and drying, the OTA amount
was 1.7 and 6.9 lg/Kg, respectively. Bucheli and Taniwaki (2002)
noted that, in the cherry fruits, the skin particles generated after
drying and processing containing OTA may be mixed with the
healthy beans, compromising the ﬁnal quality of the coffee.

Regarding the samples processed by wet method, the results
showed that the removal of coffee husk (cherry dehusking) or of
the husk and mucilage (cherry despulping) reduced bean contam-
ination, thus indicating that fungi and OTA contamination would
be concentrated in the skin until before processing. Frank (1999)
reported that pulping would signiﬁcantly reduce the risk of OTA
contamination
during
subsequent
fermentation
and
dry-
ing.According to Bucheli et al. (2000), coffee bean skin is the main
substrate for the development of ochratoxigenic fungi. Besides
eliminating a number of OTA–producing microorganisms, skin re-
moval accelerates drying, decreasing the risk of fungi development
and OTA production. However, the initial quality of the fruits har-
vested, the presence of OTA-producing fungi as well as the process-
ing site conditions can certainly contribute to the formation OTA in
coffee processing by wet method (Bucheli & Taniwaki, 2002).
The groups formed by the fractions mixed, cherry, non-ripe,
dried, cherry deshusking and cherry despulping were classiﬁed as
having a lower risk of contamination by OTA.
The results indicated that the asphalt coffee yard presented less
risk than the ground coffee yard for the ﬂoating coffee samples,
probably due to the fact that these samples, still humid, remained
in contact with the soil, favoring infection and OTA synthesis by
the ochratoxigenic fungi, a condition less favored by the asphalt
coffee yard, proving that soil seems to be the major source of och-
ratoxigenic fungi (Frank, 1999).
No signiﬁcant difference among coffee yard types was found in
the fraction coffee swept from ground for the samples contami-
nated above 5.0 lg/Kg. These results suggest that by the time the
coffee swept from ground samples were sent to the coffee yard,
they already have a degree of toxigenic fungi or even OTA infection,
which may be increased if the sample is not correctly processed. A
similar result was observed in samples with contamination levels
between 0.1 lg/Kg and 5.0 lg/Kg, with no difference between the
ground and cement coffee yards, what apparently disagrees with
the recommendations of the Good Agricultural Practices that ce-
ment coffee yards favor coffee beans with lower microorganism
contamination index, providing a better quality product.
Most mixed coffee samples were dried in asphalt coffee yard
and there was a signiﬁcant difference between the asphalt and
ground type coffee yards, with the latter presenting a larger num-
ber of samples contaminated at levels between 5.1 lg/Kg and
20.0 lg/Kg and
between contamination levels of 20.01 and
>100.00 lg/Kg than the former. Moraes and Luchese (2004) also
observed a higher concentration of OTA in coffee samples dried
in ground coffee yards than in those dried in cement coffee yards.
According to the authors, coffee processing in cement platform is
important in preventing OTA contamination, while coffee beans
processed in ground (dusty) coffee yard are more favorable to con-
tamination. In contrast, Bucheli et al. (2000) observed that the
presence of OTA was lower in coffee dried in ground coffee yards
(1.4 lg/Kg) than in cement coffee yards (4.3 lg/Kg). However, the
authors stated that coffee fruit quality, climatic conditions during
drying, drying procedure, ochratoxin A-producing fungi inoculum,
and farm conditions are undoubtedly more important than type of
fermentation and drying platforms.
5. Conclusions
Based on the results obtained from the ochratoxin analyses in
the different types of samples, it was concluded that: (1) the frac-
tions bóia and varrição presented the highest levels of exposure to
risk of contamination by toxigenic fungi and OTA; (2) the fractions
bóia and mixes presented higher levels of contamination with OT A
when dried in ground patio rather than in asphalt. (3) the fraction
coffee swept from ground, regardless of type of yard (asphalt,
ground or cement), did not present any relation with the levels
of contamination with OTA. The present study showed that har-
vesting and preprocessing operations generate fractions (product
with differentiated characteristics) and coffee types presenting dif-
ferent risks of exposure to contamination by toxigenic fungi and
OTA.
The highest risk of exposure to contamination was character-
ized by fruit contact with the soil, constituted by the fraction coffee
swept from ground, and by inadequate post-harvest handling of
the product during drying in ground patios. Ground patios must
be avoided, since soil is the natural habitat of ochratoxigenic fungi
and other microorganisms as well.
The adoption of the Danger and Critical Control Points Analysis
System (APPCC) and Good Agricultural Practices (BPA) will signiﬁ-
cantly inﬂuence not only the reduction of microorganism contam-
ination risk under the conditions of coffee fruit and bean
deterioration but also the reduction of OTA.
