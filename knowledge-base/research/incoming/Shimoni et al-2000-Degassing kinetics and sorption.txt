ABSTRACT 
Carbon dioxide produced by both the Strecker degradation reaction 
(Maillard reaction) and carbohydrate pyrolysis during high temperature roasting 
of coffee, and trapped in the fresh roasted and ground (FRG) coffee, is one of 
the major problem in the industry. Coffee solids can entrap or sorb up to I0 mL 
CO,/g. The eventual evolution of this carbon dioxide during storage afer 
packaging in flexible films can cause the bursting of the package. A good 
understanding of the mechanism that governs carbon-dioxide evolution and 
equifibrium in FRG coffee will provide true control over carbon dioxide release 
and equilibrium in the package. The objective of this study was to establish the 
equilibrium parameters of carbon-dioxide sorption/desorption, anddetermine the 
rate of carbon-dioxide diffusion from the ground coffee. Carbon dioxide sorption 
in FRG coffee is best described by the Lungmuir isothenn. A higher degree of 
roasting resulted in an increase of CO, sorption in the coffee. The degassing 
kinetics of CO, from FRG coffee followed an exponential pattern, described by 
a series expansion function, which forms the basis for the simplified Crank 
solution. The range of difisivities of the various roasts was at IOl4 to IOl5 m2h. 
The temperature effect on the diffusivity can be described by the Arrhenius 
equation, with activation energy of 73.6 kJ/mole.

INTRODUCTION 
Carbon dioxide is the most important nonaromatic volatile compound other 
than oxygen found in roasted coffee. It is generated by pyrolysis of sugars and 
the Strecker degradation reaction (Hodge 1953). The amount is dependent on the 
degree of roast, and can be up to 10 mL (SPT) of carbon dioxide per gram of 
coffee, although 2-5 mL (SPT) is usually reported (Barbera 1967; Clarke 1987; 
Massini ef al. 1990; Meister and Puhlmann 1989). Thus if the maximum amount 
in the coffee is about 10 mL after roasting, than as Sivetz and Desrosier (1979) 
reported, the final roasted bean will be almost 2% by weight of carbon dioxide. 
During coffee grinding, a significant amount of the carbon dioxide produced 
during roasting can be released. This loss is most likely due to disruption of the 
regions in which the gas is entrapped, as well as decreasing the path length for 
diffusion. Heiss ef al. (1977) reported that 45% of the carbon dioxide held after 
roasting is released during the first 5 min after grinding. Barbera (1967) 
demonstrated that over 70% of the carbon dioxide is released following coffee 
grinding into 500 pm particles (sieve size No. 35). However, a significant 
amount of CO, is still retained in the coffee a at the time of packaging, i.e. 2-3 
One of the major problems in the fresh roasted ground (FRG) coffee 
industry is the eventual evolution of carbon dioxide from the ground roasted 
beans during storage after packaging, which can cause the bursting of the 
package if it is made from a flexible film. Thus many methods have been 
developed to resolve this problem including various conditioning steps prior to 
packaging to enhance release of the carbon dioxide as well as vacuum 
packaging, gas flushing with carbon dioxide to create an equilibrium situation 
in which no gas evolves, use of films or partial film patches highly permeable 
to carbon dioxide and packages with pressure relief valves of different designs 
(Radtke 1973; Heiss er al. 1977). It should be noted that due to the extreme 
sensitivity of FRG coffee to oxygen, any holding time in the presence of air 
during conditioning prior to packaging is extremely detrimental with a loss of 
10% of shelf-life per day (Heiss et al. 1977; Cardelli-Freire 1997). Thus there 
is a need to quickly desorb the CO, and then remove any residual oxygen from 
the package. In order to do so, there is a need to establish the parameters of 
carbon dioxide desorption kinetics and equilibrium conditions so as to provide 
good control over carbon dioxide release and final concentration in the package. 
From a physico-chemical standpoint, the amount of CO, held at equilibrium 
in the fresh roasted ground state will be a function of both solubility of the gas 
in the oil, solubility in the low moisture content (1 to 5% water), and adsorption 
to -OH sites and -NH, sites. These all are equilibrium processes but there can 
be an additional amount sorbed which is the amount entrapped in amorphous 
collapsed zones in the particle which is a nonequilibrium process. To date, there

Particle Size Distribution 
The particle size distribution was measured in a Malvern Particle and 
Droplet Laser Beam Sizer (Malvern, Worchester, England). Samples of 20 g 
roasted and ground coffee were weighed to the milligram and suspended in 100 
mL butanol to remove oil from the surface of the particle and prevent false 
readings due to adherence of particles. The mean particle diameter is given by 
De Brouckere mean diameter D(4,3), and the Sauter diameter D(3,2) was used 
for the mass transfer calculations (Lefebvre 1989). The De Brouckere mean 
diameter D(4,3) is used for reporting the particle size in all the experiments 
other than the diffusion measurements in which the D(3,2) is used. 
Carbon Dioxide Content 
Carbon-dioxide content was determined by the method of Hinman (1993). 
The ground coffee sample was introduced to one port of 3 port distillation flask 
(250 mL) containing 100 mL. of tri-sodium citrate solution (50 g/L tri-sodium 
citrate in a 0.25 M NaOH and the second port of the flask was connected to the 
condenser. An Ascarite trap (Thomas Scientific, Swedesboro, NJ) was weighed 
to 0.1 mg and fixed to the condenser. A syringe containing 50 mL 2.5 M H,SO, 
was connected to the third port, and nitrogen flow was regulated to -60 
mL/min. Then, H,SO, solution was slowly introduced into the flask. The flask 
was heated to 50-70C and the coffee was extracted for 1 h while stirring with 
a magnetic stirrer. The Ascarite trap was weighed to f 0.1 mg and the amount 
of CO, extracted from the coffee was measured by the weight gain of the 
column. 
Moisture Content 
Moisture content of the roasted and ground coffee was measured by 
grinding a cooled roasted coffee sample, and measuring weight loss following 
drying in an oven at 102C for 2 h (2 replicates). 
Density of FRG Coffee 
The density of FRG coffee (3 replicates) was measured by a multi 
pycnometer MVP-SDC (Quantachrome Co., Oynton Beach, FL). Samples were

cell was evacuated by a vacuum pump, and the density was measured using 
Helium as the displacement gas. Bulk density was measured by the volume of 
a weighed coffee sample in a graduated cylinder.

Degassing Kinetics 
A special apparatus was built for the degassing experiments (Fig. 1). A 90 
mm radius glass filter supported the coffee sample while a constant nitrogen gas 
flow (- 25 mL/min) maintained an oxygen free environment. An Ascarite 
column trapped the carbon dioxide that evolved from the coffee into the flushing 
gas. The amount of evolved CO, was measured by weighing the Ascarite 
column. Final CO, content in the coffee was determined using the original 
Ascarite procedure. The sum of evolved and final CO, content was used as the 
initial CO, of the coffee.

interaction with carbon dioxide. Electron microscopy was used to study the

for carbon dioxide in water at 20C is 3 . 4 5 ~ 1 0 . ~

contribute about 0.0017 mL CO,/g coffee for every 0.1 atm of CO, pressure. 
The results of the current study, in which moisture never exceeded 2.4%, show 
that the low water content of FRG coffee cannot account for the bulk of CO, 
sorption. 
Carbon dioxide is also very soluble in the coffee oil, which is about 10 to 
16% of the solids weight (Sivetz and Desrosier 1979). Unfortunately, no data 
exists for the actual solubility in oil as a function of CO, pressure. Radtke and 
Heiss (1975) reported the release of 0.45 mL/g from decaffeinated coffee, and 
0.38 mL/g from nondecaffeinated sample, and suggested that this is a result of 
porosity differences: 0.38 - nondecaffeinated and 0.49 - decaffeinated. Also, 
Saleeb (1976) indicated that (at -78.3C) the equilibrium pressure at which a 
massive CO, uptake occurs in decaffeinated coffee (450 mm Hg) is more than 
100 mm lower than that found with not-decaffeinated coffee (600 mm Hg). It 
should be noted that both these studies report higher adsorption by the ‘fat free’ 
samples. Thus it is likely that more surface is available for gas adsorption in 
defatted coffee. However, coffee oil is very similar in its composition to other 
vegetable oils. Tomoto and Kusano (1967) determined the solubility of carbon 
dioxide in vegetable oils as 1.018 mL CO, (NTP)/ mL oil (at 30C, 1 atm CO,). 
Assuming that the oil content in FRG coffee is - 15%, then the CO, fraction 
adsorbed to the oil in FRG coffee would be 0.02 mL CO,/g coffee for every 0.1 
atm carbon dioxide. This fraction, as with that in the water, is still only a minor 
portion of the total CO, adsorbed in FRG coffee. 
As previously noted, the physical sorption of carbon dioxide in FRG coffee 
is very likely in a sorption/desorption equilibrium with the surface sites in 
addition to the minor absorption in water and oil, and possible nonequilibrium 
entrapped. Hinman (1993) reported CO, sorption isotherms for FRG coffee. He 
found a linear relationship, and extrapolated the line to 0 partial pressure which 
gave 0.2 mg COJg absorption at room temperature. Hinman suggested, CO, 
sorption to coffee can be described by Henry’s law, however, he measured 
sorption at the range of 0.07 to 0.4 atm CO,, where both Langmuir and BET 
equations may fit a linear function. It should be noted, that the amounts of CO, 
adsorbed to coffee reported in his work (0.18 to 0.7 mg CO,/g) are at the range 
found in our present work. Saleeb (1976) obtained CO, sorption/desorption 
isotherms at -78.3C and found that roughly 0.5 mL CO,/g was retained in the 
coffee. In addition, the extrapolation of particle-size/adsorbed CO, made by 
Barbera (1967) suggests that coffee ground to a particle size tending to zero still

temperature. Possible explanations of this phenomena may be that the collapsed 
structures, in which the gas is entrapped, are opened due to changes in the 
viscosity of the oil or to a state change of the carbohydrate polymers. However, 
there is no data that can support either of these theories. 
Illy (1997) recently reported a technique to prolong shelf-life of espresso 
coffee based on nitrogen and carbon dioxide pressurization. This helps to 
maintain C02 inside the coffee cells, avoiding cell wall damage, package 
ballooning and as suggested, flavor release, but no data were presented to 
support this hypothesis. It is obvious that if the CO, pressure in the headspace 
is increased to that of the equilibrium value for the sorbed gas, then release 
should be zero (i.e. the definition of equilibrium) unless the amount of trapped 
gas is large. 
Our study suggests, that the release of CO, from FRG coffee can be 
described as diffusion of C02 out of a sphere under unsteady state conditions. 
In addition, the Langmuir equation can be a useful model for the carbon-dioxide 
sorption-desorption equilibrium that occurs in the package after it has been 
sealed. The data collected in this study may support the design of degassing 
processes as well as packaging procedures to achieve the desired CO, pressure 
in a package of FRG coffee.
