A B S T R A C T
Biochar is a promising amendment with diverse environmental and agronomic applications by improving soil 
properties, contributing to climate change mitigation, and promoting a circular economy. This study evaluates 
the effects of biochar from coffee pulp-CP (Coffea arabica) and oil palm empty fruit bunch-OPEFB (Elaeis gui­
neensis) on soil properties and kidney bean growth under controlled conditions. Polymers of each biomass were 
measured, and biochar was produced at different temperatures (250, 300, 350 ◦C) and applied to dry tropical 
soils at three doses (10, 20, 30 % (w/w)). After 30 days, soil physicochemical parameters and plant growth 
variables were measured. The data were analyzed to determine the variability and relationships between soil and 
plant growth characteristics following biochar treatments. Results showed that treatments increased soil mois­
ture, porosity, cation exchange capacity (CEC), soil organic carbon (SOC), and macro and micronutrients while 
decreasing bulk density. OPEFB biochar progressively increased soil pH and EC, whereas CP biochar remained 
relatively constant. Higher temperatures in CP biochar improved plant growth, while OPEFB biochar had the 
opposite effect. Coffee pulp biochar is associated with improved soil porosity, SOC, N, Fe, Ca, Mn, Zn, Cu, and 
plant growth, while OPEFB biochar is related to increments in soil moisture, EC, pH, P, K, S, Mg, and Na. 
Applying biochar, especially from coffee pulp, improved soil properties, suggesting its potential use as a soil 
amendment in dry areas. However, the contrasting effect on plant growth highlights the importance of 
considering soil and crop needs when selecting the appropriate biomass and pyrolysis conditions.
1. Introduction
Biochar, a carbon-rich product usually derived from the pyrolysis of 
plant biomass, is a versatile material known for its agricultural and 
environmental benefits. Its most common use is as a soil amendment 
since it promotes plant growth and increases crop yields by influencing 
pH and cation exchange capacity (CEC) while improving structural 
stability, moisture content, and nutrient availability (Guo et al., 2016; 
Hou, 2021). Additionally, biochar application has been shown to 
enhance soil organic carbon sequestration and significantly reduce 
greenhouse gas emissions, contributing to climate change mitigation 
(Roberts et al., 2010; Zhao et al., 2020). Biochar also supports beneficial 
microbial communities (Jien et al., 2021), immobilizes heavy metals 
(Khan et al., 2020; Madrid and Marrugo-Negrete, 2021), degrades toxic 
compounds (Fang et al., 2016; Huang et al., 2018), minimizes soil 
erosion (Wani et al., 2023), and mitigates soil salinity (Alam et al., 
2020).
In addition to these agronomic and environmental benefits, the 
properties of biochar are highly dependent on the type of biomass used 
and the conditions under which it is produced. Historically, biochar has 
been produced from various biomass sources, each imparting distinct 
physicochemical characteristics as the result of the char formation, 
depolymerization, and fragmentation processes that occur during the 
pyrolysis of cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin (Collard and Blin, 2014). 
It suggests that a wide range of agricultural biomass residues can be 
utilized to produce biochar, providing huge potential to close resource 
cycles and promote a circular economy with value-added products while 
reducing environmental impacts (Wurzer et al., 2022). In addition, 
applying biochar can alleviate soil physical limitations, improve soil 
fertility, and recover salt-affected soils in drylands (Nascimento et al., 
2023). These ecosystems are characterized by low organic matter con­
tent and face desertification and degradation caused by continuous

The urgency to restore degraded soils is particularly acute in regions 
with limited organic matter and increasing vulnerability to climate ex­
tremes. Biochar provides a promising solution, especially when derived 
from abundant local biomass sources. In Colombia, 78.9 % of dry areas 
are affected by some level of desertification, mainly driven by erosion 
and salinization (MinAmbiente, 2004). At the same time, the 
agro-industrial sector produces substantial volumes of organic byprod­
ucts, which are often poorly managed, leading to environmental 
degradation and missed opportunities to enhance soil health and agri­
cultural productivity (Pe˜naranda et al., 2020; Vargas and P´erez, 2018). 
Among these byproducts, coffee pulp (CP) and oil palm empty fruit 
bunches (OPEFB) stand out due to their abundance and potential for 
valorization. The coffee industry alone produces approximately 162,900 
tons of fresh pulp waste for every million 60 kg bags of coffee exported 
(Rodriguez and Zambrano, 2010), while oil palm processing yields 
about 350 kg of empty fruit bunch per ton of oil produced (Van Dam, 
2016).
Given their volume and composition, CP and OPEFB residues 
represent a promising feedstock for biochar production, particularly as a 
sustainable strategy to enhance soil properties. Studies have demon­
strated that coffee pulp biochar produced at 500 ◦C significantly in­
creases organic carbon content and nutrient availability while reducing 
bulk density or exchangeable acidity, leading to enhanced plant growth 
and yield, particularly in hot pepper and coffee crops (Kebede et al., 
2023; S´anchez-Reinoso et al., 2022, 2023). Similarly, biochar derived 
from OPEFB has demonstrated potential as a soil amendment in regions 
with acidic and nutrient-depleted soils, improving soil physicochemical 
properties by increasing pH, reducing acidity, and enhancing organic 
carbon and nitrogen levels when produced at 300–450 ◦C (Bakar et al., 
2015; Haque, et al., 2022a; 2022b; Mosharrof et al., 2021; Syuhada 
et al., 2016). These improvements are consistently associated with 
greater nutrient availability, enhanced crop productivity, and increased 
water and nutrient use efficiency, particularly under water-saving irri­
gation systems. However, despite these documented benefits, the effects 
of CP and OPEFB biochars have not been thoroughly investigated in dry 
tropical soils, where such amendments could offer substantial agro­
nomic and environmental advantages.
To address this knowledge gap, the present study aims to (i) char­
acterize the biochar obtained from coffee pulp and oil palm empty fruit 
bunch at different pyrolysis temperatures; (ii) determine the impact of 
varied doses of CP and OPEFB biochar on physicochemical parameters 
in soils of the dry tropics; (iii) establish the influence of the application 
of CP and OPEFB biochar on growth variables of kidney bean plants; (iv) 
delineate the variability and relationships between soil and plant growth 
parameters affected by different biochar treatments.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Study site and soil properties
Soil samples of 0–20 cm depth were collected from the Experimental 
Farm at Universidad del Magdalena, located in Santa Marta, Colombia 
(11◦13’18.31" N, 74◦11’08.80" W). This site is situated at an elevation of 
21 m.a.s.l and lies within a Tropical Dry Forest life zone characterized by 
a semi-arid climate. It experiences a unimodal-biseasonal precipitation 
regime, with an average annual rainfall of 501 mm, significant water 
deficits during the dry season, and mega-thermal conditions, with an 
average temperature of 27.3 ºC (IDEAM, 2015; Rangel and Carvajal, 
2012). The soil is poorly developed, of alluvial origin, and classified 
within the Entisol order, Psamments suborder (V´asquez et al., 2010). It 
has a sandy loam texture comprising 72 % sand, 18 % silt, and 10 % clay, 
with physicochemical properties detailed in Table 1.
2.2. Biochar production and characterization
Coffee pulp and oil palm empty fruit bunch were used to produce 
biochar at various temperatures. Initially, each type of biomass was sun- 
dried for 48 h, then ground and sieved to achieve a uniform particle size 
of 0.25 mm to ensure efficient heat transfer during pyrolysis (Tripathi 
et al., 2016). The polymer content in each biomass type was analyzed 
using acid detergent fiber (ADF) and acid detergent lignin (ADL) 
methods (ISO, 2008). The analysis indicated that CP contained 17.86 % 
lignin, 29.11 % cellulose, and 12.01 % hemicellulose, while OPEFB had 
7.05 % lignin, 46.47 % cellulose, and 23.99 % hemicellulose.
Biochar was then produced from each material at different temper­
atures (T1 = 250 ◦C, T2 = 300 ◦C, T3 = 350 ◦C) using a 60-liter muffle 
furnace (LM-E60, ARDER, Colombia). The pyrolysis temperature was 
reached at a heating rate of 10 ◦C per minute and maintained at the 
maximum temperature for 1 h (Igalavithana et al., 2019). This proced­
ure was replicated for CP and OPEFB biomass, resulting in three distinct 
types of biochar from each biomass source (Table 2).
2.3. Experimental design and set-up
A mesocosm experiment was conducted using kidney beans (Pha­
seolus vulgaris) grown in plastic pots (15 ×20 x 30 cm) under protected 
cultivation conditions in the same area where the soils were collected. 
The kidney bean seeds were initially sown in seedbeds to germinate, and 
15 days later, the seedlings were transplanted into the pots containing 
the treatments. The experimental design included biochar derived from 
CP and OPEFB, produced at three different temperatures (T1, T2, T3). 
Each type of biochar was applied at three doses (D1 = 10 %, D2 = 20 %, 
D3 = 30 % w/w) to the soil samples, which were thoroughly mixed to 
ensure uniform distribution before being added to the mesocosms. A 
control group, receiving no biochar treatment, was also established. The 
plants were irrigated daily, and no fertilizers were applied to treatments. 
The experiment was set up in triplicates for each treatment combination.

2.5. Statistical analysis
Data are presented as the means of three replicates. Specifically, 
values for soil organic carbon and plant parameters are reported along 
with their standard deviations. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and 
k-means clustering were performed to understand the trends and effects 
of each treatment on all soil and plant parameters. These analyses and 
corresponding plots were created using a Jupyter Notebook (Python 3). 
For statistical comparison among treatments for each type of biochar, an 
Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) was employed, followed by Fisher’s Least 
Significant Difference (LSD) test at a significance level of 0.05. These 
statistical tests were executed using InfoStat version 2014.
3. Results
3.1. Impact of biochar on soil physicochemical properties
3.1.1. General soil properties
The effect of the different biochar treatments on soil moisture, bulk 
density, porosity, pH, electrical conductivity (EC), and cation exchange 
capacity (CEC) is shown in Fig. 1. Significant statistical differences were 
observed between the doses applied by biochar in most parameters 
(p < 0.05); however, exceptions to this trend were identified for CP_T2 
in moisture (p = 0.50), all CP biochar in pH (p > 0.20), CP_T3 in EC 
(p = 0.11), and both CP_T1 (p = 0.08) and OPEFB_T3 (p = 0.12) in CEC.
OPEFB biochar led to more substantial increases in soil moisture, pH, 
and EC compared to coffee pulp biochar, with these enhancements 
becoming more pronounced as the doses increased (Fig. 1a, d, e). While 
moisture values in OPEFB biochar showed a similar increasing trajectory 
with each dose, the pattern in the coffee pulp biochar group differed in 
CP_T2 at a 10 % dose. Regarding pH, OPEFB biochar exhibited pro­
gressive increases with doses applied, while CP biochar remained rela­
tively constant. Concerning electrical conductivity, higher production 
temperatures result in lower EC values in OPEFB biochar across all 
doses; however, this trend is only noted at the highest dose in coffee pulp 
biochar.
Bulk density consistently declined across all treatments with 
increased biochar doses (Fig. 1b). For both types of biochar, the lowest 
production temperatures resulted in higher bulk densities, particularly 
noticeable at a 20 % dose. Additionally, increased production temper­
atures are associated with lower bulk densities in coffee pulp biochar. 
Conversely, porosity and CEC increased across all treatments (Fig. 1c, f). 
Porosity values in coffee pulp biochar are higher with increasing pro­
duction temperatures, while in OPEFB biochar, higher temperatures 
lead to lower increases in CEC.
3.1.2. Soil organic carbon
Soil Organic Carbon (SOC) increased progressively across all treat­
ments, demonstrating significant statistical differences (p < 0.05) in 
response to increasing doses (Table 3). Overall, biochar derived from 
coffee pulp exhibited higher values than OPEFB, where CP_T3 achieved 
a higher increase with 11.12 % SOC at a 30 % dose. However, biochar 
produced at 300 ◦C (T2) from both biomass types showed similar SOC 
values when applied at a 30 % dose. Additionally, it is notable that SOC 
content in coffee pulp biochar increases with the production tempera­
ture. In contrast, in OPEFB biochar, it peaks at the second temperature 
(300 ◦C) and then declines at 350 ◦C.
3.1.3. Macronutrients
The impact of biochar applications on soil macronutrients is detailed 
in Fig. 2. Overall, nitrogen content significantly increases with higher 
doses of both types of biochar (p < 0.05), with coffee pulp biochar 
showing higher nitrogen levels than palm ones produced at the same 
temperature and applied at similar doses (Fig. 2a). The most pronounced 
increase is obtained by CP_T3, which reached the maximum at doses of 
30 % with 0.93 % of total N. Notably, the lowest production tempera­
ture (T1) has a lesser impact on N content in each biochar type. How­
ever, the nitrogen values for CP_T1, CP_T2, and OPEFB_T2 are similar at 
the highest dose. Although significant variations were observed in the 
soil carbon and nitrogen contents following biochar application, the C/N 
ratio remained relatively constant, with the highest value obtained at a 
10 % dose of coffee pulp biochar produced at 250◦C.
Regarding P, K, and Mg, OPEFB biochar exhibited higher content 
across increasing doses compared to coffee pulp biochar (Fig. 2b, c, f). 
However, there were no significant differences in P content in soils 
treated with CP_T1 (p = 0.21) and CP_T3 (p = 0.23). OPEFB biochar 
consistently showed that lower production temperatures yield higher 
values for these macronutrients. In contrast, this trend of increasing 
nutrient content with lower temperatures was only observed for K values 
in CP biochar.
In the case of Ca, contrasting effects are evident between the two 
biochar groups. In coffee pulp biochar, Ca content increases with higher 
doses and is influenced by rising production temperatures. Conversely, 
in OPEFB biochar, calcium values decrease, exhibiting similar behavior 
in the treatments OPEFB_T2 and OPEFB_T3 (Fig. 2d). For S, the highest 
content was observed in OPEFB_T1, which showed a pronounced in­
crease as doses increased (Fig. 2e). The lowest S value was obtained with 
coffee pulp biochar produced at the same temperature (CP_T1), the only 
treatment displaying no significant differences between the applied 
doses (p = 0.21).
3.1.4. Micronutrients
The micronutrient content displayed differential behavior between 
the treatments applied for the two types of biochar (Fig. 3). Fe, B, and Na 
levels significantly increased with increasing doses across all treatments, 
showing statistical differences (p < 0.05) (Fig. 3a, d, f). Coffee pulp 
biochar exhibited a steeper increase in Fe content than OPEFB biochar at 
similar doses, with CP_T1 reaching the highest value at a 30 % dose. For 
Boron, OPEFB biochar showed a more variable response than CP bio­
char, which maintained a consistent upward trajectory across all treat­
ments. Regarding Na, a clear differentiation between the two biochar 
groups is observed, with OPEFB biochar presenting higher values. 
Additionally, it is noted that the production temperature influences Na 
content in coffee pulp biochar, where higher temperatures yield higher 
Na values.

pulp biochar. No significant differences were observed between doses of 
copper for any treatments (p > 0.05) (Fig. 3e). OPEFB_T2 is the only 
treatment that shows a consistent decrease in Cu content with increasing 
doses, while other treatments, such as CP_T1 and CP_T3, exhibit an 
initial pronounced increase in Cu values at lower doses, which does not 
continue at higher doses.
3.2. Influence of biochar on plant growth
The effect of adding biochar from coffee pulp and oil palm empty 
fruit bunch on kidney bean growth is shown in Fig. 4. Most treatments, 
excluding CP_T2 and CP_T3, displayed significant statistical differences 
in number of leaves between doses (p ≤0.05) (Fig. 4a). These treat­
ments exhibited more leaves than the control and OPEFB biochar, 
especially CP_T2 when applied at a 20 % dose. Concerning plant height, 
CP_T3 shows particularly significant growth (p < 0.05), reaching the 
greatest elongation at 10 % dose (Fig. 4b). No consistent pattern in plant 
height was observed across the treatments as doses increased, except for 
OPEFB_T2, where it decreased with increasing doses.
Regarding aboveground biomass, the two biochar groups exhibit 
contrasting effects; CP biochar increases, while OPEFB biochar generally 
decreases compared to the control (Fig. 4c). Significant differences 
(p < 0.05) are observed only in CP_T3 and OPEFB_T1. Notably, 
increasing the doses of biochar produced at 300◦C (T2) and 350◦C (T3) 
leads to a decrease in aboveground biomass, with this reduction being 
more pronounced in CP biochar.
Finally, belowground biomass varies across different biochar treat­
ments and doses (Fig. 4d). Coffee pulp biochar generally promotes an 
increase in root biomass, with CP_T3 showing the most significant in­
crease at a 10 % dose. On the other hand, OPEFB biochar exhibits a 
mixed response, with no significant differences across doses for any 
treatments (p > 0.10). However, the maximum belowground biomass 
within the OPEFB group is also obtained in the one produced at the 
highest temperature (OPEFB_T3).
3.3. Multivariate effects of biochar treatments on soil and plant growth 
parameters
The principal component analysis (PCA) delineated the variability 
and relationships among soil and plant growth parameters affected by 
different biochar treatments (Fig. 5). This analysis revealed that 66.10 % 
of the total variation is explained by the first two principal components 
(PC1 and PC2), which are subsequently grouped into three different 
clusters. Additionally, it is notable that the control group is distinct from 
most biochar treatments, particularly in relation to PC1.
The first cluster closely groups all the coffee pulp biochar treatments 
and indicates a substantial effect on all plant growth parameters, cal­
cium content, and micronutrients such as Cu, Zn, and Mg. Also, the 
relation of C and N soil contents with CP_T3D3 is particularly evident. 
The second cluster includes OPEFB biochar applied at higher doses (D2

4. Discussion
4.1. Influence of biomass composition and pyrolysis temperature on 
biochar properties
The initial composition of biochar obtained from the biomass of 
coffee pulp and oil palm empty fruit bunch at different temperatures was 
influenced by the hemicellulose, cellulose, and lignin content, which 
consequently impacted the soil characteristics 30 days after application 
at different doses. All treatments produced a decrease in soil bulk den­
sity and an increase in moisture content and porosity due to the 
improved particle size distribution, aligned with findings from other 
studies using similar biomass (Kebede et al., 2023; Radin et al., 2018; 
S´anchez-Reinoso et al., 2023). However, since CP biomass had higher 
lignin content, the produced biochar had a larger particle size, signifi­
cantly increasing soil porosity and decreasing soil bulk density.
Lignin provides the highest char yield compared to cellulose and 
hemicellulose, which is influenced by operating conditions and the 
chemical reactions involved (Goyal et al., 2008; Van de Velden et al., 
2010). A high heating rate favors the formation of volatile compounds, 
while a low heating rate promotes char production and offers larger 
mass yields (Bridgwater, 2003; Goyal et al., 2008; Li and Chen, 2018). 
Lignin decomposes slowly, forming a more carbon-rich and stable bio­
char due to the aromatic polycyclic structures in pyrolysis products 
(Collard and Blin, 2014; Yang et al., 2007). In contrast, biomass with 
high cellulose and hemicellulose content, such as OPEFB, undergoes 
faster pyrolysis, resulting in more extensive decomposition and volatil­
ization, leading to less carbon retention (Collard and Blin, 2014; Gani 
and Naruse, 2007). In this study, CP biomass produced biochar with 
higher carbon content, especially at lower temperatures. However, the 
highest increase in soil carbon was achieved with biochar produced at 
the highest temperature, indicating that a higher carbon content in 
biochar does not necessarily result in higher soil carbon. This is likely 
due to the more stable and recalcitrant carbon structure formed at 
higher temperatures, which is less prone to decomposition and more 
effective at long-term carbon sequestration (Li and Chen, 2018).
4.2. Impact of biochar on soil physicochemical properties
4.2.1. General soil properties
Biochar produced from diverse sources has been observed to increase 
pH, electrical conductivity, and cation exchange capacity due to its acid- 
neutralizing capacity and large amounts of extractable base cations 
(Chintala et al., 2014; Martinsen et al., 2015; Sun et al., 2022). The 
alkalinity generated by biochar application is also related to the 
increased ash content originating during pyrolysis, which adds oxides (e. 
g., Ca, Mg, K), hydroxides, and carbonates to the soil (Han et al., 2020; 
Tomczyk et al., 2020). The increase in CEC can also be attributed to the 
increased surface area of biochar, which exposes its internal functional 
groups, allowing them to acquire a negative charge when ionized 
(Fahmi et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2010).
In this study, OPEFB biochar led to more substantial increases in pH, 
EC, and CEC than coffee pulp biochar due to their higher initial pH, 
surface area, mineral content, and release of basic cations, especially 
potassium. These same behaviors were also reported for OPEFB biochar 
by Samsuri et al. (2020) and Hwong et al. (2022) when using biochar 
produced at 250 ◦C and 300–400 ◦C, respectively. Concerning CP bio­
char, its pH increased with temperatures up to 350 ◦C but resulted in 
lower values than those reported in biochar produced at temperatures 
between 450 and 500 ◦C (Kebede et al., 2023; Reyes-Herrera et al., 
2023; Rivera et al., 2022). Considering this, higher pH values could be 
expected if the production temperature increases.
Additionally, progressive increases in soil moisture were observed 
with increasing biochar application rates across all treatments. How­
ever, an atypical trend was noted in the CP_T2 treatment, where the 
10 % dose of CP biochar resulted in higher moisture retention. This 
aligns with findings from other studies reporting that moderate appli­
cations of biochar can maximize water retention, while higher doses 
may lead to diminishing returns or even reductions due to altered pore 
structure, suggesting the existence of an optimal application threshold 
for enhancing soil moisture (Frimpong et al., 2025; Ndede et al., 2022).

4.2.2. Soil nutrients
The availability of nutrients is influenced by the proportion between 
cations, where Mg is affected by high K contents, and this, in turn, is 
affected by the Ca and Mg contents (Agbenin and Yakubu, 2006; Jayadi 
et al., 2022). A positive relationship between Mg and K was observed in 
coffee pulp and oil palm empty fruit bunch treatments. However, a 
negative relationship between K and Ca is only evident in the OPEFB 
treatments, mainly due to the higher K concentrations in these biochars. 
This observation aligns with findings by Radin et al. (2018), who re­
ported increased K release from OPEFB biochar due to its high potassium 
content.
In addition to these cation interactions, biochar also influenced N 
dynamics in the soil. The increase in N content for all treatments, 
particularly in coffee pulp biochar, can be attributed not only to its 
initial nitrogen content but also to various biochar-induced mechanisms. 
Biochar application has been shown to accelerate microbially mediated 
nitrogen-cycling processes, potentially reducing gaseous and leaching 
losses and thereby enhancing nitrogen retention and bioavailability 
(Clough et al., 2013; Zheng et al., 2013). This effect is linked to the 
simultaneous increase in SOC and enhanced porosity, which support 
microbial activity and growth, as well as the increased CEC that 
contributed to nutrient retention (Domingues et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 
2018). These interacting factors contributed to the observed mainte­
nance of a stable C/N ratio, reflecting a balanced improvement in both 
soil fertility and structure.
Besides the improvement of soil structure, the influence on soil pH 
and chemical reactions, the enhanced nutrient retention and availabil­
ity, and stimulation of soil microbial activity, biochar treatments also 
significantly increased the availability of B and Mn in the soils through 
its intrinsic nutrient content. Soil available P also increased after biochar 
addition, especially in the OPRFB treatments, as reported in other 
studies (Bakar et al., 2015; Hwong et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2017). Similar 
studies suggest that this increase may result from increased pH in 
biochar-treated soil (Colantoni et al., 2016; Yang et al., 2017; Zaidun 
et al., 2019). However, Hwong et al. (2022) state that phosphorus can 
become less available at pH higher than 7.5, due to its association with 
Fe, Al, and Ca. This explains the lower P availability observed in CP 
treatments, which contained higher levels of these elements.
The differences in soil nutrient availability across treatments and 
doses can also be attributed to the marked influence of pyrolysis tem­
perature on the chemical composition and nutrient profile of biochar. At 
lower pyrolysis temperatures, more volatile organic compounds and 
labile forms of nutrients, especially N and P, are conserved (Chen et al., 
2011), enhancing their availability for plant uptake. As temperature 
increases, however, more extensive carbonization occurs, ash content 
rises, and more stable aromatic structures form. This process can reduce 
nitrogen availability due to volatilization but simultaneously concen­
trate non-volatile elements such as K, Ca, and Mg (Rajkovich et al., 
2012).
Micronutrients are also affected by pyrolysis temperature. Relatively 
stable elements like Fe and Mn tend to accumulate in biochar at higher 
temperatures, as they are not lost through volatilization and remain in 
the residual mineral fraction (Novak et al., 2012). In contrast, more 
volatile micronutrients such as Zn and Cu often decline with increasing 
temperature, likely due to thermal losses (Beesley et al., 2010; Cantrell 
et al., 2012). However, the pH of biochar can also influence nutrient 
availability, which tends to increase with pyrolysis temperature, 
affecting the solubility of some of these nutrients (Zhao et al., 2013). In 
addition, the increasing concentrations of nutrients like B and Na with 
higher biochar doses suggest that their release is related to ash content, 
which is greater in biochars produced at high temperatures (Brewer 
et al., 2009).
4.3. Impact of biochar on plant growth
The growth variables of the kidney bean plants exhibited 
improvements compared to the control when the soil was treated with 
CP biochar. In contrast, OPRFB biochar showed the opposite effect, 
contracting with findings in maize, where it had a positive effect in soils 
contaminated with Cd, Cu, and Pb (Abdullah et al., 2021; Samsuri et al., 
2020). Plant height is influenced by factors such as organic matter and 
total nitrogen content in the soil amendment (Yenani et al., 2021), 
which enhance the plant’s ability to absorb nutrients, conserve soil 
moisture, and enrich its fertility by improving porosity, aeration, and 
WHC (Adrinal et al., 2021; Oka, 2010; Pham et al., 2020). The number of 
leaves is influenced by the variation in organic carbon, total nitrogen 
content, exchangeable cations (e.g., K, Ca, and Mg), and pH neutral to 
alkaline (Berhe et al., 2022). This is consistent with the CP biochar 
treatments in this work, which presented higher porosity, organic car­
bon, total nitrogen, moderate Mg, K, and Ca contents, and pH between 
7.32 and 7.73. Similar findings were obtained in pepper growth using 
the coffee pulp biochar produced at 500 ◦C, where the height and 
number of leaves improved with increasing application doses (Kebede 
et al., 2023).
Regarding root growth, studies by Santos et al. (2020) found that it 
depends on the soil EC and pH, where high values of these can produce 
adverse effects. This is corroborated by the negative relationship iden­
tified between the growth variables, pH, and EC in the PCA analysis. On 
the other hand, Tangmankongworakoon (2019) observed that proper­
ties in biocarbon derived from coffee residues, such as basic pH, high 
WHC, EC between 2 and 5 dS m−1, and C/N ratio between 15 and 35, 
were favorable for the growth of corn plants. These statements align 
with the findings in this study, where the CP biochar treatments pre­
sented those characteristics, resulting in improved growth of kidney 
bean plants and coinciding with the improvement in root and leaf dry 
weight in lettuce when applying coffee pulp biochar produced at 450 ◦C 
(Rivera et al., 2022).
It has been shown that biochar applications in doses greater than 
20 % affect growth variables, particularly generating negative effects on 
dry matter and root length, as high doses cause an excess of cations, 
thereby altering the availability of nutrients for plants (Chrysargyris 
et al., 2019; Puentes-Escobar et al., 2022). Excess cations such as K⁺, 
Ca²⁺, and Mg²⁺ in the cation exchange complex can unbalance the uptake 
of other essential nutrients such as nitrogen, phosphorus, and zinc (Laird 
et al., 2010; Yuan et al., 2011). This cation saturation can also increase 
soil pH to levels that affect the availability of micronutrients such as Fe, 
Mn, and Cu, interfering with key physiological processes for plant 
development (Biederman and Harpole, 2013; Chintala et al., 2014).
Furthermore, excess biochar can increase soil adsorption capacity, 
retaining nutrients in less available forms or altering the osmotic bal­
ance in the rhizosphere, especially in soils with low buffering capacity 
(Van Zwieten et al., 2010). This explains why high doses of OPRFB 
biochar negatively affected growth in this study, possibly due to excess 
soluble salts or high EC (Lehmann et al., 2011), and aligns with the 
results of this study, where applying doses greater than 30 % led to 
lower growth variables in all treatments, especially in dry aboveground 
and belowground biomass.
5. Conclusions
This study demonstrates that biochar produced from coffee pulp and 
oil palm empty fruit bunch can improve soil physicochemical properties 
in dry tropical soils, including increased moisture retention, nutrient 
availability, organic carbon content, and reduced bulk density. While 
both biochars provided benefits, CP produced at temperatures between 
300 and 350 ◦C and applied at rates below 20 % w/w was more strongly 
associated with enhanced plant growth. In contrast, OPEFB had a 
greater impact on soil chemical properties such as CEC, moisture, pH, 
EC, and the availability of P, K, S, and Mg, with these effects becoming 
more pronounced at higher pyrolysis temperatures and doses. These 
results underscore the importance of selecting adequate biomass sour­
ces, pyrolysis conditions, and application rates based on specific soil and

Overall, this research highlights the potential of agro-industrial 
residues as sustainable biochar feedstocks for soil restoration and 
enhanced crop productivity in degraded dryland systems. Given their 
availability and low cost, biochar application offers a promising strategy 
to support circular and climate-resilient agriculture in tropical regions. 
The findings contribute valuable insight into the short-term effects of CP 
and OPEFB biochars and provide a foundation for future field-scale 
studies exploring long-term impacts, economic feasibility, and farmer 
adoption. Ongoing monitoring and site-specific management will be 
essential to fully realize the benefits of biochar while minimizing po­
tential negative impacts.
