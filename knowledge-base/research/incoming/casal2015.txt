82.1  INTRODUCTION
The “coffee and health” issue has been under scien­
tific debate for decades but, up to this date, no negative 
health effects can be directly attributed to moderate cof­
fee drinking (2–3 cups/day). Indeed, evidence of poten­
tial benefits or protective effects against some major 
diseases of the modern society, such as diabetes, cancer, 
cardiovascular disease, or neurodegenerative diseases, 
are increasing. Still, these effects are mostly based on epi­
demiological studies, and the true compounds respon­
sible or their action mechanisms remain generally to be 
clarified.
Among these potential benefits, moderate coffee con­
sumption has been associated with a reduced risk for 
important neurodegenerative diseases, as Parkinson 
or Alzheimer. Along with caffeine and the antioxidant 
pool derived from the ingestion of phenolic compounds 
and melanoidins, some attention has been given to the 
β-carbolines harman and norharman, two compounds 
present in roasted coffee with a known extent spectrum 
of physiological actions in the human body. A general 
overview on their biological activity and the technologi­
cal effects determining their presence in the coffee bever­
age will be detailed.
82.2  NORHARMAN AND HARMAN: 
THE MOLECULES
Norharman and harman are two β-carbolines, a 
chemical family widely distributed in nature. They are 
composed by a tricyclic ring system, with a pyridine ring 
fused to an indole skeleton (pyrido-indoles), and con­
tain two nitrogen compounds, being therefore second­
ary heterocyclic amines (Figure 82.1). Depending on the 
pH and solvent used, these β-carbolines can exist in four 
forms: neutral, cationic, anionic, and a zwitterion, with 
each exhibiting different fluorescence quantum yields. 
Table 82.1 presents the most important characteristics of 
norharman and harman.1–3
Harman and norharman were named after Peganum 
harmala L. when they were first discovered, together 
with other chemical congeners globally designated as 
harmala alkaloids. These β-carboline compounds also 
occur in other plants known in the traditional medicine 
field (Passiflora incarnata, Tribulus terrestris, Pueraria loba­
taets, etc.), as well as in several edible plants (wheat, rice, 
maize, soy, mushrooms, grapes, pineapple, banana, nuts) 
and fermented beverages (wine, beer, whisky, brandy).4,5 
In the 1990s, norharman and harman were detected in 
cooked meat and fish, together with the heterocyclic aro­
matic amines, being produced during the cooking proce­
dure. However, the food composition, cooking method, 
temperature, and time all seem to influence the amounts 
formed, with several studies published in the litera­
ture. Table 82.2 compiles the range of norharman and 
harman found in several food products, as described

uncooked meats and fish) do not contain norharman and

Norharman and harman are also detected in human 
tissues and body fluids, including urine, red blood cells, 
cerebrospinal fluid, and brain tissues.10 Their presence is 
the result of endogenous synthesis in the human body, 
at 50–100 ng/kg day,5,11 apparently from tryptophan 
condensation with indolalquinolines, catecholamines, 
aldehydes, or cetoacids,12 but exogenous sources (food, 
tobacco, etc.) might contribute to the amounts found.
In 2002, coffee was added to this list, with the report of 
its presence in brewed and instant coffee by Herraiz.13 As 
observed with cooked proteinaceous foods, both norhar­
man and harman in coffee are formed during roasting, 
being detected in highly variable amounts. These, how­
ever, are comparatively higher than those present in 
other foods or beverages, with levels of norharman rang­
ing from 1500 to 15,000 ng/g and harman levels from 100 
to 4000 ng/g in roasted beans, and consequently also at 
least partially in brewed coffee.13,14 These values make 
coffee the major exogenous source of these compounds 
in nonsmokers. The amounts and technological factors 
involved will be detailed in Section 82.4.

receptors, including the benzodiazepine, opiate, dopami­
nergic and cholinergic, among a few others.20 As pyroly­
sis products, formed in cooked foods, and despite the 
absence of confirmed direct mutagenicity, these second­
ary aromatic amines seem to enhance the mutagenicity 
of the other heterocyclic amines in vitro, being therefore 
regarded as probable co-mutagens.21,22 Both fields have 
been extensively studied over the past decades but defi­
nite conclusions are yet to be achieved.
Since ancient times, P. incarnata L. (Passifloraceae), a 
natural source of harman, has been traditionally used for 
anxiety, stress and insomnia treatment and its tranquil­
izing properties were demonstrated in rats.23 Indeed, 
several carbolines have an elevated affinity to the ben­
zodiazepine binding sites in the γ-aminobutyric acid-
A receptor (GABAA), acting as agonists, antagonists, 
inverse agonists or as a mixture. Therefore, depending 
on the specific action in these receptors, different psy­
chotropic effects can occur.24 Farzin and Mansouri,19 for 
instance, used the mouse forced swim test to suggest an 
antidepressant-like effect, via an inverse-agonistic mech­
anism located in the benzodiazepine receptors, for both 
harman and norharman.
As to coffee, an epidemiological study with women 
verified a reduction in the suicide numbers with mod­
erate coffee intake, which could be associated with a 
tranquilizing and antidepressive action of these com­
pounds.18 However, this is not observed for heavy coffee 
drinkers (≥8 cups/day), with an increased risk of suicide 
58% higher compared with moderate drinkers.25
Moderate coffee consumption has also been associ­
ated with a reduced rate in the development of some 
neurodegenerative diseases, namely Parkinson’s dis­
ease.26–29 The compound or compounds implicated are 
not yet elucidated but caffeine, if involved, does not 
seem to be alone the key factor in this case. Similar find­
ings were previously reported for smoking habit but, 
again, the major alkaloid, nicotine, seems to be neither 
the only nor the strongest compound contributing to 
these observations.27
Parkinson’s disease is caused by degeneration of 
dopamine neurons in the substantia nigra, causing inca­
pacity to control voluntary movements and leading to 
tremor and rigidity, and consequently akinesia and pos­
tural instability. The symptoms are reduced by l-dopa 
administration, converted to dopamine in the brain, as 
well as dopamine agonists, or monoamine oxidase-B 
(MAO-B) inhibitors that reduce dopamine breakdown. 
Indeed, MAO inhibition is increasingly reported as an 
important mechanism for a reduction rate in Parkin­
son’s disease, mainly by a reduction in the oxidative 
deamination of important neurotransmitters, including 
dopamine, the most affected neurotransmitter in this 
disease. While caffeine and nicotine are reported to act as 
antagonists for the adenosine A2 receptor, blocking ade­
nosine action on the suppression of the dopaminergic 
transmission, therefore reducing dopamine neurotoxic­
ity, norharman and harman are potent competitive and 
reversible MAO inhibitors, in both rat and humans.30,31 
The high amounts of these β-carbolines in tobacco 
smoke and roasted coffee place them in the top of the 
suspected list for compounds with probable influence on 
Parkinson’s disease course.
Herraiz and Chaparro31 demonstrated clearly that 
norharman and harman extracted from coffee brews 
exhibited inhibitory properties on recombinant human 
MAO-A and -B isozymes, catalyzing the oxidative deam­
ination of kynuramine. MAO inhibition was reversible 
and competitive, but while MAO-A was inhibited by 
both norharman and harman, MAO-B was only affected 
by norharman. The same MAO inhibiting effect was 
observed for tobacco smoke, highlighting that MAO 
inhibition is probably responsible for some of the behav­
ioral effects attributed to smoking habit, besides having 
a protective effect on the dopaminergic neurons.5,29–31 
In comparison with tobacco, however, coffee is less 
effective in MAO inhibition (60% reduction for tobacco 
against 30% for coffee).27–29
The presence of higher levels of these β-carbolines 
in the body fluids of alcoholics and heroin addicts, 
compared to nonconsumers, has also impelled several 
studies in order to verify if they are a consequence or a 
biological defense mechanism, but no definitive conclu­
sions can yet be taken.32,33
When these compounds were found in cooked pro­
teinaceous foods simultaneously with heterocyclic 
aromatic amines, known dietary mutagens and car­
cinogens, norharman and harman were also analyzed 
for their potential participation in this process. Despite 
not being mutagenic in the Ames test, they enhance the 
mutagenicity of heterocyclic amines, polycyclic aromatic 
hydrocarbons, aromatic amines and aminoazo compounds, 
in the presence of metabolic activation (liver [S9] frac­
tion), being therefore recognized as “co-mutagens.”22 
The mechanism involved was initially attributed to 
DNA intercalation, due to their planar structure, but 
the involvement of norharman in carcinogenesis via 
inhibition of cytochrome P450-related activities has 
been reported,34 suggesting that β-carbolines modulate 
chemical carcinogenesis also by controlling xenobiotic 
metabolism. These effects, however, were observed 
with isolated compounds and the global effect within 
the original food matrix might be modulated by other 
compounds. This might be applicable to coffee as well, 
a particularly complex food matrix from the chemical 
point of view.
Due to the structural similarities between harman 
and norharman with those of the synthetic neurotoxin 
1-methyl-4-phenyl-1,2,3,6-tetrahydropyridine (MPTP),

82.4  NORHARMAN AND HARMAN 
IN COFFEE
As previously mentioned, coffee is, up to date, the 
major source of norharman and harman in nonsmokers. 
However, the levels are highly variable, making it use­
ful to determine the technological factors conditioning 
their amounts in the coffee beverage, and therefore, the 
amounts ingested.
82.4.1  Influence of Coffee Species
Norharman and harman levels vary significantly 
between Arabica and Robusta coffee species. In a 
study performed by Gomes38 both compounds were 
smaller in Arabica coffee (Table 82.3), being later con­
firmed by Alves et al.14 Also, for the same coffee sam­
ple, norharman values are always higher that harman 
ones. The beverage figures are similar, with higher 
norharman amounts than harman and again lower 
amounts of both compounds in plain Arabica brews 
(Table 82.4).14
82.4.2  Roasting
Harman and norharman levels in raw (green) cof­
fee are small, varying from 10 to 100 ng/g for both 
carbolines13,38 (Table 82.3). The amounts increase sig­
nificantly during roasting but are highly variable.13,14,38 
In a detailed study with 17 coffee samples roasted in a 
convective fast roasting system at three different tem­
peratures (240, 260, and 280 °C; 4 min; model Duett-M, 
Probat), Gomes38 reported variable amounts, depending 
on the temperature used, coffee species, and geographi­
cal origin. Norharman amounts increased with tempera­
ture on both species and geographical origins, except for 
Honduras and Guatemala Arabicas, but for harman the 
figures were not so clear. Generally, in Arabica samples 
higher harman amounts were detected at the lower tem­
perature tested (240 °C) decreasing at higher tempera­
tures, again excepting Honduras. For Robusta coffee 
higher amounts were observed at 260 °C, decreasing at 
280 °C, while the Indian and Uganda samples presented 
increased amounts at 280 °C, being the samples with 
higher amounts in the study. Therefore, a tentative to 
establish correlations between the organic roast loss per­
centages (ORL), a standard measure for the roast degree, 
and the amounts of both harman and norharman, proved 
inconsistent.38
Alves et al.14 used a standard drum roasting technique, 
at constant temperature (210 °C) and using variable times 
to achieve different roasting degrees (7–11 min; Probat 
Pré 1Z (2000) from Probat), with an Arabica (Brasil) 
and Robusta (Ivory Coast) sample. Again, the amounts 
on both species were different but a clear increase was 
observed with the ORL for norharman while harman 
amounts increased up to an ORL around 11%, decreas­
ing for darker roasts (12–14%).
These results show that the roasting conditions have 
a marked influence on the amounts of β-carbolines, but 
a direct correlation with the roast degree is difficult to

82.4.3  Beverage Preparation
In a detailed study on the technological factors influ­
encing norharman and harman levels in espresso coffee, 
Alves et al.14 verified that only 30–40% of the β-carbolines 
are extracted during the preparation of a standard 
espresso (6 g/30 ml), achieving 60–70% for longer per­
colated volumes (70 ml) (Figure 82.2), and being more 
efficiently extracted in Robusta coffee. A lower extract­
ability was also verified for higher roast degrees.14
As to the coffee beverage type, and when expressed 
in micrograms per liter, espresso and mocha have higher 
amounts of both norharman and harman than press-pot 
or filter coffee.14 This is mainly a consequence of using 
different coffee:water ratios, although influenced by 
the extraction method. The best indicator of β-carboline 
ingestion, however, is per standard beverage type and 
size, but these are highly variable according to cultural 
and personal preferences. Indeed, while espresso bev­
erages are usually very small (20–40 ml), followed by 
mocha coffee, filter coffee might achieve 200 ml. The val­
ues might range from 3000 to 23,000 ng per beverage for 
norharman (3–23 μg) and from 1000 to 10,000 ng for har­
man (1–10 μg) (Table 82.4), being definitively higher than 
other known food sources.
82.4.4  Coffee Substitutes
Coffee substitutes are usually soluble (instant) pow­
ders prepared with roasted cereals, mostly based on 
extracts of roasted chicory, barley, malt, and rye, some 
with a small proportion of instant coffee added (10–50%). 
With the objective to determine the amounts of norhar­
man and harman in coffee substitute beverages, Alves 
et al.39 analyzed diverse commercial samples. All sam­
ples contained variable amounts of both β-carbolines, 
always with a higher proportion of norharman than 
harman. Still, the highest levels were found in 100% 
instant coffees (Table 82.3), followed by plain chicory 
and the lowest amounts were found with 100% barley. 
The norharman and harman content was statistically 
higher (p < 0.01) in all mixtures with coffee when com­
pared to those without it. Nevertheless, and except for

82.5  SUMMARY POINTS
	•	 Norharman and harman are two bioactive 
β-carbolines widespread in nature and endogenously 
synthetized in the human body.
	•	 Norharman and harman are MAO inhibitors, being 
increasingly associated with a beneficial effect in 
Parkinson disease but its structural similarity a 
known neurotoxin points in opposite ways.
	•	 Roasted coffee is the major known food source of 
norharman and harman.
	•	 The levels of harman and norharman in the coffee 
beverages vary widely, being dependent on roast 
temperature, coffee species, and beverage type, 
among others factors.
	•	 Coffee substitutes can also be regarded generally as 
norharman and harman sources.
