A B S T R A C T
In the current study, the presence of some essential and toxic elements, such as chromium (Cr), cobalt (Co), lead 
(Pb), manganese (Mn), nickel (Ni) and silver (Ag) was checked in the coffee grains from the Harari Loneberry 
Coffee, Harari Shurberry Coffee, Harari Mocha Coffee, Laqmati Coffee, Wild Coffee, Khawlani Sokia Coffee, and 
Khawlani Matareia Coffee. Mn, Co, Pb, Ni, Cr, and Ag concentration ranges were found to be 6.75 to 3.76, 0.065 
to 0.042, 0.832 to 0.521, 0.102 to 0.017, 0.167 to 0.054, and 0.961 to 0.192 g g-1, respectively, and all the 
elements were found to be within the range of intake. The existence of contaminating toxigenic fungi, as well as 
aflatoxin and ochratoxin, is also studied. Using TLC chromatography, 27 toxigenic isolates from the genera 
Aspergillus and Pencillium were taken from these samples and examined for the synthesis of the toxins ochratoxin 
(OTA) and aflatoxin B (AFB). Five different Aspergillus isolates, seven different Penicillium isolates, and four 
different Fusarium isolates all produced AFB1, according to the research. Four samples of coffee beans were 
discovered to be naturally tainted using HPLC. Aflatoxin B1′s quantification ranged from 2.11 to 121.38 g kg-1, 
while ochratoxin A’s ranged from 1.12 to 21.32 g kg-1. This suggests that when coffee, the most consumed 
beverage in Saudi Arabia, is stored or presented in less hygienic circumstances, it is highly contaminated with 
toxins.
1. Introduction
After water, coffee is the most consumed beverage worldwide 
(Laitala et al., 2008). The amount of coffee consumed varies greatly by 
region. Bosnia and Herzegovina have the highest consumption in 
Southern Europe (6.1 kg per capita/year), while Finland has the highest 
consumption in Northern Europe (12 kg per capita/year). The Interna­
tional Coffee Organization estimates that 2.5 billion cups of coffee were 
used daily in 2008 (one cup is equal to 30 mL). Addictive behaviour is 
modelled by this consumption. According to genetic studies on twins, 
the heritability of coffee consumption might be estimated to be between 
39 and 56 % (Laitala et al., 2008). Coffee appears to affect health in both 
short-term and long-term ways. It’s interesting to note that dementia, 
Alzheimer’s disease, Parkinson’s disease, and type 2 diabetes may all 
benefit from its ingestion (Gongora-Alfaro, 2010). However, according 
to other experts, consuming coffee increases the risk of coronary heart 
disease. According to Higdon & Frei (2006), drinking more than five 
cups a day raised the risk of coronary heart disease by 40 to 60 %. 
Uncertain views exist on the impact of coffee consumption on the 
emergence of cancer. Some studies claim that drinking coffee makes 
some cancers more likely (Silvera et al., 2007), although other studies 
have produced contradicting findings (Li et al., 2013). Large amounts of 
this beverage shouldn’t be consumed by expectant women because they 
run the risk of miscarriage. Additionally, it is unknown how much 
caffeine is to blame for these side effects or whether other ingredients, 
including heavy metals, have a role. The environment is heavily 
contaminated with heavy metals. They are present in variable amounts 
in human meals and enter the food chain (Roychowdhury et al., 2003).
Food contamination is a severe concern since heavy metals are 
absorbed from the digestive tract and have negative effects on a variety 
of tissues. Even at relatively modest concentrations, some metals are 
hazardous and slowly build up in tissues (Beckett et al., 2007). Metals 
alter the ionic balance and mineral control, cause DNA damage and 
oxidative cellular damage, and promote the development of cancer. 
Bosnia and Herzegovina consume 6.1 kg of coffee annually, followed by 
Brazil with 5.8 kg, Lebanon with 4.8 kg, Bosnia with 2.4 kg, and Poland 
with 2.4 kg. The origin, species, and cultivar of the coffee plant all affect 
the complex chemical makeup of coffee (Iwanowska et al., 2013). The

prepare and industrially process green beans affect the concentrations of

also result from packaging and storage. Because of the complexity of 
coffee’s chemical makeup, a comprehensive list of every component

The primary toxigenic fungus genera (Aspergillus, Penicillium, and 
Fusarium) are natural coffee contaminants and are present from the field 
to the warehouse, according to studies on the microbiology of coffee 
cherries and beans (Mislivec et al., 1983). P. variadile, P. verrucosum, P. 
viridcatum, P. chrysognum, P. commune, P. palis, and P. cyclopium are 
some of the species that belong to these toxic genera (Bokhari Fardos & 
Jee, 2002). Ochratoxin is a toxin produced by harmful Aspergillus fungi, 
according to studies. Aspergillus ochraceus, Aspergillus carbonarius, 
and Penicillium verrucosum are thought to be the three main fungus 
species that generate ochratoxin A (OA) in nature, with Aspergillus niger 
contributing sporadically (Pitt, 1987).
A. ochraceus was found to produce OA by Van der Merwe et al. 
(1965), however this species has generally been considered an uncom­
mon fungus, with the exception of long-stored food products (Van der 
Merwe et al., 1965). P. verrucosum is mostly linked to cereals and is 
thought to only occur in chilly temperate settings. In the past ten years, 
sources of OA have been identified as A. carbonarius and A. niger (Pitt & 
Hocking, 1997). Although the presence of OA in green coffee beans has 
been noted by a number of writers in quantities ranging from 0.2 to 360 
Ag/kg (Abarca et al., 1994), its origin is still unknown. Although it is 
often believed that A. ochraceus is the primary source of OA in coffee, 
there is little conclusive data to support this.
The current research distinguishes itself by focusing on the Saudi 
Arabian context, using advanced detection techniques, and providing a 
comprehensive assessment of both heavy metals and mycotoxins. These 
elements contribute to a more detailed understanding of contamination 
risks in coffee consumed in Saudi Arabia and offer valuable information 
for improving food safety standards and public health measures in the 
region.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Identification of fungal isolates
Round processed coffee beans were directly plated onto filter paper 
that had been dampened with sterile distilled water in order to isolate 
fungus. From each sample of coffee beans, 30 beans were chosen at 
random. To enable the isolation of fungi, present in the inside of the 
beans, 15 were disinfected with 1 % hypochlorite for 2 min before 
plating, and 15 were not. Fungi estimate was done using the seed-plate 
approach. Then, seeds and rose-bengal (1/15,000) as a bacteriostatic 
agent were added to the potato dextrose agar (PDA) medium (200 g of 
potato, 20 g of dextrose, and 159 g of agar). Each seed sample was 
placed on ten different plates. Tsubouchi et al. (1987) state that the 
plates were incubated at 28 ◦C for 7–10 days, during which time the 
growing fungi were identified, numbered, and calculated per 100 seeds 
in each sample.
2.2. Detection and preparation of mycotoxins in coffee beans
2.2.1. Sample preparation
Each finely ground coffee bean sample (viz, Harari Loneberry Coffee, 
Harari Shurberry Coffee, Harari Mocha Coffee, Laqmati Coffee, Wild 
Coffee, Khawlani Sokia Coffee, Khawlani Matareia Coffee, Yafeay Isae 
Coffee, Yafeay Tsawy Coffee, Yafeay Ketty Coffee, Yafeay Nakheby 
Coffee), weighing 30 g, was added to a 250 mL Erlenmeyer flask along 
with 120 mL of an acetonitrile: 4 % aqueous potassium chloride solution 
(9–1), according to Scudamore et al. (1998). The mixture was shaken for 
two hours at a medium speed setting on a wrist action shaker, then 
filtered through Whatman No 1 filter paper into a separating funnel. The 
funnel received a 50 mL aliquot of isooctane, which was agitated for one 
minute to destroy the extract. The funnel received a 50 mL aliquot of 
isooctane, which was agitated for one minute to destroy the extract. The 
bottom layer was gathered and defeated once more. The defeated layer 
was extracted from a 50 mL aliquot using a solution of 25 mL HCl (1.0 
mol l-1) and 50 mL dichloromethane. Over a layer of sodium sulphate, 
the bottom organic layer was collected. Two further aliquots of 
dichloromethane were added to the funnel to extract the upper aqueous 
layer. On a vertex evaporator, the mixed organic layers were evaporated 
till dry. To clean up the GPC, the residue was dissolved in 4 mL of ethyl 
acetate. Using ethyl acetate at a flow rate of 4 mL/min as the mobile 
phase, a 1 mL aliquot of this solution was injected onto the GPC column. 
On a rotary evaporator, the percentage that eluted between 16 and 26 
min was collected in a 100 mL round-bottom flask and evaporated 
almost completely. A glass vial measuring 8 mL was used to transfer the 
leftover solution. Dichloromethane was added to the vial and divided 
into two portions, each of about 1 mL, to rinse the round-bottom flask. 
Following evaporation to dryness on a sample concentrator under a 
gentle stream of nitrogen at 400C and reconstitution in 1 mL of aceto­
nitrile: water (3–1) according to Pittet et al.’s instructions (Avalos Funez 
et al., 2013), the contents of that vial were used. Following cleanup and 
extract evaporation, the leftover material was dissolved in 750 l of 
acetonitrile. The vial was sealed and heated for roughly two minutes at 
40 ◦C. The extract was mixed with a 250-l aliquot of a 0.01 M lead ac­
etate solution before being chilled at 50C for about two minutes. Before 
being quantified by HPLC, this was filtered using a 0.2 um Acrodisc 
PTFE filter (Pittet et al., 1996).
2.3. Separation and quantification
Aflatoxins: A HPLC system (LC-2050C 3D) connected to a Shimadzu 
RF 350 dichloromethane detector was used to detect and estimate af­
latoxins. Utilising a detector set at wavelengths of 360 nm excitation and 
440 nm emission, the aflatoxins were found by their fluorescence (A.O. 
A.C., 1995).
Ochratoxin: A was discovered utilising fluorescence with an emission 
wavelength of 500 nm and an excitation wavelength of 335 nm (A.O.A. 
C., 1990). The complete processing of coffee beans is shown in Fig. 1.
2.4. Metal analysis
Eleven types of coffee, including the Harari Loneberry, were used in 
the study. Coffee varieties include Harari Shurberry, Harari Mocha, 
Laqmati, Wild, Khawlani Sokia, Khawlani Matareia, Yafeay Isae, Yafeay 
Tsawy, Yafeay Ketty, and Yafeay Nakheby. For each sample, minerali­
zation was carried out in three different ways. Coffee infusions can be 
mineralized by passing them through a Whatman paper filter after being 
infused with 1 g of coffee in 27 mL of water. 10 mL of the filtrate were 
mineralized at 140 ◦C for 5 h in tubes with 1 mL HNO3 and 0.1 mL 
HClO4. ICP-MS, (Inductively Coupled Plasma Mass Spectrometer 
ICPMS-2030 Series) developed by Perkin Elmer in Warsaw, Poland, used 
induction-coupled plasma technology in conjunction with mass spec­
trometry to measure the concentrations of the heavy metals Mn, Co, Ni, 
Cr, Ag, and Pb. Three estimates of each metal concentration were made 
from each sample of coffee. Normally, the samples were delivered as 
quickly as possible to the lab. To prevent the production of any toxins or 
microbial contamination before examination, they were collected and 
stored at 20 ◦C.

3. Results and discussion
3.1. Fungi recovered
110 isolates from 35 species and 22 genera were found in 11 different 
types of coffee (Table 1). There was just one species of Rhizopus, 
compared to five Aspergillus species and seven Penicillium species. The 
coffee samples included 54 % A. flavus and 59.6 % A. nigeria contam­
ination. The most frequent penicillin’s, P. arenicola and P. oxalicum, 
were found in samples with respective concentrations of 50.5 % and 
45.5 %. 66.7 % of the coffee was infected with R. stolonifera. The other 
fungal isolates, which ranged in frequency from moderate to rare, were 
present in <33.3 % of the coffee. Other research results (El-Kady et al., 
1995) from the study of coffee mycoflora and medicinal plants corrob­
orated this trend. Takatori et al. (Takatori, Watanabe, Udagawa, & 
Kurata, 1977) originally identified the main ingredients of Aspergillus 
and Penicillium spp. as Harari Shurberry coffee, Harari Mocha coffee, 
and Laqmati coffee. Misra (1981) and Roy et al. (1988) isolated the 
microorganisms from Khawlani Sokia coffee. Also isolated from Yafeay 
Isae and Yafeay Tsawy coffee were Alternaria, A. flavus, A. niger, 
Chaetomium dolicholrichum, Fusarium verticillioides, P. oxalicum, and 
Rhizopus. Aspergillus flavus, Aspergillus niger, Aspergillus sydowii, 
Aspergillus ochraceus, Penicillium, and Rhizopus spp. as well as other 
spices are most frequently isolated in Khawlani Matareia coffee, ac­
cording to Ath-Har et al. (1988). Aspergillus ochraceus, Aspergillus 
luchuensis, Aspergillus nidulans, Fusarium oxysporum, Fusarium mon­
iliforme, Chaetomium spp., Penicillium citrinum, R. stolonifer, and A. 
alternate were among the most frequently found fungi in drug plant 
isolates (Aziz & Youssef, 1991). The three fungus that affected Yafeay 
Isae Coffee the most were Aspergillus flavus, Aspergillus ochraceus, and 
Aspergillus versicolor (Christensen, 1975). As a result of neutral exterior 
dust contamination during storage in humid conditions, fungal species 
contamination resulted (Christensen, 1975). Fungi fall into the ecolog­
ical categories of field and storage fungi. The Khawlani Sokia Coffee, 
Laqmati Coffee, and Harari Mocha Coffee are the three principal field 
fungal genera. Contrarily, storage moulds are fungi, mostly of the 
Aspergillus and Penicillium species, which are found on plants at 
moisture levels that are commonly present in items that are stored (Abou 
Donia, 2008). The main determinants of when fungal contamination of 
the spices may occur are coffee processing, storage, and transportation.
3.2. Mycotoxin production by fungal isolates
The generation of mycotoxins by isolates from coffee beans is dis­
played in Table 2. Aflatoxin B1 was produced by 30 % of Aspergillus 
flavus isolates, up to 550 g kg-1 (Harari Shurberry coffee), but no 
Aspergillus sydowii isolates were discovered to be capable of producing 
this toxin. A. flavus and Aspergillus niger are thought to have created 
aflatoxins, according to a number of reports (Diener & Davis, 1966). 
Aflatoxin generation from Aspergillus species isolated from wild coffee 
was examined by Batista et al. (2003). According to their findings, three 
out of the ten Aspergillus flavus isolates produced aflatoxin B1 and B2.
Only seven isolates of Penicillium produced OTA (80 %) with an 
average output of 221.30 g kg-1, although numerous isolates of Fusarium 
species were shown to be capable of producing ochratoxin A. The ability 
of Aspergillus and Penicillium fungus to create ochratoxins is well 
documented. Scopulariopsis brevicaulis, Trichoderma harzianum, Ulo­
cladium chartarum, Alternaria alternate, Gliocladium roseum, and 
Myrothecium verucaria, among other species of fungi, were found to be 
capable of producing ochratoxin and were isolated from crops in warmer 
climate zones as well as from coffee beans (Diener & Davis, 1966). These 
findings are in line with our data. Only three of the examined coffee 
isolates produced a considerable amount of ochratoxin A, according to 
Mantle and Chow’s study on the synthesis of ochratoxin on media by 
isolates of Rhizoctonia solani and Ulocladium botrytis from coffee 
(Northolt et al., 1979).

Because the potential effects of coffee use on health have not yet been 
definitively established, the investigation of metal concentrations in 
coffee is important.
Manganese (Mn): The main way that the general public is exposed 
to manganese (Mn) is through food. Vegetarians may consume more 
manganese because foods made from plants typically contain higher 
levels of the mineral. The liver, kidney, pancreas, and adrenals have 
been found to contain the highest tissue levels of manganese. The central 
nervous system is undoubtedly the most important target for man­
ganese’s harmful effects on humans, according to the facts currently 
available. Manganism is a syndrome defined by apathy, anorexia, 
muscle discomfort, and clumsy, delayed limb movement. Yafeay Ketty 
coffee has the highest Mn content (6.75), whereas Wild coffee had the 
lowest (3.76). The U.S. National Research Council estimated that an 
adult’s healthy dietary consumption should range from 730 to 1825 mg 
year (Pepeljnjak & Cvetnic, 1981). As a result, our research have found 
that Mn consumption from coffee is extremely low relative to demand. 
The annual demand for manganese is not even close to being met by the 
quantity of manganese used through coffee.

Cobalt (Co): Cobalt commonly exists in the body of a human as a 
part of vitamin B12. The majority of tissues contain this element, with 
the kidneys, liver, and bones containing the most of it. Red blood cell 
formation and the healthy operation of the neurological system depend 
on it in the human body. In mice, a high serum cobalt ion concentration 
exerts deleterious effects on the heart and liver but has no discernible 
impact on the kidney (Mantle & Chow, 2000). The cobalt content was 
determined to be lowest in Harari Shurberry coffee (0.042) and highest 
in Yafeay Isae coffee (0.065). These amounts are substantially less than 
the element’s annual requirement.
Nickel (Ni): The haematopoietic process, iron metabolism, and ab­
sorption of iron are all affected by nickel. Nickel exposure in humans can 
occur when they inhale contaminated air, consume contaminated food 
or drinks, or smoke cigarettes. Exposure to nickel can also happen when 
skin comes into touch with nickel-contaminated soil or water. Nickel is 
necessary in modest amounts, but if it is consumed in excessive amounts, 
it can be harmful to human health. The International Agency for 
Research on Cancer (IARC) has acknowledged that nickel and several of 
its derivatives are carcinogenic. Lung embolism, respiratory failure, 
birth defects, asthma and chronic bronchitis, allergic reactions, the 
development of lung, larynx, and prostate malignancies, and cardiac 
conditions are all side effects of an excessive intake of nickel. Ni has a 
daily upper consumption limit of 1 mg that is acceptable. Our findings 
imply that Ni content ranged from 0.102 in Khawlani Matareia coffee to 
0.017 in Harari Loneberry coffee, with Khawlani Matareia coffee having

Chromium (Cr): Chromium is found everywhere, including in bio­
logical systems, soil, and water. Due to its importance in human nutri­
tion, trivalent chromium (Cr III) is typically the only element considered 
during evaluation. Through its impact on insulin activity, chromium 
affects the metabolism of carbohydrates, lipids, and proteins. The pre­
cise structure of the biologically active form of chromium is also un­
known, as is the mechanism. Since adding chromium to the diet to 
supplement it reduced fasting blood glucose levels, increased glucose 
tolerance, lowered insulin levels, and decreased total cholesterol and 
triglyceride levels, chromium-deficient diabetics have benefited from 
chromium intake (Freeland-Graves, 1994). There isn’t a defined Rec­
ommended Dietary Allowance (RDA) for chromium at the moment. 
Harari Loneberry coffee (0.167) has been discovered to have the highest 
coffee patterns for Cr, whereas Yafeay has the lowest patterns.
Silver (Ag): One of the transition metals we regularly come into 
contact with is silver. The silver ions found in packaging, such as banger 
casings, prolong freshness while thwarting bacterial development and 
division. They are also easily distributed throughout the environment 
and enter the food chain. Bacteria, mould, and mildew can’t grow when 
silver ions are present. Silver makes up about 1 mg of the human body, 
but if it builds up, it can lead to death or the skin condition argyria, 
which is characterized by blue-grey darkening of the eyes, nose, and 
throat. Silver nanoparticles (AgNPs) are deadly to human tumours, ac­
cording to study conducted in Spain, and it may be important clinically 
that tumour cells are more susceptible to the cytotoxic action of these 
nanoparticles (Liu et al., 2010). Between 4–5 g and 20–40 g of silver 
have been reported to be required to cause generalized Argyreia in 
people. In humans, the deadly toxic dosage ranges from 50 to 500 
mgkg-1body weight. On the acceptable daily intake, there are no sta­
tistics. The occasional instances of more severe neurologic, renal, or 
hepatic problems brought on by colloidal silver consumption are sig­
nificant (Freeland-Graves, 1994). According to our findings, Harari 
Loneberry coffee had the lowest amount of ag content (0.192) and 
Yafeay Isae coffee had the highest amount (0.921).
Lead (Pb): A heavy metal that is frequently found in nature is lead. 
Approximately equal amounts of lead are consumed by and inhaled by 
the general population. Multiorgan toxicity is linked to lead exposure 
over an extended period of time. The amount of lead that can be ingested 
without it harming the body is still unknown because no acceptable 
threshold for lead exposure has yet been established (Mooradian et al., 
1994). According to the Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Reg­
istry, lead poisoning is referred to as "plumbism" and is indicated by 
blood lead concentrations that are >25 g/dl for adults and 5 g/dl for 
children. Long-term exposure to lead or its salts can result in nephrop­
athy, colic-like gastrointestinal pains, as well as possible blood pressure 
elevation and paralysis in the fingers, wrists, or ankles (Avalos Funez 
et al., 2013). Children absorb around 50 % of the lead they consume, 
compared to adults who only absorb about 6 % of it, making them more 
susceptible to lead poisoning. Infants who are breastfed may experience 
delayed intellectual and motor development as a result of maternal 
exposure to Pb. Additionally, recent studies have demonstrated the ef­
fect of lead on gene expression.

coffee drinker per year = 33 g per day, assuming that only 64 % of 
people consume coffee. The regional distribution of lead intake gener­
ally varies greatly. For instance, data from INSERM (1999) show that 
people in urban regions of the Russian Federation, Poland, Cuba, 
Thailand, and India in the 1980s experienced exceptionally high expo­
sure rates of 83 g per day = 30 mg/year from water, air, dust, and food.
After 1990, low levels of urban exposure were discovered in Austria, 
Scandinavia, the Netherlands, Germany, and Luxemburg: 9 g per day =
3.3 mg/year. Therefore, it can be claimed that an additional 12 mg Pb 
from coffee per year would significantly raise the intake above these 
thresholds. According to Fewtrell et al. (2004), lead can cause a variety 
of harmful health effects, some of which can occur at low amounts. Due 
to the effects of elevated blood pressure and lead-induced mental 
retardation, lead was believed to be responsible for 0.9 % of all diseases 
in the world. Thus, it would be expected that coffee will increase the 
burden of this condition. However, coffee use would not increase Pb 
levels thought to be responsible for lead poisoning.
Previous research by Al-Mohizea et al. (2016) reported the presence 
of lead and cadmium in coffee samples from the Middle East, with 
concentrations generally within the permissible limits. Our study, 
however, found little higher levels of these heavy metals in a significant 
portion of the samples. This discrepancy could be due to differences in 
coffee sourcing, processing methods, or environmental factors influ­
encing contamination. Al-Mohizea et al. focused primarily on imported 
coffee, whereas our study included both imported and locally processed 
coffee, potentially explaining the higher contamination levels observed.
A study by Al-Hazmi (2014) found ochratoxin A in 20 % of coffee 
samples from Saudi Arabia, with levels mostly below the EFSA safety 
thresholds. Our findings indicate a little higher prevalence (25 %) and 
higher levels of OTA contamination. This increase might be attributed to 
changes in storage conditions, climate variations, or differences in the 
coffee supply chain over time. Al-Hazmi’s study was conducted several 
years ago, and the observed increase in OTA levels in our study could 
reflect a worsening of storage practices or climatic conditions conducive 
to fungal growth.
Al-Jasser (2010) identified Aspergillus and Penicillium species in 35 
% of coffee samples in Saudi Arabia, with higher prevalence compared 
to our findings (20 %). The higher prevalence in their study could be due 
to fungal contamination. Al-Jasser’s research utilized older fungal 
identification techniques, whereas our study employed advanced mo­
lecular techniques, potentially leading to more accurate and improved 
detection rates.
The comparison reveals that contamination levels of heavy metals 
and mycotoxins in coffee consumed in Saudi Arabia might be increasing, 
which raises significant public health concerns. These differences 
highlight the need for continuous monitoring and stricter regulatory 
measures. Future research should focus on.
Investigating the sources of heavy metal contamination in the 
coffee supply chain to implement effective mitigation strategies.
Exploring the impact of climate change on mycotoxin production 
and fungal contamination in coffee.
Developing and promoting best practices for coffee storage and 
processing to minimize contamination risks.
Conducting longitudinal studies to assess trends in contamination 
levels over time and evaluate the effectiveness of interventions.
Our study adds to the growing body of literature on coffee contam­
ination by highlighting the current levels of heavy metals and 
mycotoxin-producing fungi in coffee consumed in Saudi Arabia. By 
comparing our findings with previous research, we have identified sig­
nificant trends and differences that underscore the need for enhanced 
regulatory oversight and further research. Addressing these contami­
nation issues is crucial for ensuring the safety of coffee consumers and 
protecting public health in Saudi Arabia.
4. Conclusion
Our investigation demonstrated that the levels of Mn, Co, Ni, Cr, and 
Ag were too low to have any effect on health. However, Pb may be 
hazardous if it builds up in the body, such as in Bosnians, but not to the 
point where lead poisoning results. A more accurate metal contamina­
tion management strategy may be required for these products given the 
high lead content in some coffees. It should be emphasized that exces­
sive use of these heavy metals may put one’s health in jeopardy. The 
common mycotoxigenic fungus A. flavus and A. niger found in spices 
suggest that mycotoxin contamination may exist. Mycotoxins are pro­
duced more frequently in food due to numerous circumstances. One of 
them is improper food storage, which causes the release of several my­
cotoxins into food when stored at high temperatures and humidity. The 
manufacturing process’s post-harvest phases, which include drying and 
storage, are among its most crucial ones. Insufficiently controlled stor­
age conditions can make food susceptible to mycotoxins. Aspergillus, 
Fusarium, and Penicillium, as well as the fungal poisons they produce, 
frequently cause food contamination when is handled and stored 
improperly.
