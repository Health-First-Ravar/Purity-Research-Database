ABSTRACT
NAFLD (Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease) is an increasingly spread metabolic status characterized by increasing fat
accumulation in the liver, obesity closely associated with it, sedentary habits and insulin resistance. Since there is
currently no recognized pharmaceutical treatment, lifestyle changes continue to be the mainstay of treatment. Coﬀee,
a globally consumed beverage rich in bioactive and useful compounds like caﬀeine, cafestol, kahweol, and chlorogenic
acids, has emerged as a potential hepatoprotective agent. Evidence from epidemiological experiments, animal studies,
and human trials suggests that regular coﬀee drinking may help decrease hepatic fat levels, improve antioxidant defenses,
prevent inﬂammatory pathways, and prevent or slow ﬁbrosis progression. Additionally, coﬀee may enhance insulin
sensitivity and regulation of metabolism, indirectly reducing NAFLD-related burden. While variability in outcomes exists
because of diﬀerences in preparation of coﬀee, individual genetic elements, dose and study design, overall, the evidence
suggests that moderate coﬀee consumption may have a signiﬁcant role in liver health dietary regimens. More thorough
clinical research is necessary to prove causation and provide precise guidelines, though.
Keywords: Coﬀee, Liver health, NAFLD, Inﬂammation, Caﬀeine, Antioxidants

2. Pathophysiology of NAFLD
2.1. Disease progression
Several pathophysiological operations lead to the
development of NAFLD (non-alcoholic fatty liver dis-
ease), which is a progressive disease rather than a
single stage. With a higher impact of long-term issues,
including liver cancer and cirrhosis, every stage indi-
cates a deeper level of liver involvement. Steatosis of
the liver, an illness where fat, mostly in the form of
triglycerides, builds up inside hepatocytes, is usually
the ﬁrst sign of the illness. Known as simple fatty
liver, this level is typically asymptomatic and con-
sidered benign. However, Steatosis can advance into
more harmful stages in certain people, especially if it
continues in the presence of signals of inﬂammatory
or metabolic stress [15, 97].
The changes of the liver into non-alcoholic steato-
hepatitis (NASH) at the second level. In addition to
building up fat, this also requires immune cell in-
ﬁltration and the beginning of hepatocellular harm,
which is normally manifested by degeneration of hep-
atocytes that balloon and increased liver enzymes.
In this ﬁeld, inﬂammation is an important agent and
oxidative stress is considered a primary cause of the
damage to the cells (Tilg et al., 2021; [71]). The
3rd level of the disease’s course, ﬁbrosis eﬀect, can
develop from untreated NASH. Collagen and other
matrix extracellular compounds are deposited in the
liver, causing deformation architecture and a de-
crease in hepatic function. The ﬁbrotic process is
driven by the promotion of hepatic stellate cells and
retention by pro-ﬁbrotic cytokines, like PDGF and
TGF-β [29].
Cirrhosis, a condition characterized by regenerative
nodular, irreversible miss and severe scarring of liver
function, might eventually arise from persistent ﬁbro-
sis. Portal hypertension, decompensation of hepatic,
and hepatocellular cancer are all noticeably increased
by cirrhosis [8, 48]. At this level, there are few re-
maining curative options, and transplantation of the
liver might be the only way to recover. It’s critical to
remember that not every patient moves through these
levels in a straight line; some patients can stay in the
steatosis or NASH stage for years. Moreover, comor-
bidities such as type 2 diabetes, insulin resistance,
and obesity tend to hasten the course of the dis-
ease [15]. When evaluating preventive measures that
could halt or stop this pathological cascade, including
coﬀee intake, it is very important to have a thorough
understanding of the gradual NAFLD development.
2.2. Risk factors
A number of lifestyle-related and metabolic danger
indicators are inextricably linked to the onset and
development of NAFLD. However, slim people can
develop NAFLD, and metabolic dysfunction is the pri-
mary reason for the majority of instances (Fig. 1).
2.2.1. Obesity
One of the most well-established markers of NAFLD
is excess body fat, particularly visceral adiposity.
Obese people’s fatty tissue produces more free fatty
acids (FFAs) into the bloodstream. Therefore, the
liver absorbs these FFAs and then stores them as
triglycerides, which causes liver steatosis. Addition-
ally, cytokines of pro-inﬂammatory like IL-6 and
TNF-α, which aggravate liver inﬂammation and help
in the advancement of the disease, are produced by
adipose tissue [15, 97]. Additionally, obesity dis-
rupts normal hepatic lipid metabolism by changing
adipokine signaling and enhancing insulin resistance.
2.2.2. Insulin resistance
The pathophysiology of NAFLD is mainly driven by
weakened insulin sensitivity. The liver becomes less
sensitive to insulin’s suppression of lipogenesis and
gluconeogenesis when it is insulin-resistant. Buildup

2.2.3. Metabolic syndrome
Nowadays, NAFLD is commonly regarded as the

is a group of disorders that includes hypertension,
central obesity, dyslipidemia, and hyperglycemia.
The chance of developing NASH and ﬁbrosis from
basic steatosis is greatly increased when these char-
acteristics coincide. Hepatic damage is caused by
inﬂammation throughout the body, dysfunction of
endothelial cells, and altered lipid proﬁles in people
with metabolic syndrome [48].
2.2.4. Sedentary lifestyle
Physical inactivity raises the risk of NAFLD both
directly and indirectly. Increased body fat, decreased
insulin sensitivity, and impaired mitochondrial func-
tion are all linked to sedentary activity, and these
factors all contribute to the development of hep-
atic fat. Conversely, even in the absence of notable
weight reduction, regular exercise enhances insulin
signaling, encourages fatty acid oxidation, and lowers
intrahepatic fat content [8, 15]. One major factor
contributing to the global NAFLD epidemic is physical
inactivity, which is a result of the increasing inci-
dence of screen time and desk-bound jobs. A chain
reaction of metabolic dysfunction is produced when
these risk variables frequently interact and reinforce
one another. The mainstay of NAFLD prevention and
management continues to be addressing these modi-
ﬁable variables, including insulin resistance, obesity
and sedentary behavior.
2.3. Key pathogenic mechanisms
A conﬂuence of molecular assaults inside the liver
drives the progression of NAFLD from basic accumu-
lation of liver fat to more severe forms, including
ﬁbrosis, non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH), and
ultimately cirrhosis. Chronic inﬂammation, oxidative
stress, lipotoxicity, and mitochondrial dysfunction
are some of the most important processes inﬂuencing
the course of disease (Fig. 2). In addition to useless
hepatocytes, these interconnected trails also sustain
a vicious cycle of damage and compromised regener-
ation.

2.3.1. Oxidative stress
The accumulation of ROS (reactive oxygen species),
a consequence of high fatty acid oxidation in hepato-
cytes, is a deﬁning mark of NAFLD pathogenesis. An
imbalance between the production of ROS and the
antioxidant of liver defense systems outcomes from
peroxisomal β-oxidation and dysregulation of mito-
chondrial operations in the overloaded liver. DNA
damage, Lipid peroxidation, and modiﬁcation of pro-
tein are the results of this oxidative imbalance, and
they are all factors that lead to hepatocellular damage
[29, 76]. Moreover, oxidative stress triggers ﬁbroge-
nesis by enhancing hepatic stellate cells (HSCs) and
amplifying inﬂammatory indicators.
2.3.2. Lipotoxicity
Some lipid types, like diacylglycerols, ceramides
and FFAs, are very useless to liver cells and go beyond
mere buildup of fat. These lipotoxic components have
the force to cause hepatocyte death, dysfunction of
mitochondria, and endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress.
Also, lipotoxicity aggravates dysfunction of metabolic
dysfunction by enhancing insulin resistance and in-
terfering with lipid transport in the liver ([65]; Tilg
et al., 2021). These lipids have harmful results that
extend beyond energy storage; they are mediators of
bioactive inﬂammation and liver damage.
2.3.3. Chronic inﬂammation
An important agent in the growth of NAFLD to
NASH is persistent low-grade inﬂammation. DAMPs
(Damage-associated molecular patterns), which are
produced when hepatocytes are aﬀected by lipotoxi-
city and oxidative stress, activate Kupﬀer cells along
with other innate immune components. According to
Chalasani et al. [15], these immune cells generate
pro-inﬂammatory cytokines such as TNF-α, IL-6, and
IL-1β, which intensify local inﬂammation and attract
more inﬂammatory cells. In addition to harming liver
tissue, the chronic inﬂammatory milieu also plays
a role in metabolic instability and systemic insulin
resistance.
2.3.4. Mitochondrial dysfunction
Hepatic energy metabolism depends heavily on
mitochondria, especially when it comes to the
metabolism of fatty acids. Because of the poor
mitochondrial integrity in NAFLD, β-oxidation is hin-
dered, ATP synthesis is reduced, and ROS generation
is elevated. Patients with severe illness have been
shown to exhibit structural alterations in their mi-
tochondria, including enlargement and disruption of
the cristae. Mitochondrial dysfunction also releases
signals that enhance apoptosis and activate ﬁbrogenic
pathways, thereby accelerating disease progression

pathogenic network that turns hepatic steatosis, a

3. Coffee composition and hepato-protective
compounds
3.1. Major bioactive molecules in coffee
Coﬀee is a chemically rich infusion that contains
a wide range of physiologically active components,
making it much more than just a popular beverage.
The possible preventive eﬀects of several of these
elements on liver function, especially in the setting
of NAFLD, are becoming more widely acknowledged.
Caﬀeine, kahweol, chlorogenic acids, cafestol, and
other polyphenols are some of the most researched
(Fig. 3). These substances seem to aﬀect oxidative
stress, ﬁbrogenesis, inﬂammation, lipid metabolism,
and other important pathological processes associ-
ated with NAFLD. Chemical compositions of coﬀee
were presented in Table 1.
3.1.1. Caﬀeine
The most well-known ingredient in coﬀee, caﬀeine,
has several functions related to liver protection. It
has been demonstrated to improve lipid metabolism,
mainly by encouraging the oxidation of fatty acids
and decreasing the accumulation of fat in the liver.
Caﬀeine aﬀects energy expenditure and, through acti-
vating pathways related to lipolysis, may help reverse
hepatic steatosis. Furthermore, by preventing the ac-
tivation of hepatic stellate cells, which are essential
for the formation of liver ﬁbrosis, caﬀeine has shown
promise as an anti-ﬁbrotic agent [4, 52, 69]. Caﬀeine
is a particularly pertinent chemical in the context of
fatty liver because of these combined activities.
3.1.2. Chlorogenic acids
Types of polyphenolic chemicals found in large
quantities in coﬀee, particularly in green coﬀee
beans, are called chlorogenic acids. These com-
ponents are strong antioxidants that can decrease
oxidative stress, a main cause of liver damage in
NAFLD, by neutralizing ROS. So, Chlogenic acids
have been conducted to improve insulin sensitivity
in addition to their antioxidant activity, which helps
decrease one of the primary metabolic agents that
contribute to the accumulation of fatty liver (Olthof
et al., 2001; [51]). To further certain their hepato-
protective proﬁle, they may possibly impact lipid
metabolism and change the glucose absorption.

Diterpenes such as kahweol and cafestol are mostly
found in unﬁltered coﬀee species, like French press

3.1.4. Polyphenols
One of the best positions to obtain food polyphe-
nols, which have activities to anti-inﬂammatory and
antioxidant, is coﬀee. These compounds prevent in-
ﬂammatory markers pathways, which content on
blocking NF-κB, a modulator key of the generation of
cytokines pro-inﬂammatory. Also, Polyphenols may
disrupt the growth of NASH by decreasing liver
inﬂammation among this mechanism [39, 91]. More-
over, through the gut-liver axis, polyphenols can help
to improve the gut microbiota, which is becoming
more and more confess for its signiﬁcance impact
in liver health. These bioactive coﬀees’s ingredients
work together to give a multifaceted defense towards
the main causes of NAFLD. Together, these results
imply that frequent, moderate coﬀee drinking may
provide a natural way to enhance liver function, es-
pecially when combined with other healthy lifestyle
choices.
3.2. Brewing matters: How coffee preparation affects
bioavailability
Coﬀee’s ﬁnished chemical makeup and, therefore,
its possible impacts on liver health are greatly in-
ﬂuenced by the method preparation. The amount
and availability of important bioactive components
are changed by diﬀerent brewing techniques, which
could either increase or decrease the drink’s hep-
atoprotective beneﬁts. Interpreting the ﬁndings of
epidemiological and experimental studies on coﬀee
and NAFLD requires an understanding of these dis-
tinctions.
3.2.1. Filtered vs. unﬁltered coﬀee
The diﬀerence among ﬁltered and unﬁltered coﬀee
is among the most signiﬁcant. Paper ﬁlters are used to
ﬁlter out lipophilic substances, such as the diterpenes
kahweol and cafestol, from ﬁltered coﬀee. Although
these substances have been demonstrated to have
positive eﬀects on liver cells in animal models and in
vitro, they are also known to raise serum cholesterol
levels when taken in excess [87]. These diterpenes
are present in trace amounts in ﬁltered coﬀee, such
as those made with drip techniques or percolators,
which may lower cardiovascular risk while still pro-
viding caﬀeine and polyphenols. On the other hand,
unﬁltered coﬀee such as French press, boiled, or Turk-
ish styles retains much higher concentrations of these
compounds, potentially oﬀering stronger anti-ﬁbrotic
eﬀects in the liver, but at a possible cost to lipid
proﬁles [6].
3.2.2. Espresso vs. drip coﬀee
Espresso, which is brewed under pressure and uses
ﬁnely ground beans, diﬀers signiﬁcantly from drip
coﬀee in its concentration of bioactive molecules.
Despite being served in smaller volumes, espresso
typically delivers higher levels of caﬀeine and chloro-
genic acids per milliliter due to its brewing intensity.
However, since the overall serving size is smaller,
the total intake may be comparable to that from

extraction time also inﬂuence the release of antioxi-

3.2.3. Decaﬀeinated vs. regular coﬀee
Decaﬀeinated coﬀee undergoes chemical or water-
based processes to remove caﬀeine while aiming to
preserve ﬂavor and other compounds. Interestingly,
many of the liver-protective eﬀects of coﬀee appear
to persist in decaf varieties, suggesting that caﬀeine
is not the sole agent responsible for the observed ben-
eﬁts. Polyphenols, chlorogenic acids, and diterpenes
remain largely intact in decaﬀeinated coﬀee, espe-
cially if the processing method is gentle. Studies have
shown that decaf coﬀee retains antioxidant and anti-
inﬂammatory properties, which may still contribute
to reducing hepatic fat accumulation and mitigating
oxidative stress in NAFLD [42, 68]. Thus, decaf may
oﬀer a viable option for individuals sensitive to caf-
feine while still supporting liver health.
In summary, brewing method matters. The type of
coﬀee preparation not only aﬀects ﬂavor and aroma
but also shapes the proﬁle of compounds that reach
the liver. While all forms of coﬀee can potentially
oﬀer hepatoprotective beneﬁts, the balance between
beneﬁts and risks—especially regarding cholesterol—
may vary depending on how it’s made
4. Population-based and observational
evidence
4.1. Epidemiological ﬁndings
Over the past two decades, a substantial body
of population-based research has drawn connections
between regular coﬀee consumption and improved
liver health, particularly in individuals at risk for
non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) and non-
alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH). Observational stud-
ies, drawn from diverse populations and varying
consumption patterns, have repeatedly demonstrated
favorable liver-related outcomes in habitual coﬀee
drinkers.
One of the most consistently reported associa-
tions is the observation of lower liver enzyme levels
particularly alanine aminotransferase (ALT), aspar-
tate aminotransferase (AST), and gamma-glutamyl
transferase (GGT) among individuals who regularly
consume coﬀee. These enzymes, which typically rise
in response to liver inﬂammation or damage, tend
to remain within normal ranges or be signiﬁcantly
reduced in coﬀee consumers, suggesting a protective
or stabilizing eﬀect on liver cell integrity [55, 72].
Beyond enzyme proﬁles, coﬀee intake has also
been linked to improved liver histology, speciﬁcally
lower degrees of ﬁbrosis. In patients with known liver
disease or metabolic syndrome, higher coﬀee con-
sumption has been associated with reduced ﬁbrosis
scores, indicating a slower progression toward cirrho-
sis. This eﬀect seems to hold true even in populations
with established liver damage, which hints at coﬀee’s
potential in modifying disease trajectory [10, 36].
Numerous extensive epidemiological researchers
have discovered a negative correlation between cof-
fee drinking and the incidence or severity of NAFLD
and NASH in terms of disease risk. People who drink
more coﬀee, usually two cups or more a day, seem to
be less likely to be diagnosed with non-alcoholic fatty
liver disease. The importance of non-caﬀeine bioac-
tive chemicals is highlighted by several researches
that indicate this preventive beneﬁt may apply to
both caﬀeinated and decaﬀeinated variants [14, 98].
The reliability of results across several cohorts and
geographic locations increases the likelihood of a real
biological eﬀect, even though these investigations are
observational and cannot establish causation. Also,
these results lay the groundwork for additional mech-
anistic study and clinical tests to investigate coﬀees
possible as an addition in the prevention or treatment
of liver disease.
4.2. Meta-analyses support
The protective link among coﬀee drinking and
NAFLD has been reconﬁrmed by a many of overall
meta-analyses appeared in the last ten years. Reg-
ular consumption of coﬀee is linked with a lower
risk of NAFLD and its linked histological and bio-
chemical signals, according to these studies, which
bringing together data from extensive observational
groups and clinical studies. The signiﬁcant statisti-
cally negative association among coﬀee intake and
grew liver enzymes, in particular AST and ALT, which
are early signals of inﬂammation and hepatic steato-
sis, is a common result among these meta-analyses.
In study by, Kennedy et al. [40] showed that person-
nel who consumed coﬀee regularly had continuation
little odds of high transaminases compared to non-
drinkers, suggesting a stabilizing impact on function
of liver at the population level.

preecha et al. [93] collected data from tow longi-
tudinal and cross-sectional studies, including over
430,000 volunteers. Their analysis revealed a sig-

5.1. Reduces hepatic fat

the development of non-alcoholic fatty liver disease
(NAFLD) is the abnormal accumulation of triglyc-

hepatic steatosis. Coﬀee appears to exert a mitigating
impact on this process through two principal biologi-

suppression of de novo lipogenesis (the synthesis of
new fat).
The improvement of AMPK (AMP-activated protein
kinase), an energy-sensing enzyme that is important
for controlling metabolism of lipid, is a factor mech-
anism linked to coﬀee’s ability to reduce hepatic
fat. The enhancement of AMPK causes the liver’s
balance of energy to convert from storing the fat
to breaking down these fats. It has been conducted
that AMPK signaling in hepatic cells is prompted by
a many of coﬀee’s bioactive compounds, most no-

mitochondrial fatty acid oxidation and reduce ex-

been reported to block signiﬁcant lipogenesis-related
enzymes, including synthase of fatty acid and ACC

ability to form new fatty acids from carbohydrates.
Also, the liver is more potential to turn too much
from glucose into fat in people with hyperglycemia
or insulin resistance, this is especially important.
It is thought that chlorogenic acid, which reduces
the expression of sterol regulatory element-binding

of these lipogenic methods [57].
Furthermore, regular coﬀee consumption has been
shown to lower liver fat concentration in both human
and animal studies (Fig. 4). Hepatic levels of triglyc-

improved when coﬀee or extracted chlorogenic acids
were supplemented in animal studies of diet-induced
steatosis [83]. The importance of those processes in
real-world contexts is supported by human studies
that showed even modest coﬀee consumption was
linked to decreased liver fat as determined by imaging
techniques [92]. All of these results point to coﬀee’s
interference with the fundamental metabolic dysfunc-

Coﬀee can prevent steatosis from developing and
possibly cure the accumulation of fat in early-stage
non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) by increas-
ing lipid metabolism and inhibiting lipid production
at the cellular level.

In non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD), oxida-

of liver injury and disease progression. The excessive
accumulation of triglycerides and free fatty acids in

damage, lipid peroxidation, and DNA injury, while
also activating inﬂammatory pathways. As such, en-
hancing the liver’s antioxidant status is a important
therapeutic aim and this is where coﬀee’s potential
beneﬁts are evident.
Coﬀee intake signiﬁcantly decreased oxidative

5.4. Anti-ﬁbrotic properties
Liver ﬁbrosis is an important stage in the pro-
gression of NAFLD, representing the body’s chronic
wound-healing response to sustained hepatic injury
and inﬂammation. Hepatocellular carcinoma, liver
failure, or cirrhosis can result from this ﬁbrotic pro-
cess if it is not treated. Hepatic stellate cells (HSCs),
which are normally dormant cells that store vitamin
A, are the primary mediators of ﬁbrosis in the liver.
When the liver is injured, these cells become active.
When HSCs are activated, they change into cells that
resemble myoﬁbroblasts and produce a lot of extra-
cellular matrixes (ECM), especially collagen types I
and III. This causes the hepatic tissue to become stiﬀer
and scarred.
Emerging research has shown that coﬀee, and its
key bioactive compounds, may interfere with the
ﬁbrogenic process by targeting these very mecha-
nisms. One of the primary ways coﬀees exert its
anti-ﬁbrotic action is by inhibiting the activation and
proliferation of hepatic stellate cells, thereby reduc-
ing ECM deposition [4, 89]. Animal studies have
consistently demonstrated lower levels of liver ﬁ-
brosis in coﬀee-treated groups compared to controls
exposed to ﬁbrogenic agents.
This protective eﬀect appears to involve the down
regulation of pro-ﬁbrotic genes, such as transform-
ing growth factor-beta (TGF-β) and connective tissue
growth factor (CTGF) both of which are pivotal in
activating HSCs and perpetuating ﬁbrotic signaling
cascades [4, 55]. Coﬀee polyphenols, such as chloro-
genic acid, as well as kahweol and cafestol, have
been shown to interfere with these ﬁbrotic pathways,
potentially by modulating cellular redox states and
suppressing inﬂammation-driven activation of stel-
late cells.
Furthermore, human observational studies align
with experimental ﬁndings, showing that individu-
als with higher coﬀee intake tend to have lower
liver stiﬀness measurements (an indirect measure of
ﬁbrosis) and a reduced risk of advanced ﬁbrosis, es-
pecially among populations with NAFLD or chronic
liver conditions [14, 55]. The correlation between
regular coﬀee consumption and reduced ﬁbrosis risk
is robust across various study populations, including
those with obesity, diabetes, or viral hepatitis co-
morbidities.
In sum, coﬀee seems to oﬀer multi-layered anti-
ﬁbrotic protection by both preventing the patho-
logical activation of hepatic stellate cells and down
regulating key ﬁbrotic mediators at the gene ex-
pression level. This dual mechanism provides further
support for coﬀee’s potential as a low-cost, accessible
dietary component that may slow the progression of
NAFLD toward its more advanced and irreversible
stages.
5.5. Improves insulin sensitivity
Insulin resistance lies at the core of metabolic
dysfunction in NAFLD, acting as both a driver and
a consequence of hepatic fat accumulation. When
insulin signaling becomes impaired—particularly in
muscle, adipose tissue, and the liver—the body
struggles to regulate glucose and lipid metabolism
eﬀectively. This metabolic imbalance promotes in-
creased hepatic de novo lipogenesis, reduced fatty
acid oxidation, and elevated circulating free fatty
acids, all of which contribute to hepatic steatosis.
Therefore, strategies that enhance insulin sensitivity
are crucial in preventing or reversing the progression
of fatty liver disease.

7.3. Variability in outcomes
Despite the promising evidence linking coﬀee

9. Limitations in current literature
Although there is increasing evidence that coﬀee
may help prevent or lessen non-alcoholic fatty liver
disease (NAFLD), there are a number of important
limitations in the current research that should be
carefully taken into account. These ﬂaws not only
weaken the validity of the results drawn thus far,
but they also point to crucial areas that require more
investigation.
9.1. Lack of randomized controlled trials (RCTs)
Most of the information that is currently avail-
able about the link between coﬀee drinking and
non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) is from
cross-sectional or observational research. These con-
ceptions can be useful in spotting possible linkages,
but they are disabling to demonstrate causation. It is
diﬃcult to deﬁnite whether coﬀee intake is directly
accountable for the liver advantages that have been
showed or whether the eﬀect is being driven by other
related lifestyle agents in the lack of large-scale RCTs
(randomized controlled trials) [20, 54]. To deﬁne
clear reason and eﬀect linkages and eliminate con-
founding changes, RCTs are crucial.
9.2. Variable diagnostic criteria for NAFLD
The variation in NAFLD diagnosis between re-
searches is another essential problem. To detect liver
steatosis, some paper only utilizes imaging pathways
like transient ultrasound or elastography, while oth-
ers reported more deﬁnitive but intrusive pathways
like liver biopsy. Although ultrasound is non-invasive
and beneﬁts for large people, its sensitivity is re-
stricted, especially when it comes to discovering
early-level ﬁbrosis or improve steatosis [79, 95]. This
discrepancy can ﬁnd in incorrect disease severity clas-
siﬁcation and do it challenging to compare results
from diﬀerent papers.
9.3. Inconsistent coffee types and doses
The variety of coﬀee varieties, preparation meth-
ods, and intake amounts evaluated in various re-
searches presents an additional challenge. Many
researches do not determine the quantity of roasting
or caﬀeine amounts, while some level on ﬁltered cof-
fee, while others trials espresso or unﬁltered brews.
Moreover, the dose of coﬀee intake is frequently
self-reported and not always evaluated, ranging from
imprecise phrases like “cups every day” to precise
measurements of milligrams of caﬀeine or certain
polyphenols [50]. It is challenging to ascertain the
ideal formulation, frequency, and quantity of coﬀee
required to produce therapeutic beneﬁts because of
these discrepancies.
9.4. Short study durations
Finally, many of the available human studies—
especially interventional trials—are limited by short
follow-up durations, often ranging from a few weeks
to several months. Since NAFLD and its progression
(to NASH and ﬁbrosis) are chronic, slowly evolving
conditions, short-term studies may not fully capture
the long-term impact of coﬀee on liver health [14].
Moreover, beneﬁcial eﬀects observed in the short
term may not persist, while other beneﬁts—such as
ﬁbrosis attenuation—may only become evident over
extended periods.
In conclusion, while current research points toward
coﬀee’s promise as a hepatoprotective agent, caution
is needed in interpreting the ﬁndings due to method-
ological weaknesses. Standardized diagnostic tools,
well-designed RCTs, controlled doses of chemically
characterized coﬀee, and longer study durations are
needed to advance this ﬁeld and translate observa-
tional evidence into clinical practice.
10. Future research directions
There is still a glaring need for more thorough
and nuanced research, even if existing studies indi-
cate that coﬀee may have preventive eﬀects against
NAFLD and related liver diseases. Future studies of
coﬀee’s eﬀects on liver function must go beyond ob-
servational correlations and explore the molecular,
clinical, and individual components.
10.1. High-quality, long-term randomized controlled
trials
Well-designed, randomized controlled trials (RCTs)
are necessary to draw ﬁrm results. A liver biopsy
or other advanced imaging modalities should ideally
conﬁrm histological endpoints, such as changes in
steatosis, inﬂammation, ﬁbrosis, and hepatocellular
ballooning. These studies should ideally be long-
term in order to capture the gradual progression and
possible reversal of NAFLD. By using controlled set-
tings, researchers can reduce confounding from other
lifestyle factors and isolate the precise eﬀects of cof-
fee.

10.2. Targeted research on isolated coffee

While whole coﬀee shows promise, it remains
unclear which individual components are most re-

should focus on isolated bioactive constituents such
as chlorogenic acids, caﬀeine, cafestol, and kahweol,

molecules, clarify dose–response relationships, and
possibly allow for the development of pharmacologi-
cal or nutraceutical interventions for NAFLD.
10.3. Personalized nutrition and genetic variability
An emerging frontier in nutrition science involves
the tailoring of dietary recommendations based on
individual genetic proﬁles and metabolic responses.
Genetic polymorphisms—particularly in genes re-
lated to caﬀeine metabolism (e.g., CYP1A2) or insulin
sensitivity—may inﬂuence how individuals respond
to coﬀee consumption. Future research should ex-
plore how nutrigenomics and personalized nutrition
could optimize the hepatoprotective beneﬁts of coﬀee
in diﬀerent population subgroups.
10.4. Integrative dietary strategies for liver health
Rather than considering coﬀee in isolation, it is im-
portant to evaluate how it ﬁts within broader dietary
frameworks. For instance, combining coﬀee with
other anti-inﬂammatory or antioxidant-rich dietary
components—such as those in the Mediterranean or
DASH diets—may produce synergistic eﬀects on liver
function and metabolic outcomes. Long-term cohort
studies and intervention trials should investigate how
coﬀee interacts with other food patterns, physical
activity levels, and lifestyle modiﬁcations to form
comprehensive strategies for NAFLD prevention and
management.
In summary, although current evidence paints a
promising picture, a shift toward precision-focused,
mechanistically informed, and clinically rigorous re-
search is needed to fully understand the role of coﬀee
in liver health. This future work has the potential not
only to validate coﬀee’s place in clinical nutrition but
also to guide evidence-based recommendations for
public health.
11. Conclusion
The body of evidence accumulated over recent
year’s points toward coﬀee as a potentially safe,
natural, and cost-eﬀective adjunct in the manage-
ment of non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD).
It is a dietary component that is uniquely posi-
tioned and has signiﬁcant public health importance
due to its widespread global intake, accessibility,
and bioactive proﬁle. Coﬀee has a number of hep-
atoprotective qualities, including anti-inﬂammatory,
anti-ﬁbrotic, antioxidant, and metabolic regulation

of NAFLD from reducing hepatic fat accumulation
and improving insulin sensitivity to dampening ox-

The idea that moderate coﬀee consumption typically
two to three cups per day may provide liver-speciﬁc
beneﬁts is supported by the consistent inverse associ-
ations found in population-based and interventional
studies, even though causality has not yet been con-

able safety characteristics. Since there are currently
no approved pharmacotherapies for NAFLD, lifestyle
change is still the mainstay of treatment. Therefore,
a low-barrier intervention that shows promise is in-
cluding moderate coﬀee drinking into dietary plans.
Before making clear recommendations, however, re-
searchers and doctors must wait for more robust
clinical validation, particularly in certain subgroups
such individuals with severe ﬁbrosis, comorbidities,
or genetic variants impacting caﬀeine metabolism. In
summary, coﬀee has signiﬁcant potential as a com-

preventing NAFLD, even though it is not a cure-all

promise into reality by using individualized health
plans and top-notch research.
