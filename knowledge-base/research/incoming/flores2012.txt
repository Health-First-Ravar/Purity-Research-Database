A method using liquid chromatography coupled to tandem-mass spectrometry (LC–MS/MS) with triple
quadrupole in selective reaction monitoring mode was developed to determine organic acids (glutamic,
tartaric, quinic, malonic, malic, shikimic, a-ketoglutaric, pyruvic, citric, succinic and fumaric acids) in
fruits (melon, grape, peach, orange, lemon) and vegetables (green and red pepper, tomato, lettuce and
lamb’s lettuce). The negative ion mode of ESI was used. All the validation parameters were found to be
within the range of acceptance for all the compounds. Different matrix effects were observed in the stud-
ied vegetables and samples were quantiﬁed by the standard additions approach. The main advantages of
this method are its selectivity, which is one of the main weaknesses of most methods when analysing
organic acid in complex matrices, and its simplicity since little sample preparation is needed.

1. Introduction
Organic acids strongly inﬂuence the organoleptic properties of
fruits and vegetables, particularly as regards ﬂavour, colour and
aroma. They are responsible for fruit sourness or acidity which is
ranked relative to citric (Kader, 2008). In addition, organic acids
indirectly affect the phenolic metabolism by altering pH, and act
as precursors of phenolics and ﬂavour compounds (Galdon,
Rodriguez, Rodriguez, & Romero, 2008; Kader, 2008).
Determination of the organic acid proﬁle is important when
characterising different genotypes or when studying the inﬂuence
of maturity or agronomic factors on fruit quality. Several methods
have been developed for identifying and quantifying individually
organic acids in different vegetal matrixes by using electrophoretic
and chromatographic methods (Mato, Suarez-Luque, & Huidobro,
2005). Reported methodologies include capillary electrophoresis
(Castineira,
Pena,
Herrero,
&
Garcia-Martin,
2002;
Mato,
Suarez-Luque, & Huidobro, 2007), which presents advantages
including high resolution, simplicity and short analysis times, but
also disadvantages, such as lower reproducibility; gas chromatog-
raphy (GC), mainly with ﬂame ionisation (FID) and mass spectrom-
etry (MS) detectors (Jham, Fernandes, Garcia, & da Silva, 2002;
Molnar-Perl, 1999); liquid chromatography (LC) with ultraviolet
(UV) (Zheng et al., 2009), chemiluminescent methods (Perez-
Ruiz, Martinez-Lozano, Tomas, & Martin, 2004), electrochemical
methods (Kotani, Miyaguchi, Tomita, Takamura, & Kusu, 2004),
refractive index (RI) (Scarlata & Hyman, 2010), conductivity
(Magne, Mathlouthi, & Robilland, 1998) and mass spectrometry
(MS) detectors (Erro, Zamarreno, Yvin, & Garcia-Mina, 2009; Fer-
nandez-Fernandez et al., 2010). Among all these methods, GC and
LC coupled with mass detection are considered the most sensitive
and selective methods for analysing organic acids. However, GC
methods are usually tedious since most short-chain organic acids
are not volatile, and so prior sample derivatization is needed
(Giumanini, Verardo, Della Martina, & Toniutti, 2001; Saraji &
Mousavinia, 2006). This paper describes an analytical method–
liquid chromatography tandem-mass spectrometry (LC–MS/MS)
with triple quadrupole in selective reaction monitoring (SRM)
mode focused on sensitivity and selectivity – for the simultaneous
determination of organic acids in vegetables and fruits, avoiding
sample derivatization. Unlike other previous studies, the present
work focuses on several matrixes (ﬁve fruits and ﬁve vegetables)
and allows most of the predominant organic acids in fruit and veg-
etables to be determined.

3. Results and discussion
3.1. Liquid chromatographic determination
Preferable mobile phase used in LC-techniques contains an ion-
isation boosting agent, such as formic acid, triﬂuoroacetic acid
(TFA) or ammonium acetate, to improve the chromatographic peak
shape and to optimise ionisation in the MS interface. According to
Temesi
and
Law
(1999),
who
investigated
the
electrospray
ionisation mass spectrometry response to different eluent addi-
tives, TFA is the worst additive for ESI in both negative- and posi-
tive-ion modes whereas formic acid is the best choice. In our study,
different concentrations of formic acid between 0.005% and 0.5% v/
v were tested as mobile phase to obtain the optimal response and
separation of organic acids. The best results related to sensitivity
and separation were achieved using 0.1% (v/v) formic acid
(pH = 2.45), at a ﬂow rate of 0.4 ml/min. Under the described chro-
matographic conditions, organic acids eluted in less than 15 min
(Table 1). LC–MS mass spectra in positive and negative modes were
recorded across the range 70–400 m/z, by injecting the individual
analytes at a concentration of 50 lg/ml. ESI negative ion mode pro-
vided the best response. A study of the optimal selective reaction
monitoring (SRM) transitions for each organic acid was carried
out using several fragmentor voltages (from 50 to 130 V) and
collision energies (from 2 to 20 V). Table 1 shows the MS condi-
tions selected for quantiﬁcation of the selected compounds. Using
these selective transitions, the absence of interference was con-
ﬁrmed for organic acids with similar retention times. Fig. 1 shows
extracted ion chromatograms of a standard (10 lg/l, except pyru-
vic 100 lg/ml) of organic acids.

0.981 and 0.999 (Table 2). The effect of co-eluting matrix compo-
nents, which can cause ion suppression or enhancement of the
analyte, is especially relevant when using HPLC tandem mass spec-
trometry. In order to validate the assay, the matrix effect was eval-
uated following the post-extraction spike method in the different
studied matrices (Van Eeckhaut, Lanckmans, Sarre, Smolders, &
Michotte, 2009). For that, the response of the organic acids in solu-
tion were compared with the response of these compounds in veg-
etal material spiked at different concentrations included in the
linear range. The ratio of the slopes for the solvent and matrix
(Ss/Sm) was determined to provide information about the enhance-
ment or suppression of the signal in the different matrices (Tables
3 and 4). Both suppression (Ss/Sm > 1) and increase (Ss/Sm < 1) of the
signals were observed as a result of the effect of the different
matrices. As regards glutamic acid, fruits showed a low matrix ef-
fect, whereas vegetables tended to decrease the signal showing Ss/
Sm ratios from 1.69 to 2.48. For malonic and a-ketoglutaric, the dif-
ferent matrices had a mild effect, the ratio ranging from 0.64 to
1.13 and from 0.83 to 1.07, respectively. For tartaric, quinic, shiki-
mic, succinic and fumaric acids, the different matrices showed a
suppressive effect on the signal, except in some cases with melon
and pepper, when values closed to 1 were reached. Finally, an
overall suppressive effect of fruit and vegetable matrices on the
signal for malic, pyruvic and citric acids was observed (except for
lettuce). As a consequence of the different matrix effects observed,
a standard addition protocol, rather than calibration curves, was
used to quantify the studied organic acids in the samples, dividing
the extracts into different aliquots. One aliquot was analysed as
such, and increasing known amounts of the standard analytes were
added to the other aliquots.
In order to evaluate the extraction efﬁciency of the proposed
method, the recoveries were studied by comparing the concentra-
tion of the different organic acids in the different samples (C0),
with those of samples spiked with the target organic acids before
extraction (Cn), at two levels, depending on the matrix (Tables 3
and 4). The spiking levels were presented as mg/100 g in fresh
weight (FW), taken into account weight of vegetal material used
for the extraction, the volume of vegetal extract and the standard
solution concentration. All the samples were extracted with the
proposed method and recoveries were calculated according to
the equation R = 100  (Cn  C0)/Cspiked. Recoveries ranged from
73% to 119% for all the organic acids in the different assayed
matrices.
3.2.2. Repeatability, detection limits and quantiﬁcation limits
The repeatability of the chromatographic method was evalu-
ated by injecting (ten times) the analytes in the solvent at a con-
centration of 10 lg/ml and a sample of each vegetable material
fortiﬁed with the different organic acids, and determining the
relative standard deviation (RSD) values for the retention times
and for peak areas. The intra-day RSD values for all the organic acid
and matrix types ranged from 0.1% to 0.9% for the retention times
and from 0.5% to 6.9% for peak areas. Inter-day RSDs for the reten-
tion times and peak areas were below 1.1% and 8.3%, respectively
(Table 2).
The limits of detection (LOD) and quantiﬁcation (LOQ) of the
proposed method were determined as the amount for which the
signal-to-noise ratio (S/N) was 3 and 10, respectively. Bearing in
mind the presence of a matrix effect and the fact that no ‘‘blank’’
matrices were available in most cases, LODs were estimated by
extrapolation of the S/N of the extract with known amounts of ana-
lytes. In the studied matrices, LODs ranged from 0.01 to 0.67 mg/
100 g for glutamic, 0.01 to 15.16 mg/100 g for tartaric, 0.01 to
1.23 mg/100 g for quinic, 0.01 to 0.02 mg/100 g for malonic, 0.29
to 11.55 mg/100 g for malic, 0.01 to 0.11 mg/100 g for shikimic,

3.3. Sample determination
Fruits and vegetables were analysed following the extraction
methods described above. For accurate quantiﬁcation of the sam-
ples, the standard additions approach was followed for each matrix
(Table 5). The values found agreed with those reported for these
fruits and vegetables in previous studies. Citric and malic were
the major organic acid in melon (Tang, Bie, Wu, Yi, & Feng, 2010)
and peach (Wu et al., 2005); in grape, tartaric and malic acid
showed the highest concentration of all the organic acids detected
(Soyer, Koca, & Karadeniz, 2003); in orange, citric followed by
malic were the major organic acids (Cancolon & Xu, 2002) although
glutamic and quinic acids were also present in large quantities; in
lemon, citric represented the highest proportion followed by malic,
glutamic, quinic, and a-ketoglutaric acids.
As regards ‘‘fruity’’ vegetables, citric, malic, quinic (in pepper)
and glutamic (in tomato) were the major acids, and other organic
acids were detected at lower levels. As previously reported, organic
acid concentrations in pepper depended on the ripening stage so
that fully mature fruits (red) has lower malic concentrations and
much higher citric concentrations than green fruits (Flores, Hellin,
Lacasa, Lopez, & Fenoll, 2009). Leafy vegetables showed a high con-
centration of malic and citric. Moreover, lamb’s lettuce showed
higher concentrations of glutamic and quinic, and lower concentra-
tions of malonic and tartaric than lettuce, whereas concentrations
of shikimic, a-ketoglutaric, fumaric and succinic levels were simi-
lar for both vegetables. Finally, pyruvic is a ﬂavour precursor that
has been shown to be correlated with perceived pungency in veg-
etables such as onion (Galdon et al., 2008). It can be also found at
relative low concentration in a few fruits such as apples and grapes

4. Conclusions
The new proposed HPLC–MS/MS method allows the simulta-
neous determination of major and minor organic acid in fruits
and vegetables. This method is very simple, involves little sample
preparation so that a large number of samples can be simulta-
neously extracted, and is quite fast (total analysis time does not
exceed 15 min). The use of the MS/MS detector provides reliable
results and increased selectivity compared with other detection
techniques, without requiring sample derivatization. According to
the validation study, the sensitivity, linearity, repeatability and
recovery of the method are satisfactory. The described method
can be used in routine analysis for the determination of the main
organic acids in melon, grape, peach, orange, lemon, green and
red pepper, tomato, lettuce and lamb’s lettuce, and may be easily
extended to other matrices and organic acids.
