Effect of roasting on ochratoxin A level in green coffee beans inoculated with 
Aspergillus ochraceus 
Haruo Tsubouchi, 1 Katsuhiko Yamamoto, 1 Kazuo Hisada, ~ Yoshio Sakabe ~ & Shun-ichi Udagawa 2 
1Nagoya City Health Research Institute; 2National Institute of Hygienic Sciences, Nagaja, Japan 
Keywords: Ochratoxin A, Heat stability, Green coffee beans, Aspergillus ochraceus 
Abstract 
The heat stability of ochratoxin A in green coffee beans inoculated with Aspergillus ochraceus was studied. 
Heat treatment (roasting) at 200~ 
for 10 or 20 min reduced the levels of ochratoxin A by only 0-12~ 
in 
the dried whole beans. Almost all of the ochratoxin A was infused into the coffee decoction when the roasted 
samples were ground and extracted with boiling water. Therefore, the reduction of ochratoxin A concentra- 
tion of contaminated coffee beans by roasting under these conditions is ineffective. 
Introduction 
Mycotoxins have been detected infrequently and 
at low levels in green coffee beans (6-8, 11, 14). A 
partial explanation for such low concentrations is 
the inhibitory effect of caffeine in coffee beans on 
fungal growth and/or mycotoxin production (1-3, 
9). In recent papers (14-16), however, we have 
reported the natural occurrence of ochratoxin A in 
imported green coffee beans; some isolates of 
Aspergillus flavus Link and A. ochraceus Wilhelm 
from these green coffee beans grew well and 
produced relatively high levels of aflatoxins and 
ochratoxins in liquid medium containing 0.1-1.0~ 
caffeine or ground green coffee medium, and these 
isolates had the ability to degrade caffeine in the 
medium (15, 16). 
The removal or reduction of mycotoxins in food 
has been demonstrated by several groups of wor- 
kers (12). In the case of green coffee beans, the 
process involved roasting ground green coffee 
(200~ 
5 min) spiked with ochratoxin A and 
aflatoxin B1; this procedure resulted in destruction 
of approximately 800/0 of the mycotoxins (4, 7, 8). 
The present study investigated heat stability of 
ochratoxin A produced by growing A. ochraceus on 
green coffee beans which were subsequently 
processed in various ways.

Discussion 
Natural occurrence of ochratoxin A in green cof- 
fee beans was first reported by Levi et al. (7). In 
their survey, ochratoxin A was found at levels from 
trace to a concentration of 80/~g/kg in three of 68 
samples (4.4070) of green coffee beans. Buchanan et 
al. (2) reported that caffeine had little effect on the 
growth of A. ochraceus, but did show an inhibitory 
effect on ochratoxin A accumulation. Their results 
suggest that coffee beans are relatively resistant to 
ochratoxin A contamination. Levi (8) concluded 
that the probability of finding various mycotoxins 
(viz. ochratoxin A, aflatoxin, sterigmatocystin) in 
the coffee beverage was very low, and recommend- 
ed that studies on mycotoxins in coffee should be 
discontinued. It was also demonstrated that ex- 
perimental roasting (20 min at about 200~ 
of 
ground green coffee beans and ground wheat de- 
stroyed 80 to 90% of ochratoxin A spiked in the 
samples (7). 
Our results indicate that the roasting treatment 
of whole green coffee beans (sample Nos 1-4) at 
200 ~ for 10 or 20 min has little effect on the level

method of introducing the toxin into the ground 
beans. Levi et al. spiked the ground beans with 
ochratoxin A. In the present work, the toxin was 
produced by inoculating whole green beans with 
toxigenic A. ochraceus. The toxin produced in this 
manner may be more strongly heat-resistant as a re- 
sult of binding of ochratoxin A to components of 
the beans and may represent natural contamination 
of caffee beans more accurately. The present results 
also indicate that ochratoxin A in roasted ground 
coffee prepared from contaminated green coffee 
beans can be extracted virtually intact into the cof- 
fee decoction (Table 4) and the toxin was not de- 
stroyed by boiling water. 
In a recent paper (14), we reported that ochratox- 
in A was found at low levels of 9.9-46.0/~g/kg in 
four of 22 samples (18.2%) of imported green cof- 
fee beans collected from commercial markets in Ja- 
pan. Furthermore, we reported that some strains of 
toxigenic A. flavus and A. ochraceus isolated from 
green coffee beans grew well and produced high lev- 
els of aflatoxins in YES liquid medium containing 
0.1-1.0% caffeine (15, 16). It seems clear that these 
strains have the potential to grow on moist green 
coffee beans and to produce significant amounts of 
ochratoxins and possibly aflatoxins. It has been 
shown in the present work that if green coffee 
beans are contaminated with ochratoxin A, almost 
all of the toxin can be included in a coffee beverage 
made from them. These findings suggest strongly 
that continuation of surveillance for mycotoxin 
contamination of green coffee beans is necessary.
