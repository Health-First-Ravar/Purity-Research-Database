and identiﬁcation of key bitter taste compounds

Abstract Sensory-guided fractionation of a roasted coffee
brew by means of sequential solvent extraction, ultraﬁltra-
tion, and RP-HPLC demonstrated a group of ethyl acetate
soluble compounds formed from O-hydroxycinnamoyl
quinic acid derivatives upon coffee roasting as the key
compounds contributing to the bitter taste of roasted
coffee beverages. LC-MS/MS studies, 1D- and 2D-
NMR spectroscopy, syntheses, and model roast exper-
iments with 5-O-caffeoyl- and 5-O-feruloylquinic acid
led to the unequivocal identiﬁcation of 3-O-caffeoyl-γ-
quinide (2a), 4-O-caffeoyl-γ-quinide (3a), 5-O-caffeoyl-
epi-δ-quinide (4a), 4-O-caffeoyl-muco-γ-quinide (5a), 5-
O-caffeoyl-muco-γ-quinide (6a), 3-O-feruloyl-γ-quinide
(2b), and 4-O-feruloyl-γ-quinide (3b) as intense coffee bit-
ter tastants. Besides these individual bitter compounds, a
highly complex and intensely bitter HPLC fraction was
isolated from the ethyl acetate extractables of coffee brew.
Application of COSY spectroscopy and alkaline hydrolytic
degradation gave strong evidence that the bitter taste of that
fraction is due to a multiplicity of rather complex quinic
acid lactone isomers multiply esteriﬁed with p-coumaric
acid, caffeic acid, ferulic acid, 3,4-dimethoxycinnamic
acid, and quinic acid, respectively. As representatives
of this fraction, 3,4-O-dicaffeoyl-γ-quinide (10), 3,5-O-
dicaffeoyl-epi-δ-quinide (11), and 4,5-O-dicaffeoyl-muco-
γ-quinide (12) have been isolated, puriﬁed, and identiﬁed
as strongly bitter-tasting compounds in roasted coffee. For
the ﬁrst time, bitter taste recognition thresholds were deter-
mined for the individual compounds showing that, strongly

Introduction
Beside to its stimulatory effect, the popularity of a freshly
prepared coffee beverage is mainly due to the sheer enjoy-
ment of the alluring aroma as well its attractive and well-
balanced taste proﬁle. Flavour research performed within
the last 15 years has shown that just a few out of several
hundreds of volatile compounds really contribute to the
characteristic overall aroma of roasted coffee. By means
of gas chromatography-olfactometry, quantitative analysis,
and aroma reconstitution experiments it could be impres-
sively demonstrated that out of several hundreds of coffee
volatiles just about 25 odorants are essentially needed to
mimic the typical odour of a coffee brew [1–3]. Compared
to the aroma-active volatiles, the available information on
the molecular basis for the typical bitter taste of coffee
brews, which develops during roasting of the beans, is
rather fragmentary.
More then 20 years ago, ﬁrst investigations performed
by Chen [4] revealed that the alkaloids caffeine and
trigonelline, already present in the raw coffee bean, are ac-
countable for a maximum of 10–30 and 1%, respectively,
to the overall bitterness of a coffee beverage. The author
suggested that, in particular, roast compounds formed from
tasteless precursors during bean roasting are the key elici-
tors of the bitter taste of coffee brews. More precisely, sev-
eral heterocyclics are suggested as potential bitter-tasting
agents in roasted coffees such as, e.g. furfurylalcohol [5],
5-hydroxymethyl-2-furanaldehyde, pyrazines and various
trigonellin thermolysis products [6]. In addition, a series of
cis- and trans-conﬁgured 2,5-diketopiperazines have been
reported as bitter constituents of roasted coffee [7], but the
contribution of all these heterocyclic compounds to the cof-
fee bitter taste still remains unclear. Other research groups

493
found some evidence that O-caffeoylquinic acids (chloro-
genic acids) and their roast products may be a cause of the
observed bitter taste of coffee [8]. Although a bitter-tasting
fraction isolated from roasted coffee was reported to con-
tain lactones of such chlorogenic acids, neither the isolated
and puriﬁed compounds have been sensorially evaluated
so far, nor any human bitter detection thresholds have been
determined [9]. In addition, quinic acid, a well-known ther-
mal degradation product of chlorogenic acids, was reported
to exhibit an aspirin-like bitter taste at a threshold level of
10 ppm. The concentration of that compound in roast cof-
fee brew was found to exceed this threshold concentration
by a factor of 20, thus suggesting some evidence for its
taste contribution [10, 11]. Since these data reported so far
in the literature are rather contradictory, it has to be con-
cluded that the molecular nature of the key bitter tastants
in roasted coffees is still unclear.
In order to answer the puzzling question as to which
non-volatile, key taste compounds are responsible for the
attractive taste of food products, we have recently devel-
oped the so-called taste dilution analysis as a powerful
screening procedure for taste-active non-volatiles in foods
[12]. This approach, combining instrumental analysis and
human bioresponse, recently led to the discovery of various
previously unknown taste compounds such as thermally
generated bitter compounds [12], cooling compounds in
dark malt [13], bitter off-tastants in carrot products [14],
the taste enhancer alapyridaine in beef bouillon [15], and
astringent key taste compounds in black-tea infusions [16].
Aimed at deﬁning the bitter taste of roasted coffee on a
molecular level, the objectives of the present investigation
were, to screen a roasted coffee beverage for its key taste
compounds by applying taste dilution techniques, to isolate
and determine the chemical structure of the compounds
inducing the most intense human bitter taste response, and
to evaluate their sensory impact on the basis of their oral
recognition threshold concentrations.
Materials and methods
Chemicals
All the chemicals were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich
(Taufkirchen, Germany), solvents were obtained in HPLC-
grade from Merck (Darmstadt, Germany), and deuterated
solvents were supplied by Euriso-Top (Gif-Sur-Yvette,
France). Decaffeinated raw and roasted Arabica coffee
beans from Columbia were obtained from the coffee
industry.
Sensory analyses
Using a triangle test closely following the procedure re-
ported recently [14–16], taste recognition threshold con-
centrations were determined in water (Vitel, low mineral-
ization: 405 mg/l; pH 5.0) by a 12-headed trained sensory
panel, which in regular intervals participated in training ses-
sions with reference compounds. The values between the
individuals and three separate sessions differed not more
than one dilution step; i.e., a threshold value of 0.5 mmol/l
for caffeine represents a range from 0.25 to 1.0 mmol/l.
Preparation of coffee beverage
After grinding the coffee beans by means of an ultra cen-
trifuge mill (Retsch, Haan, Germany) equipped with a sieve
(2-mm pore diameter), aliquots (54 g) of coffee powder
placed in a coffee ﬁlter (No. 4, Melitta, Germany) were
percolated with hot water (1.1 l, 80 ◦C). The resulting cof-
fee beverage (1 l) was rapidly cooled to room temperature
and then used for sensory and chemical analyses.
High vacuum distillation
An aliquot (100 ml) of the coffee beverage was separated
into a non-volatile and a volatile fraction at 20 ◦C by means
of the SAFE (solvent assisted ﬂavour evaporation) appara-
tus [17]. The aqueous distillate and the non-volatile residue,
taken up in tap water (100 ml, pH 5.0), were subsequently
used for sensory analysis.
Sequential solvent extraction of coffee beverage (CB)
The coffee beverage (250 ml) was sequentially extracted
with pentane (3×100 ml), ethyl acetate (6×100 ml), fol-
lowed by chloroform (3×100 ml). The organic layers of
each extraction step were freed from solvent in vacuum
to yield the pentane extractables (CB-fraction I), the ethyl
acetate extractables (CB-fraction II), and the chloroform
extractables (CB-fraction III). The CB-fractions I–III as
well as the remaining aqueous coffee fraction (CB-fraction
IV) were freed from trace amounts of solvents by high vac-
uum sublimation and a triple freeze-drying procedure, their
yields were determined by weight and their taste proﬁles
were evaluated in aqueous solution (250 ml; pH 5.0) as
given in Table 1.
Multiple-step ultraﬁltration
The CB-fraction II isolated from coffee beverage (400 ml)
was redissolved in water (400 ml; pH 5.0), and then frac-
tionated by means of an ultraﬁltration cell (Amicon, Witten,
Germany) using a YM 1 membrane (Millipore, Bedford,
MA) at a nitrogen pressure of 3 bar. After freeze-drying,
the yields of the low-molecular weight fraction (LMW,
MW<1 kDa) as well as the high-molecular weight fraction
(HMW, MW>1 kDa) were determined by weight (Table
1), the residues were taken-up in water (400 ml; pH 5.0),
and used for sensory analysis.

and aqueous ammonium formate (10 mmol; pH 3.5), and

Model-roasting experiments
5-O-Caffeoyl- (500 mg), 5-O-feruloyl- (500 mg), 3,4-, 3,5-,
or 4,5-O-dicaffeoyl-quinic acid (250 mg each), respec-
tively, were mixed with water (3 ml), then dried in an open
vial at 70 ◦C for 30 min, and, ﬁnally, roasted for 18 min
at 220 ◦C for 5-O-caffeoyl- and 5-O-feruoyl-quinic acid
or for 25 min at 175 ◦C for the dicaffeoyl-quinic acids.
The mixtures were taken up in hot water (100 ml, 80 ◦C),
cooled to room temperature, and extracted with ethyl ac-
etate (5×25 ml). The organic layer of each mixture was
freed from solvent to afford the extractable roast products
of 5-O-caffeoylquinic acid (115 mg), 5-O-feruloylquinic
acid (100 mg), 3,4- (55 mg), 3,5- (50 mg), or 4,5-O-
dicaffeoylquinic acid (48 mg), respectively. The residues,
taken up in a mixture (7/3, v/v; 10 ml) of methanol and
aqueous ammonium formate (10 mmol; pH 3.5), were an-
alyzed by HPLC.

Synthesis of 5-O-feruloylquinic acid (1b)
5-O-Feruloylquinic acid was prepared following a synthetic
strategy reported earlier [18] with some modiﬁcations as
outlined in Fig. 4.
O-Acetylferulic acid. Acetic anhydride (50 mmol) was
dropped to a solution of ferulic acid (40 mmol) and DMAP
(1 mmol) in pyridine (20 ml) at 0 ◦C. After stirring for
1 h, the mixture was poured onto ice, the aqueous phase
was acidiﬁed with hydrochloric acid (2 mol/l) to pH 2 and
then extracted with ethyl acetate (3×150 ml). The com-
bined organic layers were dried over Na2SO4, and, after
ﬁltration and removing the solvent in vacuum, the residue
was treated with light petroleum/ethyl acetate (97/3; v/v;
20 ml) thus yielding the title compound as a white pow-
der (35 mmol, yield 89%). LC/MS (ESI+): 237.2 (30,
[M+H]+), 177.0 (90); 1H NMR (400 MHz; CDCl3; ar-

(H–C(4′)).

Results and discussion
Comparative sensory analysis of a coffee brew prepared
from ground raw and ground roasted coffee beans, respec-
tively, impressively demonstrated that the taste proﬁle of
the aqueous percolate prepared from non-roasted coffee is
representing astringency and sourness and is lacking any
bitterness, whereas the beverage prepared from the roasted
coffee imparted the typical complex and attractive taste
proﬁle exhibiting pronounced bitterness (data not shown).
Aimed at characterizing these thermally generated bitter
taste drivers on a molecular level, a trained sensory panel
was asked to rate the intensity of the bitter taste quality of
the coffee on a scale from 0 (not detectable) to 5 (intensely
detectable). To achieve this, the bitter taste intensity of a
25 mmol/l caffeine solution was set to the maximum inten-
sity score of 5.0. In relation to the bitter taste intensity of
that reference solution, the bitterness of the coffee beverage
was judged with a high score of 4.0.
To gain ﬁrst insight into the volatility as well as the hy-
drophobicity of the compounds imparting the bitter taste,
the coffee beverage (CB) was fractionated by high-vacuum
distillation at 20 ◦C using the SAFE apparatus [17] into a
volatile and a non-volatile fraction, as well as by sequen-
tial solvent extraction affording the pentane soluble CB-
fraction I, the ethyl acetate soluble CB-fraction II, the chlo-
roform soluble CB-fraction III, and the remaining aqueous
phase (CB-fraction IV). The non-volatile CB-fraction and,
after removing the solvents in vacuum, the CB fractions
I-IV were dissolved in water (pH 5.0) in their “natural”
concentrations, and, together with the aqueous volatile CB-
fraction, were then judged for bitter taste intensity on the
5-point sensory scale. As given in Table 1, the bitterness
of the coffee beverage was exclusively located in the non-
volatile fraction evaluated with a bitter intensity of 4.0.
After sequential solvent extraction of the beverage, by far
the highest bitter intensity was detected in CB-fraction II
judged with a bitter score of 3.5, followed by CB-fraction
III, which was just evaluated with a very low bitter intensity
of below 0.5.
In order to gain ﬁrst insight into the molecular weight
of these bitter compounds, CB-fraction II was further
separated by means of ultraﬁltration using a membrane
with a cut-off of 1 kDa. Both fractions, the high-molecular
weight (HMW; >1 kDa) as well as the low-molecular
weight fraction (LMW; <1 kDa) were freeze-dried and
re-dissolved in their “natural” concentrations. Sensory
analysis demonstrated that the bitter taste perceived with
an intensity of 3.5 was just detectable in the LMW fraction,
whereas the HMW fraction did not show signiﬁcant bitter

504
taste (Table 1). All these data clearly indicate that the bitter
taste of the coffee beverage is caused by low-molecular
weight, hydrophobic, non-volatile compounds. As the
CB-fraction II consisted nearly exclusively of LMW
compounds (Table 1), the following taste dilution analysis
was focused on that fraction aimed at sorting out the
strongly bitter-tasting coffee compounds from the bulk of
less taste-active or tasteless substances.
Activity-guided screening for bitter compounds
To rate the bitter compounds in their relative taste impact
by means of the taste dilution analysis [12], an aliquot of
the CB-fraction II was separated by RP-HPLC (Fig. 1A),
and the efﬂuent was separated into 15 fractions which were
freeze-dried and made up with water (pH 5.0) to the same
volume. Each fraction was stepwise diluted one-to-one with
water (pH 5.0) and presented in order of increasing con-
centrations to the trained sensory panel. The panelists were
asked to evaluate the taste quality and to determine the de-
tection threshold in a triangle test. As this so-called taste
dilution (TD)-factor is related to its taste activity in water
[12], the 15 HPLC fractions were rated according to their
relative taste impact (Fig. 1B). As reﬂected by their high
TD-factors of 256, fractions no. 5, 8, and 15 were evalu-
ated with the highest bitter taste impact (Fig. 1B), closely
followed by fraction no. 12 or 4, 6, and 13 judged with a
somewhat lower TD factor of 128 or 64, respectively. All
the other fractions were evaluated with signiﬁcantly lower
taste impacts. Therefore, the following identiﬁcation ex-
periments were focused on the bitter compounds in HPLC
fractions no. 4–6, 8, 12, 13, and 15.
Identiﬁcation of bitter compounds in fractions no. 4–8
To gain ﬁrst insight into the structures of the compounds
imparting the intense bitter taste sensation of HPLC no.
fractions 4–8, these compounds were isolated from CB-
fraction II in a preparative scale by means of polyamide
column chromatography. After application of the crude
fraction and rinsing the column with water, a bitter-tasting
fraction was eluted with methanol. This bitter isolate was
then analysed by RP-HPLC and compared to the chro-
matogram obtained by HPLC-TDA. The chromatogram
of the isolate showed the peaks no. 1–5 (Fig. 2A) cor-
responding to the bitter compounds in the fractions no. 4–8
detected by means of HPLC-TDA (Fig. 1). The UV–vis
spectra of these compounds revealed the absorption max-
ima at 235, 300, 324 nm as expected for caffeoylquinic
acid derivatives. In addition, LC-MS analysis revealed
an intense [M+1]+ ion with m/z 337 for each of these
compounds, thus indicating caffeoylquinic acid derivatives
lacking one molecule of water. Preparative HPLC revealed
suitable amounts of peaks no. 3 and 5 (Fig. 2A) for1H-
and homonuclear 2D-NMR spectroscopy (COSY). Com-
parison of the NMR data obtained for these isolates with
those reported earlier in the literature [21] led to the un-
equivocal identiﬁcation of the bitter tastants in peaks no. 2
and 5 (Fig. 2A) or HPLC/TDA fractions 5 and 8 (Fig. 1)
as 3-O-caffeoyl- and 4-O-caffeoyl-γ-quinide (2a and 3a,
Fig. 3).
As the amounts and purity of the bitter tastants isolated
from peaks no. 1, 3, and 4 (Fig. 2A) were too low to en-
able a structure identiﬁcation, the following experiments
aimed producing higher amounts upon thermal treatment
of 5-O-caffeoylquinic acid. To achieve this, 5-chlorogenic
acid was dry-heated for 18 min at 220 ◦C and, after cool-
ing, the brown material was dissolved in water. This solu-
tion, exhibiting an intense bitter taste, was extracted with
ethyl acetate, the organic extract was separated by means
of polyamide chromatography as detailed above, and the
methanolic isolate was then analysed by RP-HPLC. Com-
parison of the compounds obtained from the roasted 5-
caffeoylquinic acid (Fig. 2B) with the chromatogram of the
coffee isolate (Fig. 2A) by means of HPLC-DAD, HPLC-
MS/MS, and sensory analysis demonstrated the identity of
the taste compounds in both extracts, just differing signif-
icantly in the concentration ratios of the individual com-
pounds. Therefore, it was concluded that all the bitter tas-
tants in HPLC fraction no. 4–8 are roast products of O-
caffeoylquinic acids.
1H- and COSY NMR spectra of the caffeoyl quinide iso-
lated from peak 3 (Fig. 2B) revealed a strong coupling be-
tween the methine protons H–C(3) and H–C(5) and the pro-
ton resonating at 5.07 ppm. This proton could be assigned
as H–C(4) since only this proton is able show homonuclear
coupling with H–C(3) and H–C(5), but not to H–C(2) and
H–C(6), respectively. The proton at H–C(3) showed cou-
pling to the protons H–C(2) and H–C(4), whereas H–C(5)
is connected to the methylene group H–C(6) and the me-
thine proton at H–C(4). Comparison to the methine protons
in α-position of the lactone respectively to the OH-group,
the ester group at C(4) led to higher downﬁeld shift. In con-
sequence, carbon atom C(4) has to be linked to the caffeic
acid moiety. The small coupling constants of the methine
protons H–C(3), H–C(4), and H–C(5) indicated that all pro-
tons were in an equatorial position. Based on these data,
the bitter compound in peak no. 3 (Fig. 2) and HPLC/TDA
fraction 6 (Fig. 1) could be identiﬁed as the previously not
reported 4-O-caffeoyl-muco-γ-quinide (5a, Fig. 3).
The 1H NMR spectrum of peak no. 1 isolated from the
roasted 5-caffeoyl-quinic acid revealed coupling patterns
of the methine protons, which were identical with those
observed for the 4-O-caffeoyl-muco-γ-quinide (5a). The
major difference to 5a was the order of the chemical
shift detected for the methine protons H–C(3), H–C(4)
and H–C(5); whereas H–C(4) of 5a showed the highest
downﬁeld shift due to its linkage to the caffeic acid, and the
methine proton at H–C(5) revealed the smallest downﬁeld
shift of the three methine protons in the carbocyclus,
the NMR spectrum of the compound isolated from peak
no. 1 showed the proton signal of H–C(5) with coupling
constants of 5.3 and 1.3 Hz at 5.13 ppm, whereas the proton
H–C(4) showed resonance at 3.95 ppm. As the proton
connected to C(3) showed no signiﬁcant shifts in both
molecules, the linkage of the caffeic acid to the hydroxyl
group at C(5) of the quinide was proposed for the structure

505
of that molecule. Taking all these data into account, the
bitter tastant in peak no. 1 (Fig. 2) and HPLC/TDA fraction
4 (Fig. 1) could be identiﬁed as the previously not reported
5-O-caffeoyl-muco-γ-quinide (6a; Fig. 3). In addition,
the spectroscopic analysis of the compound isolated from
peak 4 (Fig. 2) and HPLC/TDA fraction 7 (Fig. 1) led
to its identiﬁcation as the 5-O-caffeoyl-epi-δ-quinide
(4a; Fig. 3). Although a 5-O-caffeoyl-quinide has been
already reported in the literature [9], the stereochemistry
of the 5-O-caffeoyl-epi-δ-quinide identiﬁed in the present
publication is different from that described earlier. In
summary, the bitter taste compounds identiﬁed by means of
HPLC/TDA in fractions no. 4–8 (Fig. 1) are 5-O-caffeoyl-
muco-γ-quinide (6a) in fraction 4, 3-O-caffeoyl-γ-quinide
(2a) in fraction 5, 4-O-caffeoyl-muco-γ-quinide (5a) in
fraction 6, 5-O-caffeoyl-epi-δ-quinide (4a) in fraction 7
and 4-O-caffeoyl-γ-quinide (3a) in fraction 8.
Taking all these ﬁndings into account, it can be con-
cluded that upon coffee roasting O-caffeoylquinic acids
are converted into the ﬁve mono lactones following the
chemical pathways outlined in Fig. 3. As reported recently
[22], 5-O-caffeoylquinic acid (1a) is converted upon trans-
esteriﬁcation of the caffeoyl moiety into the isomers 3-
O-caffeoyl- (Ia) and 4-O-caffeoyl-quinic acid (IIa). The
axial orientation of the hydroxyl group at C(5) of these iso-
mers facilitates their rapid lactonization, giving rise to 3-O-
caffeoyl- (2a) and 4-O-caffeoyl-γ-quinide (3a). Epimeriza-
tion of the hydroxyl group at C(4) and C(3), respectively, in-
duces the conversion of 1a to 5-O-caffeoyl-epi-quinic acid
(IIIa) and 5-O-caffeoyl-muco-quinic acid (Va), thus giving
rise to 5-O-caffeoyl-epi-δ-quinide (4a) and 5-O-caffeoyl-
muco-γ-quinide (6a) upon lactonisation (Fig. 3). Finally,
transesteriﬁcation of the caffeoyl moiety from HO-C(5)
to HO-C(4) of 5-O-caffeoyl-muco-quinic acid (Va) leads
to the formation of 4-O-caffeoyl-muco-quinic acid (IVa)
and 4-O-caffeoyl-muco-γ-quinide (5a). Being well in line
with the presence of 3-O-caffeoyl- and 4-O-caffeoyl-quinic
acid in coffee beans and their simple lactonization, the cor-
responding 3-O-caffeoyl- and 4-O-caffeoyl-γ-quinide are
predominating in roast coffee beverage when compared to
the other isomers (Fig. 2A). Vice versa, the lower amounts
of the other quinides in roasted coffee can be simply ex-
plained by the fact that the 5-O-caffeoyl quinic acid ﬁrst
needs to be epimerized at C(3) or C(4) prior to lactonisa-
tion.
Identiﬁcation of bitter compounds in fraction no.
12 and 13
Application of UV-vis and LC-MS spectroscopy on the
taste compounds detected in HPLC fraction no. 12 and 13
(Fig. 1) revealed the typical absorption spectra expected for
ferulic acid derivatives and an [M+H]+ ion with m/z 351,
thus indicating the presence of feruoyl quinides. Unfortu-
nately, none of the two taste compounds could be isolated
in suitable purity allowing an unequivocal structure deter-
mination by NMR spectroscopy. Therefore, the following
experiments were aimed at generating these lactones upon
roasting of 5-O-feruoylquinic acid similar as successfully
done for the caffeoyl quinides.
As 5-O-feruoylquinic acid is not commercially avail-
able, this putative taste precursor needed to be synthesised.
Following the reaction sequence outlined in Fig. 4, 5-O-
feruoylquinic acid was successfully synthesised as reported
earlier for 5-caffeoylquinic acid [18] with some modiﬁca-
tions. After ﬁnal puriﬁcation, the 5-O-feruoylquinic acid
was roasted for 18 min at 220 ◦C, and, after cooling, the
brown colored material was dissolved in water. This bitter-
tasting solution was then extracted with ethylacetate and
the extract obtained was separated by polyamide column
chromatography using water, followed by methanol as the
efﬂuent. The methanolic fraction was then analysed by
HPLC-DAD and LC-MS. The HPLC analysis revealed a
similar pattern of ﬁve peaks (data not shown) compared
to those observed for the roasted 5-O-caffeoyl-quinic acid
(Fig. 2B), and the LC-MS analysis revealed a pseudomolec-
ular ion with m/z 351 for each of these ﬁve compounds. For
their structure determination, these peaks no. 1–5 were iso-
lated by means of semi-preparative RP-HPLC. Four of the
ﬁve compounds, namely no. 1 and 3–5, were analyzed by
1H NMR and COSY spectroscopy. With the exception of
an additional methyl group, the spectroscopic data of these
compounds were nearly identical to those obtained for the
caffeoyl-quinides and allowed the identiﬁcation of peak no.
1 as 5-O-feruloyl-muco-γ-quinide (6b; Fig. 3), peak no. 3
as 4-O-feruloyl-muco-γ-quinide (5b; Fig. 3), peak no. 4
as 5-O-feruloyl-epi-δ-quinide (4b; Fig. 3), and peak 5 as
4-O-feruloyl-γ-quinide (3b; Fig. 3). In contrast to the 4-
O-feruloyl-γ-quinide, which has been proposed earlier in
the literature [9], the structures of 4-O-feruloyl-muco-γ-
quinide, 5-O-feruloyl-muco-γ-quinide, and 5-O-feruloyl-
epi-δ-quinide have previously not been identiﬁed. Due to
the low amounts obtained for the taste compound eluting
as peak 2 and the impurity of the isolate, a structure assign-
ment of that compound was not possible. On comparison
of the ﬁve 5-O-caffeoyl-quinides (2a–6a) with the four 5-
O-feruoyl-quinides (3b–6b), the 3-feruloyl-γ-quinide was
suggested as unknown compound in peak 2.
In order to check that assumption, the 3-feruloyl-γ-
quinide (2b) was prepared by independent synthesis ac-
cording the literature [19, 20] with some modiﬁcations as
outlined in Fig. 5. The spectroscopic data of the synthetic
products were well in line with those published earlier for
that lactone [23]. Comparison of MS data, UV-vis data as
well as retention time of that compound with those isolated
from peak no. 2 of the roasted 5-O-feruoyl-quinic acid mix-
ture unequivocally conﬁrmed the structure of the bitter taste
compound as the 3-feruloyl-γ-quinide (2b; Fig. 5).
In order to investigate which 5-O-feruoyl-quinide
isomers are present in the bitter fractions no.12 and 13
(Fig. 1) located by HPLC/TDA, these fractions were
screened for these lactones by means of LC-MS/MS
(ESI−) operating in the multiple reaction monitoring
(MRM) mode. To achieve this, the HPLC efﬂuent was
monitored for compounds showing the characteristic
transition reactions m/z 349→193 and 349→175, which
were selected in tuning runs using the individual reference

506
compounds. In contrast to the presence of the ﬁve caffeoyl
quinide isomers detected in coffee, only the 3-O-feruloyl-
γ-quinide (2b) eluting in HPLC fraction no. 12 (Fig. 1) and
the 4-O-feruloyl-γ-quinide (3b) eluting in HPLC fraction
no. 13 (Fig. 1) could be unequivocally identiﬁed in the
roast coffee beverage by comparison of the retention times
(RP-HPLC) and the LC-MS/MS mass transitions.
Characterization of the bitter-tasting fraction no. 15
Besides the HPLC fractions no. 4–8, 12, and 13, the HPLC-
TDA of CB-fraction II revealed also a high TD factor of
256 for the complex fraction no. 15 (Fig. 1). To gain ﬁrst
insights into the chemicals eluting in that fraction, the CB-
fraction II was separated in a preparative scale by means
of RP-18 column chromatography using methanol/water
mixtures as the efﬂuent. The fractions were analyzed by
HPLC and those containing compounds eluting in fraction
15 were combined. But independent from the stationary
phase or the solvent system used, all attempts to further
resolve the complexity of compounds in fraction no. 15
and to isolate individual compounds in sufﬁcient purity
remained unsuccessful.
Therefore, we analyzed the isolated fraction no. 15 in
toto without further separation by means of UV–vis as well
as COSY-NMR spectroscopy. The COSY spectrum of this
fraction (data not shown) exhibited the typical resonance
signals as detected before for the six-membered carbo-
cyclic ring of quinic acid derivatives with strong coupling
constants between the protons H–C(3) or H–C(5) and the
methylene groups H–C(2) or H–C(6), respectively, whereas
H–C(4) did show coupling only with H–C(3) or H–C(5).
Each of the methine protons H-(3), H–C(4) and H–C(5)
revealed a chemical shift above 5 ppm, thus indicating a
lactonization of one hydroxyl group and an esteriﬁcation
of the two other hydroxyl groups. An excerpt of the NMR
spectrum between 6.2 and 7.8 ppm displayed the typical
signal pattern observed for hydroxycinnamoyl moieties.
These data are well in the line with the UV–vis spectra
showing the typical absorption spectra expected for hy-
droxycinnamic acid derivatives. This led us to the assump-
tion that the observed complexity of the fraction no. 15 is
due to a multiplicity of quinic acid lactone isomers ester-
iﬁed with a minimum of two hydroxycinnamic acids such
as caffeic acid or ferulic acid, or with additional molecules
of quinic acid.
To further conﬁrm our hypothesis, the following exper-
iments aimed at carefully hydrolyzing the ester as well as
the lactone bonds in the complex tastants in fraction no. 15
and to study the hydrolysis products liberated. To achieve
this, aqueous solutions of fraction 15 were adjusted with
NaOH either to pH 10 and then stirred for 60 min at 20 ◦C,
or to pH 13 and then stirred for 60 min at 60 ◦C. Af-
ter acidiﬁcation to pH 5.0, the hydrolysates were analysed
by means of HPLC. As displayed in Fig. 6, HPLC anal-
ysis of the non-treated fraction no. 15 (control) showed
a complex bulk of hardly resolved compounds (Fig. 6A),
whereas the sample hydrolyzed under mild alkaline con-
ditions at pH 10 revealed a couple of well separated com-
pounds (Fig. 6B). Comparison of the retention times, UV–
vis, and LC-MS data with those of reference compounds
led to their unequivocal identiﬁcation as 3-O-caffeoyl-, 4-
O-caffeoyl-, and 5-O-caffeoylquinic acid, p-coumaric acid,
ferulic acid, 5-O-feruoyl-quinic acid as well as 3,4-, 3,5-
and 4,5-O-dicaffeoyl-quinic acids. In contrast, only four
compounds were detected by HPLC-DAD after incubating
fraction no. 15 at pH 13 at elevated temperature (Fig. 6C).
Analysis of these compounds by means of HPLC-DAD and
HPLC-MS and comparison of the data obtained with those
measured for reference compounds led to the unequivo-
cal identiﬁcation of these peaks as caffeic acid (peak 1),
p-coumaric acid (peak 2), ferulic acid (peak 3), and 3,4-
dimethoxycinnamic acid (peak 4) (Fig. 6C). In addition to
these cinnamic acids, quinic acid was detected as the major
non-phenolic organic acid in the alkaline hydrolysates by
means of high performance ion chromatography (HPIC)
with conductivity detection. A comparison of the data ob-
tained for both hydrolysis experiments clearly indicated
that under mild alkaline conditions (pH 10, 20 ◦C) only
the lactones seem to be hydrolysed since the concentration
of free cinnamic acid derivatives, which still contain the
intact ester bond, is rather low compared to the various cin-
namoyl quinic acids. More severe alkaline treatment (pH
13, 60 ◦C) then also induced the hydrolysis of the ester link-
ages, thus resulting in quinic acid and the free cinnamic
acids. Considering the data of the COSY experiment as
well as the results of the alkaline hydrolysis, it might be
concluded that the bitter-tasting fraction no. 15 is contain-
ing a multiplicity of quinic acid lactone isomers multiply
esteriﬁed with p-coumaric acid, caffeic acid, ferulic acid,
3,4-dimethoxycinnamic acid, and quinic acid.
In particular, the high amounts of caffeic acid, caf-
feoylquinic acids, and dicaffeoylquinic acids released
during alkaline hydrolysis indicated that di-caffeoylquinic
acid lactones might be involved in the formation of
bitter compounds eluting in HPLC fraction 15. In order
to further strengthen this assumption, we ﬁrst isolated
the individual 3,4-O-dicaffeoyl-, 3,5-O-dicaffeoyl-, and
4,5-O-dicaffeoyl-quinic acids from raw coffee beans.
NMR spectroscopic data of the isolates are well in line
with those of earlier reports [24], thus conﬁrming the
chemical structure of these dicaffeoylquinic acid isomers.
The individual isolated dicaffeoylquinic acids were then
separately roasted in a lab oven, the roasting products were
dissolved in water, and then sensorially evaluated. The
trained sensory panel detected an intense coffee-like bitter
taste indicating that also the dicaffeoylquinic acids indeed
are potential bitter taste precursors in coffee. HPLC/DAD
analysis and HPLC-degustation of the extracts prepared
from the three thermally treated dicaffeoyl-quinic acid
isomers (Fig. 7A–C) clearly indicated that a trans-
formation of the individual isomers (peaks 1, 2) has
taken place and additional roast compounds (peaks 3–5)
were generated. For example, the chromatogram of the
roasted 3,4-O-dicaffeoyl-quinic acid showed besides the
starting compound also the co-eluting isomers 3,5-O-
dicaffeoylquinic acid (peak 1) and 4,5-O-dicaffeoylquinic

507
acid (peak 2) formed upon transesterifaction as well as four
additional compounds (Fig. 7A) amongst which peaks 3–5
exhibited bitter taste. Interestingly, also roasting the 3,5-
O-dicaffeoyl- as well as the 4,5-O-dicaffeoyl-quinic acid
induced a transesteriﬁcation of the corresponding isomers
as well as the formation of the same bitter compounds,
just differing considerably in their yields (Fig. 7B and C).
By means of an RP-18 clean-up, the roast products elut-
ing as peaks 3–5 have been isolated and then analyzed by
LC-MS. The MS analysis revealed a quasimolecular ion
at m/z 499 for each of the isolated compounds, thus indi-
cating that a lactonisation of the quinic acid moiety in the
dicaffeoyl-quinic acids has been occurred during roasting.
To conﬁrm this assumption, the three isolated compounds
were analyzed by means of1H- and COSY-NMR experi-
ments. For all three compounds a double signal set in the
range between 6.16 and 7.67 ppm demonstrated the pres-
ence of two caffeic acid residues, in each molecule. The
major product (Fig. 7A; peak no. 3) formed upon roast-
ing of 3,4-dicaffeoyl-quinic acid showed nearly identical
coupling patterns of the methine protons H–C(3), H–C(4),
and H–C(5) as already observed for the 3-O-caffeoyl- and
4-O-caffeoyl-quinides. Based on a careful consideration
of all the NMR data and coupling constants and compar-
ison with those reported earlier in the literature [19], the
roast compound no. 3 could be identiﬁed as the 3,4-O-
dicaffeoyl-quinide (10; Fig. 8). Although this compound
has been reported earlier as an coffee constituent, which
was able to inhibit the human adenosine transporter [19,
22], its sensory activity as a bitter-tasting molecule as well
as its formation have been as yet not been demonstrated.
The remaining bitter-tasting roast products no. 4 and
5 were successfully isolated from roasted 3,5-O-caffeoyl-
and 4,5-O-dicaffeoyl-quinic acid (Fig. 7B and C). The 1H
NMR spectrum of the peak 5 (Fig. 7A–C) showed, in com-
parison to the NMR data of the 5-caffeoyl-epi-δ-quinide,
only a signiﬁcant deep ﬁeld shift of the proton H–C(3).
This effect could be explained by the esteriﬁcation of the
hydroxy group in position 3 with another molecule caf-
feic acid. The protons of the positions H–C(4) and H–C(5)
remained nearly unaffected. Taking all spectroscopic data
into consideration, peak 5 could be identiﬁed as the pre-
viously not reported 3,5-O-dicaffeoyl-epi-δ-quinide (11;
Fig. 8). The1H coupling pattern of the methine protons
H–C(3), H–C(4) and H–C(5) of peak 4 (Fig. 7A–C) was
identical to the data obtained for the 4-O-caffeoyl- and 5-
O-caffeoyl-muco-γ-quinide. Carefully considering all the
chemical shifts as well as the coupling constants observed
by 1H NMR and the COSY spectroscopy, the compound
eluting in peak no. 4 (Fig. 7) could be identiﬁed as the
4,5-O-dicaffeoyl-muco-γ-quinide (12; Fig. 8). To the best
of our knowledge, the bitter quinides 11 and 12 have not
been reported previously.
In order to conﬁrm the presence of these three dicaffeoyl-
quinides as constituents of HPLC fraction no. 15, this
fraction was screened for these lactones by means of LC-
MS/MS (ESI−) operating in the multiple reaction moni-
toring (MRM) mode. Monitoring the HPLC efﬂuent for
compounds showing the characteristic transition reactions
m/z 497→335 and 497→161, which were selected in tun-
ing runs with the individual lactones prior to analysis, the
three dicaffeoyl quinides 10–12 could be unequivocally
identiﬁed as constituent in HPLC fraction no. 15 isolated
from the coffee beverage. To the best of our knowledge
these compounds have not yet reported as contributors to
the bitter taste of roasted coffee.
Based on the observations made in our roasting experi-
ments, the individual dicaffeoyl-quinides might be formed
upon coffee roasting as outlined in Fig. 8. Upon ther-
mal treatment, the individual dicaffeoyl-quinic acids are
transformed into each other upon thermal transesteriﬁca-
tion. Lactonization of the isomer 7 upon cleavage of one
molecule of water gives rise to 3,4-O-dicaffeoyl-γ-quinide
(10). Epimerization of the isomeric 3,5-O-dicaffeoyl- (8)
and 4,5-O-dicaffeoyl-quinic acid (9) at carbon atom C(4)
and C(3), respectively, is then facilitating the forma-
tion of the 3,5-O-dicaffeoyl-epi-δ-quinide (11) and 4,5-O-
di-caffeoyl-muco-γ-quinide (12) upon lactonization. Sup-
ported by the results of LC-MS/MS studies on additional
thermal model reactions with 5-caffeoylquinic acids and
lactones (data not shown), alternatively, intermolecular
transesteriﬁcation of a caffeoyl moiety was observed to
convert 3-O-caffeoyl- (2a) and 4-O-caffeoyl-γ-quinide (3a)
into 3,4-O-dicaffeoyl-γ-quinide (10), 5-O-caffeoyl-epi-δ-
quinide (4a) into 3,5-O-di-caffeoyl-epi-δ-quinide (11), and
4-O-caffeoyl- (5a) as well as 5-O-caffeoyl-muco-γ-quinide
(6a) into 4,5-O-dicaffeoyl-muco-γ-quinide (12). Follow-
ing the same reaction principles, similar transesteriﬁca-
tion reactions seem also to occur with other acids than
caffeic acid such as, e.g. p-coumaric acid, ferulic acid,
3,4-dimethoxycinnamic acid, and quinic acid, thus reﬂect-
ing the complexity of non-resolved compounds in the key
bitter-tasting coffee fraction no. 15.
Bitter recognition threshold concentrations
Prior to sensory analysis, the purity of all compounds was
checked by HPLC-MS as well as 1H NMR spectroscopy.
To determine the human threshold concentrations for bitter
taste, aqueous solutions (pH 5.0) of the target compound
were evaluated by means of the triangle test [16] (Table 2).
All these lactones imparted a typical coffee-like bitter taste
quality with human threshold concentrations ranging from
29 to 180 µmol/l. The sensory panel described the unique
taste proﬁle of these quinides as being strongly different
from the taste quality of other known bitter reference com-
pounds such as caffeine or quinine. In addition, these com-

In summary, the data obtained demonstrate caffeoyl- and
feruloyl-quinides as well as more complex esters between
quinides and cinnamoyl derivatives as the most intense
bitter taste compounds in roasted coffee. Aimed at demon-
strating their contribution to the bitter taste of roast coffee
beverages, quantitative studies, followed by taste recon-
struction and omission experiments using these compounds
in their “natural” concentrations are currently in progress
and will be published elsewhere.
