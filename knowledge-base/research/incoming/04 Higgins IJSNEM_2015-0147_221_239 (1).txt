The Effects of Preexercise Caffeinated Coffee Ingestion 
on Endurance Performance: An Evidence-Based Review
Simon Higgins, Chad R. Straight, and Richard D. Lewis
Endurance athletes commonly ingest caffeine as a means to enhance training intensity and competitive 
performance. A widely-used source of caffeine is coffee, however conflicting evidence exists regarding the 
efficacy of coffee in improving endurance performance. In this context, the aims of this evidence-based review 
were threefold: 1) to evaluate the effects of preexercise coffee on endurance performance, 2) to evaluate the 
effects of coffee on perceived exertion during endurance performance, and 3) to translate the research into 
usable information for athletes to make an informed decision regarding the intake of caffeine via coffee as a 
potential ergogenic aid. Searches of three major databases were performed using terms caffeine and coffee, or 
coffee-caffeine, and endurance, or aerobic. Included studies (n = 9) evaluated the effects of caffeinated coffee 
on human subjects, provided the caffeine dose administered, administered caffeine ≥ 45 min before testing, 
and included a measure of endurance performance (e.g., time trial). Significant improvements in endurance 
performance were observed in five of nine studies, which were on average 24.2% over controls for time to 
exhaustion trials, and 3.1% for time to completion trials. Three of six studies found that coffee reduced per-
ceived exertion during performance measures significantly more than control conditions (p < .05). Based on 
the reviewed studies there is moderate evidence supporting the use of coffee as an ergogenic aid to improve 
performance in endurance cycling and running. Coffee providing 3–8.1 mg/kg (1.36–3.68 mg/lb) of caffeine 
may be used as a safe alternative to anhydrous caffeine to improve endurance performance.
Keywords: coffee, caffeine, ergogenic aids
On a typical day around 89% of the US adult popula-
tion consumes caffeine (Fulgoni et al., 2015). Data from 
2001 to 2010 indicate that beverages provide 98% of 
ingested caffeine, with coffee contributing the highest 
proportion (64%) (Fulgoni et al., 2015). Caffeine and 
coffee are commonly ingested by athletes as part of 
their habitual diet, but also in a supplementary capacity 
to enhance performance. One report shows that up to 
~74% of athletes had detectable levels of caffeine in their 
body during competition (Del Coso et al., 2011). Average 
urinary caffeine concentration was similar across sexes 
but varied with age, with older competitors (> 30 years) 
having higher levels than younger (< 20 years), and was 
higher in athletes from endurance sports (Del Coso et 
al., 2011). A wealth of research has reviewed caffeine’s 
potential to enhance exercise performance (Davis & 
Green, 2009; Dodd et al., 1993; Doherty & Smith, 2004, 
2005; Ganio et al., 2009; Goldstein et al., 2010; Graham, 
2001; Sokmen et al., 2008; Spriet, 1995; Tarnopolsky, 
1994). The majority of the caffeine and performance 
literature focuses on anhydrous (pure) caffeine with a 
paucity of research examining other ingestion methods 
such as caffeine in coffee. While the effects of caffeine 
on other modes of exercise (e.g., resistance training or 
anaerobic work) have been examined (Astorino & Rob-
erson, 2010; Da Silva et al., 2015), no known studies 
have examined the effects of coffee ingestion on these 
other modes of exercise, thus our review was limited to 
endurance exercise. Within the existing coffee literature 
no extensive reviews have been performed, thus, the focus 
of this review is to examine the ergogenic potential of 
preexercise coffee ingestion on endurance performance 
and perceived exertion. In the context of this review, 
coffee will refer to caffeinated coffee or decaffeinated 
coffee with added anhydrous caffeine. Although coffee 
is the main focus, a brief discussion of other ingestion 
modes is warranted.

Researchers have examined the role of varying doses of 
caffeine on performance in a multitude of sports. Preexer-
cise caffeine doses as low as 1 mg/kg (Jenkins et al., 2008)

up to 9 mg/kg (Anderson et al., 2000; Cohen et al., 1996) 
were reviewed with the conclusion that the ingestion of 
6 mg/kg or less of caffeine no more than 60 min before 
exercise improves endurance performance (Ganio et al., 
2009). Within reviewed studies, results were equivocal as 
to whether there are further performance improvements 
with doses greater than 6 mg/kg. Larger doses have been 
examined, with Pasman and colleagues reporting similar 
performance improvements between doses of 5 mg/kg, 9 
mg/kg, and 13 mg/kg (Pasman et al., 1995). Thus, there is 
a considerable body of evidence suggesting the ergogenic 
utility of caffeine in sports and competition.
The most common caffeine source within the litera-
ture is anhydrous caffeine, in pill form or dissolved in 
water. Other researchers have examined administration 
of caffeine via alternative delivery techniques, such as 
chewing gum (Lane et al., 2014; McLellan et al., 2005), 
within carbohydrate-electrolyte sports drinks (Cureton et 
al., 2007; Miller et al., 2014), caffeinated carbohydrate 
gels (Scott et al., 2014), and coffee (Butts & Crowell, 
1985; Costill et al., 1978; Demura et al., 2007; Graham 
et al., 1998; Hodgson et al., 2013; Lamina & Musa, 2009; 
McLellan & Bell., 2004; Rodrigues et al., 1990; Trice 
& Haymes, 1995; Wiles et al., 1992). Due to a variety 
of considerations and potential constraints (e.g., sport, 
required dose, palatability, etc.) that will likely influence 
an athlete’s preferred method of ingestion, it is difficult to 
recommend one delivery technique over another. With the 
astronomic rise in the popularity of coffee in recent years, 
as evidenced by one major coffee retailer’s stock value 
increasing 138% since 2010 ($55.19 per stock vs. $23.20 
per stock) (NASDAQ, 2015), focusing on the utility of 
coffee as an ergogenic aid seems pertinent.
Mechanisms of Action
Exploring the specific mechanisms behind caffeine’s 
ergogenic effects is beyond the scope of this review; 
however a brief discussion is warranted. When spe-
cifically examining endurance performance, the main 
mechanism hypothesized to account for much of caf-
feine’s performance enhancing effects is its ability to 
regulate pain, fatigue, and feelings of vigor (Goldstein 
et al., 2010). This is thought to be achieved through the 
antagonistic effect it has on adenosine receptors A1 and/
or A2a (Fredholm et al., 1999; Lynge & Hellsten, 2000) 
within the brain, relieving symptoms of central fatigue 
(Davis et al., 2003). The blockade of adenosine through 
caffeine’s ability to easily cross the blood-brain barrier 
is augmented by its muscular metabolic effects, leading 
to improved excitation-contraction coupling of skeletal 
muscle, potentiation of muscle force, work, and power 
(Spriet, 1995; Tallis et al., 2015). Aside from caffeine’s 
effects on the central nervous system, other potential 
ergogenic mechanisms have been studied including: 
1) increased free fatty acid oxidation; 2) alteration of 
mechanisms within skeletal muscle related to the regula-
tion of carbohydrate breakdown (Manore et al., 2009); 
and 3) increased mobilization of intracellular calcium 
(Ganio et al., 2009).
Efficacy of Supplementation  
With Coffee
The prevalence of caffeine ingestion by athletes before 
and during competition is high, drawing caffeine from 
sources such as energy drinks, energy gels, tablets, car-
bonated sodas, and coffee (Desbrow & Leveritt, 2006). 
Of these caffeine sources, 37% of athletes reported intent 
to ingest preexercise coffee as their vehicle for caffeine 
ingestion, making it one of the largest contributors to 
prerace caffeine intake (Desbrow & Leveritt, 2006). 
While coffee is consumed by a large percentage of 
athletes, the efficacy for improving endurance perfor-
mance remains unclear and the literature has not been 
thoroughly reviewed. Some research suggests that other 
compounds, such as dicinnamoylquinides, are produced 
during the roasting process, potentially altering the effects 
of caffeine as an adenosine antagonist, and consequently 
mitigating its benefit as an ergogenic aid (de Paulis et 
al., 2002). However, others failed to find a detrimental 
effect of coffee ingestion on the stimulatory benefits of 
caffeine when caffeine was ingested within 30 min of 
a coffee bolus (McLellan & Bell, 2004). A multitude 
of noncaffeine compounds have been identified within 
coffee, and of these compounds, chlorogenic acids have 
the greatest potential to alter caffeine’s efficacy (de Paulis 
et al., 2002). These antioxidants are esters linking caffeic 
or ferulic acids to quinic acids, which appear in plasma 
in varying concentrations following the consumption of 
coffee (Williamson et al., 2011). Specific chlorogenic 
acid metabolites reach their maximum plasma concen-
tration at different times, with ferulic acid metabolites 
appearing at 1 hr postingestion and other chlorogenic acid 
metabolites appearing later and persisting for more than 
6 hr (Lang et al., 2013). The differing plasma appearance 
rates highlight the complicated nature of the crossover 
between chlorogenic acid metabolites and caffeine, as 
caffeine also shows large interindividual variations in 
plasma appearance rates (Desbrow et al., 2009; Robert-
son et al., 1978). The chlorogenic acid concentration in 
coffee is affected by factors such as the roasting process, 
with highly roasted coffees having reduced concentra-
tions (Mills et al., 2013; Moon et al., 2009). Variations 
in the amounts and plasma appearance times for these 
metabolites complicates the comparison of studies and 
could in-part account for the varied effects of coffee on 
exercise performance.

reviews published in the area (Doherty & Smith, 2004, 
2005; Fredholm et al., 1999; Ganio et al., 2009; Goldstein 
et al., 2010; Ribeiro & Sebastiao, 2010; Spriet, 1995;

there is a paucity of data specifically focused on describ-

such as coffee. Therefore, the aims of this evidence-based 
review are threefold: 1) to evaluate the effects of coffee

Methodology
Searches of PubMed (PM), Web of Science (WoS), and 
SPORTDiscus (SD) electronic databases were performed 
using variations of the key words caffeine, and coffee, 
or coffee-caffeine, and endurance, or aerobic. These 
searches returned 165 (PM), 32 (SD), and 422 (WoS) 
results, totaling 619 articles, which were then screened 
(title and abstract) for relevance (Figure 1). Upon 
relevance screening, remaining articles were assessed 
according to the following inclusion criteria: 1) studies 
included a caffeinated coffee only condition; 2) provided 
the caffeine dose administered; 3) human subjects only; 
4) included an endurance performance measure (e.g., 
time-trial, time to exhaustion); 5) article was published 
in English.
Exclusion criteria consisted of: 1) articles only 
available as case studies, conference proceedings, or 
in abstract form; 2) studies that mixed caffeine intake 
with other substances such as carbohydrate, or other 
stimulants; 3) exercise testing performed  less than 45 
min postingestion; 4) participants with chronic disease; 
5) participants who are smokers.
An in-depth review of article reference lists was 
then performed to identify any overlooked articles. Five 
additional records were identified from other sources, 
such as the bibliographies of clinical trials (Demura et 
al., 2007; Hodgson et al., 2013), and a position stand 
on caffeine and endurance performance (Goldstein et 
al., 2010). After removal of duplicates and nonrelevant 
articles, and assessment via the aforementioned inclusion 
and exclusion criteria, nine studies were included within 
the review (Table 1).
Grading of Scientific Evidence
An evidence-based grading system (Table 2) was used 
to assess the complete weight of scientific evidence in 
each area. This table is similar to one used in a previous 
review (Cho et al., 2013) and by the American Diabetes 
Association (Association, 2014).
Results
A total of nine randomized controlled trials (RCT) (n 
= 135 subjects) were available for review (Table 1). No 
other study design was identified that fit the criteria, 
(e.g., longitudinal studies using coffee as a training aid) 
therefore this review is limited to the acute effects of 
coffee ingestion on endurance performance.
Doses of caffeine ranged from 3.0–8.1 mg/kg and 
this was either administered as regularly brewed coffee 
or as decaffeinated coffee with added anhydrous caffeine.

Coffee and Endurance Performance
All nine RCTs (n = 135) examined the effects of coffee 
on endurance performance (Table 1). Five out of nine 
studies (n = 58 of 135) showed an improvement in per-
formance over baseline testing or placebo when coffee 
was ingested (Costill et al., 1978; Hodgson et al., 2013; 
McLellan & Bell, 2004; Trice & Haymes, 1995; Wiles 
et al., 1992); however the remaining four failed to find 
any improvement (Butts & Crowell, 1985; Graham et al., 
1998; Lamina & Musa, 2009; Rodrigues et al., 1990). 
When the study contained both a caffeinated coffee and 
an anhydrous caffeine-only control (Graham et al., 1998; 
Hodgson et al., 2013; McLellan & Bell, 2004; three stud-
ies, n = 30), two (n = 21) found that coffee had similar 
ergogenic benefits to the ingestion of anhydrous caffeine 
on endurance performance (Hodgson et al., 2013; McLel-
lan & Bell, 2004). Finally, when comparing naturally 
caffeinated coffee and decaffeinated coffee with added 
anhydrous caffeine, four studies (n = 71) contained condi-
tions using naturally caffeinated coffee (Graham et al., 
1998; Hodgson et al., 2013; Lamina & Musa, 2009; Wiles 
et al., 1992), and six (n = 73) had conditions where anhy-
drous caffeine was added to decaffeinated coffee (Butts & 
Crowell, 1985; Costill et al., 1978; Graham et al., 1998; 
McLellan & Bell, 2004; Rodrigues et al., 1990; Trice & 
Haymes, 1995). Two of four studies (n = 36) containing 
naturally caffeinated coffee showed improvements in 
endurance performance relative to the control (Hodgson 
et al., 2013; Wiles et al., 1992). With regard to anhydrous 
caffeine added to decaffeinated coffee, three out of six 
(n = 30) studies reported improvements in endurance 
performance (Costill et al., 1978; McLellan & Bell, 2004; 
Trice & Haymes, 1995). Within studies that reported a 
significant difference in the change between groups, the 
mean improvement in performance among experimental 
conditions was 24.2% relative to placebo for time to 
exhaustion trials, and 3.1% for time to completion trials.

Coffee and Perceived Exertion
Six of nine studies (n = 82) examined the effects of coffee 
on perceived exertion during endurance performance 
(Butts & Crowell, 1985; Costill et al., 1978; McLellan 
& Bell, 2004; Rodrigues et al., 1990; Trice & Haymes, 
1995; Wiles et al., 1992). Of these six studies, one pla-
cebo-controlled (McLellan & Bell, 2004), and two non 
placebo-controlled studies (Costill et al., 1978; Rodrigues 
et al., 1990) found that subjects (n = 28) in a coffee 
group had a significantly lower RPE than the control 
condition. A fourth (n = 9) reported results approaching 
significance (p = .07) (Trice & Haymes, 1995). However, 
not all studies used the 6–20 Borg scale to rate exertion 
(Butts & Crowell, 1985; Costill et al., 1978; McLellan 
& Bell, 2004; Trice & Haymes, 1995), as two studies 
measured perceived exertion using a 1–10 Borg scale 
(Rodrigues et al., 1990; Wiles et al., 1992). In summary, 
a small number of RCTs (three of six), including only one 
with a placebo-control, with small sample sizes, found 
a significant improvement in RPE for coffee relative to 
a control group. Therefore, an evidence level of C was 
assigned to coffee’s effects on perceived exertion.
Discussion
The aims of this evidence-based review were: 1) to evalu-
ate the effects of coffee on endurance performance, 2) 
to evaluate the effects of coffee on perceived exertion 
during endurance performance, and 3) to translate the 
research into useable information for athletes to make 
an informed decision regarding the intake of coffee as 
an ergogenic aid. Based on the available literature, the 
strength of evidence was moderate when examining the 
effects of coffee on endurance performance, and weak 
when examining the effects on RPE, as methodological 
differences between trials were a limiting factor.
Coffee and Endurance Performance
Placebo-Controlled Studies.  Of the nine studies using 
coffee as a vehicle for caffeine ingestion, three used a true 
placebo measure (anhydrous caffeine only) to compare the 
individual effects of caffeine from coffee to anhydrously 
administered caffeine (Graham et al., 1998; Hodgson et 
al., 2013; McLellan & Bell, 2004). Two of these studies 
were in agreement, suggesting that ingesting 5 mg/kg 
of caffeine had similar ergogenic effects on exercise 
performance regardless of the vehicle used to administer 
it. The magnitude of these improvements varied due to 
the methods used to test endurance performance, with an 
energy-based time-trial yielding a 4.7% time reduction 
(Hodgson et al., 2013) and a vigorous cycle to exhaustion 
having a 28.1% time increase with coffee (McLellan & 
Bell, 2004). In contrast, the other placebo-controlled 
study reported that coffee ingestion had no effect on 
endurance performance, whereas the ingestion of an 
identical dose of anhydrous caffeine resulted in a 31% 
improvement (Graham et al., 1998).
The inconsistent findings of previous research sug-
gest that additional factors may influence the effects of 
coffee on endurance performance. However, as only 
one placebo-controlled study performed independent 
analysis of chlorogenic acid concentrations (Hodgson et 
al., 2013), it is beyond the scope of this review to assess 
their individual effects. Future research that assesses these 
noncaffeine coffee compounds, with direct comparison 
with anhydrous caffeine controls, is greatly needed to 
elucidate their antagonistic potential on the effects of 
caffeine in coffee. Moreover, recent research suggests 
considerable variation in the caffeine content (1.2–25.9 
mg/g) of individual coffee beans, depending on their 
variety and specific plant (Mazzafera & Silvarolla, 2010). 
This degree of variation within one batch of coffee beans 
may partially account for differences observed in the 
caffeine content of individual beverages when samples 
of the same brand were collected from multiple retailers 
(Desbrow, Henry, et al., 2012; Desbrow et al., 2007). 
For example, one recent analysis reported that variation 
in caffeine content was more than 15% across 4 or more 
purchases from different locations, with some brands as 
high as 30% or more, and 85% of brands had a caffeine 
content higher than claimed by manufacturers (Desbrow, 
Henry, et al., 2012). Moreover, another study found that 
caffeine content varied from 259–564 mg when the 
same coffee was purchased from the same major retailer 
on different days (McCusker et al., 2003). A variety of 
potential reasons were cited for variations in caffeine 
content of beverages purchased from the same store 
including particle size (a result of the grinding of beans), 
coffee-to-water ratio, and brewing time (McCusker et al., 
2003). This ambiguity in the dose of caffeine ingested 
with coffee makes the interpretation of coffee research 
difficult and suggests caution when comparing studies 
or recommending specific levels of coffee intake as an 
ergogenic aid. Together with factors such as the roasting 
process and individual bean variation, it is clear that when 
researching the effects of coffee, stringent standardiza-
tion needs to occur in the preparation of beverages, with 
future studies employing both standardized procedures 
and anhydrous caffeine control conditions to better char-
acterize the ergogenic/ergolytic potential of noncaffeine 
coffee compounds.
Non Placebo-Controlled Studies.  The remaining six 
studies administered coffee without a comparison with 
anhydrous caffeine (Butts & Crowell, 1985; Costill 
et al., 1978; Lamina & Musa, 2009; Rodrigues et al., 
1990; Trice & Haymes, 1995; Wiles et al., 1992). This 
lack of control makes it difficult to look at the effects

that noncaffeine coffee constituents are having on the 
ergogenic potential of ingested caffeine, and causes these 
studies to add little to the base of evidence.
When looking at the coffee literature as a whole, 
the magnitude of performance improvements following 
coffee ingestion follow a similar pattern to the reviewed 
placebo-controlled studies, with time to exhaustion trials 
eliciting substantially greater improvements of 24.2%. 
Similar to this, Rodriguez et al. found nonsignificant 
performance improvements of 24.0–27.7% across two 
trials, with a small sample size (n = 6) being a likely 
contributor to the lack of statistical power to detect a dif-
ference (Rodrigues et al., 1990). In their position stand, 
the International Society for Sports Nutrition (ISSN) 
suggest that doses of anhydrous caffeine supplementation 
between 3–7 mg/kg can yield a performance improve-
ment of ~24% (Goldstein et al., 2010). In comparison, a 
meta-analysis found that anhydrous caffeine improved 
exercise testing performance (endurance, graded, or 
short-term) by 12.3% (ES 0.41; 95% CI, 9.1–0.51) rela-
tive to a placebo control group (Doherty & Smith, 2004). 
The performance improvements seen with coffee mimic 
these numbers, however the smaller degree of improve-
ment observed with time to completion trials (3.1% 
increase) suggests that assessment methods could play 
a significant role. The assessment of endurance capacity 
has been cited as a problem in endurance literature by 
Laursen et al., who noted that there is a greater coefficient 
of variation when subjects perform tests to exhaustion 
(measured in six of the included studies) in comparison 
with a time trial such as a 1500-m run (Laursen et al., 
2007). This alone would suggest the use of time trials 
as a method of assessing endurance performance, as the 
variability in time to exhaustion could account for much 
of the variation in exercise times, but is also supported 
by the fact that a time to exhaustion test does not mimic 
any endurance sporting situation (almost all endurance 
sports require athletes to perform a specified amount of 
work or cover a distance as fast as possible).
In the contemporary coffee literature, it is unclear 
whether the roasting process changes the utility of the 
contained caffeine, and whether other compounds pro-
duced during the roasting process enhance or interfere 
with caffeine’s action (de Paulis et al., 2002). Until the 
effects of the roasting process are better characterized, 
the source of caffeine remains an important variable 
to consider when evaluating the strength of evidence. 
This factor was apparent in the reviewed studies, with 
four studies using regularly brewed coffee, mimicking 
normal coffee intake, and the rest utilizing decaffeinated 
coffee with added anhydrous caffeine. Of the four studies 
containing a naturally caffeinated coffee condition, two 
significant improvements in endurance performance were 
seen (Hodgson et al., 2013; Wiles et al., 1992). The doses 
within these four studies were within the recommended 
range (3–7 mg/kg) for caffeine intake, however coffee 
was not compared with anhydrous caffeine in two of 
these trials (Lamina & Musa, 2009; Wiles et al., 1992), 
and none measured blood concentrations chlorogenic 
acid metabolites. Previous research has shown individual 
variation in plasma levels of these metabolites over the 
4hrs following coffee ingestion which could contribute 
to some of the variation seen in performance trials, espe-
cially in studies with small sample sizes (Monteiro et al., 
2007). This lack of comparison with either a decaffein-
ated coffee, or anhydrous caffeine condition, along with 
the lack of measurement of other circulating compounds 
derived from coffee, limits the ability to compare and 
extrapolate the results.
The use of different doses of caffeine greater than 3 
mg/kg have been shown to have similar beneficial effects 
on endurance performance. When 3 mg/kg and 6 mg/kg 
of anhydrous caffeine were ingested in a blinded, cross-
over manner by well-trained male cyclists, both led to a 
significant increase in performance over the placebo, with 
no difference between doses (Desbrow, Biddulph, et al., 
2012). With doses upwards of 3 mg/kg there are minimal 
differences in endurance performance, however with 
dosages  less than 3 mg/kg, a dose-response relationship 
may occur (Desbrow, Biddulph, et al., 2012; Goldstein et 
al., 2010). As none of the studies included in this review 
used a caffeine dose below 3 mg/kg this can be considered 
a strength of the current literature. Whereas the dosage 
may not affect results within the reviewed literature due 
to a narrow range of doses, all greater than or equal to 
3 mg/kg, participants’ habitual caffeine intake may alter 
the individual response to a caffeine bolus, with lower 
postexercise catecholamine levels reported in habitual 
users when compared with nonusers (Van Soeren et al., 
1993). Due to the presence of caffeine in the diet in vary-
ing concentrations, this is a difficult variable to control, 
and as such the studies included in this review varied in 
the participants’ habitual caffeine intake.
Timing of caffeine ingestion before exercise testing 
is an important variable to consider, as caffeine may be 
absorbed into the body at different rates depending on the 
vehicle used to administer it. Our review was limited to 
studies in which coffee was consumed more than 45 min 
before endurance exercise testing. This specific demar-
cation was selected due to previous research indicating 
that the time to peak saliva concentration for coffee via 
caffeine is 42 min (Liguori et al., 1997). A strength of 
the coffee literature is that all studies included in this 
evidence-based review dosed participants with caffeine 
or coffee 60 min before exercising, apart from one which 
dosed participants 90 min before exercise (McLellan & 
Bell, 2004), and another with trials at 53 and 150 min post 
ingestion (Rodrigues et al., 1990). This parallels recent 
research suggesting that the peak performance improve-
ments are seen with exercise testing 1hr after caffeine 
ingestion, as opposed to at peak serum caffeine levels 
(Skinner et al., 2013). Additional research is needed to 
examine the effects of the timing of caffeine and coffee 
ingestion on exercise performance, to confirm these find-
ings with the addition of noncaffeine coffee compounds.
Similar to the effects of anhydrous caffeine, there is 
moderate evidence from two of three placebo-controlled, 
and three of six non placebo-controlled studies that

preexercise coffee ingestion improves endurance per-

Additional RCTs with strong experimental designs are 
needed to ascertain whether there is a limiting effect of 
other compounds within coffee on its ability to improve 
endurance performance.
Coffee and Perceived Exertion.  There is weak 
evidence to support the beneficial effects of coffee 
in reducing participants’ RPE during endurance 
performance trials. Three of six studies that assessed 
perceived exertion during endurance performance found 
beneficial effects with coffee ingestion, and only one of 
these had an anhydrous caffeine control. Although these 
studies provide only preliminary evidence, the findings 
are in conjunction with previously stated theories for 
caffeine’s ergogenic effects; improving performance 
by reducing perceptions of pain and fatigue. A meta-
analysis by Doherty and Smith found that caffeine led 
to a reduction in RPE of 5.6% (ES -0.47; 95% CI, -0.35 
to -0.59) relative to a placebo control group (Doherty & 
Smith, 2005). Using a regression analyses, they found that 
reductions in RPE accounted for ~29% of the variance 
in the improvement in exercise performance (Doherty 
& Smith, 2005). The mixed results of our review are 
surprising in light of previous caffeine literature and the 
aforementioned mechanism of caffeine action, suggesting 
that other compounds within coffee may have an 
inhibitory effect on the usual fatigue-blocking ergogenic 
benefits. This would be surprising, as research examining 
the effects of noncaffeine coffee compounds on mood 
and cognitive function show positive results (Camfield 
et al., 2013). Future studies examining the relationships 
between coffee, noncaffeine coffee compounds, and 
perceived exertion are warranted to illuminate coffee’s 
efficacy in improving performance via this mechanism. It 
is also prudent to mention that small sample sizes (range 
of n = 6–28) within the included studies could lead to 
a lack of power to detect changes in RPE. Moreover, 
the variability in participant training status (from elite 
athletes to relatively sedentary individuals) likely 
contributed additional variation to the results.
In addition to the potential improvements in endur-
ance performance, the ingestion of coffee may have 
benefits for individuals engaging in habitual physical 
activities. For example, reduced RPE was found in 
comparison with a decaffeinated coffee placebo during 
60 min of submaximal cycling in 10 recreationally active 
young-adult males (Demura et al., 2007). Although not 
included in this review due to emphasis on steady state 
exercise rather than performance measures, this could 
be beneficial for athletes during training, or the general 
public aiming to exercise for health, allowing them 
to exercise at a higher submaximal intensity without 
perceiving the extra exertion. The one study to assess 
steady state exercise within this review (Hodgson et al., 
2013) found RPE to be slightly, but not significantly (p 
= .091), lower during exercise in the two caffeine trials 
(coffee, 10 ± 0, and anhydrous caffeine, 10 ± 0), when 
compared with controls (decaffeinated coffee, 11 ± 0 and 
a placebo, 11 ± 0). However, RPE was not assessed during 
endurance performance testing, so comparisons cannot be 
drawn between submaximal and maximal efforts. Taking 
into account the small sample size of this study (n = 8), 
additional research is necessary to reproduce these find-
ings with larger sample sizes, and for different endurance 
activities (e.g., cycling, running, etc.). However, prelimi-
nary evidence suggests that coffee ingestion may have 
translational utility for recreationally-active individuals 
engaging in habitual physical activities.
The level of evidence for coffee and RPE, “weak,” 
is mainly driven by a dearth of well-designed RCTs. Of 
the six studies eligible for inclusion in this review, three 
studies reported a significant benefit of coffee for RPE, 
but each of these had a relatively small sample size (n 
= 6 to n = 28), and only one had an anhydrous caffeine 
control. Other methodological limitations contributing to 
this rating include the lack of chlorogenic acid metabolite 
measurement in ingested coffee (and any mechanisms 
related to the inhibition of caffeine action), the differ-
ing methods used to assess endurance performance, and 
variability in participant characteristics (e.g., training 
status, habitual caffeine intake, sex, etc.). Therefore, 
there is a strong need for additional research examining 
the acute effects of coffee intake on RPE during endur-
ance performance.
Practical Application and Safe Usage.  Doses found 
to have a beneficial effect on performance ranged from 
3.0–8.1 mg/kg, which practically translates to 1.5–4.0 
and 2.0–5.5 8-oz cups (50 kg female and 70 kg male, 
respectively) of regular drip brewed coffee containing 
~100 mg of caffeine (Goldstein et al., 2010; Ludwig 
et al., 2014). The NCAA added caffeine to its list of 
banned substances at urinary concentrations exceeding 
15mcg/ml (NCAA, 2015; Resource Exchange Center, 
2015). This was done due to the large interindividual 
variability in caffeine metabolism, preventing specific 
recommendations regarding upper limits for consumption. 
Limited available research estimates that one might need 
to rapidly ingest 10–13 mg/kg of caffeine (Pasman et al., 
1995; Graham, 1995; Spriet 2014), equating to around 
7–9 eight ounce cups of coffee (Ludwig et al., 2014) to 
exceed this limit. Furthermore, this level of consumption 
is not typical for most of the population and is much 
higher than intake levels used within the reviewed studies.
Across the studies included in this review, no adverse 
events or side effects related to the consumption of coffee 
were reported. Nonetheless, it is important to briefly 
discuss potential deleterious consequences associated 
with the use of coffee as an ergogenic aid. Caffeine may 
induce diuresis up to 4 hrs postingestion, in a resting 
state. However, this diuresis should not be generalized to 
exercise due to a lack of supporting literature (­Goldstein

et al., 2010). Moreover, the ISSN reported that with 
preexercise caffeine ingestion, no negative effects on 
fluid balance were seen during exercise (Goldstein et 
al., 2010). Other possible, but uncommon, side effects 
of caffeine intake include gastrointestinal distress, sleep 
disturbance, interactions with other dietary supplements 
such as creatine, and altered electrolyte balance (Tunni-
cliffe et al., 2008). There is a need for more research to 
characterize the potential side effects specifically related 
to ingestion of coffee (e.g., gastrointestinal distress, elec-
trolyte imbalance) before exercise or competition. Based 
on the studies included in this evidence-based review, 
the consumption of coffee 60–150 min before endurance 
exercise may not induce negative side effects, however 
as with all ergogenic aids, athletes are strongly advised 
to practice their intake before competition days to ensure 
palatability and identify any negative reactions.
Conclusions
Novelty
This evidence-based review is the first to specifically 
focus on the effects of preexercise coffee intake on endur-
ance performance and RPE. In opposition to previously 
reported views suggesting that other compounds within 
coffee may inhibit its ergogenic effects (de Paulis et al., 
2002), the current body of research suggests that the 
ingestion of coffee 60 min before exercise may help to 
improve performance in endurance sports such as cycling 
and running. However, the evidence is not as strong for 
coffee in reducing RPE. Further research is needed to 
better ascertain the influence of coffee intake on RPE, 
using double-blinded crossover designs, measurement 
of noncaffeine coffee compounds, and a time-trial as 
the method of performance assessment at least 60 min 
following ingestion of an anhydrous caffeine control and 
stringently standardized coffee condition.
Practical Application
The reviewed evidence suggests that preexercise coffee 
beverages containing at least 3 mg/kg (1.36 mg/lb) of 
caffeine, ingested at least 60 min preexercise, may have 
a beneficial effect on endurance performance (Costill et 
al., 1978; Hodgson et al., 2013; McLellan & Bell, 2004; 
Trice & Haymes, 1995; Wiles et al., 1992). Significant 
improvements over control conditions were found with 
doses up to 8.1 mg/kg (3.68 mg/lb), however performance 
benefits were similar to 3 mg/kg doses. This translates to 
at least 1.5–2.0, 8-oz cups of regularly drip brewed coffee 
before exercise or training, depending on body weight.
