Coﬀee is produced in tropical countries around the
Equator where climatic conditions are favourable
for fungal development and mycotoxin production;
however, mycotoxins do not only occur in the tropical
countries. The aim was to evaluate the mycoﬂora

samples of green coﬀee beans from Brazil. The myco-

method and the OTA was determined using sequential
phenyl silane and immunoaﬃnity column cleanup fol-

Introduction
Coﬀee is grown in the wide tropical belt surround-
ing the Equator between the tropics of Cancer
and Capricorn. Important roles have been ascribed
to microorganisms in coﬀee processing and produc-
tion of mycotoxins may occur because of poor drying
and storage. The magnitude and diversity of the
microbial population associated with dry (natural)
processing of coﬀee has been reported since 1936,
and of the fungi, the genera Aspergillus, Penicillium
and Cladosporium, have been the most detected at
diﬀerent stages from harvest to storage (Mislivec et al.
1983, Micco et al. 1989).
Mycotoxigenic species can be found in all major
taxonomic
groups
of
fungi,
but
the
genus
Aspergillus is undoubtedly one of the most important.
Among the mycotoxins produced by Aspergillus spp.,
aﬂatoxins and ochratoxin A are receiving attention
world-wide because of the hazard they pose to human
and animal health (Krogh 1987).
Ochratoxin A (OTA) is a secondary metabolite pro-
duced mainly by the two ubiquitous fungal genera of
Aspergillus and Penicillium. OTA was ﬁrst described
in the 1960s after the discovery of the aﬂatoxins
(van der Merwe et al. 1965) The production of OTA
by strains of A. ochraceus is predominant in warm
climates and by P. verrucosum in temperate climates
(Moss 1996). Aspergillus carbonarius and a few
A. niger strains are also OTA producers in warm
climates and they have been isolated from coﬀee
(Joosten et al. 2001, Urbano et al. 2001, Martins
et al. 2002). OTA is found in several products such
as cereal grains, nuts, coﬀee beans and roasted coﬀee
products, meats, spices, dried fruits, fruit juice,
fermented beverages and animal organs (Pohland
1992, Zimmerli and Dick 1996).
In coﬀee, OTA can be produced during drying
process, transport and storage under the inﬂuence
of
determined conditions (temperature
and aw).
The optimum temperature for OTA production
by A. ochraceus and P. verrucosum is 24–25C at
aw ¼ 0.97 (Bacon et al. 1973, Northolt et al. 1979).
The
OTA
is
not
signiﬁcantly
destroyed
during
the roasting process and is consumed with the coﬀee
brew (Studer-Rohr et al. 1995). Several studies with

contradictory results have been published concerning
the stability of OTA during the roasting process.
Tsubouchi et al. (1987) reported a reduction of
0–12% OTA after roasting. Levi et al. (1974) and
Micco et al. (1989) found a destruction of 50–100%.
Blanc et al. (1998) found that up to 80% of the OTA
was destroyed in the roasting process.
The International Agency for Research on Cancer has
classiﬁed OTA as a possible carcinogen to humans
(IARC 1993). The kidneys are the main target organ.
Nephrotoxicity is dominant and has been implicated
as the causative agent responsible for human endemic
nephropathy
in
areas
of
the
Balkan
countries
(Plesˇ tina 1996) and in Algeria and Tunisia (Creppy
et al. 1993). It has also shown teratogenic and
immunotoxic properties (Prelusky et al. 1994).
Several countries have speciﬁc regulations for OTA
at levels ranging from 1 to 50 ng g1 for foods and
from 5 to 300 ng g1 for animal feeds (FAO 1997).
In the last 10 years, importer coﬀee companies and
researchers have taken a greater interest in the myco-
logical quality of green coﬀee beans to estimate the
risk to humans imposed by OTA.
The aim of the present study was to evaluate the
incidence of fungi and OTA in green coﬀee beans
from Brazil.
Materials and methods
Sampling
Sixty green coﬀee bean samples (Coﬀee arabica) from
Brazil, collected during the 2000 harvest, weighing
about 1 kg, from diﬀerent lots, were analysed. The
sampling was conducted by the provider. Each
sample was divided into two portions: one was
examined for the presence of fungi, the other for OTA.
Pretreatment of samples for mycological analysis
Ten grams of each sample were homogenized for
3 min in 90 ml (101 suspension) peptone water
(Oxoid,
code
CM
9,
Basingstoke,
UK)
in
a
Colworth 400 Stomacher (Seward Medical, London,
UK). Ten-fold dilutions were prepared up to 104.
Mycological examination
From each dilution in peptone water, 1 ml was spread
onto plates of Dichloran Rose Bengal Chlortetracy-
cline Agar (DRBC) (Oxoid, code CM 727; SR 78)
with 0.25 ml per plate (King et al. 1984, Samson et al.
1992). The plates were incubated at 25C in the
upright position for 5 days before enumeration and
identiﬁcation of the mould ﬂora. Each isolated mould
colony was observed microscopically for morpholo-
gical characterization and identiﬁcation to genus level
(Raper and Fennell 1965, Domsch et al. 1980).
OTA determination by HPLC
Each sample (500 g) was ground using a Cross Beater
Mill, type SK1 (Retsch) and appropriately mixed
by hand. From each sample, two subsamples were
separated and analysed for the presence of OTA,
following the method described by Entwisle et al.
(2001); 15 g were extracted with methanol and 3%
NaHCO3 by shaking for 30 min. The extract was
ﬁltered, centrifuged and cleaned up on a phenyl
silane column with elution from the washed column
with methanol–water. The eluate was diluted with
phosphate-buﬀered
saline
(PBS)
and
applied
to
an
OTA
immunoaﬃnity
column,
(Ochraprep,
Rhoˆ ne-Diagnostics Technologies Ltd, Spain), which
was washed with water. The methanol eluate was
evaporated
and
the
residue
was
redissolved
in
injection solvent. Determination of OTA was carried
out by isocratic reverse-phase liquid chromatography
(HPLC).
For the recovery test, uncontaminated samples were
spiked with standard OTA solutions corresponding
to 1, 2, 4 and 10 mg kg1, and analysed in duplicate
using the same procedure as described for samples.
The average recoveries were 96.5, 92.0, 95.5 and
98.7%, respectively. The limit of detection was
0.2 mg kg1.
Results and discussion
Sixty samples of green coﬀee beans from Brazil,
collected during the 2000 harvest, were analysed
mycologically and for OTA.

A high incidence of moulds was observed in practi-
cally all samples (91.7%); only ﬁve samples did not
present mould contamination. The dominant fungal
genus was Aspergillus, including A. niger (83.3%)
at higher levels (102–103 cfu g1) and A. ochraceus
(53.3%) and A. ﬂavus (25.0%) at lower levels
(101–102 cfu g1). The occurrence and levels of the
genus Cladosporium (16.7%) and Penicillium (10.0%)
were substantially lower (table 1).
The results on the samples positive for ochratoxin A
are summarized in table 2. Twenty samples (33.3%)
of 60 were found contaminated with the toxin at
levels ranging from 0.2 to 7.3 mg kg1. Of these
positive samples, 10 (50.0%) presented levels ranging
from 0.2 to 1.0 mg kg1; six samples (30.0%) had
levels between 1.1 and 5.0 mg kg1, and four samples
(20.0%) had levels from 5.3 to 7.3 mg kg1. The con-
centration of OTA ranged from 0.2 to 7.3 mg kg1
and the average concentration was 2.38 mg kg1,
with standard deviation of 2.36. Forty samples
(66.6%) were negative for OTA. The results of this
study show that a high incidence of A. niger and
A. ochraceus (83.3 and 53.3%, respectively) in green
coﬀee bean did not present high frequency (33.3%) or
high levels of OTA (tables 1 and 2). These considera-
tions are similar to those of the Frank (1999) and
Urbano et al. (2001), where infection by A. ochraceus
and A. niger did not necessarily imply high levels of
OTA. Factors such as the nature of the substrate, the
temperature, aw, and relative humidity are important
for the fungi produce toxins, as well as the genetic
capacity of the strains.
Concerning
the
mycoﬂora in
coﬀee,
Corte dos
Santos et al. (1971) observed that in Angolan green
coﬀee beans, under good conditions for mould
growth (26C, 92% relative humidity for 3 months),
Aspergillus spp. including A. ochraceus, A. ﬂavus,
A. niger, A. tamarii, A. wentii and A. versicolor were
dominant; Penicillium spp. were also detected, but at
a much lower frequency. This kind of mycoﬂora was
also reported by Levi et al. (1974) in ﬁve samples
of mouldy green coﬀee beans. Poisson et al. (1975)
reported the presence of Aspergillus and Penicillium
spp. on Angolan coﬀee. Mislivec et al. (1983) found
93.4–100% infected coﬀee beans when they studied
the incidence of toxigenic and non-toxigenic fungi
in coﬀee beans from 31 producer countries. In 1999,
Frank, reported the presence of A. ochraceus in
samples of coﬀee from six diﬀerent countries, but
not all of the samples had detectable levels of OTA.
With reference to the incidence of OTA, Levi et al.
(1974) reported for the ﬁrst time the presence of OTA
in three samples out of 68 commercial samples of
green coﬀee beans at levels of 20–80 mg kg1. The
same author, in 1980, in a review on mycotoxins in
coﬀee, indicated the presence of aﬂatoxins, ochra-
toxin A and sterigmatocystin, at extremely low levels.
Cantafora et al. (1983) analysed 40 commercial green
coﬀee samples, of which nine were contaminated at
levels between 0.5 and 23.0 mg kg1 OTA. Micco et al.
(1989) found in 29 samples of green coﬀee a high
percentage of contamination (58.0%) with OTA, at

low, indicating that the problem is restricted and due
