Molecular and Toxigenic Characterization of Bacillus cereus and
Bacillus thuringiensis Strains Isolated from Commercial Ground
Roasted Coffee

ABSTRACT
Thirty samples of roasted ground coffee beans from 10 different commercial brands were analyzed to investigate the
occurrence and levels of Bacillus cereus and Bacillus thuringiensis strains. Strains were evaluated for their genetic diversity by
repetitive element sequence polymorphism PCR (Rep-PCR) and for their toxigenic profiles, i.e., the presence of hblA, hblC,
hblD, nheA, nheB, nheC, cytK, ces, and entFM. Survival and multiplication of B. cereus sensu lato in the ready-to-drink coffee
was determined to evaluate this beverage as a possible vehicle for B. cereus infection. B. cereus was detected in 17 (56.7%) of the
30 samples, and B. thuringiensis was detected in 8 (26.7%) of the 30 samples. Five samples did not produce any characteristic
growth. The most common gene, entFM, was detected in 23 strains (92%). The NHE complex (nheA, nheB, and nheC genes) was
found in 19 strains (76%). The HBL complex (hblA, hblC, and hblD) was found in 16 strains (64%). All strains were negative for
ces. The cytK gene was found in 16 strains (64%). The computer-assisted cluster analysis of Rep-PCR profiles using a clustering
criterion of 80% similarity revealed four main clusters. Cluster 1 was the predominant and comprised three B. thuringiensis
strains with 100% similarity, cluster 2 comprised two B. cereus strains (100% similarity), cluster 3 comprised two B.
thuringiensis strains (90% similarity), and cluster 4 comprised one B. thuringiensis strain and one B. cereus strain (85%
similarity). The cluster analysis of fingerprints generated by Rep-PCR revealed a high genetic diversity among the B. cereus
strains, suggesting that the contamination could have originated from different sources. In our experiments, when sugar was
added and the beverage was kept in thermic bottles there was a significant increase in B. cereus sensu lato levels, which may
increase the risk of food poisoning. These results highlight the need for additional studies on this subject to better evaluate coffee
as a food poisoning vehicle.
Coffee grains are produced by processing fruits of the
genus Coffea. After harvesting, the fruits are spread on the
ground (earth, platforms, concrete, or tarmac) in layers,
heaped at night, and respread each day. During the course of
10
to
25
days
of
sun
drying,
a
natural
microbial
fermentation occurs (26).
Contamination of coffee by Bacillus cereus and Bacillus
thuringiensis may occur due to the presence of these
microorganisms in soil that adheres to the coffee beans. B.
cereus, Bacillus megaterium, Paenibacillus macerans, and
Lactobacillus plantarum are the predominant bacterial
species that have been identified during the fermentation
process (26). Souza et al. (27) reported the presence of B.
cereus in several brands of commercial roasted ground coffee
sold in Brazil and exported from Brazil.
Several enterotoxins identified in B. cereus strains have
been implicated in food poisoning and other infections.
Cereulide, a small cyclic heat-stable peptide, causes emetic
syndrome, which is characterized by the occurrence of
nausea and vomiting within up to 6 h after ingestion.
Diarrheal syndrome is attributed to enterotoxins, a group of
proteins including two heat-labile toxins, hemolysin BL
(HBL;
encoded
by
hblA,
hblC,
and
hblD)
(5)
and
nonhemolytic enterotoxin (NHE; encoded by nheA, nheB,
and nheC) (14), and cytotoxin K (encoded by cytK), a single
protein similar to the b-toxin of Clostridium perfringens
type C (20). This syndrome is characterized by the occurrence
of abdominal pain and watery diarrhea 8 to 16 h after inges-
tion (13). An additional enterotoxin, enterotoxin FM encoded
by entFM (3), was reported as cytotoxic to Vero cells (6).
B. thuringiensis strains can carry the same enterotoxins
as B. cereus, suggesting that this species also may pose a
potential risk of food poisoning (4, 22).
The aim of this study was to investigate the occurrence
and levels of B. cereus and B. thuringiensis in roasted
ground coffee commercialized in Brazil and to evaluate the
genetic diversity and toxigenic profiles of the strains
isolated. The survival and multiplication of B. cereus sensu
lato in ready-to-drink coffee was determined to evaluate this
beverage as a possible vehicle of B. cereus and B.
thuringiensis infection.

MATERIALS AND METHODS
Sampling. Thirty samples of roasted ground coffee from 10
different commercial brands were analyzed. Three different batches
were analyzed for each brand.
Isolation and identification of B. cereus and B. thur-
ingiensis strains. Isolation of B. cereus and B. thuringiensis from
ground roasted coffee was performed as described by Speck (28).
A 1-g sample of ground roasted coffee was homogenized in 10 ml
of sterile Butterfield phosphate buffer (pH 7.2). After homogeni-
zation, 2 ml was transferred to a sterile tube and heated in water
bath for 5 min at 65uC. After cooling, 1 ml was transferred to a
tube containing 9 ml of sterile distilled water, which was used to
prepare three serial 10-fold dilutions. Each dilution was plated on a
selective solid medium, VRM (30). Plates were incubated at 33uC
for 24 h and screened for characteristic colonies showing
hydrolysis of lecithin. B. cereus and B. thuringiensis strains were
identified as previously described (9, 24, 30). All strains were
deposited at Colec¸a˜o de Culturas do Geˆnero Bacillus e Geˆneros
Correlatos (CCGB, Instituto Oswaldo Cruz, FIOCRUZ, Rio de
Janeiro, Brazil).
Evaluation of survival and growth of B. cereus sensu lato
in ready-to-drink coffee. To evaluate the survival and growth of
B. cereus sensu lato in ready-to-drink coffee, the beverage was
prepared in the same way as in many households in Brazil. Two
samples, one presenting 101 CFU/g and other presenting 103 CFU/
g, were selected for the assay. Each sample was assayed twice, i.e.,
with and without the addition of 15 g of commercial sugar. Before
use, the commercial sugar was spread in a thin layer in a sterile tray
and exposed to UV light for 30 min. To brew the coffee, 150 ml of
boiling sterile water was added to 10 g of ground coffee, with or
without 15 g of sugar, and homogenized. The mixture was filtered
in sterile commercial paper filters directly into thermic bottles,
where it was kept for 7 h. Quantification of B. cereus sensu lato
was done after 0 and 7 h by transferring 1 ml of the beverage to a
tube containing 9 ml of sterile distilled water, which was used to
prepare three serial 10-fold dilutions. Each dilution was plated on
selective VRM. Plates were incubated at 33uC for 24 h and
screened for characteristic colonies showing hydrolysis of lecithin.
Experiments were performed in duplicate.
Detection of L2 hemolytic fraction. All strains were tested
for enterotoxin production using the B. cereus enterotoxin reverse
passive latex agglutination test kit (Oxoid, Basingstoke, UK)
according to the manufacturer’s instructions.
Detection of enterotoxin genes. Genomic DNA of B. cereus
strains was extracted by thermal lysis according to a methodology
previously described (8). An 1-ml aliquot of the culture that had been
grown overnight at 37uC in 5 ml of brain heart infusion was
centrifuged for 4 min at 134 | g. The supernatant was discarded, the
pellet was washed in 1 ml of TE buffer (10 mM Tris, 1 mM EDTA,
pH 7.8), and the cells were resuspended in 100 ml of TE buffer. The
suspension was placed in a 100uC water bath for 10 min and
centrifuged for 30 s at 1,340 | g at 4uC. The supernatant was
collected and used as the PCR template. Genes belonging to the HBL
(hblA, hblC, and hblD) and NHE (nheA, nheB, and nheC) complexes,
ces, and entFM were amplified according to the methodology
described by Yang et al. (32). The cytK gene was amplified as
described by Guinebretiere et al. (15) and Yang et al. (32).
Bacillus Rep-PCR. Repetitive element sequence polymor-
phism PCR (Rep-PCR fingerprinting) was used for chromosomal
comparisons of B. cereus strains. The primers and the conditions
for amplification were the same as those described by Reyes-
Ramirez and Ibarra (23). Amplifications were performed with a
GeneAmp System 9700 (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA) in
a total volume of 25 ml, which contained 300 ng of each primer,
200 mM concentrations of each deoxynucleoside triphosphate,
5 mM MgCl2, 2.5 U of Taq polymerase (Invitrogen, Sa˜o Paulo,
Brazil), and 1 ml of template DNA. To visualize band patterns,
amplicons were electrophoresed in 1.2% agarose gels in Tris-
borate-EDTA buffer (89 mM Tris-borate, 2 mM EDTA, pH 8.0) at
2 V/cm for 4 h. After treatment of the gels with 0.5 mg/ml ethidium
bromide solution for 15 min, amplicons were visualized under UV
light. A 1-kb DNA molecular size marker (Invitrogen) was used as
a reference. Banding patterns were analyzed by visual inspection
and by computer-assisted analysis with GelCompar II, version 1.5
software
(Applied
Maths,
Kortrijk,
Belgium)
using
0.40%
optimization and 1% position tolerance. The similarity of the
banding patterns was assessed using the Dice index and the
unweighted pair group method with arithmetic averages (UP-
GMA). For cluster analysis, 80% similarity was the clustering
criterion (1, 21).
RESULTS
Presence of B. cereus and B. thuringiensis in coffee
samples. For the 30 samples analyzed, B. cereus was
detected in 17 (56.7%) and B. thuringiensis was detected in
8 (26.7%). Five samples did not produce any characteristic
colonies on VRM. B. thuringiensis levels ranged from 101
CFU/g (two samples) to 102 CFU/g (six samples). B. cereus
was found at higher levels than B. thuringiensis: 2 samples
had 101 CFU/g, 5 samples had 102 CFU/g, and 10 samples
had 103 CFU/g. Concomitant isolation of both species in the
same sample did not occur. However, three coffee brands
contained B. thuringiensis in two batches and B. cereus in a
third batch. Six brands contained only B. cereus.
Evaluation of survival and growth of B. cereus
sensu lato in ready-to-drink coffee. Levels of B. cereus
sensu lato were evaluated in the ready-to-drink coffee
beverage, with and without sugar. Results are shown in
Table 1. For the C4 sample, in which the initial level was
101 CFU/g, there was no significant increase in the B.
cereus sensu lato levels after incubation of the beverage
with added sugar. For the C3 sample, in which the initial
level was 103 CFU/g, B. cereus increased by 104 CFU in the
beverage with added sugar after incubation in the thermic
bottle for 7 h.
PCR amplification of toxin genes and identification
of toxigenic profiles. All PCRs generated fragments of the
expected size. The entFM gene was the most common gene
and was detected in 23 strains (92%). The NHE complex
(nheA, nheB, and nheC) was found in 19 strains (76%).
Three strains were negative for all three NHE genes, and
three strains lacked nheB amplification. The HBL complex
(hblA, hblC, and hblD) was found in 16 (64%) strains. Only
one strain was negative for all three HBL genes, and one
strain produced an amplification product for one gene. All
strains were negative for ces. The cytK gene was found in 16
(64%) strains when the PCR amplification was performed

DISCUSSION
B. cereus has been extensively reported as a foodborne
pathogen (11, 12, 17, 18). However, in only a few studies
has B. thuringiensis been isolated from food, despite the fact
that this species is able to express enterotoxins such as HBL,
NHE, and CytK and thus poses a potential risk for food
poisoning (22).
A search of the indexed literature for articles in which
the isolation of Bacillus sp. from roasted ground coffee had
been reported retrieved no results. Studies on this subject are
scarce.
In the present study, 17 B. cereus strains and 8 B.
thuringiensis strains isolated from 10 different commercial
brands of roasted ground coffee were fingerprinted by Rep-
PCR and evaluated for toxigenic potential. The cluster
analysis of fingerprints generated by Rep-PCR revealed
high genetic diversity among the B. cereus strains, with only
one cluster at 80% similarity. This result is in agreement
with that of another study involving B. cereus strains in food
(8) and suggests that the contamination could come from

Genes encoding enterotoxins were widely spread
among the strains analyzed. According to current reports,
the enterotoxins HBL and NHE are the major virulence
factors in B. cereus strains related to food poisoning
episodes (29). The frequency of enterotoxin genes and the
toxigenic patterns in B. cereus and B. thuringiensis were
similar to those described by Chaves et al. (8) for B. cereus
sensu stricto isolated from several types of food in Brazil,
except for cytK-2 whose frequency was higher in strains
isolated from coffee.
In several studies, cytK was reported as abundant
among foodborne B. cereus strains (7, 15, 25). In the present
study, the strains positive for cytK harbored the variant
cytK-2. Although CytK-2 is not as toxic as CytK-1, its
potential to cause disease cannot be underestimated (10).
The presence of cytK-1 is rare, and strains harboring this
gene belong to a distant phylogenetic group of Bacillus
cytotoxicus strains (16).
The most common toxigenic profile, type I, included
strains possessing all known genes associated with entero-
toxin production except ces. This result is in agreement with
findings from other studies of B. cereus strains from Korea
(19) and Brazil (8) and supports the fact that B. thuringiensis
is able to produce several virulence factors implicated in
food poisoning.
The presence of B. cereus in roasted ground coffee may
be due to several factors, including unsuitable low roasting
temperatures, inappropriate storage, or contamination dur-
ing the packaging process. Strains present in the green
coffee beans probably do not survive adequate roasting
processes because of the high temperatures used (around
208uC).
Brazilian legislation that established microbiological
parameters for food did not establish maximum levels for B.
cereus in roasted ground coffee (2). However, 103 CFU/g
was established for dry food (soups and broths) that are
prepared in a way similar to that for coffee, i.e., by the
addition of boiling water. Coffee is a product of the
extraction by hot water of soluble substances present in
roasted coffee grounds. Because spores can survive cooking
temperatures (31), B. cereus and B. thuringiensis may
survive
the
preparation
process
and
contaminate
the
beverage.
In Brazil, coffee is an extremely popular beverage.
Although it is usually prepared and consumed immediately,
the beverage may be brewed, frequently with sugar, and
then kept in thermic bottles for long periods of time before
consumption. This long storage occurs in households
(usually low income) where the beverage is prepared at
night and consumed in the morning and in restaurants where
the beverage is available as a courtesy on a self-service
basis. The nutrients present in the coffee itself are not able to
support the growth of B. cereus sensu lato. However, our
experiments indicate that when sugar is added and the
beverage is kept in thermic bottles there is a significant
increase in B. cereus sensu lato. Sugar plus incubation in a
thermic bottle creates a favorable environment for spore
germination and bacterial multiplication, increasing the risk
of food poisoning. Levels of L2 hemolytic fraction in the
ready-to-drink coffee were not evaluated. However, because
the majority of the strains were able to produce high levels
of L2, high levels of this toxin could be found in coffee with
added sugar. Additional experiments are necessary to
evaluate this hypothesis.
Samples of cheaper, low-quality coffee brands had
higher levels of B. cereus sensu lato. These brands usually
are consumed by low-income individuals. This fact in
association with the habit of storing the sugar-added
beverage in thermic bottles can expose this population to
an even higher risk of B. cereus food poisoning.
Coffee concentrate is used as an ingredient in various
food preparations. Spores present in this concentrate could
germinate in the prepared products because they provide the
nutrients to support bacterial growth, and many of these
products are not subjected to further sanitizing treatment
during storage. The process of preparing coffee concentrate
can lead to a concentration of the spores possibly present in
the coffee used as the raw material. Therefore, the quality of
the coffee used in these preparations should be tested.
To our knowledge, this is the first study of B. cereus
and B. thuringiensis in commercial roasted ground coffee in
which the genetic variability and the toxigenic profiles of
these strains were evaluated. The results highlight the need
for additional studies on this subject to better evaluate coffee
as a food poisoning vehicle. New legislation that establishes
safe levels for B. cereus group bacteria in roasted ground
coffee in Brazil may also be needed.
