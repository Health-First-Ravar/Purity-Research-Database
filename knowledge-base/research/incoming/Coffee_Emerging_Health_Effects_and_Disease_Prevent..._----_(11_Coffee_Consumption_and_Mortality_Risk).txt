Coffee Consumption and
Mortality Risk
Kemmyo Sugiyama, Shinichi Kuriyama, and Ichiro Tsuji
11.1
INTRODUCTION
Coffee is one of the most frequently consumed beverages worldwide, with an average
consumption of approximately 7,983,900 tons/year, according to the International Coffee
Organization [1]. Its popularity continues to increase, especially in Asian countries as the
lifestyle becomes more westernized. Therefore, even small effects of coffee on individuals
can have a large effect on public health.
Despite its extensive consumption, coffee was often considered to be somewhat unhealthy,
perhaps because coffee drinking is often associated with unhealthy habits such as smoking
and excessive alcohol drinking. However, recent studies have revealed that coffee exerts
beneﬁcial effects on human health. For instance, coffee consumption may protect against
cardiovascular disease (CVD). If so, coffee consumption may increase life expectancy,
because CVD is one of the most common causes of death worldwide.
Here, we review epidemiologic studies that have examined the relationship between coffee
consumption and mortality due to all causes, CVD, and cancer. The results of these studies
are listed in Table 11.1.
11.2
COFFEE CONSUMPTION AND
ALL-CAUSE MORTALITY
Most earlier studies agreed that mortality risk increased with coffee consumption. In 1986,
Vandenbroucke and colleagues reported that all-cause mortality increased among men who
drank 5 or more cups of coffee/ day; however, no signiﬁcant association was observed among
women [2]. On the basis of a 19-year follow-up cohort, LeGrady and colleagues reported
in 1987 that participants who drank 6 or more cups of coffee/ day had a 1.33-fold higher
risk of all-cause mortality (95% conﬁdence interval [CI], 1.07–1.65) compared with those
who drank 1 cup/day or less [3]. Lindsted et al. in 1992 [4], and Dawber et al. in 1974 [5]
also reported a slightly increased risk of all-cause mortality with coffee consumption among
men. However, other studies published in 1986, 1995, and 2010 concluded that there was no
signiﬁcant association [6–8].

In contrast, most recent studies report that coffee, consumption is inversely associated
with all-cause mortality [9–17]. For example, in 2000 Kleemola et al. reported the ﬁndings
of a 10-year follow-up cohort study showing that the relative risk for all-cause mortality
among women who consumed 7 or more cups of coffee/day was 0.62 (95% CI, 0.44–0.87)
[14]. Lopez-Garcia also reported a lower rate of mortality among coffee consumers in 2008
[17]. In 2010, a cohort study we conducted in Japan indicated that coffee consumption was
inversely associated with all-cause mortality among women [9]. The hazard ratios were 1.00
for participants who never drank coffee, 0.88 (95% CI, 0.73–1.06) for those who drank coffee
occasionally, 0.82 (95% CI, 0.66–1.02) for those who drank 1–2 cups/day, and 0.75 (95%
CI, 0.53–1.05) for those who drank 3 or more cups/day.
Interestingly, two studies conducted in 2006 [18] and 2007 [19], each reported a U-
shaped association. Andersen et al. reported hazard ratios of 0.85 (95% CI, 0.76–0.94) for
1–3 cups/day, 0.81 (95% CI, 0.72–0.91) for 4–5 cups/day, and 0.87 (95% CI, 0.76–1.00) for
consumption of 6 or more cups/ day [18].
This inconsistency between early and more recent studies may be attributable in part
to methodological differences. First, the earlier studies had small numbers of participants,
which might have led to insufﬁcient statistical power [2,3]. Second, most of the recent studies
demonstrating an inverse association between coffee consumption and all-cause mortality
included diabetes mellitus as a covariate in the multivariate model [9,11,18,19], in contrast
to studies that reported unfavorable results. Third, although every study included smoking
status as a covariate in the multivariate model, tobacco consumption has decreased; therefore,
the effect of smoking while drinking coffee also decreased over time [9]. As lifestyles have
changed, it can be assumed that the effect of unknown confounding factors on the association
has also changed.
The U-shaped association between coffee consumption and mortality seen in some studies
may be explained by methods of coffee preparation and additives used. High consumption of
unﬁltered coffee results in excessive intake of coffee lipids, increasing mortality. This issue
will be addressed later. Further, adding sugar and milk to coffee may increase the risk of
obesity and diabetes mellitus. Therefore, the true reduction of mortality may not have been
assessed accurately in most of the previous studies, and further research is needed to better
understand these issues.
In conclusion, we place more credibility on the recent studies indicating that coffee
consumption is inversely related to all-cause mortality.
11.3
COFFEE CONSUMPTION AND CVD MORTALITY
In this section, we review studies that evaluated the association between coffee consump-
tion and CVD mortality, focusing speciﬁcally on coronary heart disease (CHD) and stroke
mortality.
Results regarding the association between coffee consumption and CVD mortality have
been largely dependent on the year of investigation, similar to the results for all-cause mortal-
ity. In 1993, Greenland conducted a meta-analysis that included eight case-control studies and
14 cohort studies published between 1986 and 1992 [20]. The results of the case-control stud-
ies were fairly consistent, showing that coffee consumption was associated with an increased
mortality (rate ratio of 1.42 for 5 cups/day [95% CI, 1.30–1.55] compared with noncoffee
drinkers). In contrast, the cohort studies reported modest and variable hazard ratios.

In 2003, Jazbec and colleagues concluded that no such association was evident [16],
but more recent studies have demonstrated a beneﬁcial effect of coffee consumption on
CVD mortality. In 2006, Andersen and colleagues reported that the hazard ratios for coffee
consumption were 0.76 for 1–3 cups/day (95% CI, 0.64–0.91), 0.81 for 4–5 cups/day (95% CI,
0.66–0.99), and 0.87 for 6 or more cups/day (95% CI, 0.69–1.09) compared with noncoffee
drinkers [18]. In 2011, Mineharu and colleagues reported a U-shaped association between
coffee intake and CVD mortality among men and women [21]. Lopez-Garcia and colleagues
found a weak inverse association in 2006 [22] and found a stronger inverse association in
2008 [17] with hazard ratios among men of 1.05 for 1 cup/month to 4 cups/week (95% CI,
0.90–1.21), 1.09 for 5–7 cups/week (95% CI, 0.95–1.25), 0.95 for 2–3 cups/day (95% CI,
0.81–1.11), 0.85 for 4–5 cups/day (95% CI, 0.65–1.13), and 0.56 for 6 and more cups/day
(95% CI, 0.31–1.03) and hazard ratios among women of 1.06 for 1 cup/month to 4 cups/
week (95% CI, 0.91–1.22), 0.99 for 5–7 cups/week (95% CI, 0.87–1.12), 0.75 for 2–3 cups/
day (95% CI, 0.66–0.86), 0.66 for 4–5 cups/day (95% CI, 0.54–0.80), and 0.81 for 6 and
more cups/day (95% CI, 0.61–1.06) compared with less than 1 cup of coffee/ month. For
mortality due to CVD among women in our previous study [9], the hazard ratios were 1.00 for
no coffee consumption, 0.56 for occasional coffee consumption (95% CI, 0.36–0.86), 0.48
for 1–2 cups/day (95% CI, 0.29–0.80), and 0.45 for 3 or more cups/day (95% CI, 0.20–1.03).
In contrast, this association was not observed for men.
Focusing speciﬁcally on mortality due to CHD, earlier studies did not detect any bene-
ﬁcial effects of coffee consumption [3,11,14,21,23–28]. However, more recent studies have
reported a risk reduction with coffee consumption [7,9,13,22]. In a recent meta-analysis, Wu
et al. concluded that habitual moderate coffee consumption was associated with a lower risk
of CHD morbidity [29].
Few studies have investigated the association between coffee consumption and stroke
mortality. Most of these studies reported that coffee was not associated with a risk of
stroke mortality [7,9,30], but Mineharu and colleagues found an inverse association among
men [21].
Taken together, these results suggest that coffee consumption protects against CVD,
especially CHD, but not stroke.
11.4
COFFEE CONSUMPTION AND CANCER MORTALITY
Few studies have assessed the association between coffee consumption and cancer mor-
tality, and all of these have reported a nonsigniﬁcant relationship [4,6,9,11,17,18]. Even
fewer studies have examined the association between coffee consumption and mortality due
to speciﬁc cancers. However, in our previous study, mortality among women due to col-
orectal cancer was inversely correlated with coffee consumption, whereas mortality due to
other cancers showed no signiﬁcant association [9]. A study by Kurozawa and colleagues
showed that coffee consumption was inversely related to mortality due to hepatocellular
carcinoma [31].
The relationships between coffee consumption and the incidence of speciﬁc cancers have
been noted elsewhere. Brieﬂy, a recent review indicated that coffee exerted a strong protective
effect against hepatocellular and endometrial cancers and may also protect against colorectal
cancer [32]. We have also reported an inverse association between coffee consumption and
the risk of oral, pharyngeal, esophageal, and primary liver cancer [33,34], but not colorectal

11.5
POSSIBLE MECHANISM OF CVD MORTALITY
REDUCTION BY COFFEE
Coffee is a complex of various compounds, each of which may exert either beneﬁcial or
harmful effects on human health. Previously, the elevated risk of CVD was considered to be
due mainly to two compounds identiﬁed in the lipid fraction of coffee grounds: cafestol and
kahweol [36]. These compounds increase the plasma concentration of cholesterol, which
leads to a higher risk of coronary events. On the other hand, coffee consumption does
not increase the risk of CHD mortality in populations that mainly consume paper-ﬁltered
coffee [18,22,30]. Further, regular coffee consumption reduces susceptibility to low-density
lipoprotein oxidation [37], which would have a favorable effect on human health.
It has been reported that by increasing blood pressure, caffeine intake be harmful to some
hypertension-prone individuals; however, the hemodynamic effects of coffee and caffeine
consumption in a long term have not been sufﬁciently studied [38,39]. This short-term effect
on blood pressure may be reduced by the development of partial tolerance in individuals and
by the cumulative counteracting effects of other substances present in coffee [40].
Coffee contains numerous antioxidants. In fact, coffee is the single greatest contributor
to total antioxidant intake, accounting for approximately 66% of dietary antioxidants [41].
One study reported that the phenolic compounds of coffee in particular (chlorogenic acid,
ferulic acid, and p-coumaric acid) have a strong antioxidant capacity [42]. Experimental data
conﬁrm that these compounds are efﬁciently absorbed and increase the antioxidant capacity
of plasma [43,44]. Taken together, these results indicate that coffee may reduce the risk of
CVD mortality [45,46].
Another possible mechanism by which coffee may improve cardiovascular health may
be due to its effect on type 2 diabetes mellitus. Although caffeine reduces insulin sensitivity
[47], another coffee constituent, chlorogenic acid, reduces plasma glucose levels [48]. In
fact, a cross-sectional analysis has shown that higher consumption of decaffeinated coffee is
associated with lower concentrations of glycosylated hemoglobin [49].
Furthermore, recent studies have revealed that coffee possess anti-inﬂammatory actions
[18,50], providing an additional mechanism for the reduced risk of CVD mortality, as
atherosclerosis is accelerated by inﬂammation.
11.6
CONCLUSIONS
The ﬁndings of studies that have examined the effect of coffee consumption on mortality risk
have varied with time, reﬂecting advances in epidemiologic methodology and changes in
habits associated with coffee drinking. Recent studies agree that there is an inverse association
between coffee consumption and all-cause mortality, due mainly to lower mortality due to
CVD, particularly CHD. On the other hand, overall mortality due to cancer is not signiﬁcantly
associated with coffee consumption.
