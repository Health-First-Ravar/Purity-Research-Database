Abstract
Background and aim: In northern Sweden, consumption of both ﬁltered and boiled
coffee is common. Boiled coffee, especially popular in rural areas, is known to raise blood
lipids, a risk factor for acute myocardial infarction (MI). To our knowledge, only one epidemi-
ological study, a case-control study from Sweden, has investigated boiled coffee in MI, noting
an increased risk at high consumption levels in men, and no association in women. The aim of
the present nested case-referent study was to relate consumption of ﬁltered and boiled coffee
to the risk of ﬁrst MI.
Methods and results: The study subjects were 375 cases (303 men, 72 women) and 1293
matched referents from the population-based Northern Sweden Health and Disease Study.
Coffee consumption was assessed by food frequency questionnaire. Risk estimates were calcu-
lated by conditional logistic regression. A statistically signiﬁcant positive association was found
between consumption of ﬁltered coffee and MI risk in men [odds ratio for consumption 4
times/day versus 1 time/day 1.73 (95% CI 1.05e2.84)]. In women, a similar association was
observed, but for boiled coffee [odds ratio 2.51 (95% CI 1.08e5.86)]. After adjustment for

Introduction
According
to
ofﬁcial
statistics,
the
average
coffee
consumption in Sweden is more than 3 cups/day [1].
Despite the high consumption, the role of coffee in
cardiovascular health is unclear. A number of in vitro,
animal, and epidemiologic studies have addressed the
topic, but results have been mixed [2], and with the
exception of studies of cholesterol [3], brewing method has
rarely been considered.
The main components of coffee that have been sug-
gested to inﬂuence cardiovascular disease risk include
caffeine, diterpenes (primarily kahweol and cafestol), and
polyphenols. Findings for caffeine, the stimulant in coffee,
tend to support only weak negative effects on cardiovas-
cular health [4], with putative mechanisms including
increased blood pressure [5] and arterial stiffness [6]. A
possible cholesterol-raising effect of caffeine was not
supported by a meta-analysis of randomised controlled
clinical trials [3], but the hypothesis is still debated [7]. In
contrast to caffeine, the diterpenes in coffee increase
serum cholesterol levels [8]. The polyphenols have been
shown to have an antioxidant effect [2], which might have
a preventative effect in cardiovascular disease. In fact,
coffee has been reported to be the predominant dietary
source of antioxidants in some western countries [2].
Although the caffeine and polyphenol concentrations in
coffee are largely independent of brewing method, the
diterpene concentration is substantially affected. Filtered
coffee, the most common coffee in northern Sweden, is
prepared by running boiling water through a ﬁlter con-
taining ﬁnely ground roasted coffee beans. It has a rela-
tively low concentration of diterpenes [8], due to a lower
temperature at the contact between coffee ground and
water, and because the ﬁlter acts as a barrier for the
diterpenes. Boiled coffee, on the other hand, is prepared
by heating a mixture of coarsely ground coffee and water to
a boil, resulting in a high diterpene concentration [8].
Consistent with the higher diterpene levels, the choles-
terol-raising effect of boiled coffee is also greater than that
of ﬁltered coffee [9e11]. Historically, the consumption of
boiled coffee was widespread in Sweden, but it is now
consumed primarily in rural areas. Boiled coffee is also
associated with an outdoor lifestyle in Sweden, either
occupational (such as reindeer-herding Samis) or recrea-
tional (e.g. ﬁshing, hunting, hiking). At present, 40% of the
coffee sold in northern Sweden is boiled [1].
The
ﬁndings
of
epidemiologic
studies
of
coffee
consumption and cardiovascular endpoints, such as MI,
cardiovascular mortality and stroke, have been equivocal
[12e19]. In a recent meta-analysis, coffee consumption
was not associated with an increased risk of coronary heart
disease; prospective studies yielded mixed and uncertain
results [15]. Other recent prospective studies support an
inverse association with cardiovascular disease risk, even at
high consumption levels, in both women and men [16,17]
and in type II diabetics [18]. A possible acute effect of
coffee consumption has also been suggested, by which
events resulting from the consequent activation of the
sympathetic nervous system might trigger an MI [20]. Sex-
speciﬁc associations with cardiovascular risk have been
observed for coffee [21,22], as for some other cardiovas-
cular risk factors [23].
To our knowledge, only one previous study, a retrospec-
tive case-control study from Sweden, has considered
brewing method in an analysis of MI risk. Positive associa-
tions were reported between both ﬁltered and boiled
coffee intakes and MI risk in men, and null associations
were observed in women [22].
In the present prospective study from the population-
based Northern Sweden Health and Disease Study, we
aimed to investigate whether consumption of ﬁltered and
boiled coffee is associated with the risk of a ﬁrst MI in men
and women.
Methods
Study design and setting
This was a nested case-referent study from the two pop-
ulation-based cohorts of the Northern Sweden Health and
Disease Study (NSHDS) in which dietary intake data are
collected: the Va¨sterbotten Intervention Project (VIP, mean
participation rate 59%) and the northern Sweden WHO
Monitoring of Trends and Cardiovascular Disease study
(MONICA, mean participation rate 77%), both founded in
1985 [24]. In both cohorts, participants are invited to
undergo an extensive health examination and to complete
a lifestyle questionnaire, including a food frequency ques-
tionnaire (FFQ). As of December 31, 1999, when cases of
ﬁrst MI were identiﬁed for the present study, the VIP cohort
had 59 401 participants, and the MONICA cohort had 7215. A
study of selection bias in the VIP found little evidence of
systematic differences in social or demographic charac-
teristics between participants and non-participants [25].
Study population
An overview of the selection procedure for the study
subjects, which resulted in 375 cases and 1293 matched
referents, is shown in Fig. 1. Prospective cases of ﬁrst MI,

Subjects with missing values for any of the coffee items
(15 cases, 40 referents) or with coffee consumption data
that could not be standardized, i.e. some older versions of
the VIP FFQ and the 1990 MONICA FFQ (243 cases, 703
referents), were also excluded from the study. Finally,
incomplete case sets were also excluded (5 cases, 125
referents).
Coffee consumption
The majority of study subjects (259 cases, 972 referents)
had completed a standardized FFQ with two coffee items,
one for ﬁltered and one for boiled coffee. The remaining
116 cases and 321 referents had completed comparable
FFQs from MONICA or some earlier years of the VIP with
a single item for ‘‘coffee’’ and a separate item for ‘‘usual
brewing method’’ (ﬁltered, boiled, percolated, or instant).
For both types of FFQ, the frequency scale included
nine alternatives: never, a few times/year, 1e3 times/
month, 1 time/week, 2e3 times/week, 4e6 times/week,
1 time/day, 2e3 times/day, and 4 times/day.
To allow use of the coffee variables in the comparable
FFQs, percolated coffee (13 cases, 31 referents) and instant
coffee (1 case, 17 referents) were coded as ﬁltered coffee.
Percolating involves a ﬁltering step, instant coffee is
produced from concentrated ﬁltered coffee, and both have
a relatively low diterpene content [8]. For subjects who
had completed the comparable FFQs, coffee consumption
was thus coded as ﬁltered or boiled, with the other type
coded as ‘‘never’’ in subjects with a total consumption of
less than 3e4 times per week (6 cases, 14 referents), and
missing in subjects with a total consumption of at least 3e4
times per week (110 cases, 307 referents).
Over 20 000 VIP participants have repeated surveys at
10-year intervals. In the present study, participants from
1992/2002 and 1993/2003 (the earliest years for which the
coffee items in the two FFQs were comparable, 5058
participants) were used to assess intra-individual temporal
changes in coffee consumption.
Dietary intake
Nutrient intake data were available for 265 cases and 990
referents. The remaining 110 cases and 303 referents had
completed an older version of the VIP FFQ, in which the
coffee items were identical to the later versions, but for
which
nutrient
syntax
is
not
available.
For
these
subjects, only intakes of fruit and vegetables, alcohol,
and wine were determined. The majority of subjects
with nutrient intake data (232 cases and 873 referents)
had completed a validated 84-item FFQ [27], and 33
cases and 117 referents had completed a shorter version
of the same FFQ, with 65 questions. Nutrient intakes
were
calculated
as
described
elsewhere
[28].
For
consideration as potential confounders in the risk anal-
yses, all nutrients except alcohol were energy adjusted
using the residuals method.

Results
The median age of the cases at recruitment to the cohort
was 54 years. Follow-up times between recruitment and
ﬁrst MI diagnosis ranged from 0 to 12 years, with a median
of 3 years. Cases had higher levels of BMI, triglycerides,
cholesterol, apoB/apoA-I ratio, blood pressure than the
referents, and also demonstrated a stronger cardiovascular
risk pattern with respect to diabetes, current smoking,
lack of postsecondary academic education, and sedentary
lifestyle
(data not
shown).
Dietary factors,
including
macronutrients, fruits and vegetables, and selected vita-
mins, did not differ signiﬁcantly between cases and
referents, with the exception of a slightly higher wine
consumption in both male and female referents (data not
shown).

Discussion
The main ﬁnding of this nested case-referent study was an
increased risk of ﬁrst MI in men consuming ﬁltered coffee. A
similar tendency was observed in women consuming boiled
coffee 4 or more times per day, but the result was not
statistically signiﬁcant in multivariate analyses taking into
account established cardiovascular risk factors. To our
knowledge, this is the ﬁrst prospective study to address the
role of boiled coffee in cardiovascular disease.
Given the well-established cholesterol-raising effect of
boiled coffee [9e11], it is surprising that ﬁltered, but not
boiled coffee, increased the risk of ﬁrst MI in men. One
possible explanation is residual confounding by other life-
style factors not assessed, or not sufﬁciently assessed, by
the questionnaire employed in this study. If boiled coffee
increases
cardiovascular
risk
only
at
high
levels
of
consumption, then the low number of subjects in the
highest category (35 male cases) may have contributed to
the lack of association. For boiled coffee, the lowest
category of consumption (up to once per day) comprised
nearly two thirds of the study subjects, but using a lower
cut-off to deﬁne the reference category did not materially
affect the results.
In women, the results for boiled coffee suggested an
increased risk of ﬁrst MI in high consumers, but only in
univariate analyses. In contrast to men, ﬁltered coffee was
not associated with MI risk in women. Thus, the possibility
of sex-speciﬁc effects for coffee cannot be excluded
[21,22].
Discerning separate risk associations for ﬁltered and
boiled coffee is difﬁcult, due to relatively high colinearity.
However, mutual adjustment for the two coffee types did
not affect the results in men. In women, the risk estimates
were affected by mutual adjustment, but low power
rendered the analyses unstable.
Categorization of the coffee consumption data was
based on the frequency distribution of the coffee variables
and the range of consumption captured by the FFQ. We
chose to maintain the highest category (4 times/day),
despite low numbers of study subjects for some analyses,
because several studies have supported a threshold effect
at high consumption levels [2,13e15,22,29e32]. Combining
ﬁltered and boiled coffee had only a modest effect on the
numbers of high consumers, and total coffee consumption
was not associated with the risk of ﬁrst MI.
The food frequency questionnaires in the present study
had an occasions/day scale. Although the more common
cups/day scale might have been preferable, neither takes
into consideration the portion size or strength/concentra-
tion of the coffee consumed. Other types of coffee than
ﬁltered,
boiled,
percolated,
and
instant
were
not
addressed in the questionnaire. The questionnaire may,
therefore, not reﬂect the evolving coffee culture in
Sweden, with an increasing variety of coffees and brewing
methods available (e.g. espresso, decaffeinated coffee).
However, given their low consumption in Sweden during the
recruitment period for the study, this was probably not
a major problem. For example, in 1985e1999, espresso
accounted for less than 0.5% of the total coffee sold [1].

Altered coffee consumption habits during the follow-up
period between data collection and the diagnosis of cases
with MI could affect the risk relationships. Based on our
analysis of VIP participants with repeated health surveys 10
years apart, the consumption of ﬁltered coffee was quite
stable, whereas boiled coffee consumption decreased
somewhat with increasing age. Although not possible in the
present study, it would be interesting to assess the risk of
MI in persons with a stable, high consumption of boiled
coffee over the long term.
The number of ﬁrst MI cases included in this study was
rather small given the size of the NSHDS. This was primarily
duetothe useofseveral non-standardized versionsofthefood
frequency questionnaire in the early years of the VIP and
MONICA, which resulted in considerable exclusions. Since
cases and referents were matched for cohort and recruitment
date, bias due to these exclusions is unlikely. We chose to
present results for women, despite the low power, for the
purpose of future meta-analyses and systematic reviews.
The main strength of the study was the prospective design.
Anthropometric, lifestyle, and biological data were collected
up to 12 years prior to the MI diagnosis of cases. This minimizes
the risk of selection bias, recall bias, and reverse causation.
The MI diagnoses were veriﬁed according to stringent WHO
MONICA criteria, and the referents were individually matched
to their index case for sex, age, geographical region, and date
of recruitment to the cohort. Furthermore, the extensive
baseline data available for the study subjects allowed us to
test for a number of potential confounders.
In conclusion, in this nested case-referent study from
a population-based cohort, consumption of ﬁltered coffee
was positively associated with the risk of a ﬁrst acute
myocardial infarction in men. A similar tendency was
observed in women consuming boiled coffee 4 or more times
per day, but the result was not statistically signiﬁcant in
multivariate analyses taking into account cardiovascular risk
factors. Further investigation in a larger study is warranted.
