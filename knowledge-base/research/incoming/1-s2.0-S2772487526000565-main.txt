A B S T R A C T
Background: To investigate the association of caffeinated and decaffeinated coffee consumption with the risk of 
diabetes mellitus and gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM).
Methods: This is a cross-sectional study included 6311 women(1313 with diabetes mellitus, 574 with GDM) using 
NHANES data (2007-2018). Logistic regression models were used to evaluate the associations between coffee/ 
caffeine intake and diabetes mellitus/GDM risk. Subgroup analyses were done to test results robustness.
Results: Higher caffeinated coffee intake was associated with a decreased risk of diabetes mellitus, with ORs of 
0.80 (95%CI, 0.66-0.99) for ≤1 cup/day, 0.73 (95%CI, 0.60-0.88) for 1-2 cups/day, 0.86 (95%CI, 0.69-1.07) for 
2-3 cups/day, and 0.74 (95%CI, 0.57-0.96) for >3 cups/day. There were dose-response associations of caffeine 
consumption and the risk of diabetes mellitus. Stratified analyses highlighted obvious relationships of higher 
caffeine intake with decreased diabetes mellitus in individuals with a BMI ≥30 kg/m2 and those with lower 
physical activity levels. No significant association was found between coffee intake and GDM risk.
Conclusions: Coffee consumption, particularly caffeinated coffee, is associated with a decreased risk of diabetes 
mellitus but not with GDM. These findings suggest potential metabolic benefits of coffee.
1. Background
As one of the most popular beverages globally, coffee is rich in 
bioactive compounds and has attracted substantial attention for its po­
tential health benefits [1]. Population-based studies have shown that 
individuals consuming 3-5 cups of coffee daily have the lowest risk of 
cardiovascular diseases (CVD) [2,3]. Habitual coffee consumption is 
dose-dependently related to decreased risk of type 2 diabetes mellitus 
(T2DM) [4,5]—specifically, each additional daily cup of coffee corre­
lates with a 4% lower T2DM risk [6]. Such metabolic benefits are pri­
marily mediated by caffeine intake [7], as caffeine improves energy 
balance, higher plasma caffeine concentrations are predictive of reduced 
risks of obesity and CVD [8], for caffeine enhances the process of 
cholesterol clearance [9]. For this reason, studies exploring the 
metabolic benefits of coffee consumption should stratify their analyses 
by coffee type (i.e., caffeinated vs. decaffeinated)—a methodological 
step that has been underutilized in previous studies.
Diabetes mellitus is a chronic metabolic disorder caused by defects in 
insulin secretion, insulin action, or both, and is clinically characterized 
by hyperglycemia. Type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) accounts for over 
90% of DM cases [10]; it represents a global health challenge with a 
rapidly rising prevalence, posing a threat to millions of individuals 
across diverse populations. The association between coffee consumption 
and diabetes risk remains inconsistent, with conclusions varying 
significantly based on the study population or the type of coffee exam­
ined [11–13]. Additionally, glucose intolerance that first emerges during 
pregnancy—termed gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM)—is another 
health concern [14]. As a result of impaired glucose metabolism caused

This cross-sectional study utilized data from the National Health and 
Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) between 2007 and 2018. We 
aimed to develop a comprehensive comparative framework to assess the 
differential associations of caffeinated versus decaffeinated coffee con­
sumption on risks of diabetes mellitus and GDM among women with 
reproductive history. Additionally, we specifically examined whether 
the associations between coffee/caffeine consumption and diabetes 
mellitus diagnosis varied by age, body mass index, and physical activity 
levels.
2. Methods
2.1. Data source and participants
Data were obtained from the National Health and Nutrition Exami­
nation Survey (NHANES) spanning 2007–2018. NHANES employs a 
stratified, multistage probability sampling design to ensure national 
representativeness. The survey incorporates in-person interviews, stan­
dardized physical examinations, and laboratory analyses (https://www. 
cdc.gov/nchs/nhanes/index.html). Study eligibility criteria are detailed 
in Fig. 1. Briefly, we excluded participants aged <20 years, those 
without a pregnancy history, individuals lacking diabetes mellitus/GDM 
data, and those with incomplete coffee consumption or covariates in­
formation. The final analytical sample comprised 6311 individuals, 
including 1313 with diabetes mellitus and 574 with GDM.
2.2. Coffee consumption
Coffee consumption obtained from dietary data using two-day 24h 
dietary recalls in NHANES [16]. All participants completed one dietary 
recall by in-person interview when they underwent physical examina­
tion. A second dietary interview was performed 3-10 days later. Coffee 
intake was identified through United States Department of Agriculture 
(USDA) food code descriptions encompassing all beverages containing 
'coffee', including both caffeinated and decaffeinated varieties. Daily 
coffee consumption was calculated as the mean of the two recalls. For 
analysis, consumption was standardized to 6-ounce cups and catego­
rized (none, 0-1, 1-2, 2-3, or >3 cups/day). Caffeine intake from all 
sources was quantified in milligrams using the USDA Food and Nutrition 
Database for Dietary Studies and then stratified by quintile.
2.3. Diagnosis of diabetes mellitus and GDM
Diabetes mellitus diagnosis was derived from personal interview 
data on diabetes and laboratory data. In personal interview, individuals 
were asked for ever been told by a doctor or health professional that you 
have diabetes or sugar diabetes (DIQ010). Individuals who answered 
'yes' or 'borderline' were identified as diabetes mellitus. Additionally, 
individuals who took insulin or diabetic pills were also identified as 
having diabetes mellitus. According to laboratory data, individuals who 
with an HbA1c level ≥6.5%, fasting plasma glucose (FPG) ≥126 mg/dL 
(7.0 mmol/L), or 2-h postprandial glucose (2hPG) ≥200 mg/dL (11.1 
mmol/L) were classified as having diabetes mellitus. GDM was identi­
fied through a reproductive health questionnaire (RHQ162). Partici­
pants were asked, 'During pregnancy, were you ever told by a doctor or 
other health professional that you had diabetes, sugar diabetes, or 
gestational diabetes? (do not include diabetes that you may have known 
about before the pregnancy) '. Individuals who answered 'yes' or 
'borderline' were defined as having GDM.
3. Covariates
We included predetermined covariates based on clinical relevance, 
including age, education (less than 9th grade, 9-11th grade, ≥high 
school), BMI (<18.5, 18.5-24.9, 25-29.9, ≥30 kg/m2),race/ethnicity 
(Mexican American, other Hispanic, non-Hispanic white, non-Hispanic 
black, and other race), marital status (married/cohabiting vs unmar­
ried), family poverty-income ratio (PIR, an economic indicator of family 
income relative to the federal poverty threshold [<1.3 (low), ≥1.3 and 
< 3.5 (intermediate), and ≥3.5 (high)]), smoking status (never, former, 
and current), alcohol consumption (yes, no), physical activity level 
(inactive, moderate, vigorous, and active), total energy intake (contin­
uous, calories) and chronic diseases (yes, no). Individuals were classified 
as smokers if they had smoked at least 100 cigarettes in their lifetime, 
with further categorization into current/former smokers.
3.1. Statistical analysis
All data analyses in this study took into account the complex sam­
pling design of NHANES and utilized weighted statistical methods to 
ensure the representativeness and robustness of the finding. Specifically, 
we applied the provided sampling weights (WTMEC2YR), pseudo- 
stratum (SDMVSTRA), and pseudo-cluster (SDMVPSU) in all analyses 
to account for the study's complex design. Categorical variables were 
summarized as counts and percentages (%), while continuous variables 
were expressed as means ± standard errors (SE). Descriptive analyses 
were stratified by cups categories of total coffee intake, and differences 
in characteristics were assessed using one-way ANOVA or Chi-squared

4. Results
This study included 6311 women, comprising 1313 with diabetes 
mellitus and 574 with GDM. Table 1 presents baseline characteristics 
stratified by coffee consumption levels. The no-intake group was 
significantly younger (mean, 48 years) compared to coffee-consuming 
groups (mean, range of 53-55 years; p < 0.001). Racial composition 
varied significantly across groups (p < 0.001), with Non-Hispanic White 
participants predominating, particularly in higher consumption cate­
gories (>3 cups/day). Most participants had completed high school or 
higher education (79.8%-87.9%; p < 0.001) and reported inactive 
physical activity levels (57.2%-65.6%; p < 0.001). We also present 
corresponding weighted population number and percentage in the 
Supplementary Table S1.
As shown in Table 2, coffee consumption patterns (total intake, 
caffeinated coffee, decaffeinated coffee, and caffeine intake quintile) 
differed significantly between participants with and without diabetes 
mellitus (all p < 0.05). In contrast, no significant differences were 
observed for GDM status overall, except for decaffeinated coffee 
consumption (p < 0.05) (Table 2). We present more detailed distribution 
of coffee consumption in Supplementary Table S2.
Logistic regression analysis revealed that higher total coffee con­
sumption (>3 cups/day) was associated with a reduced risk of diabetes 
mellitus (OR = 0.74; 95% CI: 0.57-0.94; P = 0.015) (Fig. 2, Table S3). 
However, this association became marginally non-significant after full 
covariates adjustment (OR = 0.76; 95% CI: 0.57-1.02; P = 0.069). 
Stratified analyses by coffee type showed risk reduction only for 
caffeinated coffee consumption. Compared to non-consumers, the 
adjusted ORs for diabetes mellitus across increasing caffeinated coffee 
intake categories (≤1, 1-2, 2-3, >3 cups/day) were 0.80 (95% CI: 0.66- 
0.99; P = 0.037), 0.73 (95% CI: 0.60-0.88; P < 0.001), 0.86 (95% CI: 
0.69-1.07; P = 0.171), and 0.74 (95% CI: 0.57-0.96; P = 0.026), 
respectively. No significant associations were observed for decaffeinated 
coffee.
When examining caffeine intake by quintile (reference: Q1 [0-6 mg/ 
day]), higher consumption levels showed progressively reduced dia­
betes mellitus risk: Q2 (6-94 mg/day) OR = 0.81 (95% CI: 0.66-0.99); 
Q3 (94-142 mg/day) OR = 0.71 (95% CI: 0.57-0.87); Q4 (143-239 
mg/day) OR = 0.73 (95% CI: 0.59-0.91); Q5 (≥240 mg/day) OR = 0.72 
(95% CI: 0.57-0.90); all P < 0.05. Trend analysis demonstrated a sig­
nificant dose response relationship between caffeine intake and the risk 
of diabetes mellitus (P < 0.001).
No statistically significant associations were observed between GDM 
risk and total coffee intake or caffeinated coffee consumption. For 
decaffeinated coffee, a insignificant trend toward reduced GDM risk was 
observed in the ≤1 cup (OR: 1.01; 95% CI: 0.73–1.39) and 1–2 cups (OR: 
1.11; 95% CI: 0.83–1.49) groups. Similarly, when stratified by caffeine 
intake quintile, no significant association was observed between caffeine 
consumption and GDM risk (Fig. 3, Table S3).

5. Discussion
Our findings suggest that daily consumption of ≥3 cups of coffee, 
particularly caffeinated coffee, was associated with reduced diabetes 
mellitus risk. A dose-response relationship was observed for caffeine 
intake (by quintile), with higher intake associated with progressively 
lower diabetes mellitus risk. No significant associations were found 
between coffee/caffeine consumption and GDM risk, suggesting a po­
tential protective effect specific to diabetes mellitus.
As the primary bioactive compound in coffee, caffeine mediates 
metabolic benefits through multiple pathways: enhancing insulin 
sensitivity [17], improving glucose uptake in peripheral tissues [18], 
stimulating the sympathetic nervous system to increase energy expen­
diture [19,20], and promoting fat oxidation [21], while other coffee 
components (polyphenols, magnesium, potassium) contribute antioxi­
dant and anti-inflammatory effects [3]. Our findings demonstrate a 
dose-response relationship between both caffeine intake and caffeinated 
coffee consumption with decreased diabetes mellitus risk. Notably, this 
protective association was absent for decaffeinated coffee, further 
underscoring caffeine's decisive role in coffee's health benefits.
Our study found no association between coffee/caffeine intake and 
GDM risk. The pathophysiology of GDM differs fundamentally from 
diabetes mellitus. GDM results primarily from pregnancy-induced hor­
monal changes (particularly placental hormones) that cause transient 
insulin resistance and impaired insulin secretion. In contrast, diabetes 
mellitus is a chronic metabolic disorder characterized by persistent in­
sulin resistance, β-cell dysfunction, and frequent metabolic comorbid­
ities like obesity [22,23]. We hypothesize that the underlying 
mechanisms could involve complex physiological changes driven by 
hormonal fluctuations during pregnancy, which may modulate the ef­
fects of caffeine [14]. These changes lead to alterations in metabolic 
status, contributing to the transient metabolic abnormality observed in 
GDM. It's commonly recognized that the protective effects of caffeine 
against diabetes mellitus are mainly achieved through improving insulin 
sensitivity, increasing energy expenditure, and reducing fat accumula­
tion [1,3,21,24]. The findings on association caffeinated coffee intake 
with decreased risk of diabetes mellitus substantiate it in obesity group.

mitigate potential risks to both maternal and fetal health.
Stratified analyses showed that similar association of coffee and 
caffeine with diabetes mellitus in individuals with BMI ≥30 kg/m2 and 
those with lower physical activity. Obesity and sedentary lifestyles are 
linked to chronic low-grade inflammation and increased oxidative 
stress, both of which contribute to insulin resistance [29]. In these 
vulnerable individuals, the bioactive compounds in coffee, particularly 
caffeine, appear to have more pronounced effects. Caffeine enhances 
insulin sensitivity by modulating glucose metabolism and improving 
energy balance [30]. It stimulates lipolysis in adipose tissue, leading to 
increased free fatty acid release and subsequent oxidation [31], which 
helps maintain energy homeostasis. This is especially beneficial for in­
dividuals with obesity and sedentary lifestyles, where metabolic flexi­
bility is often compromised. Caffeine's antioxidant properties may also 
help mitigate oxidative stress [32], further supporting metabolic health. 
Moreover, caffeine's psychostimulant effects can enhance alertness and 
motivation, potentially encouraging individuals with low physical ac­
tivity levels to engage in more movement throughout the day. It can 
improve metabolic health by enhancing energy expenditure and pro­
moting better glucose regulation.

A major strength of our study is the large-scale national represen­
tative sample enhancing the generalization of our findings. Additionally, 
the NHANES covered a wide ages range of women of reproductive age, 
which providing an opportunity to investigate the associations of coffee 
intake with both diabetes mellitus and GDM. The specified type of coffee 
was inquired in the dietary recall review, which also allowed us to 
examine whether coffee intake was associated with diabetes mellitus 
varied by caffeinated coffee intake and decaffeinated coffee intake. 
Several limitations of the study warrant acknowledgment. First, the 
reliance on self-reported coffee consumption data may introduce biases. 
Additionally, due to the nature of public database research, we could not 
account for the impact of different coffee preparation methods on the 
observed associations, while different brewing techniques can signifi­
cantly influence the composition of bioactive compounds in coffee [3,
33]. Second, as this is a cross-sectional study, it is inherently limited in 
its ability to infer causality and remains susceptible to reverse causality. 
In future studies, we propose to use a longitudinal cohort for follow-up. 
Third, although our analyses adjusted for a range of potential con­
founders, residual confounding cannot be entirely ruled out. Lastly, the 
absence of detailed diagnostic information prevented differentiation 
between diabetes subtypes (type 1 diabetes, type 2 diabetes, latent 
autoimmune diabetes in adults, etc.), potentially introducing misclas­
sification bias. However, given that type 2 diabetes constitutes the ma­
jority of diabetes cases, we believe that the findings still provide 
valuable insights for type 2 diabetes. Future research should take into 
account subtypes of diabetes to enhance precision of the results.
6. Conclusions
In conclusion, our study suggested different associations pattern of 
coffee consumption with risk of diabetes mellitus and GDM. The asso­
ciations were also varied by the types of coffee. Overall, our study found 
that coffee consumption was associated with decreased risk of diabetes 
mellitus, particularly caffeinated coffee intake. Given the relative small 
sample size of GDM in the study population, the results should be 
interpreted with caution. Further research is warranted to validate these 
findings 
and 
to 
explore 
the 
underlying 
mechanisms 
more 
comprehensively.
