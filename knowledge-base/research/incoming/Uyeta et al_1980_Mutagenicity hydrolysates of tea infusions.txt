MUTAGENICITY OF HYDROLYSATES OF TEA INFUSIONS 
MASAHIKO UYETA, SAKAE TAUE and MASANORI MAZAKI 
Kochi Prefectural Public Health Laboratory, 2-4-1, Marunouchi, Kochi 780 (Japan) 
(Received 1 July 1980) 
(Revision received 9 September 1980) 
(Accepted 23 September 1980) 
Summary 
Hydrolysates of infusions of green tea and black tea were mutagenic by the 
Ames test. The tea infusions were prepared by a regular method and were hy- 
drolysed with 1 M HC1, hesperidinase, naringinase and human intestinal bac- 
teria. The hydrolysates were successively extracted with chloroform, diethyl 
ether and ethyl acetate. The mutagenicities of the extracts were assayed by the 
Ames test, and the mutagens were analysed by GC-MS. 
The ether extracts of the hydrolysates of infusions of both green tea and 
black tea were mutagenic to TA98 and TA100 with or without $9 mix. The 
most common mutagenic flavonols, kaempferol, quercetin and myricetin, were 
detected in the extract by GC-MS, and their combined mutagenicity repre- 
sented about 70% of the whole mutagenic activity in the extract. 
Wynder and Gori (1977) have estimated that over half of all female cancer 
deaths and 40% of all male cancer deaths may be related to food factors. Sim- 
ilar estimates have been presented by Cairns (1975) and Hirayama (1977). 
However, little attention had been paid to carcinogens--mutagens in food until 
Sugimura and coworkers made a new development in the study of mutagens in 
ordinary daily foods (Sugimura et al., 1977a, b; Nagao et al., 1977a). 
Mutagenicity of coffee, green tea and black tea had already been reported 
(Nagao et al., 1979), but the mutagenic principles have not yet been charac- 
terized. In this study we show that tea infusion hydrolysates obtained with 
acid, enzymes and human faecal bacteria were mutagenic, and that most of the 
mutagenic activity was attributable to flavonols contained in the tea. 
Abbreviation : GC-MS, gas chromatography and mass spectrometry.

Materials and methods 
Preparation of tea infusion. Japanese green tea leaves and black tea leaves 
were commercial products in Japan. The tea infusions were prepared by four 
procedures as follows. (1) 80 g of tea leaves were added to 500 ml water at 
60°C for 2 min and the solution was filtered through a glass-fibre filter, for 
green tea "Gyokuro". (2) 10 g of tea leaves were added to 500 ml water at 90°C, 
and in 1 min filtered, for green tea "Sencha". (3) 10 g of tea leaves were added 
to 500 ml boiling water, and in half a minute filtered, for green tea "Bancha". 
(4) In the test of black tea, 5 g of tea leaves were added to 500 ml of boiling 
water for 3 min and then filtered. These procedures were made in the same way 
as for making tea in Japanese daily life. 
Hydrolysis of tea infusion. Acid hydrolysis. Tea infusion was made in 1 M 
HC1 and heated on a steam bath for 1 h. 
Enzymic hydrolysis. Hesperidinase was obtained from Tanabe Chem. Co. 
Ltd., Osaka, Japan. Naringinase was purchased from Sigma Chem. Co., St. 
Louis, MO. A 100-ml tea infusion was incubated with each g of enzyme at 
pH 4 in the water-bath at 40°C over-night. 
Bacterial hydrolysis. About 0.1 g of fresh human faeces was pre-cultured in 
10 ml Brain Heart Infusion Medium (Difco Laboratories, Detroit, MI)at 37°C 
over-night. The 2 ml of resulting pre-culture was inoculated into 100 ml of tea 
infusion medium (tea infusion obtained from "Sencha" containing 0.5% tryp- 
tone and 0.5% yeast extract) and incubated anaerobically in a Gaspak Anaerobic 
System (BBL, Cockeysville, U.S.A.) at 37°C for up to 5 days. 
Extraction of mutagens from hydrolysates. Each hydrolysed infusion was 
saturated with sodium chloride and acidified to pH 3 with hydrochloric acid, 
then was extracted with 3 portions of chloroform and was successively extract- 
ed with diethyl ether and ethyl acetate in the same manner. Each solvent layer 
was evaporated to dryness under reduced pressure, and the residue was dis- 
solved in dimethylsulphoxide (DMSO) or pyridine, and was subjected to muta- 
genicity assay or determination of the mutagens, resp. 
Kaempferol, quercetin and myricetin were each added, at 20 pg/ml, to a tea 
infusion hydrolysate. Recoveries were 82.1, 85.0 and 80.5%, resp., from the 
ether extracts. 
Mutation test. Salmonella typhimurium TA98 and TA100 were used. Muta- 
genicity was tested by the pre-incubation method (Nagao et al., 1977b) which 
is a modification of the method of Ames et al. (1975). The 0.1-ml DMSO solu- 
tion of an extract of tea infusion hydrolysate was incubated with 0.5 ml of 
phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) or with $9 mix and 0.1 ml of bacterial culture at 
37°C for 20 min. Then 2 ml of soft agar was added, and the mixture was over- 
laid on minimal glucose agar. After incubation for 2 days, colonies of histidine 
prototrophs were counted. $9 mix was prepared as described by Ames et al. 
(1975). 
Identification and quantitative determination of the mutagens by GC-MS. 
A 100-pl of pyridine solution of extract from a hydrolysate was added to the 
same volume of bistrimethylsflyl trifiuoroacetamide (Wako Pure Chem. Ind. 
Ltd., Osaka, Japan) and was heated at ll0°C for 15 min in a sealed vial. The 
resulting trimethyl silyl derivatives were directly injected into a Shimadzu

Model 6020 Gas Chromatograph-Mass Spectrometer with an ionizing energy 
of 20 eV and ion source temperature of 250°C. The instrument was equipped 
with a gas~chromatographic column packed with 3% OV-17 on Gaschrom Q 
80--100 mesh (Applied Science Labs. Inc., Inglewood, CA). Authentic flavo- 
nols, kaempferol and myricetin, were purchased from Aldrich Chem. Co. Inc., 
Milwaukee, WI, and quercetin from E. Merck, Darmstadt, Germany. These 
flavonols in the extract of the hydrolysates were quantitatively determined by 
using the mass fragrnentography.

Appearance of mutagenicity in tea infusion hydrolysed with human intesti- 
nal bacteria. The ether extract of tea infusion (obtained from "Sencha") that 
was incubated with human faecal bacteria was also mutagenic to TA98 and 
TA100 with or without $9 mix. Some of the results are shown in Fig. 1. Muta- 
genicity appeared after one day of incubation, but the maximal activity

sample, as shown at A and B in Fig. 1. The activity correlated with the appear- 
ance of mutagenic flavonols, kaempferol, quercetin and myricetin, in the incu- 
bation medium.

Discussion 
Nagao et al. (1979) have already reported that coffee and tea contained two 
types of mutagen, the one acting directly on TA100 and inactivated by rat liver 
$9 mix, and the other detected after hesperidinase treatment. They also report- 
ed that, because tea leaves contain flavonoid glycosides, the mutagenicity 
exhibited after hesperidinase treatment is probably due to flavonoids. Brown 

239 
and Dietrich (1979) found that the expression of mutagenicity of flavonol 
glycosides occurs not only by mixed glucosidases but also by rat caecal bacteria 
or other sources. 
In this work, we found that mutagenicity exhibited in tea infusion after 
treatment with mixed glucosidase such as hesperidinase and naringinase was 
due to flavonols such as kaempferol, quercetin and myricetin, and that tea 
infusion also had mutagenic activity after treatment with human intestinal bac- 
teria. 
Tea leaves and tea infusion are well known to contain flavonoids, and muta- 
genic activities have been reported for these compounds (Bjeldanes and Chang, 
1977; Sugimura et al., 1977; MacGregor and Jurd, 1978; Brown and Dietrich, 
1979). Many flavonoids are readily hydrolysed by some inorganic acids and 
fi-glucosidase, and their aglycones are usually extractable with solvents such as 
diethyl ether or ethyl acetate (Markham, 1975). In the present experiments, 
the tea infusion was hydrolysed with hydrochloric acid, hesperidinase, naringin- 
ase (the latter two containing fi-glucosidase activity) and human faecal bacteria, 
and each hydrolysate was extracted with chloroform first, then successively 
with ether and ethyl acetate. 
The chloroform extract of a hydrolysate of tea infusion contained great 
amounts of caffeine and plant colouring materials, etc., but showed no muta- 
genic activity by the Ames test. The ether extract, on which the GC-MS test 
was made, contained pyrogallol, catechin gallate and 3 flavonols, kaempferol, 
quercetin and myricetin, and the extract was mutagenic to TA98 and TA100. 
The ethyl acetate extract had more polar materials such as gallic acid, epicate- 
chin, epigaUocatechin and gallocatechin, etc., and it showed no mutagenicity. 
The ether extract of hydrolysates with acid, enzymes and faecal bacteria had 
similar mutagenic activities on TA98 and TA100 with or without $9 mix in 
spite of some difference in the hydrolysis method, as shown in Table 2. 
"Gyokuro" is the highest brand of green tea, and accounts for 5% of total 
production of green tea in Japan. To make tea of best taste, the appropriate 
temperature of the hot water is 55--60°C. "Sencha" is in most common use, 
the production rate being 80%. The appropriate temperature for its infusion 
is 80--90 ° C. "Bancha" is the green tea of low price, and the total production 
is 15%. To make tea from it requires boiling water. Black tea of good quality 
it not produced in Japan; it is mostly imported. 
In these experiments, all the samples of green tea and black tea were pre- 
pared in the ordinary way as described above, Therefore, we have not tested 
the difference in appearance of mutagenic activity resulting from various brew- 
ing methods different from the usual way of making tea in Japan. The water- 
soluble materials in each tea infusion were 27.2 mg/ml for "Gyokuro", 4.0 fo~ 
"Sencha", 2.1 for "Bancha" and 3.5 for black tea for the samples shown in 
Table 1. It was presumed that those amounts of water-soluble materials corre- 
sponded to the mutagenic activity in the hydrolysate of each tea infusion. 
Mutagenicity was not detected in either extract of unhydrolysed tea infu- 
sion, but mutagenicity of hydrolysates was observed in the ether extract. It was 
assumed that the lack of mutagenicity in unhydrolysed tea was due to the fol- 
lowing: the flavonols exist as glycosides in tea infusion, and the flavonol 
glycosides themselves do not show mutagenic activity and are not extracted 

240 
either with solvents such as chloroform, ether or ethyl acetate. Therefore, 
because no mutagenic activity was detected in the extract of unhydrolysed tea 
infusion, it is suggested that hydrolysed tea infusion had mutagenic activity 
originating from mutagenic flavonols as the aglycone of glycosides. 
Work on the carcinogenicity of flavonoids that have recently been found to 
be mutagenic is presently in progress (Brown, 1980). It would be interesting to 
know about the carcinogenicity of flavonoids, because flavonol glycosides are 
found in edible portions of the majority of plants, e.g. citrus and other fruits, 
berries, leafy vegetables, roots, spices, legumes, cereal grains and teas (Herr- 
mann, 1976;.Brown and Dietrich, 1979; Brown, 1980). The estimated daily 
intake of flavonoids in the average American diet amounts to about 1 g 
(Brown, 1980). These mutagenic flavonoids in natural foods must have existed 
throughout human history, and human beings have long been exposed to them. 
Thorough research will be required to elucidate what this means in terms of 
public health.
