How Does Roasting Process Inﬂuence
the Retention of Coffee Aroma Compounds
by Lyophilized Coffee Extract?
I. L´OPEZ-GALILEA, I. ANDRIOT, M. P. DE PE˜NA, C. CID, AND E. GUICHARD
ABSTRACT: The aims of this study were (1) to study the effect of lyophilized coffee extract on the retention of aroma
compounds and (2) to study if aroma compounds selected are differently affected by the lyophilized coffee extracts
obtained from conventional and Torrefacto coffee brews prepared by filter coffeemaker and by espresso coffee ma-
chine. Variable amounts of lyophilized coffee extracts, relative to coffee powder, containing different percentages of
high molecular weight compounds, mainly melanoidins (value given in parentheses), were obtained: 20.9% (14.8)
and 24.9% (23.3), respectively, for conventional and Torrefacto coffee brew prepared by filter coffeemaker and 18.1%
(18.8) and 20.7% (57.5), respectively, for conventional and Torrefacto coffee brew prepared by espresso coffee ma-
chine. The retention of aroma compounds increased by increasing the lyophilized coffee extract concentration
and was found to be dependent on the aroma compounds. The retention of aroma compounds was found to be
slightly different depending on the brewing procedure employed, showing lyophilized coffee extracts obtained with
espresso coffee machine had higher retention values that those extracted by filter coffeemaker. Retention capacity
of lyophilized coffee extracts obtained from the conventional and the Torrefacto roasted coffee did not show differ-
ences except in the case of ethyl nonanoate.
Keywords: aroma compounds, brewing procedure, coffee, high molecular weight compounds, Torrefacto
Introduction
F
ood aroma is one of the most important characteristics, es-
pecially in the case of coffee brew, for the acceptance of
food products by consumers. Perceived intensity of aroma com-
pounds is not only dependent on the physical properties of the
components in consideration (nature, volatility, concentration,
and so on) but it also depends on the composition of the food
matrix (Guichard 2002). Especially interactions between flavor
compounds and biopolymers have been widely studied. Proteins
(Andriot and others 2000), phenolic compounds (King and Solms
1982), carbohydrates (Jouquand and others 2006), or lipids
(Piraprez and others 1998) could change the flavor release profile
and thus could be responsible for a decrease in the intensity of
the flavor perception. In the case of coffee brew, nonvolatile com-
pounds are mainly composed of melanoidins, which are formed
in the last stage of the Maillard reactions between reducing car-
bohydrates and amino compounds. Beyond their nutritional, bi-
ological, and health implications (Delgado-Andrade and Morales
2005), which have made melanoidins a subject of growing interest,
they play an important role in coffee aroma compounds. First, they
are involved in the aroma development during the roasting pro-
cess (Ciori 2002); second, they are some of the responsible com-
ponents for aroma degradation in coffee brews (Mayer and others
2000); and finally, they play a nonnegligible role in the retention of
coffee volatile compounds. Hofmann and others (2001, 2002) sug-

Charles-Bernard and others (2003, 2005) confirmed these results

On the other hand, the coffee roasting process is highly observed
as a determining factor in high molecular weight (HMW) com-
pound development. The increase of the roasting degree results
mainly in an increase of the HMW compound content (Delgado-
Andrade and others 2005). Moreover, Andriot and others (2004) ob-
served a decrease in esters retention by a lyophilized coffee extract
(LCE), including melanoidins, by increasing the coffee-roasting de-
gree. Charles-Bernard and others (2005) showed an increase in the
magnitude of the interactions of some volatile compounds with the
dark coffee brew as compared to the light one. Ottinger and Hof-
mann (2000) observed how melanoidin spectrum was significantly
affected by the roasting process.
In spite of the results that showed the key role of the coffee
roasting process in melanoidin development, the Torrefacto roast-
ing process (with added sugar) that is used in several countries
of southern Europe and South America has not been studied so
far. However, brewing procedure could also have an influence on
the amount and type of compounds extracted. The effects of the
different brewing procedures on the antioxidant properties of cof-
fee brews (S´anchez-Gonzalez and others 2005) or in flavor profile
(Rocha and others 2003; L´opez-Galilea and others 2006) have al-
ready been assessed, which can induce different retention capaci-
ties of the corresponding coffee extracts.

Materials and Methods
Volatile compounds
Volatile compounds used in this study were 2,3-pentanedione,
hexanal, octanal, furfuryl methylsulfide, ethyl nonanoate, and
2-acetylpyrrole (Aldrich, Saint Quentin Fallavier, France), 2-ethyl-
3,5(6)-dimethylpyrazine (Acros organic, Noisy le Grand, France),
and guaiacol (Intl. Flavor and Fragance, Longvic, France). Physic-

Table 1. Aroma solution was prepared daily in an acetate buffer
(sodium acetate solution, 6.8 g/L), adjusted to pH 5.3 correspond-
ing to the pH of coffee previously measured. After preliminary
analyses taking into account the quantification by GC-FID, the
final concentration of volatile compounds was the following: 2,
3-pentanedione, hexanal, octanal, furfuryl methylsulfide, and ethyl
nonanoate: 10 μL/L; 2-ethyl-3,5(6)-dimethylpyrazine and guaiacol
60 μL/L, and 2-acetylpyrrole 200 mg/L. Purity of compounds was
previously analyzed by GC coupled to a flame ionization detector
(> 90%) and is reported in Table 1.

Coffee samples
Two commercial roasted coffee samples of the same brand were
purchased from a local market: a conventional roasted coffee blend
arabica/robusta and a 100% Torrefacto roasted coffee robusta vari-
ety as whole beans that was ground, for 40 s, using a home grinder
(model Moulinex 980 26-F). The roasting process conditions are
shown in Table 2. Espresso coffee brew was prepared from 42 g
of ground roasted coffee for a volume of approximately 1200 mL
of water using an espresso coffee machine (model Saeco Aroma,
Italy). Fixed espresso coffee preparation conditions were followed
using, in the espresso machine pump, a pressure equal to 15 bar a
temperature of 90 to 96 ◦C, and an extraction time of 4 min. Filter
coffee brew was prepared from 42 g of ground roasted coffee for a
volume of approximately 1200 mL using a filter coffeemaker (model
KF 147 aroma Select, Braun) equipped with a Melitta paper filter
(Cofresco Ib´erica S.A., Spain). The extraction time was of 7 min at
a temperature of 90 ◦C. The same quantity of coffee was used for
the 2 brewing procedures in order to eliminate the influence of the
quantity of coffee.
Coffee extract isolation
Coffee samples obtained as described previously were ex-
tracted with dichloromethane (2 × 200 mL) according to Hof-
mann and others (2001). Defatted solutions were deposited in
small aluminum containers and then frozen at –20 ◦C for 1 night.
Freeze-drying was performed by FTS Systems Inc. (N.Y., U.S.A.).

Aroma retention measurement
Analyses were done in triplicate in flasks (20 mL) closed with a
silicone rubber Teflon cap. For each sample to be tested, a blank
aroma sample (2 mL aroma solution + 2 mL buffer) and a test sam-
ple (2 mL aroma solution + 2 mL lyophilized coffee extract solution)
were compared. Different equilibrium times were tested (Figure 1).
After 90 min, peak areas of aroma compounds remained stable.
Thus, each vial was equilibrated for 90 min in a thermostatic bath
at 60 ◦C (usual coffee brew consumption temperature) just before
measurements. Both static headspace (S-HS) and headspace cou-
pled with solid phase micro extraction (HS-SPME) analyses were
automated using a MPS2 multipurpose sampler (Gerstel Applica-
tions, Brielle, the Netherlands).
S-HS analysis. Injections of vapor phase (1 mL) were made
on TRACE gas chromatograph ultra (Thermo Electron Corp.)
equipped with a FID detector and a DB-Wax column (J&W Scien-
tific, i.d. 0.32 mm, 30 m, film thickness = 0.5 μm). The temperature
program was from 40 to 240 ◦C at 3 ◦C/min rate. Injector and de-
tector temperatures were, respectively, 240 and 250 ◦C. Helium was
used as carrier gas with a linear velocity of 27 cm/s at 143 ◦C.
HS-SPME-GC analysis. As already reported, the extraction ef-
ficiency of the SPME methods depends on several factors such as
the nature of the coating of the SPME fiber and the trapping time.
On the basis of previous studies, the SPME fiber Stableflex 2 cm-
50/30 μm DVB/CAR/PDMS (Supelco Co., Bellefonte, Pa., U.S.A.)
was chosen because its 3-component composition gives high re-
coveries for analytes with different structures and polarities se-
lected in the present study (Roberts and others 2000; Bichi and oth-
ers 2002). Equilibrium time and quantities of sample were the same
as in S-HS analysis. An absorption time of 90 s was chosen so as
not to saturate the fiber. The final concentrations of volatile com-
pounds were chosen to be in the linear range of the SPME fiber
and at a ppm level for compounds alone and in mixture. After ex-
traction, the SPME fiber containing the headspace volatile com-
pounds was immediately inserted into the GC injector set at 250 ◦C
for 10 min, 1 min for desorption, and 9 min for cleaning. Chro-
matographic conditions were the same as those described for S-HS
analysis.
For HS-GC and SPME-HS-GC, we verify that we are in the
linearity range of the detector for the concentration of aroma
compounds.

Color analysis
Color analysis was carried out on ground roasted coffee using a
tristimulus colorimeter (Chromameter-2 CR-300, Minolta, Osaka,
Japan). The instrument was standardized on a white tile before
each sample measurement. The color parameters corresponding to
the uniform color space CIELAB (1986) were obtained directly from
the apparatus. Within the approximately uniform space CIELAB, 2
chromaticity coordinates a∗and b∗as well as lightness L∗are de-
fined. Coordinate a∗takes positive values for reddish colors and
negative values for greenish ones whereas b∗takes positive val-
ues for yellowish colors and negative values for bluish ones. L∗pa-
rameter is an approximate measurement of lightness taking values
within the range of 0 (black) and 100 (white).
Statistical analysis
Results obtained are given as percentage of aroma compounds
retention by lyophilized coffee extract in comparison with blank
aroma solution. The global effect of coffee roasting process and
lyophilized coffee concentration on the retention of aroma com-
pounds was analyzed by a 3-way analysis of variance (ANOVA). Stu-
dent’s t-test was applied to color data with a level of significance
of 99% and retention mean. All statistical analyses were performed
with the Statgraphics Plus 4.1 software.
Results and Discussion
T
he color parameters of the ground coffee samples are reported
in Table 2. Conventional and Torrefacto coffee samples present
statistical differences in L∗, a∗, and b∗parameters showing lower
values in all cases for the conventional coffee. Nicoli and others
(1997) also observed a decrease in L∗, a∗, and b∗parameters with
increasing roasting process, which confirms that Torrefacto cof-
fee was subjected to a less intense roasting process than the con-
ventional one. This lower temperature is applied in the Torrefacto
roasting process to avoid an excess of burnt caramel flavor.
Variable amounts of LCE were obtained: all details concerning
LCE are reported in Table 2. Higher percentages of HMW com-
pounds were obtained in the Torrefacto coffee brews than in the
conventional ones. It could be explained because the addition
of sugar in the Torrefacto roasting process has a great effect on
the development of the Maillard reactions and consequently in
the formation of HMW compounds such as melanoidins. More-
over, the roasting time was approximately 3 times longer for Tor-
refacto coffee than conventional coffee, which could also explain
the higher percentages of HMW compounds for Torrefacto coffee.
With regard to the brewing procedure, the espresso coffee machine
showed higher efficiency extracting HMW compounds than the fil-

To reproduce the large variability of chemical compounds
present in the coffee brew aroma, 8 volatile compounds were se-
lected for testing. Six of the 8 aroma compounds were selected
for their important contribution to coffee brew aroma (L´opez-
Galilea and others 2006): 1 diketone (2,3-pentanedione), 2 straight
chain aldehydes (hexanal and octanal), a sulphur compound (fur-
furyl methylsulfide), 1 pyrazine (2-ethyl,3-5(6)-dimethylpyrazine),
and 1 phenolic compound (guaiacol). Moreover the retention of
2,3-pentanedione, guaiacol, and 2-ethyl-3,6-dim´ethylpyrazine was
also studied by Charles-Bernard and others (2003). We add 2-
acetylpyrrole for the class of pyrroles, because Yanagimoto and oth-
ers (2002) show that this compound has the more important impact
of antioxidant potential. Ethyl nonanoate was also tested in com-
parison with previous study (Andriot and others 2004). As quan-
tification of guaiacol and 2-acetylpyrrole was not possible using
S-HS, they were quantified by HS-SPME. The amount of aroma
compounds released in the vapor phase was measured above blank
aroma solutions and above test solution (that is, LCE + aroma solu-
tion). The results were given as percentage of aroma compound re-
tention in comparison with blank aroma solutions in acetate buffer.
Percentages of retention were calculated using both techniques
(S-HS and HS-SMPE) for furfuryl methylsulfide and the results are
not significantly different (results not shown).
A 3-way ANOVA was performed to establish (1) the influence
of brewing procedure, (2) the impact of the coffee roasting pro-
cess, and (3) that of the LCE concentration on the retention of
aroma compounds. Statistical results are reported in Table 3. In
most cases, significant interactions between roasting procedure,
lyophilized coffee concentrations, and brewing procedure have
been observed, but with a lower F value than the individual effects.
The concentration effect is highly significant for all compounds ex-
cept for guaiacol. The brewing procedure effect is highly significant
for all compounds except for 2-acetylpyrrole. Finally, the roasting
process effect is weaker than the other effects and is only signif-
icant for 3 compounds: octanal, 2-ethyl-3,5(6)-dimethylpyrazine,
and guaiacol. The effect of each parameter is described in more de-
tail in the following paragraphs.

Conclusions
I
n this article, 2 analytical methods (S-HS and HS-SPME) were
employed to study the effect of the lyophilized coffee extracts,
containing melanoidins, from conventional and Torrefacto coffee
brews brewed by filter coffeemaker and espresso coffee machine on
the retention of aroma compounds. The obtained results allow us to
conclude that the retention of aroma compounds depends on the
roasting procedure and especially on the lyophilized coffee extract
concentration and on the brewing procedure used. The retention
of aroma compounds increased by increasing the lyophilized coffee
extract concentration and was found to be dependent on the aroma
compounds. As Hofmann and others (2001) already reported, that
the composition of melanoidins influences the retention of some
aroma compounds, we supposed that these differences may be due
