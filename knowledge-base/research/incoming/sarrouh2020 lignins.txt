Abstract 
Lignin has aroused great interest in the scientific and economic areas because of its aromatic and complex nature. It can be 
used as a raw material for obtaining aromatic aldehydes but the great potential of lignin is not satisfactorily utilized. The 
interest is justified by the fact that, when its structure is spun off through pre-treatment processes, it produces some phenolic 
compounds of important industrial interest. These are bioactive compounds that can be used to promote benefits to human 
health, such as reducing the incidence of degenerative diseases like cancer and diabetes, as antioxidant, ant mutagenic activi-
ties, anti allergic, anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial effects. The present work aimed to the extraction and identification 
by UPLC-MS of biomolecules found in alkaline lignin hydrolysate from coffee husk. These compounds were classified 
as terpenes with relevant biological activities which make them interesting for pharmaceutical and cosmetic industries as 
discussed in literature.

Statement of Novelty
Lignin is known for its complex aromatic structure. It con-
fers rigidity, impermeability and resistance to microbiologi-
cal and mechanical attacks on plant tissues. In bioprocesses 
involving the conversion of lignocellulosic biomass, such as 
coffee husk, into high value-added products, lignin hydro-
lysate (also known as, effluent, liquor or extract) is gener-
ated. This work aimed to valorize this hydrolysate through 
solvent extraction of different biomolecules of pharmaceuti-
cal interest. These biomolecules were identified by UPLC-
MS as terpenes with important biological activities for 
human health, as discussed in literature.
Introduction
Lignin and Pretreatment Methods
Lignin is known to be a three-dimensional biopolymer with 
polyphenolic structure and also for being the second most 
abundant polymer in nature, after cellulose [1]. It is an amor-
phous, recalcitrant and water insoluble substance of high 
molecular weight and complex chemical structure consisting 
of an aromatic system composed of phenylpropane units, 
which is covalently bound to cellulose and other polymers 
of the plant cell wall [2]. Figure 1 shows, in a simplified way, 
the structure of a eucalyptus lignin.
In the biomass, the structure of lignin is linked to hemi-
cellulose and cellulose by covalent and non-covalent bond-
ing, forming the lignocellulosic biomass. The side chains 
and hemicellulose branches interact with the cellulose, 
providing stability, elasticity and flexibility to the structure. 
Thus, the hemicellulose acts as a bridge between the cel-
lulose and lignin, since their side chains allow interaction 
between them [3]. The combination of the components of 
lignocellulosic material provides high resistance to chemi-
cal, enzymatic and microbial attacks. Thus, a pretreatment 
is needed to access the biomass to be facilitated [4].
In second generation ethanol industry, delignification in 
alkaline medium is designed to remove the lignin and make 
more accessible cellulose structure to enzymatic action, 
aimed at producing glucose and subsequent conversion to 
ethanol [5]. On the other hand, phenol, guaiacol and syrin-
gol units are mainly present in the complex structure of the 
more or less fragmented lignin [6]. Rabelo [7] points out that 
many researchers believe that the action of alkaline solutions

Biomolecules Found in Lignin Extract
In plants, the phenolic compounds are extremely important, 
in view of play important roles in the growth and repro-
duction of plants, since protect them against predators and 
pathogens. The phenols are known to represent the largest 
class of phytochemicals agents, i.e. compounds not nutrients 
but which possess biological activities of interest to living 
beings as antioxidant activities, antiallergic, antimicrobial, 
antithrombotic, anti-inflammatory, hypocholesterolemic, 
cardioprotective and vasodilatory [10]. These attributes 
allow the neutralization or scavenging free radicals and the 
chelation of metals, acting in the initiation stage and further 
oxidation process [11]. Phenolic compounds can be clas-
sified into different groups because of the large number of 
phenol rings, and also how these rings are linked together. 
In view of this, there are three main groups: phenolic acids, 
flavonoids and tannins [12].
The Flavonoids are the largest groups of free phenols in 
plants (Fig. 4). They are part of plant defense mechanism 
and are synthesized from phenylpropanoids and acetate. 
These are precursors to certain groups of substances as ali-
phatic amino acids, terpenoids, fatty acids, among others 
[13]. They have activity against allergies, hypertension, 
viral diseases, inflammation, arthritis, mutations and car-
cinogenesis, cancer and AIDS [14].
Tannins are considered to be associated with extracts 
the lignocellulose fibers are in the peel of the vegetables, 
roots, leaves, sap and seeds [15]. They are classified into 
two groups: hydrolysable and condensed tannins [15, 16]. 
The hydrolysable tannins have ester carbonyl bonds that 
are easily broken under acidic and basic conditions giv-
ing rise to carbohydrate and phenolic acids [17]. They are 
often used in pharmaceutical, given its antiviral, antibacte-
rial, and antihepatotóxic, by the inhibition of xanthine oxi-
dase and monoamine oxidase and inhibition of glycosyl-
transferase [18]. The present work aimed to the extraction 
and identification by UPLC-MS of biomolecules found in 
alkaline lignin hydrolysate from coffee husk.
Material and Methods
Coffee Husk
For the extraction assays coffee husk "in natura" was col-
lected from Santa Clara farm, located in the municipality 
of Oliveira, Minas Gerias State, Brazil (20° 41′ 48.2″ S 
44° 49′ 42.0″ W). The farm cultivates mainly “Arabica 
coffee” species. Such species represents 70% of Brazilian 
total coffee production. Husks were ground in a household 
blender and the particles sizes were standardized using a 
sieve (Bertel Indústria Metalúrgica LTDA) with a 1.70 mm 
(12 mesh) aperture.

Reagents
The following reagents and materials were used for the 
execution of the experimental assays in this work. Concen-
trated sulphuric acid (99%) and ethil acetate (A.C.S. grade) 
were purchased from Impex/Brazil. Ultra pure water (Mil-
lipore), sodium hydroxide 98% (Sigma-Aldrich), methanol 
and formic acid (HPLC grade, Sigma-Aldrich/Brazil). Hex-
ane (A.C.S. grade, Dinâmica Química/Brazil). Kieselgel S 
(0.063–0.2 mm, Riedel-de Haën/Brazil).
Alkaline Hydrolysis of Lignin Fraction from Coffee 
Husk
For the alkaline hydrolysis of lignin fraction from coffee 
husk, a solution of sodium hydroxide 10% (w/v) was pre-
pared using distilled water. Subsequently, a mixture of 5.0 g 
of coffee husk (dry basis) and 50 ml of the alkaline solution 
was added in a 125 ml erlenmeyer flask. Solid/liquid ratio 
was set at 1:10 in all hydrolysis tests performed. Next, the 
erlenmeyeres were autoclaved (CS, Prismatec Autoclaves) 
at 120 °C for 60 min. The samples were then filtered using 
filter paper (Qualy—Φ 12.5 cm) and stored in glass tubes at 
4 °C for posterior analysis [19].
Solid Phase Extraction of Biomolecules from Lignin 
Alkaline Hydrolysate
Solid phase extraction (SFE) is considered to be a simple; 
quick; have a low cost; providing extracts that are relatively 
free from interfering, have high recoveries with good accu-
racy and precision for the analytes of interest [20]. For the 
extraction of biomolecules found in alkaline lignin hydro-
lysate, sulfuric acid (99%) was added until soluble lignin 
complete precipitation. Samples were filtered using Cellu-
lose Ester Membrane (CEM), with 0.45 µm porosity and 
47 mm diameter (APROLAB). The filtered solution was then 
separated in a silica column (0.063–0.2 mm). Where, 1.0 g 
of the filtered solution was administrated to a 40 cm sílica 
packed glass column (Kieselgel S). The elution occurred 
using 5.0 ml of methanol, 1.0 ml of ethyl acetate and 1.0 ml 
of hexane. The extracts of each solvent were collected for 
posterior identification [20].
Ultra Pressure Liquid Chromatography Coupled 
to Mass Spectrometer
For the identification of the molecular mass of the extracted 
biomolecules and alkaline hydrolysate, the samples were 
injected in an H-Class UPLC SM-FTN Waters (Milford, 
Massachusetts, EUA) equipped with a mass spectrometer 
(MS) Waters Xevo G2-XS QTOF (ESI-QTOF), A col-
umn Cortecs T3 (2.1, 100 mm × 1.8 µm) was used for the 
separation of the studied biomolecules. The mobile phases 
used were: (A) water with formic acid 0.1% (v/v), and (B) 
methanol with formic acid 0.1% (v/v). The gradient was pro-
grammed as follow: solvent A:solvent B, 99:1 for 0.5 min, 
60:40 from 0.5 min until 8 min, 1:99 from 8.0 min until 
11.0 min and 99:1 until 15.0 min. Other chromatographic 
conditions were 0.3 ml min−1 of mobile phase flow rate and 
1.0 μl of injection volume. The MS parameters were set as 
follows: negative and positive ionization mode, aquisition 
MSE Continuum, m/z 50–1200, LE 6 eV, HE 10–30 eV, 
resolution mode 30,000 FWHM and scan rate 5 Hz.
Results and Discussion
Phenylpropanoids are aromatic compounds consisting of a 
phenylpropane structural core found in several essential oils. 
These compounds can have 5 carbon atoms (C) named as 
hemiterpenes, 10C (monoterpenes), 15C (sesquiterpenes), 
20C (diterpenes), 30C (triterpenes) and 40C (tetraterpenes). 
They possess a complex structure in which organic functions 
such as ester, ether, ketone, hydrocarbons and alcohols can 
be present. Such compounds may be isolated from all parts 
of plants, but mainly from seeds and/or flowers by steam 
distillation or solvent extraction [21].
In this work, lignin liquor was obtained by submitting 
coffee husk to an alkaline hydrolysis reaction. The obtained 
hydrolysate was then used in an extraction process using 
solvents with different polarities. For this reason, four sol-
vents: water, methanol, ethyl acetate and hexane were chosen 
to be employed in liquid–solid extraction, since they can 
cover a wide range of polarity. The lignin solid content was 
found to be in the order of 11% (w/v) in both the alkaline 
hydrolysate and the extracted liquor. Data obtained from sol-
vent extraction and ultra HPLC identification assays showed 
that a total of twelve biomolecules (Table 1), were isolated 
using the four studied solvents (water and methanol as polar 
solvents, ethyl acetate as a solvent with intermediate polar-
ity and hexane as a nonpolar solvent). These biomolecules 
exhibited polarity-conferring organic functions such as alco-
hols, ethers, esters, as well as nonpolar functions such as 
benzene ring and hydrocarbons, according to their chemi-
cal structure, as shown in Table 1. The fact that the studied 
biomolecules can be obtained in both polar and nonpolar 
solvents is considered interesting from technical and eco-
nomical point of view, for further purification studies and 
high scale production.
The natural occurrence and biological activities of the 
identified biomolecules (Table 1) were evaluated according 
to various published studies. Examples of these cases are 
presented hereafter.

Morus, a plant genus from the family of Moraceae, is used

bisbenzylisoquinoline alkaloid-tetrandrine. Tetrandrine can

Conclusions
Lignin is of great interest in both academic and industrial 
segments due to its aromatic composition with antioxidant 
activities. This interest is due mainly to the fact that lignin 
extracts can be used as a raw material for obtaining bioactive 
molecules, important for human welfare. Alkaline hydrol-
ysis has been an effective technique for the extraction of 
lignin serum, which has been characterized in this work for 
containing different bioactive molecules of pharmaceutical 
interest. It was observed that terpenes originated from coffee 
husk lignin extracts possess biological activities important 
for human health, as discussed in literature.
