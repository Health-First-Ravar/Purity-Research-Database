Effect of the post-harvest processing procedure on OTA occurrence
in artificially contaminated coffee

Abstract
The purpose of this work was to study how the type of post-harvest process, i.e. natural preparation known as the dry
method, and two wet processes, affected contamination and toxin production up to the green coffee stage. Batches were
contaminated with ochratoxin A or with OTA-producing strains of Aspergillus ochraceus and Aspergillus niger. For OTA
artificial contamination, hulling or husk removal caused a reduction of OTA. When A. ochraceus was inoculated at low level, its
growth was hampered by indigenous mould flora contrary that observed with A. niger. The fungal counts and OTA assays
showed that the best way of limiting the development and impact of contaminating toxigenic flora bfrom the fieldQ was the
physical wet method.

1. Introduction
Post-harvest processing of coffee cherries is
carried out in producing countries using two
processes. The dry method consists of a natural
drying stage (in the sun) or an artificial drying stage,
followed by mechanical dehulling. In the wet
method, cherries are pulped, which eliminates the
most of the pulp. The mucilage removal is tradition-
ally performed by natural fermentation for varying
lengths of time depending on climatic conditions and
fruit ripeness. Lastly, beans are dried and this step is
followed by a mechanical dehulling (Barel and
Jacquet, 1994; Avallone et al., 2001). Mechanical
mucilage removal from coffee has been developed in
some countries, following ecological and economic
pressures requiring a reduction in water consumption
and in organic matter discharges (Mendez et al.,
2001).

Ochratoxin A (OTA) is a secondary metabolite
produced by some moulds of the genera Aspergillus
and Penicillium. OTA was reported to have neph-
rotoxic, immunotoxic, teratogenic and carcinogenic
effects (Ho¨ler, 1998; Pfohl-Leszkowicz and Casteg-
naro, 1999). Studies on OTA presence in coffee
(Levi, 1980; Studer-Rohr et al., 1994; Nakajima et
al., 1997; Leoni et al., 2000; Romani et al., 2000;
Urbano et al., 2001), its possible carcinogenic role
in man and the project to set a European Union-
wide limit for coffee have made it necessary to be
able to detect possible sources of contamination
throughout the commodity chain. There are primar-
ily three OTA producing moulds, Aspergillus niger,
Aspergillus carbonarius and Aspergillus ochraceus
(Frank, 2001; Joosten et al., 2001). Even though
several studies have shown a relation between the
frequency and toxigenic potential of these moulds
depending on coffee post-harvest processes, the
mechanisms by which coffee is contaminated by
toxigenic strains are not yet clear (Mantle, 2002).
Understanding under what conditions OTA is
produced could help in mastering the processes
and reducing the incidence of OTA contamination in
the field.
Ochratoxin can be due to cherries contamination
bin the fieldQ by toxigenic moulds, with toxin
production occurring before or after harvesting. These
OTA producing strains can remain during or after the
process resulting in potential hazard. Presence of
ochratoxin may also be due to recontamination. It has
been shown that OTA is primarily formed in the pulp
and husk when cherries are dried in the sun (Frank,
2001). OTA concentration in green coffee beans has
been found to be up to 100 times lower than in the
husk, which suggests the hypothesis of surface
contamination (Bucheli and Taniwaki, 2002) and a
reduction in the existence of OTA producing strains
through the removal of external layers (Frank, 1999).
Data published on the impact of the wet method on
OTA presence are limited. In 1999, Frank observed
that there was usually a reduction in the number of
filamentous fungi during fermentation, to the benefit
of yeasts (Frank, 1999). On the other hand, it was
assumed that when the pulp was removed, an
important substrate for the development of OTA
producing strains was also eliminated (Joosten et al.,
2001).
The purpose of this work was to study how the
type of process affected mould occurrence and toxi-
genesis up to the green coffee stage, using artificial
contamination by OTA or by OTA producing strains.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Coffee origin and sampling
Batches of Coffea arabica cherries used in this
study were obtained from the Coatepec region,
Veracruz state, Mexico (2001–2002 harvest). Fruit
picking and sorting were carried out by hand to obtain
uniform ripeness.
2.2. Mould strains studied
Two ochratoxin A producing strains were used: A.
ochraceus Wilhelm (MULC 44640) and A. niger van
Tieghem (MULC 44639). They were isolated from
green coffee (C. arabica from Coatepec region).
These strains, that are representative of the behaviour
of the blocalQ species, were deposited in the BCCMk/
MULC Culture Collection (Leuven Catholic Univer-
sity, B-1348 Louvain-la-Neuve, Belgium). Their OTA
production was checked on the FDA reference
medium (Tournas et al., 1995) and on coffee contain-
ing medium (Mantle and Chow, 2000) (results not
shown).
2.3. Inoculum preparation
A. ochraceus and A. niger were grown on Potato
Dextrose Agar (PDA) (Biokar, Beauvais, France),
pH 3.5 at 25 8C for 5 days. Spores were collected
by scraping in a 0.01% solution of Tween 80. After
counting with a hemacytometer and adjustment with
0.01% Tween 80, 10 ml of the suspension (8107
conidia/ml) were used to inoculate 40 g of coffee
cherries. In some of the experiments, an initial
batch of 40 g of coffee cherries was inoculated
directly, and another two batches after either
disinfection with chlorine (soaking for 2 min in a
0.4% sodium hypochlorite solution, then rinsing in
sterile water), or sterilization by autoclaving for
20 min at 121 8C. The cultures were incubated at 25 8C
for 8 days.

2.4. Artificial contamination of coffee cherries
2.4.1. OTA contamination
Samples of coffee cherries (40 g) were contami-
nated by 15 min exposure to a 15 ml of water solution
containing 10 Ag of Ochratoxin A (A. ochraceus
OTA, Sigma, St. Quentin, France). These samples
were used to contaminate batches (2 kg) of coffee
cherries, which were then homogenised (by stirring)
for 30 min.
2.4.2. Contamination with toxigenic strains
Batches (2 kg) of coffee cherries were inoculated
with 40 g of contaminated cherries, as indicated
above, either directly or after disinfection. After
homogenization by stirring, the batches were left at
ambient temperature for 48 h prior to processing.
2.5. Post-harvest processes tested in model system:
experimental design
Three post-harvest processes, i.e. natural prepara-
tion, known as the dry method, and two wet methods,
were studied in the laboratory for batches contami-
nated with OTA, or with OTA producing strains.
For the dry method, the coffee cherries were dried
on a tray in the sun to 12% humidity. The resulting
coffee is known as dried cherries. It was dehusked by
hand to obtain natural green coffee beans.
For the wet methods, the skin and most of the
pectin-rich mesocarp was removed mechanically
using a manual disk mill. Then the mucilage still
sticking to the parchment was removed, either by
fermentation for 48 h at ambient temperature, or
physically using sterilised sand, the latter operation
resulting in mucilage removal by abrasion. The coffee
was then washed and oven-dried at 45 8C. The
resulting coffee beans are known as parchment coffee.
Green coffee beans were obtained after manual
parchment removal.
2.6. Microbiological analysis
Cherries and coffee beans were diluted (1:10 w/v)
in 0.1% peptone water and spread-plated onto DG18
agar culture media (Samson, 1991). The results were
expressed in CFU/g. Cherries and coffee beans were
also directly plated on Petri dishes (10 beans per plate)
containing DG18 agar (Hocking and Pitt, 1980;
Guiraud, 1998). The plates were incubated at 25 8C
for 5 to 7 days, the results being then expressed as a
percentage of infected beans.
2.7. OTA quantification
Coffee samples were analysed for OTA according
to Nakajima et al. (1990). The samples were extracted
with methanol and 3% sodium bicarbonate (50:50),
filtered and cleaned through an immunoaffinity
column (OchraprepR, Rhoˆne Diagnostics, Scotland).
The OTA was quantified by high performance liquid
chromatography (HPLC). The HPLC system con-
sisted of a Shimadzu LC-10ADVP System with
fluorescence detector (Shimadzu Corporation, Japan).
The mobile phase was distilled water/acetonitrile/
glacial acetic acid (51:48:1). The flow rate was 1 ml/
min. OTA was detected by absorption at 333 nm
excitation and 460 nm emission, and a retention time
of 13.3 to 13.5 min.

3.2. Variation in contamination by OTA producing
strains
3.2.1. Inoculation with A. ochraceus
When a batch of crude cherries was directly
inoculated with A. ochraceus, it appeared that the
strain was not properly able to establish in the
medium. This could be explained by the fact that the
number of cherries contaminated at t=0 was not high
enough to detect A. ochraceus, i.e. below the
detection limit (5101 CFU/g) of the method used.
Moreover, an abundant fungal flora was present
(with a majority identified as Penicillium miniolu-
teum) that could be hindered the implantation of A.
ochraceus. Consequently, irrespective of the process
studied, it was never possible to detect neither A.
ochraceus or OTA presence at the various stages
studied.
In order to achieve effective contamination, we
carried out inoculation with cherries treated to limit
the development and/or effect of native flora. An
untreated control did not give effective establish-
ment, but A. ochraceus did develop well on cherries
disinfected with sodium hypochlorite (but mixed in
this case with some other moulds that had resisted
treatment) or heat-sterilised. We thus inoculated 2
kg of untreated coffee cherries with cherries
inoculated after heat-disinfection. Contamination by
A. ochraceus measured at the outset (after 48 h)
was 1.2105 CFU/g.
For the wet methods, pulping did not lead to any
substantial change in contamination (Table 2). After
mucilage removal by fermentation, there was a slight
drop in the flora, whilst after physical mucilage
removal there was a substantial drop, as there only
remained 100 CFU/g. After dehulling, values below
5101 CFU/g were obtained, i.e. the detection limit.
However, a check by direct inoculation revealed 25%
of contaminated beans for parchment coffee and green
coffee produced by fermentation, whilst no detection
was possible on green coffee beans obtained by the
physical method.
For the dry method, dry cherries displayed a
content of under 5101 CFU/g (the method’s detec-
tion limit). The check by direct inoculation of beans
gave 40% infection for dry cherries and 30% for green
coffee beans.
As regards the ochratoxin A content produced
during this experiment (Table 2), it can be seen that
the initial OTA content of the cherries increased in 48
h from 0 to 1.9 Ag/kg. Variation in that content during
processing was substantially different from that
observed during trials involving direct contamination
by OTA.
The wet method with fermentation only revealed a
reduction in the final stage (hulling); the physical
method gave a similar though more favourable result,
as the green coffee obtained only contained traces of

4. Discussion
In this work, we contaminated batches of cherries
coffee using OTA concentrations similar to those that
can be found naturally (5 Ag of OTA/kg of cherries).
Such OTA presence has been reported. Bucheli et al.
(2000) found that cherries were not contaminated with
OTA at the time of harvest, but Taniwaki et al. (2002)
found ripe and overripe cherries contaminated on the
tree but usually with concentrations of b5 Ag/kg.
However, contamination with OTA producing strains
was carried out at high concentrations (1.7F0.1105
CFU/g) to take into account the strong competition
with natural flora observed at the outset of this study.
The presence of OTA producing strains on coffee
cherry was shown (Pitt et al., 2001; Urbano et al.,
2001; Taniwaki et al., 2002) on samples taken from
different regions of Brazil, with infection percentages
varying from 0% to 0.25%; however, it was reported
(Frank, 1999; Bucheli et al., 2000) that fresh cherries
were not subject to Aspergillus proliferation, which
was attributed to the existence of natural microbial
population that protects them from invasion.
Tests involving contamination with OTA revealed
differences between the processes. In the wet meth-
ods, pulping led to a different decrease depending on
whether or not fermentation was used. The greater
decrease with the physical method could be explained
by the removal of an external toxin-rich layer,
whereas the bwetterQ and longer fermentation method
may have favoured toxin penetration (by diffusion)
into the parchment. Hulling then eliminated the toxin
through loss of another bouterQ layer. The increase in
OTA content during the dry method could only be
explained by neosynthesis from toxigenic contami-
nating strains; the duration and conditions of that
process were compatible with development of a
microflora giving rise to toxigenesis (7 days at
25F5 8C). Dehusking removed the toxin with the
outer layer.
This result needs to be critically examined due to
the type of contamination by soaking used, which was
propitious to so-called bsurfaceQ type penetration, and
thereby made mucilage removal and hulling opera-
tions particularly effective. It was for that reason that
contamination carried out with toxigenic strains likely
to produce toxin in situ and therefore at bmore
internalQ levels of the bean made it possible to achieve

evaluations that tallied more with those occurring

Contamination with the two strains studied
revealed substantial differences. A. ochraceus was
unable to establish itself properly in the presence of
the native microbial population, and was therefore
susceptible to competition, which was not the case
with A. niger, which established itself easily. How-
ever, A. niger did not produce toxin as quickly or in
such large quantities as A. ochraceus under our
study conditions. For A. ochraceus, its non-establish-
ment in terms of abundant development does not
mean an absence of risk, as conidia might remain
alive during the process and keep their post-process
development potential. The type of process used
affected the evolution of the population studied, but
overall the results were similar for both strains. As
for OTA, some stages of bouterQ layer removal were
propitious to decontamination: this was clear for
mucilage removal by the wet methods. However,
hulling had little effect, no doubt because of a
dispersion-recontamination effect. The best results
were obtained with the physical wet method that
appears to be the best way of limiting the risk of
OTA production.
In terms of variation in the OTA content resulting
from the fungal contaminations carried out, there were
substantial differences when compared to the results
obtained by direct contamination with OTA. These
differences were evidently linked to our contamina-
tion methodology. Indeed, in this case, mucilage
removal was much less effective, which is compatible
with the fact that the toxin was able to migrate or be
synthesised in deeper zones of the bean, through
mycelium establishment. This was also true for
hulling in the dry method and in the wet method
with fermentation. In the first case, this could also be
explained by the existence of conditions propitious to
toxigenesis, as indicated above. In the second case,
this could be explained by bean wetting, which
promoted diffusional transfer. Lastly, it was confirmed
that the best way to limit the development and impact
of contaminating toxigenic flora from the field
remains the physical wet method.
Little work had previously been undertaken in this
perspective, in particular none on the impact of using
the physical wet method, as differences between the
processes had particularly been considered in terms or
economics, environmental impact and the consequen-
ces for flavour quality (Quintero, 1999; Mendez et al.,
2001). It is clear that bsusceptibilityQ to OTA
production and its health consequences need to be
taken into account in a global discussion on the choice
of process.
It needs to be checked whether the type of post-
harvest process used affects green coffee propensity
for recontamination (or reinfection) by toxigenic
strains during storage, enabling toxin production,
by affecting the intrinsic quality of beans (linked to
their physico-chemical properties). This aspect will
be covered in future work on toxigenesis in green
coffee.
