11.1 Introduction
Proteins are macromolecules made up of one or more linear chains of amino acids organized in unique spatial
structures for each type of protein. The human body uses proteins and amino acids obtained through the diet
for the proper development of different vital functions (Wu et al., 2014). The nutritional quality of proteins
depends on their amino acid profile (essential and nonessential amino acids (EAA)), in addition to other
factors such as digestibility, bioaccessibility, or the presence of antinutritional compounds (Hoffer, 2016).
Taking into account the amount of proteins, different nutrition, and health claims can be applied to food
products. A nutrition claim is any declaration that affirms, suggests, or implies that a food has specific
beneficial nutritional properties due to its energy intake, nutrients, or other substances (Regulation EC 1924,
2006). On the other hand, health claims are those that relate food or one of its components to health (The
European Commission, 2012). Table 11.1 summarizes nutrition and health claims made on foods based on
their protein content.

The recommended daily amount of protein is 0.80 g per kilogram of body weight for both men and women
with modest levels of physical activity (The European Commission, 2020b). Considering the male average
weight of 72 kg and the female average weight of 63 kg, 58 g, and 50 g of proteins should be consumed daily,
respectively. Protein needs of the population can be met by sources of animal protein such as meat, fish, eggs,
or dairy products; or vegetable protein sources such as cereals, grains, and seeds. In the last decade, the
demand for food protein increased, especially animal‐source protein. The consumption of animal‐source
protein is associated with increased greenhouse gas emissions and overutilization of water (Wu et al., 2014).
Therefore, one of the main objectives of the current European Commission's Farm to Fork Strategy, and the

initiatives under the new European Green Deal, is to increase the availability and source of alternative proteins
such as plant, microbial, marine and insect‐based proteins, and meat substitutes to search for new food
sources to replace animal protein (The European Commission, 2020a).
Alternative proteins are estimated to account for 11% ($290bn) of the market by 2035, primarily due to plant‐
based proteins reaching price parity by 2023 with overall continuous growth in global protein market (worth
trillions of dollars) (Watson, 2021). Moreover, plant‐based protein consumption is expected to grow in three
stages; 12, 16, and 7% CAGR (compound annual growth rate) for 2020–2025, 2025–2030, and 2030–2035,
respectively (Watson, 2021). Alternatives are sought to meet the consumer's demands for a healthier diet,
respectful for the environment and animal welfare, and to improve sustainability problems associated with
meat production. These alternatives can be classified into two large groups:
1. Meat substitutes that claim to mimic texture, appearance, and organoleptic properties of meat products.
This group includes foods formulated with plant sources such as soybeans, cereals, or legumes (Sha &
Xiong, 2020); cultured meat produced in vitro using tissue‐engineering technology (Li et al., 2020); or
mycoproteins obtained by fungal fermentation (Hashempour‐Baltork, Khosravi‐Darani, Hosseini, Farshi, &
Reihani, 2020).
2. Protein‐rich foods that are not intended to imitate the texture of meat, such as vegetables, proteins
extracted from algae and microalgae (Torres‐Tiji, Fields, & Mayfield, 2020), proteins isolated from insects
(Jantzen da Silva Lucas, Menegon de Oliveira, da Rocha, & Prentice, 2020), and the revalorization of
proteins from by‐products generated during agro‐industrial processing (Gomes Almeida Sá, Moreno, &
Carciofi, 2020).
Protein extraction from food enables production of concentrates and isolates with high protein content, which
can be used in the preparation of food to improve its nutritional profile, or as a dietary supplement. Milk has
been widely employed to obtain these type of ingredients (Meena, Singh, Panjagari, & Arora, 2017). However, it
is still of animal‐source origin and many consumers may have intolerances or allergies, or do not want to
consume this animal source for ethical or ideological reasons (vegan population). Other natural protein
sources commercially available are:
1. Daily diet vegetables, legumes, and grains. Concentrates and isolates of soy, rice, pea, and hemp proteins
are commercially available for their use as food ingredient. Other plants such as potatoes, quinoa, corn,
lentils, sesame, peanuts, chia seeds, and coffee beans can also be employed to obtain proteins.
2. Insects, algae, and microalgae. Although insects are an animal‐source protein, they have attracted
increasing attention as both human food and animal feed ingredient since they can be grown on low‐value
feeds and have a low carbon footprint (Hawkey, Lopez‐Viso, Brameld, Parr, & Salter, 2021). The Farm to
Fork Strategy also indicates that algae should become an important source of alternative protein for a
sustainable food system and global food security (The European Commission, 2020a).
3. Food waste. Obtaining proteins from food waste minimizes waste disposal, maximizes the resources, and
adds market value to different products. A potential sustainable source of proteins is oil extraction
residues from seeds such as sunflower or pumpkin seeds (Gomes Almeida Sá et al., 2020).
Within the use of food waste as a source of proteins, coffee by‐products may be a potential alternative to
obtain protein enriched‐extracts. Nowadays, there is a concentrated protein powder commercially available
from green coffee beans called CoffeeProtein, and has excellent sensory profile, smooth texture, great capacity
for mixing with different formulations, and is free of allergens such as gluten, lactose, egg, nuts, and soy
(Applied Food Sciences, 2019). However, to the best of our knowledge, information is limited regarding the
feasibility of coffee by‐products as a sustainable source of proteins.
The coffee cherry is composed by an outer skin that covers the pulp (also known as “coffee cascara”’)
followed by a viscous and highly hydrated layer of mucilage, a thin yellowish endocarp, the parchment and,
finally, the silverskin that covers each hemisphere of the green coffee bean (Esquivel & Jiménez, 2012). To
obtain the green coffee bean, cascara, mucilage, and parchment are generated as by‐products. Once the green
coffee bean is obtained, it is roasted and the silverskin is released during the roasting process. Finally, spent
coffee grounds (SCGs) are generated as a by‐product when the coffee beverage is prepared at home or
processed for soluble/instant coffee (Iriondo‐DeHond, Iriondo‐DeHond, & del Castillo, 2020). World
consumption of coffee was projected at 166.3 million 60 kg bags for 2020/2021 (International Coffee
Organization, 2021). The by‐products generated during the whole process account for up to 90% of the dry
weight of the cherry. This large volume of waste, which is a source of contamination and a risk for

environmental sustainability, is composed by a massive number of nutrients and nonnutrients of great interest
in nutrition and health such as proteins (de Melo Pereira et al., 2020; Iriondo‐DeHond, Iriondo‐DeHond, & del
Castillo, 2020). The present chapter is an updated literature review of the potential of green coffee beans and
coffee by‐products as nutritious, safe, and sustainable sources of proteins for human consumption.
11.2 Coffee as an Alternative Protein Source
The green coffee bean and the by‐products generated during the coffee cherry processing present different
protein content (Figure 11.1a).

When the coffee cherry is depulped, 39 kg of cascara are produced from 100 kg of mature coffee cherries
(Ferrão, 2009). Considering that the protein content of coffee cascara is around 9.55% (Iriondo‐DeHond et al.,
2020), this by‐product can be a potential alternative source to animal‐based proteins. On the other hand, the
amount of proteins described in coffee parchment is lower than 1% (Iriondo‐DeHond, Iriondo‐DeHond, & del
Castillo, 2020). Although the amount of coffee parchment produced is similar to that generated by coffee
cascara (Ferrão, 2009), the application of this by‐product as a protein source is not proposed because of its
low protein content.
The green coffee bean has been recently proposed as an alternative plant‐based protein source (Applied Food
Sciences, 2019). Average protein content in C. arabica and Coffea canephora beans is approximately 11.5% dry
basis (Clifford, 1985). Immature fruits at harvest lead to defective green beans that reduce the quality of the
beverage (Mazzafera, 1999). Since the protein content of defective beans is similar to green beans from
mature fruits (Mazzafera, 1999), instead of being discarded, these defective beans can be upcycled as a
sustainable source of proteins.
Protein has been extracted from green coffee (Coffea arabica) beans (12.8% protein) by four different
methods (CP‐I‐CP‐IV); one of them simulating soy glycinin extraction (CP‐I; 40.8% protein) and another
without chlorogenic acid and fat removal (CP‐III; 40.6% protein) (Rawel, Rohn, & Kroll, 2005). CP‐III extraction
resulted in reduced α‐subunit content of the 11S proteins. Protein yield ranged between 7 and 45% with 40–
50% protein content of the extracts, and protein solubility of CP‐I‐CP‐IV was 45, 55, 50 and 98%, respectively,
in deionized water. In addition, all protein extracts exhibited antioxidant activity (5–35 mM Trolox/L).

Mature green coffee beans express maximum amount of the storage (11S globulin) proteins in the presence of
other potential storage proteins (7S‐like, sugar‐dependent 1[SDP1 that encodes a patatin domain], SDP1‐like,
glutelin A2, A3, and patatin 6) (Cheng, Furtado, & Henry, 2018) (Figure 11.2).
Figure 11.2 The 7S globulin‐like (AOA6PWNJ1–1 [LOC113734078]) Coffea arabica protein consists of 440

The 11S endosperm protein of commercial coffee species (Coffea Arabica and Coffea canephora) accounts for
approximately 45% of total grain protein, representing 5–7% of the coffee bean dry weight. The structure,
typical of 11S plant (Brassica napus, Vicia sativa, Oryza sativa, and Cucurbita maxima) storage protein, consists
of α (predominantly hydrophilic) and β (evenly hydrophilic and hydrophobic section) subunits (32 and 20 
kDa, respectively) and 6 polypeptides (80, 52, 32, 22, 16, and 12 kDa) on 2DE SDS‐PAGE (Rogers et al., 1999).
The 11S coffee endosperm protein is encoded by the csp1gene or gene family expression.
Under nonreducing conditions, 11S green coffee storage protein displays two major protein bands (58 and 38 
kDa) and four less abundant bands (170, 150, 16, and ≤ 14 kDa). The 58 kDa fraction reduces into high (acidic
α‐component, ~32–38 kDa) and low (basic β‐isoform, ~ 20–22 kDa) molecular weight subunits (Abdelfatah
Ali, 2013; Rawel et al., 2005). The surface‐exposed α‐chains of the 11S storage protein are the most reactive
and susceptible to covalent reactions with phenolic compounds, whereas the β‐chains located in the inner part
of the 11S protein remain intact (Rawel et al., 2005). The 11S coffee storage protein (300–400 kDa) separates
under nonreducing conditions (SS or urea) into 6 subunit (55–60 kDa) pairs connected by a disulfide link
(Abdelfatah Ali, 2013). Circular dichroism (CD) spectroscopy revealed the predominance of the β‐sheet (28–
39%) conformation in the secondary structure, a molecular feature strongly influencing 11S globulin
functional properties. The contents of α‐helix, β‐turn, and unordered secondary structure were 4.1–7.7, 18.6–
20.3, and 36.2–48.4%, respectively, for C. arabica (Brazil and Guatemala) and C. robusta (Uganda and
Indonesia) (Abdelfatah Ali, 2013). The low α‐helix content indicates high digestibility of the green coffee 11S
protein. Coffee 11S protein is acidic; 33–44% soluble at pH 2–3; and 56–79% at pH 4. The antioxidant capacity
of 11S protein (~3 mM TE/g protein) is due to its strong covalent binding with polyphenols (hydroxycinnamic
acid‐caffeoylquinic acid [CQA]) since it decreases (~1.6 mM TE/g protein) when extracted in the presence of
PVV. 11S coffee protein modification with CQA is responsible for its antioxidant capacity and physicochemical
properties such as emulsion characteristics (emulsifying capacity and stability). CQA alters the structural
(secondary structure α‐helix, β‐strand, β‐turns or unordered) properties of 11S protein modifying its surface
hydrophobicity that is responsible for physicochemical characteristics (thermal stability [DSC], emulsion
characteristics) (Abdelfatah Ali, 2013). The coffee 11S proteins do not behave like soybean glycinin at low ionic
strength (2 mM Tris, pH 8), but rather resembles other 11S proteins that do not dissociate under these
conditions (Acuña et al., 1999).
Green coffee 11S seed storage protein, particularly the α‐subunit, oxidatively fragments during aerobic
incubation (37 °C, 0.02 M sodium borate, pH 8.5 containing trypsin, 48 hr) releasing free amino acids
(Montavon, Duruz, Rumo, & Pratz, 2003). However, the 11S storage protein forms adducts with polyphenols
prior to roasting and the protein subunits integrate into the melanoidins structure formed during roasting

thereby participating in coffee aroma (Montavon, Mauron, & Duruz, 2003). Two‐dimensional mapping of green
beans revealed the expression of 16 and 5 (polypeptides) proteins in C. canephora and C. arabica, respectively.
Moreover, another 8 proteins were upregulated and one suppressed in C. canephora compared to C. arabica
(Gil‐Agusti et al., 2005). Thirty proteins identified by reversed‐phase‐high performance liquid
chromatography‐electrospray ionization‐tandem mass spectrometry RP‐HPLC‐ESI‐MS/MS were differentially
expressed; 3 peptides from proteins including glyceraldehyde‐3‐phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPD) [key
enzyme in intermediary metabolism catalyzing the oxidation and subsequent phosphorylation of substrate
aldehydes to acyl phosphate]; 4 peptides from phospholipid hydroperoxide glutathione peroxidase [protects
cells and enzyme from oxidative damage]; two 11S‐α chain peptides; two11S fragment peptides; one 11S‐β
chain peptide; and two 11S‐ α/β dimer peptides (Gil‐Agusti et al., 2005).
Proteins, peptides, and amino acids participate in coffee aroma production due to their interaction with sugars
in the Maillard reaction during roasting. Thirty peptides (4–10 kDa, pI range primarily within pH 4–6 and 7–9)
have been identified in coffee beans accounting on average for 0.59 and 0.43% of dry mass (5.9 and 4.3 g/kg
dwb) in C. arabica and canephora, respectively. C. canephora has fewer peptides in the 8–10 kDa than C. arabica
(Mazzafera, Schimpl, & Kiyota, 2019). However, peptide content did not differ significantly between five C.
arabica and four C. robusta types, although peptide composition differed significantly (Abdelfatah Ali, 2013).
Peptide composition changed significantly, although peptide content remained unchanged during dry
processing. Five to seven active protease enzymes (~⅓ of the total coffee bean protein) are present in green
coffee and are stable in the intact beans for months because they are bound to arabinogalactans in the cell
wall (Mazzafera et al., 2019).
Other proteins have also been reported in coffee beans. For example, a class III chitinase of C. arabica has
been identified as the first known coffee allergen Cof a 1 (Manavski et al., 2012). The Cof a 1 cDNA encodes a
32 kDA (290 amino acids) C. arabica class III acidic endochitinase with high amino acid similarity to other class
III plant chitinase (concavalin B and hevamine [major latex allergen]), a pathogenesis‐related (PR) protein,
presumably heat stable and therefore not destroyed by roasting green coffee beans and the digesting
procedure. Another allergen in green coffee bean (18–40 kDa; ~12 mg/g roasted ground coffee) is probably a
heterogeneous glycoprotein that can be precipitated at pH 4–4.5. An acid phosphatase and a trypsin inhibitor
are also presumed to be part of the green coffee protein (Spiller, 1998).
By‐products generated during the roasting and brewing process present the highest protein content among all
the coffee by‐products. Protein content in coffee silverskin ranges from 16.2 to 18.6% (Iriondo‐DeHond,
Iriondo‐DeHond, & del Castillo, 2020). Although this by‐product has a high protein content, only 20 kg of
silverskin are produced from 1 ton of green coffee beans (Supracafé S.A., pers. comm.). In contrast, 650 kg of
SCGs are generated from the processing of 1 ton of green coffee beans (Mussatto, Machado, Martins, &
Teixeira, 2011). SCGs contain high amount of proteins, and the values reported by some authors were 6.7–
9.9% (Lago, Antoniassi, & Freitas, 2001), 12.8–16.9% (Cruz et al., 2012), 13.6% (Campos‐Vega, Loarca‐Piña,
Vergara‐Castañeda, & Oomah, 2015), and 11.2% (Martinez‐Saez et al., 2017). Other authors observed that the
mean protein content is 13.6% after soluble coffee preparation (Mussatto, Ballesteros, Martins, & Teixeira,
2011; Silva, Nebra, Machado Silva, & Sanchez, 1998). SCGs protein content exceeds the amount present in
coffee beans due to the concentration of nonextracted components in coffee residues during coffee
preparation (Martinez‐Saez et al., 2017).
Kjeldahl method has been normally used for the determination of protein content in coffee samples. Based on
the interference of other nitrogen‐containing compounds in this protein determination method, protein values
of coffee samples present some limitations. Coffee beans and by‐products contain nonprotein nitrogen
compounds such as caffeine, trigonelline, niacin, purines, pyrimidines, and inorganic nitrogen (Delgado,
Vignoli, Siika‐aho, & Franco, 2008; Martinez‐Saez et al., 2017). In fact, a study (Machado, Costa, Pimentel,
Oliveira, and Alves, 2020) showed that one‐quarter of the total nitrogen of coffee silverskin corresponded to
the nonprotein fraction. Protein content also varies depending on the brewing and roasting processes, when
the proteins can be fragmented, polymerized, and/or integrated into melanoidins (Bravo et al., 2012; Cruz et
al., 2012).
In summary, taking into account the abundance and the protein content of each coffee by‐product, besides
green coffee beans, we propose the upcycling of cascara and SCGs as an alternative source of plant‐based
proteins. The amount of protein in these by‐products is higher than protein levels in milk and similar to that
found in eggs, which are conventional animal‐based protein sources (Figure 11.1b). Coffee beans and by‐
products present lower protein values when compared to soybeans, which are one of the richest sources of
protein, approximately 40% of dry weight (Singh, Meena, Kumar, Dubey, & Hassan, 2015). However, proteins

from soy and coffee beans have similar structure. Most of the proteins in both products are seed storage
proteins. In the green coffee bean, it is estimated that they can account for 40% of the total protein (López‐
Pedrouso, Bernal, Franco, & Zapata, 2014), 11S globulin being the principal component of these proteins
(Acuña et al., 1999; Baú, Mazzafera, & Santoro, 2001; Mazzafera et al., 2019). Other proteins have also been
identified in green coffee beans, such as 7S‐ and a 2S‐type protein (Mazzafera et al., 2019), which are also
present in soybeans (Preece, Hooshyar, & Zuidam, 2017; Singh et al., 2015). In the case of coffee by‐products,
information is limited on the molecular characterization of their proteins. Further research is needed to study
the nature of proteins from coffee by‐products, especially cascara, silverskin, and SCGs, in order to use them as
an alternative source of animal‐based proteins.
11.3 Protein Extraction from Coffee and Coffee By‐products
Through different production processes, added value protein concentrates and isolates can be obtained for
use in the food industry. The product resulting from this extraction can be classified according to its protein
content into: (a) protein flours, which contain up to 65% protein; (b) protein concentrates, 65–90% protein;
and (c) protein isolates, ≥ 90% protein. Protein extracts obtained from by‐products of the food industry are
proposed for their upcycling in the human diet to use as food ingredient or dietary supplement, or as techno‐
functional ingredients due to their emulsifying, gelling, foaming, and water absorption properties (Pojić, Mišan,
& Tiwari, 2018).
Due to the similarity between soy and coffee proteins, processes commonly used for soy protein extraction
can be applied on coffee samples. Large‐scale processes for soybase preparation for protein extraction
involve 4 steps: soaking the soybeans, grinding in cold water, filtering, and cooking at 93–100 °C for 30 minutes
(Preece et al., 2017). Conventional soy protein extraction methods also involve alkali extraction using sodium
hydroxide, lime, ammonia, or tri‐basic sodium phosphate to bring the pH to slightly alkaline reaction for a
better protein extraction (Berk, 1992). To date, the methodology described in the scientific literature for the
extraction of coffee proteins from defective coffee beans and coffee by‐products (silverskin and SCGs) is
summarized in Table 11.2.

11.3.1 Protein Extraction from Defective Coffee Beans
Defective green coffee beans are discarded during the grading and selection of the coffee fruits to obtain a
quality beverage. Proteins were extracted from defective green coffee beans using water extraction at different
pH and precipitation by acidification or by adding salt (Castrillón‐Monroy & Riaño‐Luna, 1999). After studying
different extraction conditions, the maximum protein was extracted at pH 9, 40 °C for 2 hr and extraction ratio

of 1 : 8 (defective coffee beans:water). The extraction efficiency was 90.4% at lower volume (1 : 6.5 ratio),
taking into account that the amount of protein in defective coffee beans was 34.95 mg per gram of coffee. Two
methods were used for protein precipitation; maximum amount of precipitated protein was obtained by
acidification (pH 3.4). On the other hand, isolates precipitated by HCl addition and with calcium chloride had
55.9 and 25.2% protein, respectively. The isolate with the highest protein content and the best properties was
obtained by acid precipitation.
11.3.2 Extraction of Coffee Silverskin Proteins
Three different methods for protein extraction from coffee silverskin (14.6% protein) have recently been
proposed (Wen, Zhang, et al., 2020): conventional extraction by pH variation and isoelectric precipitation,
microwave‐assisted extraction (MAE), and ultrasound‐assisted extraction (UAE). The best yield using
conventional extraction was obtained by sequential alkaline‐acid extraction with 0.6 M NaOH +0.6 M HCl for
24 hours. In this case, increasing solvent concentration and time significantly increased extraction yield (Wen
et al., 2020). The MAE (434.7 W, 20 min) with 0.6 M NaOH recovered significantly higher (p < 0.05) percentage
of protein compared to conventional extraction. These results indicate that microwaves allow a notable
reduction in the extraction time due to high matrix rupture and components transfer. The UAE was carried out
at a power of 38 W/cm2 for 10 min and during the extraction by alkaline medium at 0.6 M. During the same
period (10 min), UAE produced a threefold increase of protein extraction. In summary, both UAE and MAE are
potentially rapid and effective tools for protein extraction from silverskin.
Pérez‐Míguez et al. (2019) also extracted proteins from coffee silverskin to obtain protein hydrolysates. Three
protocols were used to recover the proteins: (a) solid–liquid extraction using a mixture of water and an
organic solvent as extractant, (b) subcritical water extraction, and (c) high‐intensity focused ultrasound (HIFU)
using different buffer solutions containing different additives as extracting solvents. However, protein recovery
was low (Table 11.2).
Iriondo‐DeHond et al. (2019) obtained a nutrient enriched coffee silverskin extract (CSE) by aqueous
extraction easily applicable on an industrial scale. Although the main objective of this extraction was not to
produce a protein‐enriched extract, the protein content of CSE (16.7%) was obtained by stirring a mixture (50 
g silverskin/L, 10 min, 100 °C), filtration and lyophilization.
11.3.3 Protein Extraction from SCGs
Two methods have been used to obtain protein isolates from SCGs using Tris or urea buffers (Valdés et al.,
2020). Tris buffer and urea extractions yield <0.3 and 2.9 mg of protein/100 mg of SCGs, respectively. Proteins
were obtained form SCGs derived from different brewing processes (espresso, French press, Italian, and
paper filter filtered coffee). All the coffee brews employed to obtain the SCGs were treated with the same ratio
of coffee and water, type of coffee, degree of roasting, and degree of grinding. For this reason, the only factors
that differentiate the SCGs were the pressure applied, temperature, and time, which was different for each
method of coffee preparation. As mentioned, the highest protein content was obtained in espresso coffee
grounds, which were obtained by a higher pressure extraction but for a considerably shorter time. Therefore,
proteins are retained in the SCGs and do not pass into the coffee brew.
11.4 Nutritional Value of Coffee Proteins
After carbohydrates and dietary fiber, protein is the most abundant macronutrient found in coffee by‐products
(Iriondo‐DeHond, Iriondo‐DeHond, & del Castillo, 2020). Depending on the origin, species, varietal, roasting,
and brewing, a particular by‐product could reach the nutritional claim of “source of proteins.” As shown in
Table 11.1, a food product can claim to be a “source of proteins” when at least 12% of the energy value of the
food is provided by protein. This nutrition claim is applicable to cascara, green coffee beans, silverskin, and
SCGs.
Protein quality is determined by its EAA composition, digestibility, and bioavailability of its amino acids. Table
11.3 shows the amino acid content of coffee by‐products that can be a source of alternative plant‐based
proteins and also of the conventional protein sources (milk, egg, and soy). Of the total amino acids present in
coffee cascara, 33% correspond to EAA. Major amino acids are aspartic acid, glycine, and glutamic acid.
Tryptophan was not detected, but its presence could not be discarded since the acidic conditions necessary for
the quantification provoke its hydrolysis (Iriondo‐DeHond et al., 2020). Compared to other conventional
protein sources, cascara presented lower values of total and EAA (Table 11.3).

Green coffee beans contain 44% EAA, values close to that reported for the conventional protein sources such
as milk, egg, and soybeans. Glutamic and aspartic acids are the major amino acids of the coffee seed proteins
(Table 11.3). In addition, amino acids such as leucine, alanine, lysine, and serine were also found in reasonable
amounts (Shimizu & Mazzafera, 2000). The 11S protein is abundant in glycine, leucine, and glutamine and poor
in sulfur‐containing amino acids (cysteine and methionine) with 11 amino acids in high concentrations (5–

11%) (Rogers et al., 1999). Free amino acid contents were 2.7–4.8 and 3.5–6 mg/kg (dry matter) for Coffea
arabica and C. robusta, respectively (Abdelfatah Ali, 2013). However, Coffea canephora has higher free amino
acids (0.15–2.5%) than C. arabica varieties (except for glutamate) (Mazzafera et al., 2019). Total free amino
acid content differed between C. arabica and C. robusta (216–274 vs 159 mg/100 g of ground beans) due to
differences in genotype, environment and postharvest treatment, maturation, and storage. Moreover, total free
amino acids are higher in green than in roasted coffee (131 vs 86 mg/100 g) (Abdelfatah Ali, 2013).
Interestingly, the amino acid content in green coffee beans changes not only during postharvest treatment but
also during storage which can be attributed to the Maillard reaction (Arnold & Ludwig, 1996; Bagdonaite &
Murkovic, 2018). Total amino acid content of green coffee beans was higher than that found for milk and
similar to eggs.
Regarding the amino acid profile of silverskin, 46% of total amino acids correspond to EAA. Aspartic and
glutamic acids followed by leucine, alanine, and glycine were the major amino acids found in this coffee by‐
product. Serine, asparagine, and arginine presented the highest values of free amino acids, Machado et al.
(2020) observed that in coffee silverskin, the major amino acids were aspartic and glutamic acids, and the
BCAAs are found in the following amounts: leucine (7.6%), isoleucine (4.7%), and valine (5%) and also for
proline (5.2%) and arginine (5%). They also found tryptophan, but in less quantity (0.8%). As for the green
coffee beans, silverskin presented higher values of total amino acids compared to milk and similar proportion
of EAA to milk, eggs, and soybeans (Table 11.3).
With regard to EAA, they comprise 42% of the total amino acids of SCGs (Martinez‐Saez et al., 2017), values
equivalent to those found in milk (Table 11.3). Aspartic acid, threonine, glutamic acid, glycine, and leucine
presented the highest values among all the amino acids of SCGs (Campos‐Vega et al., 2015; Martinez‐Saez et al.,
2017).
The branched chain amino acids (BCAA) valine, leucine, and isoleucine are important for the prevention and
risk reduction of type 2 diabetes and other vascular or inflammatory stress‐induced pathologies (Abolbaghaei,
Oomah, Tavakoli, & Hosseinian, 2018). The green coffee bean, silverskin, and SCGs have values of BCAA similar
to those of the conventional protein sources, ranging from 17 to 21 g/100 g of total amino acids (Table 11.3).
On the other hand, the aromatic amino acids (AAA) tyrosine, phenylalanine, and tryptophan are associated
with protein and other essential secondary metabolites synthesis involved in nervous signals transmissions
and reducing reactive oxygen species in the brain (Parthasarathy et al., 2018). Cascara, the green bean, and
SCGs have lower values of AAA compared to milk, egg, and soy. However, silverskin has AAA content
comparable to milk (Table 11.3). Proteins with high BCAA, Fischer ratio (BCAA/AAA), and low content of AAA
are searched for producing physiologically functional foods for specific needs, such as in patients with
malnutrition associated with cancers, burns, trauma, and liver failure, and for nutritional support of children
with chronic or acute diarrhea or milk protein allergies. SCGs have the highest Fischer ratio, even higher than
milk, egg, and soy. The other coffee derivatives have similar Fischer ratios compared to the traditional protein
sources (Table 11.3).
Protein efficiency ratio (PER) was calculated as another protein quality parameter, from the amino acid
composition based on the following equations (Mir, Riar, & Singh, 2019):
PER1 = −0.684 + 0.456 × Leu − 0.047 × Pro
PER2 = −0.468 + 0.454 × Leu − 0.105 × Tyr
PER3 = −1.816 + 0.435 × Met + 0.78 × Leu + 0.211 × His −0.944 × Tyr
PER values below 1.5 indicate low‐quality proteins, and values above 2 are considered to be typical of high‐
quality proteins. Taking this information into account, as for the traditional protein sources (milk, egg, and
soy), all coffee derivatives except cascara have PER values >2, showing the high protein quality of these
products.
SAT1 and SAT2 parameters were calculated as satiety indicators. According to Volk et al. (2020), increased
intake of leucine, isoleucine, lysine, threonine, and tryptophan (SAT1) elevates their plasma levels that are
associated with increased satiety. Threonine and tryptophan (SAT2) might be the ones particularly responsible
for this increased satiety (Volk et al., 2020). SAT1 and SAT2 values indicate that SCGs might have the most
powelful hunger suppression effects after their intake. The green coffee bean and silverskin showed SAT1 and
SAT2 values comparable to milk, egg, and soy (Table 11.3). Considering all the protein quality parameters
calculated for coffee by‐products, dietary supplements and/or food fortification based on these by‐products
may be an interesting approach to contribute to sustainable health of the global population.

11.5 Food Safety of Coffee Proteins
Some of the requirements that must be considered for approval of any novel food or ingredient according to
regulation (EU) 2015/2283 are the absence of toxicity and food safety and low allergenic potential. Allergy
caused by coffee protein isolates or concentrates has never been reported, and there is few available
information on this matter. Studies performed by Applied Food Science, company that commercializes the
CoffeeProtein green coffee concentrate, indicate that it is free of allergens such as gluten, lactose, eggs, nuts,
and soy (Applied Food Sciences, 2019). Previous research by our group (Rios et al., 2020) also support the
absence of gluten in cascara. In this study, the amount of gluten present in bread formulated with cascara
extract and gluten‐free flour was determined, and gluten levels were lower than 3 ppm. The limit established
by the European Commission in Regulation (EC) 828/2014 for the labeling of products as “gluten‐free” is 20 
mg/kg (ppm). Therefore, the bread developed in this study was certified as safe for the population that has
nutritional requirement for a gluten‐free diet. To the best of our knowledge, the allergenicity of other coffee
by‐products has not been reported so far.
Considering the available data, the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) has considered coffee cascara as a
traditional food from a third country following Article 14 of Regulation (EU) 2015/2283 and does not raise
any safety objections to placing this product on the market within the EU (European Food Safety Autority,
2021). However, coffee silverskin is considered a novel food and still needs to undergo EFSA's authorization
procedure according to the Regulation (EU) 2015/2283 before it can be introduced into the European food
market. Regarding SCGs, they have been recently considered not novel, and they can be used as food
ingredients in the European market (European Parliament and of the Council, 2021).
11.6 Biological Properties of Coffee Proteins and Their Derivatives
The health claims ascribed to other food proteins (Table 11.1) may be applied to coffee proteins. Further
investigation should be conducted to demonstrate this hypothesis. No previous studies on that field are
available since the use of coffee as a source of proteins has been recently proposed (Applied Food Sciences,
2019). However, according to the increased requirements of new sources of food proteins, this is a line of
research of great interest.
The EFSA Panel on Dietetic Products, Nutrition and Allergies (NDA) on the basis of the scientific data so far
available, has concluded that there are insufficient evidences to establish a cause–effect relationship between
consumption of food peptides and their biological effects in vivo (EFSA Panel on Dietetic Products, 2009).
Consequently, EFSA has not authorized health claims for food peptides yet. More research should be
performed in order to complete the scientific evidences on the health‐promoting properties of peptides
formed during the human digestion, hydrolysis with commercially available enzymatic preparations and
chemicals. In order to achieve the goal for coffee proteins, an updated literature review on the bioactive
properties of coffee peptides released by the activity of human digestive enzymes and other enzymes
commonly used in food industry by in silico or in vitro approaches is provided in this section of the chapter
(Table 11.4).

Peptides obtained after hydrolysis with digestive enzymes may have health‐promoting properties in the
human body if they are bioavailable after digestion in physiological concentrations and remain intact until
reaching the target organ (Segura‐Camposa, Chel‐Guerreroa, Betancur‐Anconaa, & Hernandez‐Escalante,
2011). The in silico study carried out by Vinecky (2015) indicated the possible release of peptides possessing
opioid properties from dehydrin proteins from green coffee beans during the gastric digestion with pepsin
(Table 11.4). In addition, according to the data generated by employing BIOPEP UWM database, peptides
generated by digestion of the 11S globulin from green and roasted coffee beans and SCGs with digestive
enzymes may inhibit dipeptidyl peptidase‐IV (DPP‐IV) and angiotensin I‐converting enzyme (ACE) and possess
antioxidant properties (Ribeiro, de Rocha, & Prudencio, 2021). Peptides from the protein extracts of silverskin
samples submitted to different roasting process were obtained in vitro by enzymatic digestion employing
pepsin and pancreatin (Pérez‐Míguez et al., 2019). Of the 13 peptides obtained, only one (TLNGV) may be
associated with potential antibacterial properties using the BIOPEP database. On the other hand, protein

hydrolysates obtained using gastrointestinal digestive enzymes showed antioxidant properties and capacity to
reduce micellar cholesterol solubility in vitro (Pérez‐Míguez et al., 2019). Since these compounds can be
generated in vivo after gastrointestinal digestion and possess antioxidant, hypocholesterolemic, and
hypotensive properties, they can potentially help to reduce the risk of noncommunicable chronic diseases
such as metabolic syndrome and cardiovascular diseases. These properties should be studied in clinical trials.
The enzymes commonly used in the food industry generate large amount of peptides from coffee by‐products
(Table 11.4). Pérez‐Míguez et al. (2019) associated antioxidant and hypocholesterolemic properties to the
peptidic fraction obtained by alcalase and thermolysin hydrolysis of coffee silverskin. Furthermore, the in vitro
ACE‐inhibitory effect has been evaluated in peptides obtained after the hydrolysis of SCG proteins with
alcalase and thermolysin (Valdés et al., 2020). Results from this study showed that the hydrolysates possessed
antioxidant properties and inhibited ACE, the latter being highest when SCGs samples were obtained from
dark/medium roasted coffees, and after polyphenol removal. The studies by Pérez‐Míguez et al. (2019) and
Valdés et al. (2020) indicate that thermolysin activity allows to obtain peptides with better health‐promoting
properties from these by‐products than alcalase. The antioxidant peptides obtained from coffee by‐products
using these enzymes can be used as food preservatives to avoid microbial growth and extend the shelf life of
the food product. However, the study of the impact of adding these compounds in the right amount to achieve
this effect must be considered in order to keep the sensory quality of the final food product.

11.7 Conclusions
Coffee proteins are already commercially available (CoffeeProtein, Applied Food Sciences) for the formulation
of foods for human consumption. CoffeeProtein consists of proteins (30%), dietary fiber, and other
compounds (amino acids, antioxidants, vitamins, and minerals) with health‐promoting properties. Similar
products may be obtained from coffee by‐products, which contain proteins in a range of concentration from 4
to 19%. Protein isolates and concentrates from both green coffee beans and coffee by‐products (especially
cascara, silverskin, and SCGs) can be obtained using extraction methods similar to those employed for soybean
protein (water or alkali extraction) that are easily scalable at an industrial level. Green coffee beans and coffee
by‐products (cascara, silverskin, and SCGs) contain higher amounts of protein than milk (3%) and similar
values when compared to eggs (12%). In addition, percentage of essential amino acids (32–46%) and other

protein quality parameters are equivalent to that found for conventional protein sources such as milk, egg, and
soybean. Therefore, coffee proteins can be used as a sustainable alternative to animal‐based proteins. Peptides
generated in vitro by enzymatic hydrolysis of isolated coffee proteins that have antioxidant properties can be
used to better preserve the quality of foods and extend their shelf life. Coffee and its by‐products are natural
and sustainable sources of proteins in combination with other compounds of nutritional interest, such as
dietary fiber. Novel ingredients obtained based on coffee proteins would be sustainable and hypoallergenic
and suitable for all populations including those with special diets for health (celiac) or ideological (vegans)
reasons.
