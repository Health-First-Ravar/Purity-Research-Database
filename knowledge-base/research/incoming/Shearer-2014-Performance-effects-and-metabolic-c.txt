Performance eﬀects and metabolic consequences of caﬀeine
and caﬀeinated energy drink consumption on glucose disposal
Jane Shearer and Terry E Graham
This review documents two opposing eﬀects of caﬀeine and caﬀeine-containing
energy drinks, i.e., their positive eﬀects on athletic performance and their negative
impacts on glucose tolerance in the sedentary state. Analysis of studies examining
caﬀeine administration prior to performance-based exercise showed caﬀeine
improved completion time by 3.6%. Similar analyses following consumption of
caﬀeine-containing energy drinks yielded positive, but more varied, beneﬁts, which
were likely due to the diverse nature of the studies performed, the highly variable
composition of the beverages consumed, and the range of caﬀeine doses adminis-
tered. Conversely, analyses of studies administering caﬀeine prior to either an oral
glucose tolerance test or insulin clamp showed a decline in whole-body glucose
disposalof~30%.Theconsequencesofthisresistanceareunknown,buttheremaybe
implications for the development of a number of chronic diseases. Both caﬀeine-
inducedperformanceenhancementandinsulinresistanceconvergewiththeprimary
actions of caﬀeine on skeletal muscle.

INTRODUCTION
From a performance perspective, there is no doubt that
caﬀeine is ergogenic.1–4 An edition of the journal Science
from the 1890s notes that “caﬀeine in small and repeated
doses, about 60 mg/day, which may be prescribed to sol-
diers on the march, facilitates muscular work.”5 Since
that time, thousands of studies have documented the
performance-enhancing beneﬁts of caﬀeine in activities
ranging from sprints to endurance sports. Given the
ability of caﬀeine to enhance performance, it is hardly
surprising that this is a major marketing message for the
manufacturers of caﬀeine-containing energy drinks.
Caﬀeine-containing energy drinks are beverages that
generally contain mixtures of simple sugars, caﬀeine, and
a combination of vitamin and herbal preparations.6,7
Marketed to boost energy,increase awareness,and reduce
fatigue, there has been an exponential increase in sales of
such products worldwide, especially among adolescents
and young adults.8,9
Typically, caﬀeine-containing energy drinks contain
anywhere from 25 to 250 mg of caﬀeine in various forms,
from alkaloid caﬀeine to caﬀeine-containing plant
extracts.6,10–12 Aside from caﬀeine, many of these prepara-
tionsareknowntocontainhundredsof othercomponents
including vitamins, amino acids, and phenolic and anti-
oxidant compounds.7 Although these other ingredients
may have biological activities, it is the caﬀeine that is
largely responsible for the ergogenic properties of
caﬀeine-containing energy drinks. While the impact of
caﬀeine, including caﬀeine contained in energy drinks,
may be positive for performance, the eﬀects of caﬀeine,
particularly on glucose metabolism in the sedentary
state are detrimental. Acute consumption of caﬀeine in

impair whole-body glucose disposal and to cause
hyperinsulinemia as well as hyperlipidemia. Caﬀeine
interferes with the actions of insulin,and it is important to
appreciate that this hormone aﬀects the metabolism of
lipids as well as carbohydrates.
With regard to carbohydrate homeostasis, caﬀeine-
containing energy drinks deliver both a readily absorb-
able form of glucose in combination with a dose of
caﬀeine suﬃcient to impair glucose metabolism. Most
caﬀeine-containing energy drinks contain between 6 and
27 grams of carbohydrates, primarily in the form of
simple sugars at concentrations around 11–12% by
volume.6,7 Given that the majority of the population in
the United States is considered overweight or obese13,14
and levels of daily exercise have dropped to record
low levels,15–17 this aspect of caﬀeine and caﬀeine-
containing energy drink consumption warrants careful
consideration. Impaired glucose disposal and its accom-
panying hyperinsulinemia are risk factors for the devel-
opment of such chronic diseases as the metabolic
syndrome, cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and
cancer.18–21
This review considers two focused aspects of caﬀeine
and caﬀeine-containing energy drink consumption: 1)
their impact on athletic performance and 2) their eﬀect on
glucose tolerance. These physiological eﬀects are seem-
ingly opposite, but represent the interconnected “yin and
yang” of caﬀeine consumption. In particular, both eﬀects
appear to be mediated,to a large extent,by skeletal muscle.
Possible sites of action in this tissue are discussed herein.
While caﬀeine is the primary focus of this review, it is
recognized that caﬀeine-containing energy drinks contain
other potentially active ingredients and these ingredients
may interact with, or alter the actions of, caﬀeine; the
possible eﬀects of these interactions have rarely been
evaluated.
METHODS
Identiﬁcation of relevant studies
A PubMed search was conducted for studies testing the
impact of caﬀeine and/or caﬀeine-containing energy
drinks on exercise performance or glucose tolerance.
Articles were restricted to those published in English,
and the following key words were used to conduct
the search: caﬀeine, exercise, performance, glucose,
insulin resistance, oral glucose tolerance, clamp, insulin
clamp, time trial, coﬀee, Red Bull, energy drink, or the-
ophylline (a dimethylxanthine with actions similar to the
trimethylxanthine, caﬀeine).Various combinations of the
search words were used. References from the retrieved
articles were examined to identify possible additional
articles. Studies were included if they met the following
conditions: 1) performed on human subjects from 1990
onwards (while some invaluable studies on caﬀeine were
conducted prior to this time, there were few, if any,
energy drinks on the market prior to 1990); 2) included a
control group receiving a placebo (water or other bever-
age without caﬀeine) −studies administering coﬀee were
also excluded, as clear diﬀerences between the eﬀects of
coﬀee and caﬀeine on both performance and insulin sen-
sitivity have been well-documented22–24; 3) participants
were randomly assigned to treatment conditions and sub-
jects were blinded to their study grouping; 4) ﬁndings
were reported in a quantiﬁable format, such as time, dis-
tance, or power generated for performance studies;
insulin sensitivity for oral glucose tolerance tests using
the insulin sensitivity index (ISI) as deﬁned by Matsuda
and DeFronzo25 or the exogenous glucose infusion rate
for insulin clamp studies; 5) appropriate statistical mea-
sures were used and reported in the analysis; e.g., if a
study failed to report standard means or errors, subject
numbers and/or characteristics, it was excluded. Review
articles and letters to the editor were not eligible for
inclusion,nor were abstracts and posters from conference
proceedings.
Many of the initially identiﬁed studies did not meet
the aforementioned conditions and were consequently
excluded.26–71 Eligible manuscripts were collected and
subdivided into separate data sets. The ﬁrst group exam-
ined the eﬀects of caﬀeine on timed performance72–95
(reported in seconds or minutes) on a given task. This
data set provided a reference point for the examination
of performance-related studies investigating caﬀeine-
containing energy drinks.The impact of caﬀeine on short-
term,high-intensityexercisehasbeenpreviouslyreviewed
by Astorino and Roberson and is available elsewhere.96 In
the second group, the same data search was conducted
using caﬀeine-containing energy drinks classiﬁed by
endurance/performance time97–103 as well as distance and
power. Distance studies, team sport situations104,105
(soccer, rugby; assessed in distance covered), were
included as were power activities.106–108
The third group of studies evaluated the inﬂuence of
caﬀeine or theophylline on oral glucose tolerance
tests23,95,109–113 and insulin clamps.114–117 All oral glucose
tolerance tests examined caﬀeine or theophylline admin-
istered prior to a glucose challenge, typically a 75 g
glucose load.Blood samples were obtained at baseline and
subsequent time points. Subject responses to placebo and
caﬀeine were then compared and mean diﬀerences were
reported. For the the majority of the insulin clamp
studies, the treatment (placebo, caﬀeine, theophylline)
was administered approximately 60 minutes prior to the
clamp protocol.

Data selection and interpretation
Data extracted from original reports for included
author(s), PubMed identiﬁcation number, year of publi-
cation,subjecthealthstatus,caﬀeinedose,formof caﬀeine,
oral glucose tolerance test results, insulin clamp data
and mean performance data (±standard deviation).
Attempts were made to include all eligible data retrieved.
Where possible,data on diverse sporting events and popu-
lations were presented. Studies involving special popula-
tions, such as those experiencing pregnancy, type 1
diabetes, type 2 diabetes, gestational diabetes, obesity, and
weight loss, were noted. In some cases, studies included
separate trials examining the impact of caﬀeine dose
(e.g.,3,6,9 mg/kg118,119) separated by an appropriate wash-
out period or time (e.g., caﬀeine administered for 0, 7,
or 14 days111). Many of these serial studies were collected
on the same subjects under varied conditions and con-
tained valuable information on the impact of additional
parameters on caﬀeine and caﬀeine-containing energy
drinks.
Data analysis
Due to the nature of the data collected and the inclusion
of repeated measures, a simple ﬁxed-eﬀects model was
used to estimate eﬀect sizes between placebo and caﬀeine
or caﬀeine-containing mixtures. Because of the diverse
nature of the studies assessed,covariates were not consid-
ered. Models and eﬀect sizes were calculated for data sets
with more than ﬁve independent studies. For all models,
sensitivity analyses were conducted by repeating the
analyses while removing each study, in turn, to examine
whether the overall eﬀects resulted from a single study for
caﬀeine and caﬀeine-containing energy drinks.Estimated
model coeﬃcients with corresponding 95% conﬁdence
intervals (CI) are shown, where applicable, percent
changes for a given variable are reported. Note that each
study listed does not necessarily represent an indepen-
dent replicate. Studies with multiple trials (e.g., variable
caﬀeine dosing, eﬀect of habituation, timing of adminis-
tration, pre and post weight loss, etc.) are listed indepen-
dently. These are not true replicates and, as such, the
analysis is weighted accordingly. For example, if a study
conducts an oral glucose tolerance test pre and post
weight loss,120 each trial has a weighting of 0.5 and this
study counts as 1.0 total in the overall analysis.

Results of this analysis are shown in Figure 1. A total of

Where appropriate, the type of exercise and distance

in time (performance). The overall eﬀect size for all

Studies are listed in ascending order by duration of
the activity (Figure 1). There is a signiﬁcant correlation

clear that the mean diﬀerences in absolute performance
time between placebo and caﬀeine trials would be greater
with increasing exercise duration. The present analysis
also allowed for a cursory examination of caﬀeine dose on
time-trial performance.The studies were classiﬁed by two
ranges of caﬀeine dose: 2–5 mg/kg and >5 mg /kg (data
not shown). For those studies administering the same
bolus (mg) to all subjects, the dose was approximated by
dividing the dose (mg) by the mean reported body mass
(kg).76 Performance improvements with these dose ranges
were approximately 3% for 2–5 mg/kg and approximately
7% for >5 mg/kg.

Caﬀeine-containing energy drinks, game
performance, and power-based activities
Two studies examined the eﬀects of a caﬀeine-containing
energy drink (Red Bull™) on simulated game situations
(Figure 3A). These studies analyzed the impact of the
caﬀeine-containing energy drinks on distance covered in
meters (m). Administration of 1.3 mg/kg and 80 mg
(1.23 mg/kg for a 65 kg individual) of caﬀeine resulted in
an increase in the distance covered in simulated rugby and
soccer matches of 386 m and 231 m, respectively.38,39
These data were included because of their applicability to
team sports; however, due to the small sample size (only
two studies), no statistically valid conclusions on eﬀect
size could be made.
Five studies examining the impact of caﬀeine-
containing energy drinks on power/sprint activities were
identiﬁed.56,57,106–108 Of these,three met the eligibility crite-
ria for inclusion (Figure 3B).106–108 All studies assessed
power in watts (W).Activities included Wingate (a test of
anaerobic exercise potential performed on a cycle ergo-
meter), bench press, and a half-squat. Results of the sensi-
tivity analysis showed that one investigation (data circled
in Figure 3B) by Del Coso et al.106 (1 mg/kg caﬀeine, half-
squat) biased the eﬀect size for this measure. Although
underpowered, the results showed an estimated average
improvement of approximately 7 Watts. There appeared
to be a small, but positive, eﬀect of caﬀeine-containing
energy drinks on power activities of short duration.

DISCUSSION
Caﬀeine is the most widely consumed psychopharm-
acuetical in the world. While the most common manner
of consumption is via coﬀee or tea, the alkaloid form is
directly added to numerous food products including
drinks, gum, chocolate, and candy.122 It is estimated that
caﬀeine-consuming adults in North America eat or drink
an average of 300 mg per day, while adolescents and
young adults consume 100 mg per day.123 At present, reli-
able data on the amount of caﬀeine-containing energy
drinks being consumed are unavailable. However, based
on robust sales ﬁgures, it is clear that caﬀeine-containing
energy drinks are being readily consumed. It should be
noted that this review does not consider caﬀeine con-
tained in coﬀee. Coﬀee is known to contain hundreds of
other biologically active compounds including antioxi-
dants. In fact, coﬀee consumption accounts for approxi-
mately 60–70% of total daily antioxidant intake in the
form of polyphenols in the modern diet124,125 and it dose-
dependently reduces the risk of type 2 diabetes.126–130 Risk
reductions for type 2 diabetes have been shown for both
caﬀeinated and decaﬀeinated coﬀee, implying that non-
caﬀeine components of the coﬀee bean are respon-
sible.125,131,132 The majority of caﬀeine-containing energy
drinks contain pure alkaloid caﬀeine, containing no ben-
eﬁcial polyphenols and, therefore, should not be assumed
to have equivalent health beneﬁts to coﬀee.
In this review, two seemingly opposite eﬀects of caf-
feine and caﬀeine-containing energy drinks were evalu-
ated. Regardless of the form of caﬀeine administration
(alkaloid versus caﬀeine-containing energy drinks), there
was a positive impact on performance. This positive
impact extended to timed performances as well as simu-
lated game situations and power-based activities. Con-
versely, in a sedentary state, caﬀeine impaired insulin
action and, thus, glycemic regulation. When considering
the impact of both phenomena, skeletal muscle is the
major common tissue involved. Owing to its large mass
and high metabolic activity, this tissue is essential to
maintaining glucose homeostasis.
Caﬀeine, caﬀeine-containing energy drinks,
and performance
The results of the present analyses showed caﬀeine
administration, primarily of the puriﬁed alkaloid form, to
elicit a 3.6% increase in timed performance across varied
activities and exercise intensities. This level of enhance-
ment is in close agreement with the ﬁnding in a literature
review by Ganio et al.133 that found caﬀeine ingestion to
improve athletic performance by 3.2%. As in the present
review, the authors noted that the results were highly
variable and dependent on the dose and timing of caﬀeine
ingestion as well as the type, intensity, and duration of
the activity performed. It should also be noted that this
level of enhancement is less than the approximately
30%22,118,119,134–138 observed in studies in which subjects are
asked to exercise to volitional exhaustion.
In the studies included in this review, caﬀeine was
administered in doses ranging from approximately 2 to
7 mg/kg. Examination of caﬀeine dose showed an
approximate 3% increase in performance between 2 and
5 mg/kg, and an increase of 7% for doses >5 mg/kg. The
reasons for these dose-dependent diﬀerences are not
known. Performance beneﬁts with caﬀeine have been
noted with doses as low as 1.5–3 mg/kg caﬀeine, with
minimal further increases in performance with doses
above these levels.76,79,100,138,139 Low doses of caﬀeine
enhance
performance, while
limiting
adverse
side
eﬀects. Graham et al.113 administered 3, 6, or 9 mg/kg
of caﬀeine and asked subjects to exercise to exhaustion.
The results demonstrated identical improvements in
time with 3 mg/kg and 6 mg/kg, but no performance
gain at 9 mg/kg. Del Coso et al.106 tested the eﬀects on
power activities of two doses of caﬀeine-containing
energy drinks providing 1 mg/kg or 3 mg/kg of caﬀeine.
They found no improvement in resistance exercise with
1 mg/kg, but approximately a 7% increase in power
on both half-squat and bench press with 3 mg/kg.106
Pallarés et al.140 found performance improvements in
muscle strength and power at 3, 6, and 9 mg/kg caﬀeine
during weight lifting (25–50% 1 repetition maximum),
yet only 9 mg/kg improved performance at the highest
workloads (90% 1 repetition maximum). Doses above
9 mg/kg have been found to have minimal performance
beneﬁt despite causing the greatest increase in circulating
catecholamines.118 Such high doses have been associated
with numerous ill eﬀects including jitteriness, headaches,
tachycardia, and nausea.118,119,140 Pharmacologically, con-
suming more than 6 mg/kg appears to saturate the
hepatic metabolism of caﬀeine, resulting in minimal
increases in paraxanthine, a primary caﬀeine metabolite

dopamine receptors, and the liver cytochrome P450,
CYP1A2 (accounting for 95% of caﬀeine metabolism by
the liver147–149) all impact an individual’s response to
caﬀeine.147–149

consumed from caﬀeine-containing energy drinks also

caﬀeine-containing energy drinks on performance of

small, beneﬁcial gains were noted. Studies by Del Coso
et al.104,105 evaluated the impact of caﬀeine-containing
energy drinks on both soccer and rugby performance.
Results showed the ingestion of 3 mg/kg in a sugar-free
caﬀeine-containing energy drink improved jump height,
power output, distance covered, running speed, and the
number of sprints performed within a game.104,105
There appears to be little diﬀerence in performance
when caﬀeine is administered in an alkaloid form
versus as a component of a caﬀeine-containing energy
drink, which supports the hypothesis that caﬀeine is
the dominant active ingredient in caﬀeine-containing
energy drinks. In addition to caﬀeine, the carbohydrate
contained in most caﬀeine-containing energy drinks
would also be expected to yield a small, but signiﬁcant,
improvement in endurance performance. When caﬀeine
is taken in combination with a carbohydrate load,
the eﬀects appear to be additive. A study by Cox
et al.79 evaluated time trial performance in cyclists and
determined that caﬀeine alone resulted in a perform-
ance time improvement of 2.2%, while added carbohy-
drate resulted in an additional 1.0% improvement in
performance time.79
Most studies included a short caﬀeine withdrawal
period prior to exercise (12–48 h) to attempt to control
for habituation.150,151 The subjects in such studies exhib-
ited a wide range of consumption levels and toler-
ance,138,139 and it is well known that prolonged caﬀeine use
dampens caﬀeine-induced humoral and hemodynamic
responses, including elevations in blood pressure, heart
rate, circulating catecholamines, renin, and free fatty
acids.152 However, it does not appear that habitual intake
of caﬀeine aﬀects its impact on exercise. Van Soeren
et al.136 examined the performance beneﬁts of caﬀeine in
habitual caﬀeine consumers following 0, 2, or 4 days of
caﬀeine withdrawal. Results showed no impact of with-
drawal on the enhancement of performance. Therefore,
one would expect performance enhancement regardless
of the frequency of caﬀeine or caﬀeine-containing energy
drink consumption.
Caﬀeine and insulin resistance
While caﬀeine contained in caﬀeine-containing energy
drinks has clear performance beneﬁts for athletes,
perhaps more important from a public health perspec-
tive are the eﬀects in the sedentary individual. Given the
low physical activity levels in the population and the
number of caﬀeine-containing energy drinks sold on an
annual basis, it is reasonable to propose that while
caﬀeine-containing energy drinks are being consumed
by athletes for performance purposes, many are pur-
chased by the typical North American, who could very
likely be sedentary and/or overweight or obese.13,14,153,154
In a sedentary state, the combination of caﬀeine and
simple carbohydrate results in whole-body insulin
resistance.155,156
To date, no studies have speciﬁcally examined the
impact of caﬀeine-containing energy drinks on glucose
tolerance and insulin sensitivity. However, there are
ample data examining the eﬀects of caﬀeine on both
parameters across a range of doses, subjects, and experi-
mental conditions. The oral glucose tolerance tests
reviewed here involved the administration of caﬀeine
approximately 60 min prior to consuming a glucose load
(75 g). This test is relevant as it mimics a day-to-day situ-
ation in which caﬀeine is ingested followed by a meal
shortly thereafter. It is also worth noting that individuals
in North America are almost always in a postprandial
state in their waking hours.157,158
The present analysis found that caﬀeine lowered the
ISI by 1.3, on average. Insulin resistance was also evident
in the area under the curve for insulin, where caﬀeine
caused an approximate 30% increase. Although clinical
cutoﬀs for ISI are not well established, an ISI of <2.5 is

The insulin clamp is widely regarded as a gold stan-
dard test in the assessment of insulin sensitivity.160–162
Caﬀeine resulted in a 27% decline in the level of glucose
required to maintain glycemia (glucose infusion rate).
This translates into a third less glucose being taken up
by the body and an even larger reduction in skeletal
muscle glucose disposal. As the majority of oral glucose
tolerance and clamp studies analyzed within the pre-
sent data set used a caﬀeine dose of 5 mg/kg, analysis
of the impact of caﬀeine dose per se on glucose homeo-
stasis could not be assessed. However, Beaudoin et al.53
speciﬁcally examined this question and found caﬀeine
to disrupt insulin sensitivity in a dose-dependent
manner (insulin AUC for OGTT increased 5.8% for
each mg/kg increase in caﬀeine), and there was, notably,
no apparent threshold. This suggests the caﬀeine dose
of a caﬀeine-containing energy drink would impede
insulin action. Given that the half-life of caﬀeine is
in the range of 6 h,163 insulin activity could be impaired
for some hours after ingestion of a caﬀeine-containing
energy drink. It also appears that adaption is unlikely to
occur. In the various studies examined, many subjects
had consumed coﬀee daily for decades and were still
responsive. Dekker
et al.111
examined
this
directly
by having caﬀeine-naive individuals consume 5 mg/kg/

Application of this work has a limitation in that the
diet also contains protein and fat, not solely carbohy-
drate. Lane et al.28 examined the impact of consuming
caﬀeine (125 g) 1 hour prior to a mixed meal tolerance
test and found that caﬀeine increased the rise in glucose
by 21% and insulin AUC by 48%. Likewise, Moisey
et al.69 demonstrated glucose tolerance to be impaired
during the administration of both a low- and a high-
glycemic-index mixed meal. There are also interactions
between lipid levels and caﬀeine that aﬀect glucose tol-
erance. Beaudoin et al.95 administered an oral fat load
and, 5 h later, either placebo or caﬀeinated coﬀee fol-
lowed by an oral glucose tolerance test. This study
clearly demonstrated that the elevated lipids and caﬀeine
were independent, additive factors impeding glucose
disposal.
Role of skeletal muscle
Performance and insulin resistance. The mechanisms and
biochemical pathways responsible for caﬀeine’s eﬀects on
athletic performance and glucose intolerance are not
completely understood.It is common that critical biologi-
cal processes contain redundancies so that if one is
altered, the process is minimally aﬀected and, thus,
various experimental manipulations with pharmacologi-
cal agents are not necessarily deﬁnitive. Nevertheless,
skeletal muscle is key to both phenomena and scientists
are able to replicate each eﬀect in isolated skeletal muscles
exposed to caﬀeine. This model is invaluable as the
muscle-speciﬁc impacts of caﬀeine can be delineated
from those of the central nervous system. Caﬀeine is a
non-speciﬁc adenosine receptor antagonist. Adenosine is
a purine nucleotide continually produced, released, and
metabolized by ATP-producing tissues. Upon being
released, adenosine can bind to adenosine receptors on
the same or adjacent cells. Adenosine receptor subtypes
(A1, A2A, A2B, and A3) are largely tissue-speciﬁc and
belong to the transmembrane family of G protein-
coupled receptors.164 The primary receptor expressed in
skeletal muscle is A1, with A2A and A2B also being
expressed.165
Caﬀeine-mediated beneﬁts on performance. Numerous
mechanisms have been proposed for the eﬀects of caf-
feine on skeletal muscle performance. Among these are
sparing of muscle glycogen, enhanced glucose uptake,
reduced lactate accumulation, and enhanced fatty acid
uptake.3 Given that performance enhancement can be
shown for activities ranging from intense activities
lasting a minute or less to those lasting >90 min, it is
highly unlikely that a single change in metabolism could
be the major explanation. There are few data to support
the popular theory that caﬀeine acts to signiﬁcantly
perturb muscle metabolism and the ratio of carbohy-
drate to fatty acid utilization in this tissue,1,3,119 and
such modiﬁcations are unlikely to aﬀect exercise lasting
less than 30–40 min. However, there is considerable evi-
dence that a very fundamental mechanism in muscle is
aﬀected by caﬀeine via alterations to the sarcoplasmic
reticulum, ryanodine calcium receptors, and patterns of
excitation-contraction coupling.166–170 In particular, caf-
feine increases the intracellular concentration of Ca2+,
resulting in altered ion handling1 and increased force
production during shortening.168,171 These eﬀects appear
to be time-, temperature-, and ﬁber type-dependent, with
greater eﬀects in slow- versus fast-twitch muscle due to
diﬀerences in Ca2+ kinetics.170,172
Tallis et al.169 examined the impact of caﬀeine
(70 μM, representing a high physiological level) on
submaximal and maximal power output of isolated

Detrimental impacts of caﬀeine on glucose tolerance.
There is a direct eﬀect of caﬀeine on resting skeletal
muscle glucose uptake; speciﬁcally, there is an interact-
ion between insulin-stimulated mechanisms, although
the exact signaling pathways have not been fully
elucidated.175–177 While it is initially surprising that this
eﬀect is not observed in active muscle, in this state
circulating insulin has declined to very low levels and
muscle translocates the glucose transporter GLUT4 to the
sarcolemma independent of insulin. In the sedentary
state, adenosine antagonism likely alters glucose utiliza-
tion at least one of three stages: extracellular delivery,
membrane signaling, or intracellular glucose signaling
and processing.178–180 This coordination between nutrient
ﬂow, uptake, and metabolism is essential for matching
glucose supply with demand.181
The extracellular delivery of glucose depends on
blood ﬂow and capillary recruitment as well as spatial
barriers. Insulin stimulation is key to glucose delivery,
resulting in capillary recruitment and nutritive ﬂow
to skeletal muscle.182–187 Wasserman181 has elegantly
demonstrated this to be the site of >50% of the resis-
tance to glucose uptake at physiological insulin levels. It
is well accepted that adenosine has vasoactive proper-
ties, exerting local vasodilator actions; however, the
exact impacts have been debated since the early
1960s.188–190 Infusion of adenosine increases both vascu-
lar ﬂow and glucose uptake during both rest and
exercise.190–192 Employing positron emission tomography
to assess ﬂow and glucose uptake Heinonen et al.192
showed adenosine infusion to increase glucose uptake in
healthy young men at rest. Conversely, administration of
adenosine receptor antagonists reduces both ﬂow and
glucose uptake.175,190,193 It is highly likely that caﬀeine
results in changes in microvascular perfusion and
glucose delivery to skeletal muscle. Graham et al.194
noted a modest, but signiﬁcant, increase in peripheral
resistance, but not bulk ﬂow, in the legs of men at rest
and during exercise. At rest, caﬀeine would not neces-
sarily alter bulk blood ﬂow; rather, it would alter nutri-
tive ﬂow, thereby contributing to impaired insulin
resistance.184,195 During exercise, enhanced ﬂow in the
muscles would reduce the detrimental impact of caﬀeine
delivery and, thus, its inhibitory eﬀects on insulin action
in skeletal muscle.117
The second putative site of caﬀeine action is that of
insulin signaling. Antagonism of the adenosine A1
receptor has been shown to be directly involved in
insulin signaling.177 Thong et al.196 examined glucose
uptake in isolated rat soleus muscle exposed to combi-
nations of insulin, and adenosine agonist or antagonist.
Under basal conditions, there was no eﬀect on glucose
uptake of adenosine removal or the addition of the
agonist
or
antagonist.
However,
under
insulin-
stimulated conditions, adenosine removal resulted in a
50% decline in glucose uptake. Conversely, the addition
of a selective adenosine A1 agonist dose-dependently
limited this inhibition. Examination of speciﬁc insulin
receptor pathway components showed caﬀeine to have
no impact on insulin receptor tyrosine kinase, insulin
receptor
substrate
1-associated
phosphatidylinositol
3-kinase activity, or protein kinase B /Akt.117 Therefore,
while the presence of adenosine has been shown to be
an integral component of insulin signaling in skeletal
muscle,197–199 the exact site(s) of the inhibition has yet
to be elucidated. A further complication is that caﬀeine
can increase the phosphorylation and activity of the
energy sensor AMP kinase.117,200 Activation of AMP
kinase with caﬀeine results in enhanced glucose uptake.
As such, reconciliation of this increase with what is
observed in vivo (i.e., decrease in whole-body glucose
disposal with caﬀeine) warrants further investigation201
and, as noted above, there must be numerous redundan-
cies in these metabolic processes that are inﬂuenced by
caﬀeine.
There may be more than one direct action of caﬀeine
on glucose utilization. Reports indicate that caﬀeine-
exposed muscle has reduced glucose uptake, with
no eﬀect on glucose oxidation. In human skeletal
muscle, there is no evidence that caﬀeine alters glucose
concentrations, hexokinase, glucose-6-phosphate, citrate

decrease skeletal muscle insulin sensitivity and glucose

CONCLUSION
Review and analysis of data examining the relationship
between caﬀeine and performance enhancement con-
ﬁrms what has been known since the 1890s,5 i.e., caf-
feine, even in small doses, results in performance
beneﬁts across a range of athletic activities. It appears
that these beneﬁts are also conferred by caﬀeine-
containing energy drinks. While caﬀeine beneﬁts perfor-
mance, a possible adverse eﬀect on whole-body glucose
tolerance has also been demonstrated, with caﬀeine
impairing glucose tolerance by approximately 30%. At
present, the long-term consequences of caﬀeine-induced
insulin resistance are not known and warrant further
study. Both eﬀects appear to be mediated, at least in
part, by skeletal muscle. Despite a large volume of lit-
erature on the topic, current understanding of the exact
mechanisms by which caﬀeine enhances performance
and causes insulin resistance in skeletal muscle are
incompletely understood.
