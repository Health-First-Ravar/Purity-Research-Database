Abstract
Background: A large amount of cohort studies addressed coffee consumption and risk of coronary heart disease (CHD) and yielded
inconsistent results. We conducted a meta-analysis to estimate the pooling effects.
Methods: We searched for all published English articles indexed in MEDLINE or PubMed from January 1966 to January 2008. Twenty-one
independent prospective cohort studies, which tested CHD risk by coffee consumption, were identified. A general variance-based method
was used to pool the relative risks (RR). 15,599 cases from 407,806 participants were included in pooling the overall effects.
Results: As compared to the light coffee consumption (b1 cup/d in US or ≤2 cups/d in Europe), under the random-effects model, the pooled CHD
RRs (95% CI) for all studies combined were 0.96 (0.87–1.06), 1.04 (0.92–1.17) and 1.07 (0.87–1.32) for the moderate (1–3 or 3–4 cups/d), heavy
(4–5 or 5–6 cups/d) and very heavy (≥6 or ≥7 cups/d) categories of coffee consumption (all pN0.05); Moderate coffee consumption showed
significantly lower CHD RR (95% CI) of 0.82 (0.73–0.92) (pb0.001) in women, and of 0.87 (0.80–0.86) (p=0.001) in men and women followed
≤10 years.
Conclusion: Our findings do not support the hypothesis that coffee consumption increases the long-term risk of coronary heart disease.
Habitual moderate coffee drinking was associated with a lower risk of CHD in women.

1. Introduction
Coronary heart disease (CHD) is one of the leading causes
of mortality in industrialized countries. CHD is responsible
for about 19% of all deaths in the Unite States and UK in
recent years, and is rapidly becoming a major cause of death
worldwide [1]. Up to 80% of cases of coronary heart disease
can be attributed to lifestyle factors, such as unhealthy diet,
physical inactivity, and smoking [2].
Coffee is among the most widely consumed beverages in
the world. In the United States, for example, 52% of all
persons aged 10 years or older drink coffee [3]. Because of
the wide consumption of coffee, its effects on CHD could
have considerable public health and clinical implications.

extensively examined whether coffee consumption increases
risk of coronary heart disease (CHD). However, the issue
remains controversial. Case–control studies have tended to
be positive, whereas prospective cohort studies have tended
to show no associations among individuals with higher
coffee consumption [4,5].
A recent meta-analysis had pooled only 10 of 21
independent cohort studies [8–27] using unclear inclusion
and exclusion criteria. The study might thus provide a biased
coffee–CHD association due to existing substantial publica-
tion bias as discussed by the authors, because the majority of
eligible cohort studies [8–11,13,14,16,20,22–24,27] were
not included in the study [4]. In the early 1990s, three meta-
analyses assessed the pooled association of coffee consump-
tion and CHD risks [5–7]. Since few studies published before
1990 provided confounder-adjusted relative risks (RRs) and
95% confidence interval (CI), and coffee consumption was
significantly associated with many CHD risk factors (ie,
smoking), their pooled crude RRs might thus be over-
estimated. Since then, eight new studies had been published
and three studies had updated their results [18–27]. These 11
independent studies included 10,810 cases from 255,534
participants. Most of them provided confounder-adjusted
RRs. We performed a meta-analysis of prospective observa-
tional studies to examine the pooled relative risk of CHD at
various levels of coffee consumption.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Identification of studies
We searched for all published articles indexed in MED-
LINE and PubMed database from 1966 to January 21, 2008.
Literature searches were performed by an expert, using the
terms in title, abstract, keywords, Medical Subject Headings
(MeSH) words as follows: (i) coffee or caffeine; (ii)
cardiovascular disease (CVD), coronary heart disease,
coronary diseases, myocardial infarction, myocardial ische-
mia, or ischemic heart disease (IHD), CHD, CVD, or IHD;
(iii) cohort, longitudinal, prospective, follow follow-up,
incidence, relative risk, risk ratio, hazard ratio, or hazards
model; (iv) (i) and (ii) and (iii). We also perused the
bibliographies of retrieved articles.
2.2. Inclusion and exclusion criteria
For inclusion, studies had to fulfill the following criteria:
(i) have a cohort study design; (ii) report RRs or hazard ratios
and their corresponding 95% confident intervals (95% CIs)
of the relevant outcomes, or the number of case and total
population at risk at baseline relating to every category of
coffee intake; (iii) provide the frequency and amount of
coffee consumption, which allowed for standardized cate-
gorization of intake; (iv) outcomes were coronary diseases,
ischemic heart diseases, myocardial infarction, or total
cardiovascular diseases; (v) reports were written in English;
and (vi) study participants at baseline were aged 18 years and
over.
Studies were excluded if: (i) case–control, cross-sectional
or experimental designs were used; (ii) only two categories
of coffee intake; (iii) mixed healthy diet was reported, in
which the effect of coffee could not be separated; only
caffeine was reported, and not coffee itself; (iv) all
participants at baseline had cardiovascular diseases or
diabetes; and (v) the outcomes were stroke, cerebrovascular
disorders, and cerebrovascular accidents.
We assumed that the publications were generated from the
same cohort when they were done by the same research
group at the same setting and time, and had the same study
population. For these multiple publications of the same
cohorts, we only included the publication with the complete
study populations [23] rather than any sub-groups of them
[28], with the longest follow-up period among those with the
same study population and outcomes [11,12,17,18,26,29], or
those including both incident CHD cases and CHD deaths
[20,24] rather than those with CHD deaths only [30,31].
Data from the same cohort were used only once in the same
overall effect analysis and sub-analyses.
2.3. Data extraction and assessment of study quality
Two investigators independently abstracted the data and
evaluated the quality of studies included in this meta-
analysis using a standardized data-collection form. Discre-
pancies were resolved by repeated examination of the articles
by another investigator, author discussion and referencing
the original publication.
For each eligible study, we extracted information on: title,
author names, year and source of publication, country of
origin, study design, sampling methods, region of the study
cohorts, study size, follow-up years, participant character-
istics, measurement of coffee consumption and outcomes,
and adjustment for potential confounders. The RRs and their
95% CI of overall effect and those in each sub-group,
according to sex, smoking, sub-types of CHD, were
extracted as measures of the association between outcomes
and coffee consumption. The number of new cases over the
follow-up period and the number of total exposed population
at baseline relating to every category of coffee intake were
also extracted. When different adjustments were made, those
RRs (95% CIs) with the most adjusted factors were
extracted. We did not contact authors to request additional
information.
The quality of each article was scored according to criteria
similar to those described by Pavia et al. [32]. Total score
ranged between 0 (worst) and 22.5 (best). Items for screening
potential selection bias included sampling population (general
or defined), sampling methods (random/census or non-
random), response rate (N75% or not), drop-out rate (b25%
or not), and clear inclusion and exclusion criteria given or not

assessing if the RRs (95% CIs) were adjusted for age, sex,
smoking, alcohol drinking, physical activity, BMI, family

statistical analysis of demographic data (yes or no), precise p
values or 95% CIs given or not, power calculation, and test
statistic specified or not (sub-total score, 0–2.5).
2.4. Statistical analysis
We conducted separate meta-analyses for different levels of
coffee consumption categorized into four levels as did in a
meta-analysis of coffee and type 2 diabetes [33]: the light (the
referent), US studies, b1 cup per day; European studies,
≤2 cups per day; moderate: US studies, 1–3 cups/d, European
studies, 3–4 cups/d; heavy: US studies 4–5 cups/d, European
studies, 5–6 cups/d; and very heavy level: US studies, ≥6
cups/d; European study, ≥7 cups/d.
We estimated the pooled risks and 95% CIs of CHD for
the moderate, heavy and the very heavy categories by the
general variance-based method that requires information on
the RRs or hazard ratios and their 95% CIs for each study
[34]. When no 95% CIs were presented, they were calculated
by transforming probability values. The 95% CIs were used
to assess the variance and the relative weight of each study.
Adjusted RR and their 95% CI, when available, were
preferred. Crude RR (95% CI) was used in some studies
which only presented adjusted RRs but not their 95% CIs.
Between-study heterogeneity was assessed separately for the
differences of RRs for included studies within each category
[34]. Results without heterogeneity were pooled using fixed-
effects model; otherwise, DerSimonian and Laird random-
effects model was used [34].
If the median coffee consumption from more than one
category in a single study fell into the same category of
intake in our meta-analysis, we pooled these RRs within each
category for each study with inverse variance weight and
used the combined estimate for that group. If the individual
study reported risks based on multiple outcomes (eg, fatal,
and non non-fatal CHD) or on sub-groups (eg, male and
female), one pooled RR was also calculated for the study in
question per coffee category before pooling the RRs of the
studies in that category. If original studies did not use the
lowest category as the referent, RR and its 95% CI were
recalculated relative to the light category.
We did sensitivity analyses by sub-group analyses
according to sex, region, outcome, confounder adjustment,
follow-up duration and study quality. Heterogeneities in
pooling RRs between sub-groups were assessed separately
for studies included within each category [35]. Weighted
least squares meta-regression was also conducted to identify
determinants of the between-study heterogeneity. In the
meta-regression, natural logarithms of the RR estimates for
each level of coffee consumption from the individual studies
were used as the outcome variable. The explanatory
variables included gender (percentage of men), region,
different outcomes, smoking (percentage), mean follow
follow-up years, mean or median age at baseline and
confounder adjustment. Regression was weighted by the
reciprocal of variance of the RR estimates. We used funnel
plot asymmetry to detect any publication bias in the meta-
analysis, and Egger's regression test to measure funnel plot
asymmetry [36]. SAS (version 9.1, SAS Institute, Cary, NC,
United States) was used for the above analyses.

3.2. Data quality
The mean (SD) overall quality score of the studies was
13.5 (3.5), with a range of 7.5–19.0. The mean (SD) sub-
scores for the assessments of selection, information and
confounding biases and data analysis were 4.0 (0.8), 5.0
(3.2), 4.0 (0.8) and 1.5 (0.4), respectively. Of the 21
independent cohort studies, 13 studies adjusted for age and
smoking, 16 for gender, 9 for BMI and dietary factors, and 7
for physical activity. For the assessment of coffee consump-
tion, nine studies used validated FFQ methods, whereas the
remaining 12 studies did not mentioned if their FFQ had
been validated before. Five and sixteen studies determined
coffee consumption by using face-to-face interview and self-
administrated questionnaire, respectively. Nineteen studies
gave clear event criteria and assessed the events using
hospital records.
3.3. Overall effects of coffee consumption on CHD
A total of 407,806 participants and 15,599 new cases were
included in pooling the overall effects. Under the random-
effects model, the pooled RRs of CHD for all studies combined
were 0.96 (95% CI: 0.87, 1.06) for the moderate, 1.04 (95%
CI: 0.92, 1.17) for the heavy and 1.07 (95% CI: 0.87, 1.32) for
the very heavy categories of coffee consumption, respectively
(all pN0.05). There was significant between-study hetero-
geneity within the three categories. The p values for
heterogeneity in the results were less than 0.01 for the three
coffee consumption categories. (Fig. 2 and Table 2).
3.4. Sensitivity analyses and publication bias diagnostics
Sensitivity was examined by sub-analyses. Coffee con-
sumption had a weak but statistically significant protective
effect in women rather than in men. The pooled RRs of CHD
for women were 0.82 (95% CI: 0.73, 0.92), 0.87 (95% CI:
0.67, 1.13), and 0.90 (95% CI: 0.61, 1.34) in the moderate,
heavy and very heavy categories of coffee consumption in
comparison to the light coffee intake group. There was a
marginally significant difference in CHD RRs between men
and women in the category of moderate coffee consumption
(p=0.020). Similar trend of the gender difference remained in
the sub-analysis of those studies that controlled for smoking.
The RRs (95% CIs) in the tree coffee groups were 0.94 (0.77,
1.15), 1.00 (0.77, 1.29) and 1.10(0.78, 1.57) in men, and 0.81
(0.72, 0.91), 0.81 (0.63, 1.04) and 0.81 (0.69, 0.94) in women,
respectively (p for gender difference: 0.48, 0.25 and 0.11). The
pooled relative risk of CHD was slightly lower in Europe than
in the United States in the moderate category (p=0.099), but
similar in the heavy and the very heavy categories (p=0.55 and
0.42). No significant sub-group-heterogeneity in the pooled
RRs was observed when using different outcomes (fatal CHD,
non-fatal CHD, and all CHD). Both confounder-adjusted RRs
and crude RRs and their 95% CIs were obtained from 11

coffee consumption (p=0.966, p=0.154 and p=0.139,
respectively), indicating no evidence of substantial publica-
tion bias in these three categories.

bias. Our findings do not support the hypothesis that coffee
consumption increases the risk of coronary heart disease in
either men or women.
Four meta-analyses assessing coffee consumption and
risk of CHD were published previously. Sofi et al. [4]
analyzed only 10 cohort studies and concluded that a
moderate-to-high coffee consumption was not significantly
associated with an increased risk of CHD development (RRs:
1.04–1.16, pN0.05). Similar results were reported in the
three meta-analyses of 11–15 cohort studies published in
1992–1994 [5–7]. Our findings were generally consistent
with the results of these this previous meta-analysis of cohort
studies [4–7], although the included studies were quite
different. These results showed that the recent published
evidence had not significantly altered the pooled effects.
4.1. Mechanisms of coffee on CHD risk
Coffee consumption may have duplex effects on
cardiovascular system. Mechanisms of how coffee cause or
protect against CHD have recently been reviewed in details
by Cornelis and El-Sohemy [46]. In brief, coffee consump-
tion may increase the CHD risk by increasing serum
cholesterol and homocysteine, blood pressure, endothelial
dysfunction and aortic stiffness [47–49]. The cholesterol-
raising effect is probably influenced by the brewing method
[47]. Boiled coffee contains cafestol and kahweol that
powerfully elevate the levels of serum total and LDL
cholesterol [48]. These substances were largely trapped by
the use of paper filter in coffee preparation [48]. Thus, the
filter coffee may be a weaker association, if any, with the
cholesterol level. A case–control studied also reported a
lower relative risk of non-fatal myocardial infarction in filter
coffee consumers than those drinking boiled coffee [50].
These findings suggested that the type of coffee drinking in
different countries, or even in the same country, may partly
explain the conflicting results of the studies. Among the 21
cohort studies included in this meta-analysis, six cohort
studies (U.S. 3, European 3) reported that filter brewing was
the predominant method [16,19,25,26,51]. Klag et al. (U.S.)
[18] and Kleemola et al. (Finland) [23] reported that their
subjects mainly consumed percolated coffee or unfiltered
coffer. Two articles mentioned an increased proportion of
filtered coffee consumption [23,25]. However, there was no
sub-analysis stratified by boiled and filter coffee in the cohort
studies involved in this study. We could not determine if the
long-term effect of filter coffer differed from boiled coffer on
CHD development in this meta-analysis. Further studies
were needed to clarify this issue.
Caffeine, mainly obtained from coffee, may be another
important factor that increases the CHD risk. Caffeine may
decrease insulin sensitivity [52], increase serum homocys-
teine [53] and blood pressure [54]. However, a larger cohort
study reported a significantly dose-dependent lower risk of
heart disease mortality associated with caffeinated beverage
consumption [44]. On the other hand, coffee is also a rich
source of strong antioxidants, like chlorogenic acid,
flavonoids, melanoidins, and various lipid-soluble com-
pounds such as furans, pyrroles, and maltol [48]. Moreover,
coffee is also rich in potassium, niacin, and magnesium.
These substances may improve insulin sensitivity [55],
decrease inflammation markers [56], lower diabetes risk [33]
and thus reduces CHD risk. In addition, different genotypes
and gene–environment interactions may also partially
account for the variations in coffee effects on CHD among
individuals, populations and studies [46,57].
We observed that moderate coffee drinking was signifi-
cantly associated with lower CHD risk in women rather than
in men. A few studies also reported a lower risk of CHD or
diabetes in women than in men [23,58]. However, the reason
for the gender difference is unknown. Coffee consumption is
strongly associated with smoking. The prevalence of
smoking was much higher among men than women.
Lopez-Garcia et al. [26] reported very similar RRs for CHD
at all coffee categories between men and women of never
smoking. Our findings showed a lower coffee related CHD
risk in women than in men in the sub-analysis of those studies
that controlled for smoking, although the gender difference
was not significant. These findings suggested that smoking
might be related to the gender difference, and adjustment for
smoking might not completely eliminate its influence on the
coffee–CHD association. Many epidemiologic studies have
demonstrated that smokers usually have more unhealthy
lifestyle habits (eg, physical inactivity, alcohol abuse) than
non-smokers [10,59]. Although smoking was adjusted for in
the majority of the studies, not all smoking-related unhealthy
habits were controlled for in the studies included in this meta-
analysis. The differences in smoking practices between men
and women and the associated lifestyle habits may partially
explain the observed gender differences in the association
between coffee consumption and CHD risks.
4.2. Validity of the study
The present study is robust because the prospective
design is not subject to the problems of recall and selection
bias. The study participants in 12 of 21 studies were selected
from general population, and this would eliminate selection
222
J. Wu et al. / International Journal of Cardiology 137 (2009) 216–225

bias at baseline. Long-term cohort studies are likely to suffer
from lost-to-follow-up bias. However, coffee consumption
was not associated with the loss rate [60]. Egger's regression
test also indicated no evidence of substantial publication bias
in this study. We also observed that the pooled association
between coffee consumption and CHD risk was not
significantly affected by the study quality.
Furthermore, most studies included in our meta-analysis
had a large sample size and long duration of follow-up.
Cohort studies are a potentially powerful approach to assess
the long-term effects of coffee consumption on CHD risk.
Although outcome evidence from long-term RCTs is ideal,
these studies are extremely difficult to implement on a
practical basis. In fact, most RCTs with coffee intervention
only had a short duration of several months and also smaller
sample size [47,61]. Such short durations and small sample
size are not appropriate to assess the long-term effect of
coffee consumption on CHD risk.
In cohort studies, the validity of exposure and outcome
assessments would greatly affect the validity of the studies.
Many validation studies suggested that coffee consumption
assessment had a relatively high validity and reproducibility
[62]. The correlation coefficients between coffee drinking
assessed by FFQ and by diet records were 0.93 in US men,
0.78 in US women [62,63] and 0.61 in Swedish women [64].
Our stratified analysis showed that coffee drinking tended to
have lower RRs in the studies with a follow-up duration of
equal or less than 10 years than those over 10 years. One
plausible explanation is that the validity of long-term coffee
assessment would decrease with the follow-up duration
when using a single baseline coffee measurement. We did not
report the pooled CHD RRs in relation to an average coffee
consumption of multiple measures over the follow-up
periods since only two studies reported such results.
Confounding bias is a major factor that harms the validity
of cohort studies as compared to RCTs. Smoking, a well-
established risk factor of CHD, was strongly associated with
coffee consumption, and may distort the coffee–CHD
association. Eleven of the 21 independent cohort studies
had adjusted for smoking. Other common potential con-
founders, such as age, BMI, physical activities, dietary
supplements and baseline relevant health status, were
controlled for in the majority of the studies included in this
meta-analysis. Moreover, we also assessed to what extent
that confounders would change the pooling effects by
comparing the heterogeneity between the RRs pooled by
using the confounder-adjusted RRs and the crude RRs. The
pooled adjusted RRs tended to be smaller than the pooled
crude RRs of the same studies. The results of pooled adjusted
RRs showed that moderate and heavy coffee drinking was
associated with a significantly lower risk of CHD. The
differences between the pooled adjusted RRs and pooled
crude RRs were not statistically significant in the three coffee
categories (pN0.05) (Table 2). Therefore, we could not
exclude the studies without providing adjusted RRs from the
overall effect calculation.
4.3. Limitations
This study has several limitations. Firstly, our study
only included cohort studies. Although cohort studies are
often regarded as superior to case–control studies of diet
and chronic disease because of their ability to capture
long-term intake and its long-term effect, they are inferior
at capture recent intake. Coffee might act as a triggering
event in people with chronic CHD, leading to an MI [23].
Cohort studies are inferior at capture this acute or short-
term effect than do case–control studies because coffee
consumption may change over time, and most cohort
studies assessed the CHD risk by coffee consumption at
ten years (median 12 years) ago at baseline rather than by
recent intake. Therefore, the pooling effect in our study
might only reflect the long-term effect on CHD risk. Our
findings suggested that moderate coffee consumption
should have no effect or slightly protective effect on
CHD development in healthy persons. A recent study
also reported that both moderate and heavy coffee
consumption did not increase the risk of MI or CHD
death in persons once they have already developed CHD
[65]. Therefore, coffee consumption should not increase
the cumulative risk of CHD or CHD related events over a
relatively long-term in both health adults and CHD
patients, even if coffee might have adverse acute effect
on CHD.
Secondly, most original studies reported the coffee intake
using the cup. It was possible there was misclassification of
coffee consumption since there was no standard definition of
cup size and volumes may vary among these studies. Thirdly,
we did not discuss group heterogeneity by the type or
method of preparation of coffee because few studies reported
such stratified results. Fourthly, the strong heterogeneity in
some estimates may restrict the stability of this meta-
analysis. Fifthly, the characteristics of events and the referent
coffee group were not always comparable among studies.
Finally, only articles published in the English language were
included.
In conclusion, this meta-analysis does not support the
hypothesis that coffee consumption increases the long-term
risk of CHD. Habitual moderate coffee drinking was
associated with a lower risk of CHD in women.
