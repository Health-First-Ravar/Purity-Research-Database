INTRODUCTION  
Alzheimer’s disease (AD) represents the most prevalent neurodegenerative disorder and is the 
leading cause of dementia worldwide. Such clinical features may simply be the consequence of highly 
accelerated and widespread neuronal destruction, resulting from the cumulative effects of the 
multifactorial primary and secondary degenerative processes characterizing Alzheimer's pathology. The 
pathology of AD is classically defined by the progressive accumulation of extracellular amyloid-beta (Aβ) 
plaques and the formation of intracellular neurofibrillary tangles (NFTs) derived from 
hyperphosphorylated tau protein (Az-zahra et al., 2022; Deyts, Thinakaran & Parent, 2016; Selvaraj et 
al., 2020). A crucial factor in the pathogenesis of this disease is the aggregation of short peptides, amyloid 
beta, with 42 amino acids long. Amyloid beta originates from APP, which remains when APP is cleaved 
by two protease enzymes, such as beta-secretase (BACE-1) and gamma-secretase. Amyloid beta (Aβ) is 
involved amyloidogenic pathway. It undergoes conformational changes to form a tertiary structure of 
beta sheets that aggregate to form amyloid fibrils. These fibrils are deposited outside neurons in dense 
formations known as senile plaques or neuritic plaques. In the normal pathway, called the non-
amyloidogenic pathway. Amyloid beta aggregation leads to neuron death and, consequently, the 
shrinkage of the cerebral cortex and the cognitive impairment that characterize Alzheimer's disease 
(Hampel et al., 2021). Furthermore, the present Aβ plaque is notably upregulated in the brains of AD 
patients and is implicated in mediating crucial pathological processes, including neuroinflammation, 
excitotoxicity, and the regulation of synaptic plasticity that is essential for memory formation. This 
fundamental lack of disease-modifying therapies underscores an urgent and continuous need to identify 
and validate novel compounds capable of interfering with the foundational mechanisms of AD

pathogenesis. Bioactive compounds alternate as agents to target a precursor associated with disease 
(Shobana & Muralidharan, 2024; Martinez, 2024). Further, increasing attention has been directed 
toward modulating lifestyle factors and investigating naturally occurring compounds that exhibit 
neuroprotective properties (Islam et al., 2025).  
Coffee is becoming popular nowadays. It contains some potential bioactive compounds for health 
purposes. Caffeine (1,3,7-trimethylxanthine) is arguably the most widely consumed psychoactive 
substance globally and has emerged as a particularly promising candidate. The intake of caffeine through 
the diet appears to exert protective effects against the progression of dementia, specifically against AD, 
and thus represents a promising therapeutic approach (Zhai & Kwakowsky, 2023). Besides, some 
bioactives such as diterpene, trigonelline, and chlorogenic acid are contained in coffee. Chlorogenic acid 
has shown neuroprotective function via the inhibition of acetyl cholinesterase (Assa et al., 2021; Eticha 
& Bedassa, 2020). And trigonelline has shown neuroprotective effects for Alzheimer’s disease, too (Liang 
et al., 2022). Monoterpenes and diterpenes are reported as neuroprotective agents (Hadipour et al., 
2024), but diterpene cafestol from coffee is underexplored and needs to be investigated its potency for 
APP inhibition. 
Consumption of caffeine has shown many positive effects in various human and animal experiments. 
Moderate caffeine consumption possesses the capacity to abolish AD-related cognitive dysfunction and 
Aβ neuropathology in validated murine models of Alzheimer's disease (Stazi et al., 2022). We assume 
that when drinking coffee, can get an intake of several compounds at once. Therefore, this study aims to 
computationally investigate and characterize the bioactivity of caffeine, diterpene cafestol, chlorogenic 
acid, and trigonelline as neuroprotective agents by employing molecular modelling techniques. 
Specifically, we utilize molecular docking to predict the preferred binding affinities of small molecules 
in coffee within selected AD target active sites, which focus on APP. The results of this in silico approach 
are expected to provide critical structural and energetic insights, offering robust, molecular-level 
validation for the observed neuroprotective effects of caffeine, diterpene, trigonelline, and chlorogenic 
acid, significantly contributing to the design of effective therapeutic leads for Alzheimer's disease. 
MATERIALS AND METHODS  
Prediction of The Small Molecule Structure and Bioavailability 
The two-dimensional (2D) structures of caffeine, chlorogenic acid, trigonelline, and diterpene 
cafestol are predicted with the PubChem online server. The ligand structure of four small molecules was 
predicted using PubChem and visualized in Discovery Studio, then saved in PDB format.  SwissADME 
(https://www.swissadme.ch/) is used to study Physicochemical, pharmacokinetic properties, and drug-
likeness scores. It predicts the estimation of distribution, absorption, metabolism, and excretion (ADME) 
characteristics and potential as drug likeness. Physicochemical parameters include topological polar 
surface area (TPSA), molecular weight, lipophilicity (iLOGP), hydrogen bond donors and acceptors, 
solubility, and rotatable bonds. These properties were assessed in accordance with Lipinski’s rules of 
five (Lipinski, 2004). 
Three-dimensional protein structure prediction of APP 
Amyloid-beta precursor protein (APP) is retrieved from UniProt with accession numbers: P05067-
1 was saved in FASTA format. Amino acid sequences predicted its three-dimensional (3D) structure with 
the PHYRE 2.2 protein fold recognition server and chosen with 100 confidence score and 96 i.d. % 
(Powell et al., 2025). 
Molecular Docking of APP with the small molecules 
It performs blind docking and uses Autodock Vina with PyRx 0.8 8 to compute possible interactions 
between APP and small molecules based on their interaction site (Trott & Olson 2010). The visualization 
of the docking results using Accelrys Discovery Studio 4.0. This analysis will identify interactions 
between receptors and ligands, and preview hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic interactions, and Van Der 
Waals forces, which contribute to the stability of the receptor-ligan complex.

Biological potential and availability prediction 
This in silico study includes the evaluation of caffeine, trigonelline, diterpene, and chlorogenic acid 
as bioactive compounds in coffee, as ligands. Four ligands were analyzed through molecular docking, 
ADME profile, and assessment of biological potential. This multiparametric study will preview the 

Himmah & Dewi. Journal of Coffee and Sustainability| 64 
 
identification of favorable binding interactions with the APP peptide and assess their pharmacokinetic 
and bioavailability. Furthermore, we predict the probability that each compound has potential as a drug-
likeness score. Molecular weight, hydrogen bond donors, acceptors, rotatable bonds, lipophilicity 
(iLOGP), topological polar surface area (TPSA), and solubility were analyzed. Based on Lipinski’s rule of 
five, four ligands have potency, based on their molecular weight <500g/mol and soluble in water (Table 
3). Trigonelline has a lower lipophilicity than others that recorded -3.11, and chlorogenic acid has a low 
drug-likeness score of 0.11. All four ligands have potency in accordance with Lipinski’s Rules of Five. On 
the other hand, Diterpenes cafestol and caffeine have the highest drug-likeness score among others, and 
the drug-likeness score is 0.55. 
In docking results, chlorogenic acid demonstrates superior in-vitro efficacy based on binding 
affinity, and diterpene is in second position. caffeine and diterpene cafestol show advantages in in vivo 
bioavailability criteria (ADME/drug-likeness), diterpene cafestol has the potential to be developed as a 
drug compound, posing as a promising compound than the three others.

     CONCLUSION 
Four bioactives in coffee, as ligands, can bind APP peptides that show potential inhibition based on 
their binding affinities and interactions.  Docking between APP with caffeine in 8 amino acid residues, 
with diterpene in 3 amino acid residues, with trigonelline binds in 3 amino acid and with chlorogenic 
acid binds in 5 residues. The highest binding energy was observed in APP with chlorogenic acid, showing 
a binding energy score of -6.7 kcal/mol. Diterpene cafestol becomes the best candidate based on its 
binding energy -6.5 kcal/mol, has hydrogen bond and hydrophobic bond that interact with APP’s 
residues, and has good bioavailability from five parameters than other ligands. Therefore, future 
research is warranted to explore additional phytochemicals in coffee and other plants that may inhibit

investigation in humans is needed to consolidate the findings and define the significant results.
