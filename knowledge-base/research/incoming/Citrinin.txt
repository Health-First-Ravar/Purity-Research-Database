Introduction
The mycotoxins citrinin (CIT) and ochratoxin A (OTA) 
are produced by some Aspergillus and Penicillium spe-
cies (Frisvad et  al. 2006), and can co-occur as contami-
nants in grains, cereal products and feeds (EFSA 2006, 
2012). CIT and OTA are both nephrotoxic to animals and 
have been implicated in the aetiology of Balkan endemic 
nephropathy (BEN) in humans (Flajs and Peraica 2009; 
Petkova-Bocharova et  al. 1991; WHO/IPCS 2001). OTA 
is carcinogenic in rodents and has been classified by IARC 
as a possible human carcinogen (group 2B), whilst CIT 
is in group 3 (not classifiable as to its carcinogenicity to 
humans) because of limited evidence for carcinogenicity 
in experimental animals (IARC 1986, 1993). Since risks 
for human health from mycotoxins depend upon exposure, 
tolerable intake (health-based guidance) values have been 
derived by scientific committees, and many countries have 
set regulations on maximal mycotoxin levels in food (EU 
Commission Regulation 1881/2006). In the case of OTA, 
EFSA (2006) derived a tolerable weekly intake (TWI) of 
120 ng/kg bodyweight/day, and for different foods, a legal 
limit, e.g. 5 μg OTA/kg for unprocessed cereals and roasted 
and ground roasted coffee beans, was set (EU Commission 
Regulation 1881/2006).

Abstract  Citrinin (CIT) and ochratoxin A (OTA) are 
mycotoxins produced by several species of the genera 
Aspergillus, Penicillium and Monascus. Both can be pre-
sent as contaminants in various food commodities and 
in animal feed. The occurrence and toxicity of OTA and 
human exposure have been intensively studied, but for CIT 
such data are scarce by comparison. Recently, dihydroc-
itrinone (DH-CIT) was detected as main metabolite of CIT 
in human urine, and co-occurrence of CIT and OTA was 
shown in human blood plasma (Blaszkewicz et al. in Arch 
Toxicol 87:1087–1094, 2013). In light of these new find-
ings, we have now investigated the toxicity of the metab-
olite DH-CIT in comparison with CIT and analysed the 
effects of mixtures of CIT and OTA in vitro. The cytotoxic 
potency of DH-CIT (IC50 of 320/200 μM) was distinctly 
lower compared with CIT (IC50 of 70/62 μM) after treat-
ment of V79 cells for 24 and 48 h. Whereas CIT induced 
a concentration-dependent increase in micronucleus fre-
quencies at concentrations ≥30 μM, DH-CIT showed no 
genotoxic effect up to 300 μM. Thus, conversion of CIT 
to DH-CIT in humans can be regarded as a detoxification 
step. Mixtures of CIT and OTA exerted additive effects in 
cytotoxicity assays. The effect of CIT and OTA mixtures 
on induction of micronuclei varied dependent on the used 
concentrations between additive for low μM concentra-
tions and more-than-additive for high μM concentrations. 
Effects on cell cycle were mostly triggered by OTA when 
both mycotoxins were used in combination. The implica-
tions of our and related in vitro studies are discussed with

Biomonitoring can be regarded as complementary and

methods for CIT analysis in human blood and urine (Blasz-

metabolite in several urines of children and adults. This 
metabolite (DH-CIT) was identified previously in a kinetic 
study in rat urine as a minor product (Dunn et al. 1983), but 
has not been characterized so far with regard to its toxic-
ity. To investigate whether CIT metabolism to DH-CIT is a 
toxication or a detoxification reaction, we have now charac-
terized the cytotoxic and genotoxic potency of DH-CIT in 
relation to CIT by neutral red uptake and by micronucleus 
assays in V79 cells.
The new findings from analysis of human blood plasma 
are indicative for co-exposure to both CIT and OTA (Blasz-
kewicz et  al. 2013) and in line with available data from 
dietary analysis (EFSA 2012). As CIT and OTA are both 
nephrotoxic, their co-occurrence also raises the question 
of additive or synergistic toxicity. Therefore, toxic effects 
(cytotoxicity and genotoxicity) induced by binary mixtures 
of CIT and OTA were also investigated since published 
data on combined effects were controversial (see discus-
sion and Table 1). As we can refer to a large database for 
the toxicity of different mycotoxins in V79 cells (Behm 
et al. 2012; Föllmann et al. 2000, 2009), we used this cell 
line for the present study.
Materials and methods
Materials
Citrinin and OTA were obtained from Sigma–Aldrich, 
Taufkirchen, Germany. Dihydrocitrinone was from Ana-
lytiCon Discovery, Potsdam, Germany. All other chemicals 
were purchased in analytical grade from Sigma–Aldrich, 
Taufkirchen, Germany, and sterile plastic materials were 
from Sarstedt, Nümbrecht, Germany. Cell culture media 
and supplements were purchased from PAN, Aidenbach, 
Germany.
Cell culture
V79 cells (Chinese hamster lung fibroblasts) purchased 
from the Deutsche Sammlung von Mikroorganismen und 
Zellkulturen GmbH (Braunschweig, Germany) were rou-
tinely cultured in DMEM medium supplemented with 
10  % (v/v) foetal calf serum (FCS) and 1  % (v/v) peni-
cillin (100  units/mL)/streptomycin (100  μg/mL), in a 
humidified atmosphere of 5 % CO2 at 37 °C. Cells were 
regularly checked for mycoplasma contamination and split 
twice weekly when cultures had reached 80 % confluence. 
V79 cells of passages 20–34 were used in studies on the 
cytotoxicity and genotoxicity of graded concentrations of 
individual compounds and binary mixtures. For treatment 
concentrations in the different assays, see text below and 
Figs. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7 and 8.
Neutral red uptake (NRU) assay
Cell viability was measured by means of NRU, an assay 
well suited to determine cytotoxicity of various compounds 
including mycotoxins (Babich and Borenfreund 1991; 
Behm et al. 2009, 2012; Föllmann et al. 2009). Here, 4,000 
V79 cells were seeded in 200 μl culture medium per well, 
in transparent 96-well microtiter plates and precultured for 
24 h. Culture medium was then withdrawn, and cells were 
treated with graded concentrations of single mycotoxins 
(0.1–100 μM CIT, 0.1–300 μM DH-CIT and 0.1–100 μM 
OTA) or binary mixtures (3, 10 and 20 μM OTA combined 
with 1, 10, 50 and 100  μM CIT, respectively) in culture 
medium with 5 % FCS for 24 or 48 h; the final solvent con-
centration did not exceed 0.1 % (v/v) in mycotoxin-treated 
cultures and concurrent solvent controls. All experiments 
were performed in triplicate (three independent assays) 
with eight replicates for each concentration.
After the treatment period, the medium was withdrawn 
and replaced by fresh culture medium with neutral red 
dye (NR, 0.05 mg/ml). After incubation for 3 h, the cells 
were washed twice with PBS and fixed with 0.2 ml fixa-
tive (glacial acetic acid/water/ethanol, 1:49:50) per well, 
which brings the dye from cells into solution upon 20 min 
of shaking (600  rpm) on a plate shaker (MTS2, IKA® 
Labortechnik, Staufen, Germany). Then, NR absorbance 
was measured with a plate photometer (SpectraFluor Plus 
microplate reader, Tecan, Crailsheim, Germany) at 540 nm; 
NR uptake in treated cells was expressed in per cent rela-
tive to the solvent control (100 %), and IC50 values were 
derived from the concentration–effect curves.

In vitro micronucleus (MN) assay
The MN assay with V79 cells was conducted as described 
previously (Behm et  al. 2009; Dorn et  al. 2008; Föllmann 
et al. 2007) with minor adaptations. Per 25-cm2 flask 5 × 105 
cells were seeded in DMEM medium with supplements (see 
above). On the next day, the culture medium was changed to 
remove non-attached cells. The following day, the medium 
was removed and replaced by fresh culture medium contain-
ing graded concentrations of test compounds. For compari-
son of CIT and DH-CIT, 10–100 μM CIT and 1–500 μM 
DH-CIT were used. For comparison of CIT and OTA and 
their combined effects, CIT was used from 0.1 to 50  μM 
and OTA from 0.1 to 30 μM for treatment of single myco-
toxin and 5, 10, 20 and 30 μM OTA combined with 10, 30 
or 50 μM CIT for binary mixtures. Reference chemicals act-
ing as aneugen (VCR = vincristine 8.33 nM) or as clastogen 
(4-NQO = 4-nitro-quinoline oxide 5 μM) were added from 
DMSO stock solutions. Controls were treated with solvent 
alone (DMSO 0.1 % v/v) in culture medium.
After 24 h (a period of about 1.5 cell cycles for V79 cells 
to allow expression of MN), the medium was removed, and 
cells were detached by disaggregation with trypsin/EDTA 
(0.05  % trypsin/0.02  % EDTA) and then subjected to 
hypotonic conditions with 0.4 % KCl. After centrifugation 
(130×g, 10  min), cell pellets were re-suspended in 5  ml 
ice-cold methanol/acetic acid (3:1) as fixative for 15 min 
at −20 °C. The fixation step was repeated three times, and 
the cell pellet was finally re-suspended in 0.5 ml fixative. 
Some drops of the suspension were transferred onto glass 
slides, and cells were fixed by heat treatment. The slides 
were stained with acridine orange solution (0.05  mg/ml)

Flow cytometry
Analysis by fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) 
was used to quantify the distribution of cells in cell cycle 
phases including apoptotic cells according to established 
methods (Dorn et  al. 2008; Krishan 1975; Riccardi and 
Nicoletti 2006) with minor adaptations. V79 cells (1 × 106 
in 25  cm2 culture flasks) were precultured for 24  h and 
then treated with fresh culture medium containing 5, 10, 
20 and 30 μM of OTA or 10, 30, 50 and 100 μM of CIT, or 
mixtures of OTA and CIT (5, 10, 20 and 30 μM OTA com-
bined with 5, 10, 30 and 50 μM CIT) or the solvent only 
(controls). After the treatment period, first the cell cul-
ture supernatant was collected and centrifuged to include 
also detached cells. The resulting pellet was then com-
bined with the cells harvested after trypsinization (0.05 % 
trypsin/0.02 % EDTA). Cells were suspended in medium 
with 40 % FCS, centrifuged at 200×g for 8 min and then 
resuspended in medium, and cell density was adjusted 
to 1x106 cells/900 μl medium; then, 100 μl DMSO was 
added, and samples were frozen (−80 °C). Prior to FACS 
analysis, the cells were resuspended in 2 ml PBS and cen-
trifuged again (200×g for 8 min 4 °C). The pellet was dis-
solved in 378 μl lysis buffer (consisting of 15 mM Tris, 
2  mM Na2EDTA, 0.5  mM spermine tetrahydrochloride, 
80  mM KCl, 20  mM NaCl, 0.1  % (v/v) Triton X-100 
adjusted to pH 7.5 with HCl and 15 mM 2-mercaptoetha-
nol). Then, 2 μl RNAse (10 mg/ml in distiled water) and 
20 μl propidium iodide (1 mg/ml in distiled water) were 
added, and the samples were incubated for 30 min in the 
dark at 4 °C. Flow cytometry was carried out on a FACS 
Calibur™ (Becton and Dickinson, Heidelberg, Germany) 
with an argon laser (488  nm for excitation), and 10,000 
cells were analysed per sample.
Data were analysed by means of “CellQuest Pro” soft-
ware in histograms of fluorescence intensity (reflecting 
DNA content) versus cell counts. The peak located at rela-
tive fluorescence intensity of 200 corresponds to cells in 
G0/G1 phase with a diploid (2n) DNA content, the peak 
located at 400 (DNA content of 4n) to those in G2/M phase 
and that in between to cells in S phase. For each sample, 
the fraction of cells in the distinct cell cycle phases and 
the percentage of apoptotic cells (broad hypodiploid peak) 
were determined.

Results
Toxicity of citrinin and dihydrocitrinone in vitro
Neutral red uptake assays conducted in V79 cells with 
24-h and 48-h treatment revealed a clearly lower cytotoxic 
potency of DH-CIT compared with the parent compound 
CIT (Fig. 1a, b). The IC50-values for DH-CIT of 320 μM 
(24-h treatment, Fig.  1a) and 200  μM (48-h treatment, 
Fig. 1b) are much higher compared to 70 μM and 62 μM 
for CIT. These results are indicative that the metabolism 
of CIT to DH-CIT can be regarded as a detoxification 
reaction.
Also, micronucleus assays with CIT and DH-CIT 
showed an explicit difference with respect to their geno-
toxic potential. Whereas CIT induced a pronounced 
increase in micronucleus frequency at concentrations 
≥30  μM (Fig.  2a), the treatment with DH-CIT did not 
result in an increased frequency of micronuclei up to con-
centrations of 300 μM (Fig. 2b).
Taken together, these results demonstrate that the metab-
olite DH-CIT exerts a much lower toxicity than the parent 
compound CIT. Because of the low toxicity of its metab-
olite, only CIT was used in further experiments aimed to 
evaluate the effects of mixtures with the mycotoxin OTA.
Toxicity of CIT and OTA mixtures in vitro
The cytotoxicity of CIT and OTA was determined with 
the NRU assay in V79 cells treated for 48 h (Fig. 3). The 
IC50-values obtained for single mycotoxins of 58 μM for 
CIT and 17 μM for OTA show that CIT is less cytotoxic 
compared to OTA. For the following experiments aimed to 
evaluate the effect of combined exposure to CIT and OTA, 
we chose three OTA concentrations which included a low 
concentration that had not induced measurable cytotoxic-
ity (3 μM), a medium concentration (10 μM) that induced 
weak cytotoxicity and a high concentration (20 μM) that 
had induced strong cytotoxic effects in cytotoxicity assay 
of the single agent. These OTA concentrations were com-
bined with different CIT concentrations (1–100 μM) that 
alone exerted no cytotoxicity to high cytotoxicity. We cal-
culated which effect can be expected when we assume an 
additive effect for cytotoxicity. The expected effect values 
were compared with the measured results from the mixture 
experiments in V79 cells (Fig. 4a–c). Since the expected 
degree of cytotoxicity (based on the assumption of an 
additive effect) was not significantly different from values 
measured in the real experiments, we can conclude that the 
effect of the two mycotoxins for the endpoint of cytotoxic-
ity is additive for this cell system.

ity” (based on the effects of individual mycotoxins) and then 
compared with the experimental data. The results are shown 
in Fig. 6 as net increase in micronucleus rate above back-
ground. When a low OTA concentration (5 μM) was com-
bined with increasing CIT concentrations (10, 30 or 50 μM), 
the measured effect was not different from that expected 
for additive action. At higher OTA concentrations (10 and 
20 μM), the measured effect was as expected in the presence 
of 10 μM CIT, but more-than-additive (synergistic) for mix-
tures with 30 and 50 μM CIT. For combinations of a high 
OTA concentration (30 μM) with 10–50 μM CIT, the meas-
ured effect was lower-than-additive. The latter result could 
be due to strong cytotoxicity (with an increase in apoptotic 
cell death) and/or inhibition of cell cycle progression ham-
pering expression of micronuclei. Thus, effects of mycotoxin 
treatments were further analysed by flow cytometry.
Effects on cell cycle
Initially, the effect of CIT and OTA was determined for 
the single compounds by FACS analysis of the entire cell 
population from mycotoxin-treated cultures (Fig.  7). OTA 
(5–30  μM) induced a concentration-dependent increase in 
the cell fraction in G2/M phase, whilst CIT induced a nota-
ble G2/M arrest only at higher concentrations (≥50  μM). 
Similarly, the fraction of cells in subG1 (apoptotic cells) was 
increased in the mycotoxin-treated cultures at concentrations 
found to exert cytotoxicity in V79 cells (see above, Fig. 3).
The results from treatment of V79 cells with OTA/CIT 
mixtures are shown in Fig. 8: combining a given OTA con-
centration (5, 10, 20 or 30 μM) with CIT concentrations 
between 5 and 30 μM had no clear impact on the cell cycle 
(compared to the effect of OTA alone), whilst the higher 
CIT concentration (50 μM) caused a further increase in the 
fraction of cells in G2/M. Taken together, these results from 
flow cytometric analysis show that simultaneous treatment 
with rather high mycotoxin concentrations induced a G2/M 
arrest, which may hamper expression of micronuclei. On 
the other hand, mixtures of individually non-effective or 
weakly effective concentrations of the mycotoxins had no 
additional impact on the cell cycle.
Discussion
As first step to enable biomonitoring studies for an expo-
sure assessment, we have recently developed a sensitive 
method for CIT analysis in human blood and urine: appli-
cation in a pilot study revealed the presence of both CIT 
and OTA in serum of German adults (Blaszkewicz et  al. 
2013). Moreover, we detected DH-CIT as major metabolite 
of CIT in several urines, an interesting finding as nothing 
was known so far about the fate of CIT within the human 
organism. The new biomonitoring data prompted us to 
conduct in vitro studies on (i) the toxicity of the identified 
metabolite in relation to the parental CIT and (ii) the com-
bined effects of CIT and OTA on cells. These results are 
now discussed.
Toxicity of dihydrocitrinone (DH‑CIT) in relation to CIT
Already in 1983, DH-CIT was identified as minor metab-
olite in rat urine (Dunn et  al. 1983), but its toxicity has 
not been characterized since then. In the present in vitro 
study, we have shown that DH-CIT is clearly less cytotoxic 
compared to parental CIT (Fig. 1). Concentrations which 
reduce NR uptake by 50 % (IC50) are 3.2–4.5-fold lower for 
CIT than for its metabolite. Also, the genotoxic potential of 
DH-CIT is much lower compared to CIT as demonstrated 
by micronucleus assays in V79 cells: CIT caused a strong 
induction of micronuclei at 50 μM, whereas only tenfold 
higher levels of the metabolite led to a notable increase in 
micronuclei (Fig. 2). Taken together, these new results sup-
port the conclusion that metabolism of CIT to DH-CIT can 
be regarded as a detoxification reaction.
The CIT concentration range found in this study to effi-
ciently induce micronuclei is comparable to that reported 
in previous studies with V79 cells (Pfeiffer et  al. 1998), 
with HepG2 cells (Knasmüller et al. 2004), HEK 293 cells 
and human lymphocytes (Chang et  al. 2011; Dönmez-
Altuntas et al. 2007). Treatment of cells with 30-40 μM 
CIT or more (50-100  μM with human lymphocytes) 
caused induction of predominantly kinetochore-posi-
tive micronuclei indicative of its aneuploidogenic activ-
ity (Knasmüller et  al. 2004; Pfeiffer et  al. 1998) and a 
decrease in the ratio of binucleated/mono-nucleated cells 
(Dönmez-Altuntas et al. 2007). In all the in vitro studies 
including our own, CIT concentrations lower than 30 μM 
did not elicit an increase in micronucleus frequency or a 
mitotic arrest.
The results show that CIT at high μM concentrations is 
genotoxic to cultured mammalian cells. Yet, in light of its 
mode of action (aneuploidogen) and a lack of mutagenic 
activity demonstrated in other assays (reviewed in EFSA 
2012), a threshold for genotoxicity of CIT could exist at 
low internal concentrations resulting from dietary exposure. 
On the other hand, as consumption of mycotoxin-contami-
nated foods can result in exposure to both CIT and OTA, it 
is important to study also toxicity of mixtures (Speijers and 
Speijers 2004).

Combined effects of CIT and OTA in vitro
The combinatory effects of CIT and OTA have been stud-
ied previously in several cell types by means of different 
endpoints and with variable outcomes (Table  1). When 
concentrations of agents applied in the mixture are already 
effective (e.g. cytotoxic) when tested individually, the out-
come for the mixture is often an additive or synergistic 
(more-than-additive) effect, for example in rat liver cells 
(Creppy et al. 1980), in pig lymphocytes (Bernhoft et al. 
2004) or in various kidney-derived cell lines (Bouslimi 
et al. 2008a, b; Heussner et al. 2006; Klaric et al. 2012). 
But the effect of mixtures also depends on the used con-
centrations in the CIT/OTA combination: for instance, 
Föllmann et al. (2000) observed no additive or synergis-
tic effect in cytotoxicity assays with V79 cells or primary 
urinary bladder epithelial cells for CIT/OTA combinations 
tested at low concentrations of the two mycotoxins (up to 
1 μM each in PUBEC and up to 10 μM in V79 cells) in 
serum-free medium. Similarly, when Knecht et al. (2005) 
applied low concentrations of CIT (0.25 and 1 μM) along 
with 0.025 or 0.05 μM OTA in human proximal tubule cell 
cultures, there was no influence on the OTA-induced cas-
pase 3 activity (apoptosis marker). With 2.5 and 5 μM CIT 
in the mixture, the overall effect was antagonistic, and for 
higher CIT concentrations (7.5 and 15 μM), the combined 
effect was additive. Klaric et al. (2012) found in porcine 
kidney cells for CIT/OTA mixtures (at concentrations that 
exert notable toxicity in serum-free medium), effects from 
antagonistic to additive and also synergistic (Table  1), 
dependent on the endpoint measured (cytotoxicity, apopto-
sis/necrosis or genotoxicity).
In the present in vitro study, the effects of CIT and OTA 
mixtures also varied with the endpoint and the concentra-
tions used: for cytotoxicity (Fig. 4a–c), we found overall 
an additive effect, but for genotoxicity (Fig. 6) the observed 
outcome varied from additive to more-than-additive for 
5 to 20 μM OTA combined with 10–50 μM CIT. But the 
higher OTA concentration of 30 μM combined with CIT 
resulted in a lower-than-additive (antagonistic) effect. The 
latter result resembles antagonistic effects for the CIT/OTA 
mixture in the micronucleus assay described by Klaric 
et  al. (2012). They noted that “the number of micronu-
clei induced by combined OTA + CIT exposure does not 
reflect their real genotoxic potential, since micronucleus 
formation was disabled by high toxicity and increased cell 
death”. On the other hand, CIT may decrease the uptake of 
OTA in cells as observed by Knecht et al. (2005), although 
they noted that this may not be the only explanation for 
an antagonistic effect of CIT at certain concentrations. 
Another explanation for a quasi protective effect of CIT 
may be induction of heat shock protein (hsp 70) in cells, as 
proposed by Bouslimi et al. (2008b).
Expression of micronuclei is hampered when mitotic 
arrest occurs or by increased cell death (vide supra). Thus, 
we examined by FACS analysis the effect of CIT and OTA 
(or mixtures) on cell cycle distribution in V79 cells: OTA 
induced a concentration-dependent increase in apoptotic 
cells and an arrest in G2/M between 5 and 30 μM (Fig. 7), 
whilst a clear effect of CIT alone was only observed at 
concentrations above 50 μM. In HEK293 cells, CIT also 
induced a G2/M arrest between 50 and 100  μM appar-
ently due to its ability to interfere with tubulin polymeri-
zation and microtubule organization (Chang et  al. 2011). 
Also, OTA has been shown to inhibit cell cycle progres-
sion by arresting cells in G2/M phase in several cell lines 
(Cui et al. 2010; Kamp et al. 2005; Palma et al. 2007). As 
further discussed below, also for this endpoint the effec-
tive OTA concentrations varied considerably for cell treat-
ments in the absence or presence of serum in the culture 
medium. In the present study, the effect of binary myco-
toxin mixtures on V79 cells was mainly based on the 
impact of OTA (5–30 μM), whereas an effect of CIT on the 
cell cycle appeared only at high (≥50 μM) CIT concentra-
tions (Fig. 8). It is worth noting that in this and the other 
experiments, treatment concentrations are higher than those 
measured in animals or in humans.
Caveats in an extrapolation of in vitro results to the in vivo 
situation
In vitro studies of hazardous compounds or mixtures usu-
ally use relatively high concentrations of the test agents, 
in our case mycotoxins or their metabolite, to characterize 
the toxic effects on cells. From studies with single com-
pounds discussed above and those for mixtures compiled in 
Table 1, it is evident that the effective OTA concentrations 
clearly differ for cells treated in the absence or presence 
of serum in the culture medium. This is due to the high 
affinity of OTA to serum proteins (Chu 1971; Galtier et al. 
1979) that decreases the fraction of free (unbound) OTA 
available for uptake in the cell. Accordingly, we and others 
found that serum content in cell cultures clearly affects the 
cytotoxic potency (IC50 values) of OTA (e.g. Degen et al. 
1997; Grosse et al. 1995). This explains also much lower 
effective concentrations for OTA in studies on its effects 
in mixtures with CIT when cell is treated in serum-free 
medium compared to studies using serum (Table 1): treat-
ment of cells in serum-free medium with OTA results in 
toxicity already at sub-μM concentrations, whereas about 
20-fold higher (μM) concentrations are found to exert tox-
icity in serum-containing cultures. The effective CIT con-
centrations do not differ so much, as this mycotoxin has no 
strong affinity to serum proteins.
Another point finally to be reflected is the relationship 
between effective concentrations in vitro and the internal

levels resulting from in vivo mycotoxin exposure in humans 
and animals. Although it is difficult to predict intracellular 
concentrations in target organs in vivo, data available for 
blood concentrations can provide some orientation on cir-
culating mycotoxin levels. In a subacute toxicity study with 
CIT in pigs given intragastric doses of 0.02  mg/kg  b.w. 
once daily for a period of 8 weeks, the mean plasma con-
centration varied between 84 (week 6) and 111 (week 8) 
ng/ml (Sándor et  al. 1991). Thus, pig plasma levels of 
0.34–0.44 μM were found after repeated exposure to doses 
considered to correspond to an average CIT contamination 
in feed in Central Europe. Much lower CIT plasma lev-
els (about 0.001 μM) were measured recently in German 
adults (Blaszkewicz et al. 2013; Ali N et al. unpublished 
data). There is a much better data base for internal OTA 
levels in animals and humans. The repeated oral adminis-
tration of OTA doses (70 or 210 μg/kg b.w./day) known to 
cause tumours in the chronic rodent bioassay resulted at 
90 days in plasma concentrations of 2.34 and 7.44 μM in 
male rats (Rached et al. 2007). In another study with male 
rats given orally 120 μg/kg b.w./day, the blood plasma con-
centration at 60 days was 22 μM, but lower (2.31 μM) in 
kidney homogenate (Petrik et al. 2003). In humans, mean 
OTA blood (plasma or serum) levels resulting from dietary 
intake within the European Union range between 0.4 and 
4 nM (EFSA 2006; EC/SCOOP 2002), whilst higher con-
centrations, up to 100 nM (0.1 μM) OTA, have been found 
in BEN patients (Hult et al. 1982; Petkova-Bocharova et al. 
1991). Thus, circulating OTA levels in humans are orders 
of magnitude lower than in experimental animals or con-
centrations applied in in vitro studies for hazard character-
ization. On the other hand, as discussed by Mally (2012) 
in a recent review, there is the possibility of OTA uptake 
in proximal tubule epithelium as an early event leading to 
local enrichment in kidney target cells. Therefore, chronic 
exposure to mycotoxins should be kept low. When dietary 
exposure does not exceed tolerable daily intake values, it 
is highly unlikely that critical internal concentrations are 
reached, or combination effects for CIT and OTA as found 
in vitro will exacerbate their toxicity.
Conclusions
Our comparative data on the cytotoxicity and genotoxicity 
of CIT and DH-CIT clearly indicate that formation of this 
metabolite can be regarded as a detoxification reaction.
The major outcome of our studies on the combined 
effects of CIT and OTA in V79 cells supports the view that 
additive and/or synergistic effects occur only at rather high 
concentrations, far above those observed in vivo in humans 
with “normal” mycotoxin background exposure. In an 
approach to get closer to the in vivo situation, combined 
effects of lower concentrations should be tested, also in 
typical target cells.
