Coffee and Caffeine Protect against Liver Injury Induced by
Thioacetamide in Male Wistar Rats

Abstract: Coffee intake has been inversely related to the incidence of liver diseases, although there are controversies on whether
these beneﬁcial effects on human health are because of caffeine or other speciﬁc components in this popular beverage. Thus, this
study evaluated the protective effects of coffee or caffeine intake on liver injury induced by repeated thioacetamide (TAA)
administration in male Wistar rats. Rats were randomized into ﬁve groups: one untreated group (G1) and four groups (G2–G5)
treated with the hepatotoxicant TAA (200 mg/kg b.w., i.p.) twice a week for 8 weeks. Concomitantly, rats received tap water
(G1 and G2), conventional coffee (G3), decaffeinated coffee (G4) or 0.1% caffeine (G5). After 8 weeks of treatment, rats were
killed and blood and liver samples were collected. Conventional and decaffeinated coffee and caffeine intake signiﬁcantly
reduced serum levels of alanine aminotransferase (ALT) (p < 0.001) and oxidized glutathione (p < 0.05), ﬁbrosis/inﬂammation
scores (p < 0.001), collagen volume fraction (p < 0.01) and transforming growth factor b-1 (TGF-b1) protein expression
(p  0.001) in the liver from TAA-treated groups. In addition, conventional coffee and caffeine intake signiﬁcantly reduced pro-
liferating cellular nuclear antigen (PCNA) S-phase indexes (p < 0.001), but only conventional coffee reduced cleaved caspase-3
indexes (p < 0.001), active metalloproteinase 2 (p  0.004) and the number of glutathione S-transferase placental form (GST-
P)-positive preneoplastic lesions (p < 0.05) in the liver from TAA-treated groups. In conclusion, conventional coffee and 0.1%
caffeine intake presented better beneﬁcial effects than decaffeinated coffee against liver injury induced by TAA in male Wistar
rats.
Introduction
Coffee is one of the most frequently consumed beverages
worldwide, and its moderate intake has been considered to be
beneﬁcial for human health [1,2]. Consumption of more than
three cups per day has been inversely related to the incidence
of liver diseases, particularly because of the potential to reduce
the risk of non-alcoholic fatty disease, ﬁbrosis/cirrhosis and
development of hepatocellular carcinoma in patients with or
without hepatitis B and/or C viruses [3–7]. This popular bev-
erage is a complex mixture that contains a variety of volatile
and non-volatile compounds, including caffeine, chlorogenic
acid and diterpenes cafestol and kahweol [1,2]. However, con-
troversies exist to whether these beneﬁcial effects on human
health are because of caffeine or other speciﬁc components of
coffee [4–7].
Various experimental studies have indicated that coffee per
se or their speciﬁc compounds contain antioxidant properties
and protective effects against oxidative DNA damage, tissue
damage and ﬁbrosis induced by hepatotoxicants in rats and
mice [8–11]. Shi et al. [10] observed a decrease in transform-
ing growth factor b-1 (TGF-b1), vascular endothelial growth
factor (VEGF), collagen I and III and bcl-2 mRNA expression
in the liver from carbon tetrachloride (CCl4)-treated male
Sprague-Dawley (SD) rats receiving instant coffee intragastri-
cally (i.g., 150–300 mg/kg, b.w.). Shin et al. [11] reported
protective effects against liver ﬁbrosis induced by dimethylni-
trosamine (DMN) in male SD rats receiving instant coffee
(3%) or decaffeinated coffee (100–300 m/kg, b.w.). Moreover,
treatment with the diterpenes cafestol and kahweol (10–
100 mg/kg b.w., i.g.) reduced the deleterious effects of CCl4
in male ICR mice [8]. In contrast to the protective effects of
instant or decaffeinated coffee, unﬁltered Turkish coffee intake
increased liver damage induced by CCl4 in male SD rats [12].
Therefore, these ﬁndings on the protective role of coffee
intake on chemically induced liver injury and ﬁbrosis are still
inconclusive and often controversial because of different cof-
fee concentration and preparations used.
Persistent liver injury has been associated with the develop-
ment of inﬂammation, progressive ﬁbrosis, cirrhosis and ulti-
mately hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) [13,14]. Liver ﬁbrosis
is characterized by an increase in deposition of extracellular
matrix (ECM) components, mainly collagens and proteogly-
cans [13–15]. This ECM deposition initially occurs because of
hepatocyte loss by apoptosis or necrosis and inﬂammation
[13,14,16,17]. If liver injury is stopped, regression of ﬁbrosis
occurs by increased activity of collagenases and gelatinases
that will degrade the excessive ECM [13,14,16,17].
Acute liver injury and ﬁbrosis/cirrhosis can be induced
experimentally by various chemicals, such as CCl4, thioacetamide

(TAA), ethionine and DMN [18,19]. TAA is metabolized into
TAA-sulfoxide by CYP 2E1 and/or by the FAD-monooxygen-
ase and further transformed into intermediates and other polar
molecules that induce hepatic necrosis [19,20]. Oxidative
stress has been implicated in the process of ﬁbrogenesis, and
many agents, including TAA, stimulate free radicals formation
[18,19]. Free radicals generated by the bioactivation of TAA
can deplete glutathione (GSH) and other antioxidant defences
in hepatocytes and cause lipid peroxidation [19–21]. Thus,
experimental models of toxic liver injury induced by TAA
may be useful to evaluate the pharmacological effects of can-
didate hepatoprotectants, including coffee and caffeine.
Various epidemiological and experimental studies have
reported beneﬁcial effects of coffee and/or caffeine in the pre-
vention of liver disease, but are still inconclusive and often
controversial. Considering that coffee is one of the most popu-
lar beverages in the world and that repeated liver toxicity can
lead to ﬁbrosis and cirrhosis, which may increase the risk for
HCC, this study aimed to investigate the modifying effects of
conventional and decaffeinated coffee or caffeine on liver
injury induced by repeated TAA administration in male Wistar
rats.
Material and Methods
Animals and treatments. Sixty 4-week-old male Wistar rats were
purchased
from
the
Multidisciplinary
Center
for
Biological
Investigation (CEMIB, UNICAMP, Campinas, SP, Brazil) and housed
in polypropylene cages (ﬁve animals/cage) covered with metallic grids
in a room maintained at 22 ± 2°C, 55 ± 10% humidity and with a
12-hr light: dark cycle. They were fed a commercial rat chow
(LABINA, Purina, Paulínia, SP, Brazil) and tap water ad libitum
during a 2-week acclimatization period. Then, the animals were
randomly allocated into ﬁve groups: one untreated group (G1) and
four
groups
(G2–G5)
treated
intraperitoneally
(i.p.)
with
TAA
200 mg/kg body-weight (b.w.; Co., St. Louis, MO, USA) twice a
week for 8 weeks [22]. Concomitantly, rats received either drinking
water
(G1
and
G2),
freshly
brewed
conventional
coffee
or
decaffeinated coffee (G3 and G4, 8 g of powder in 140 mL of hot
water, then ﬁltered and allowed to cool down to room temperature) or
0.1% caffeine (Sigma-Aldrich Co., St. Louis, MO, USA) in the
drinking water (G5) [22,23]. This dosage of caffeine was chosen
because it is the similar to the concentration of caffeine present in the
coffee administered to the rats. All solutions were prepared daily and
offered ad libitum to the rats in aluminium foil-wrapped bottles to
avoid light decomposition. They were the sole drinking ﬂuid for the
rats during the experimental period. After 8 weeks of treatment, rats
were killed and blood and liver samples were collected. Body-weight
and
food/liquids
intake
were
measured
daily
throughout
the
experimental period. The protocols used were consistent with the
Ethical Principles for Animal Research adopted by the Brazilian
College of Animal Experimentation (COBEA).
Caffeine and chlorogenic acid analysis. Samples of conventional and
decaffeinated coffee were obtained in a local supermarket. A blend
of Coffea arabica and Coffea robusta species cultivated in the states
of Minas Gerais and São Paulo (Brazil), with non-defective beans,
known average granulation, and roasted at a high temperature were
used here. Caffeine and chlorogenic acid content in the conventional
and decaffeinated coffee drunk by animals were analysed by isocratic
high-performing liquid chromatography as previously described [22].
The system consisted of a Constametric Pump, model 3500 (Thermo
Separation Products, Fremont, CA, USA), a manual injector, model
7161 (Rheodyne, Cotati, CA, USA) equipped with a 20-lL loop and
a detector system, model
525 (Lab Alliance, San Jose, CA, USA).
The
signal
produced was
transmitted
to
a
Data
Jet
integrator
(Thermo Separation Products, San Jose, CA, USA). The separation
was achieved on a 10-lm C18 column (Phenomenex, Torrance, CA,
USA). Mobile phase consisted of a 0.2% aqueous metaphosphoric
acid solution:acetonitrile (80:20 v/v) with detection at 330 nm for
chlorogenic acid and 275 nm for caffeine with a ﬂow rate of
1.0 mL/min. [24]. The concentrations of caffeine and chlorogenic
acid were calculated from standard curves prepared with puriﬁed
standards (Sigma-Aldrich, Co.) and data are expressed as mg/mL of
the average from triplicate samples.
Tissue processing, histology and immunohistochemical procedures.
Immediately before necropsy, blood samples were collected and serum
levels of the enzyme alanine aminotransferase (ALT) were measured
spectrometrically to monitor hepatotoxicity [25]. At necropsy, the liver
was removed and weighed, and samples were either ﬁxed in 4%
phosphate-buffered formalin during 24 hr for parafﬁn embedding or
snap-frozen in liquid nitrogen for gelatin zymography and western
blot analysis. Parafﬁn embedded samples were sliced into 5-lm-thick
sections and stained with haematoxylin–eosin (HE) for histological
analysis, stereological-morphometric analysis of collagen ﬁbres by
Picrossirius’s
red
and
for
immunohistochemical
detection
of
glutathione S-transferase placental form (GST-P), proliferating cell
nuclear antigen (PCNA) and cleaved caspase-3, metalloproteinase-2
(MMP-2) and TGF-b1 markers. Liver sections stained for Picrosirius
were analysed in a Leica DMLB 80 microscope connected to a Leica
DC300FX camera and the digitalized images were analysed using the
image analyser Leica Q-win software Version 3 (Leica, Heidelberg,
Germany) [26]. Liver sections stained for HE were evaluated for liver
injury (LIS) and inﬂammation (IS) scores, both classiﬁed on a scale of 0–
3. [27]. A median liver injury score was calculated as follows: (LIS+IS)/2.
GST-P, PCNA, cleaved caspase-3, MMP-2 and TGF-b1 expression
in liver sections was immunohistochemically detected using a polymer
system (NovoLink Max Polymer Detection System; Leica/Novocastra,
Newcastle, UK). Brieﬂy, histological sections were put on poly-L-
lysine-coated slides, deparafﬁnized and rehydrated with graded alcohol
Then, they were subjected to Pascal pressure chamber antigen retrieval
in 0.01 M citrate buffer (pH 6.0) at 120°C for 3 min. (MMP-2 and 9
and caspase-3 antibodies) or a pre-treatment with 0.25% trypsin
(Sigma-Aldrich) for 15 min. (TGF-b1). Slides were treated with 3%
H2O2 in phosphate-buffered saline for 15 min., non-fat milk for
60 min., anti-PCNA (clone PC10, 1:200 dilution; Dako Corporation,
Carpinterie, CA, USA), anti-GST-P 7-7 (clone 311, 1:1000 dilution;
Medical and Biological Laboratories Co., Tokyo, Japan), anti-MMP-2
(clones C-19, 1:200 dilution; Santa Cruz Biotechnology, CA, USA),
anti-TGF-b1 (clone 9016, 1:50 dilution; R&D Systems, Minneapolis,
MN, USA), cleaved anti-caspase-3 (EUA, 1:200 dilution; Cayman
Chemical Co., MI, USA) antibodies for 16 hr at 4°C, after-primary
blocked for 30 min. and polymer for 30 min. Chromogen generation
was achieved with 3,3-diaminobenzidine tetrahydrochloride (DAB;
Sigma-Aldrich Co.) as the substrate to demonstrate the sites of
peroxidase binding. The slides were counterstained with Harris’s
haematoxylin.

Gelatin zymography for MMP-2. Frozen liver samples (300 mg) were
mechanically homogenized with a Polytron homogenizer in 50 mM

containing 30 lg protein were subjected to electrophoresis in 8% SDS

gelatin substrate was present at 0.1% ﬁnal concentration in the gel.
The gels (0.75 mm thick) were electrophoresed for 2 hr at 100 V,
4°C, in a Bio-Rad MiniProtean II system (Bio-Rad Laboratories, Inc.,
Richmond, CA, USA). After electrophoresis, the gels were washed by
gentle shaking at room temperature with 2.5% Triton X-100 (two
changes) for 1 hr. Gels were incubated overnight (16 hr) in 50 mM
Tris–HCl buffer (pH 8.4) containing 5 mM CaCl2 at 37°C. After
incubation, gels were stained with Coomassie Brilliant Blue R-250.

Goat anti-b-actin antibody (1:1,000; Santa Cruz Biotechnology) served
as

After

were further probed with corresponding HRP-conjugated antibodies
and detected by means of chemiluminescence (Pierce® ECL Western
Blotting Substrate; Bio-Rad Laboratories, Hercules, CA, USA). TGF-

analysis of the bands as integrated optical density (IOD). The TGF-b1

Statistical analysis. Statistical analysis was performed using the
Jandel Sigma Stat Software (Jandel Corporation, San Rafael, CA,
USA). Data from body-weight, body-weight gain, absolute and
relative liver, PCNA-labelling and apoptosis indexes, GST-P-positive
PNL and IOD (MMP-2 and TGF-b1) and median liver injury scores
were analysed by One-Way ANOVA or a nonparametrical test (Kruskal–
Wallis). The incidence of liver damage and inﬂammation parameters
was examined using v2 test or the Fischer exact test. Data were
considered statistically signiﬁcant when p < 0.05.
Results
General ﬁndings, ALT levels, liver injury, GSH and GSSG
levels.
There was a signiﬁcant reduction in body-weight gain and
food and liquid intake in all TAA-treated groups (G2–G5),
throughout the experimental period, when compared with
untreated control group (G1) (p < 0.001) (table 1). Caffeine
and chlorogenic acid concentrations in conventional coffee
were 1005 and 414 lg/mL (~ 0.1 and 0.04%) and in decaf-
feinated coffee were 90 and 284 lg/mL (~ 0.009 and 0.03%).
The average daily intake of caffeine and chlorogenic acid was
19 and 8 mg/rat/day in the conventional coffee group (G3),
1.7 and 5 mg/rat/day in the decaffeinated group (G4) and 17
and 0 mg/rat/day in the 0.1% caffeine group (G5). After
8 weeks, all TAA-treated groups (G2–G5) presented lower body-
weight, absolute liver weight and serum ALT levels than the
untreated control group (G1) (p < 0.001) (table 2). Groups receiv-
ing conventional and decaffeinated coffee and 0.1% caffeine (G3–
G5) had signiﬁcantly lower serum ALT levels than the untreated
control group TAA only-treated group (G1) (p < 0.001, table 1).
Repeated TAA treatment induced liver injury mainly char-
acterized by hepatocyte death, inﬂammation, ductal and oval
cells proliferation and collagen deposition. Groups receiving
conventional and decaffeinated coffee and 0.1% caffeine
(G3–G5) had signiﬁcantly lower serum ALT levels than the
TAA only-treated group (G2) (p < 0.001, table 1). Resulting
in a lower median liver injury score (p < 0.001) in these
groups (ﬁg. 1, table 2). In Picrossirius staining, a clear reduc-
tion (p < 0.01) in collagen ﬁbres volume fraction (%) was
observed in the liver from conventional and decaffeinated cof-
fee and 0.1% caffeine-treated groups (G3–G5) when compared
with the TAA only-treated group (G2) (ﬁgs 1 and 2).
A decrease in reduced glutathione (GSH) associated with an
increase in oxidized glutathione (GSSG) levels were observed
in the liver from the group treated with TAA only (G2) when
compared with the untreated control group (G1) (p < 0.03 and
p < 0.04, respectively) (ﬁg. 3). Groups receiving conventional
coffee and 0.1% caffeine (G3 and G5) presented attenuation
in GSH levels when compared with the TAA only-treated
group (G2) (p < 0.05).

Discussion
This study evaluated the protective effects of conventional
and decaffeinated coffee beverages or caffeine intake against
liver injury induced by repeated TAA administration in male
Wistar rats. A clear hepatoprotective effect of conventional
and decaffeinated coffee beverages and caffeine was observed
for the most of the biochemical and morphological changes
induced by TAA. Importantly, the doses used here (equivalent
to approximately 3–4 cups of coffee per day) are similar to
that postulated to have beneﬁcial effects against human liver
diseases, including ﬁbrosis, cirrhosis and HCC [2,31].
Experimental studies have shown that repeated TAA admin-
istration induces liver damage, ﬁbrosis and/or cirrhosis associ-
ated with hepatocyte death and activation of Kupffer cells
(KC) and hepatic stellate cells (HSCs) [19,20,32]. The TAA
regimen used in this study induced liver damage characterized
by high ALT and GSSG levels, PCNA and cleaved caspase-3
labelling indexes, inﬂammation and ﬁbrosis with collagen
deposition, MMP-2 activation and increased TGF-b1 protein
expression. These effects induced by repeated TAA adminis-
tration were attenuated by conventional and decaffeinated cof-
fee and caffeine intake, as demonstrated by histological and
biochemical analyses, cell proliferation and apoptosis indexes,
but mainly by TGF-b1 protein expression, a well-known prof-
ibrotic agent [33,34]. These ﬁndings conﬁrm the beneﬁcial
effects of coffee and against liver injury induced by other
hepatotoxicants [8–11,35]. On the other hand, this study is the
ﬁrst to demonstrate in a single experiment the protective
effects of the most popular coffee beverages adjusted by same
caffeine concentration and roasting process.

TGF-b1 is a proﬁbrogenic cytokine produced by inﬂamma-

Thus, a decrease in TGF-b1 protein expression in the groups
receiving conventional and decaffeinated coffee and caffeine
might be linked to the reduction in collagen deposition and
ﬁbrosis. Gressner et al. [36] reported that caffeine strongly
down-modulates expression of TGF-b-induced connective tis-
sue growth factor (CTGF) in the hepatocytes by stimulating the
degradation of SMAD 2 (a TGF-b effector), inhibition of
SMAD3 phosphorylation and up-regulation of peroxisome pro-
liferator-activated receptor gamma (PPAR-c). Interestingly, a
reduction in hepatic TGF-b1 expression in the group receiving
decaffeinated coffee indicates that caffeine may not be the
solely hepatoprotective compound in coffee beverages. Never-
theless, caffeine seems to play an important role against liver
injury because the scores for ﬁbrosis/inﬂammation were lower
in the group receiving conventional coffee and caffeine. It is
possible that this additional protective effect of caffeine may be
due to its action as a non-selective adenosine A (2A) receptor
antagonist during TAA toxicity, because adenosine is a potent
endogenous regulator of inﬂammation and tissue repair [37].

The ability of caffeine to modulate key enzymes of the
hepatic microsomal system, especially induction of CYP1A2
in human beings and CYP2C in mice or rats, has been
reported [40]. As consequence, coffee and caffeine have the
potential to interfere with the metabolism of various drugs and
hepatotoxicants and can modify the balance between activation
and excretion of these substances [41]. Caffeine is metabolized
in the liver by the cytochrome P450 enzyme system into dim-
ethylxanthines mainly by CYP1A2 enzymes [40,41]. Because
90% of caffeine metabolism is carried out by CYP1A2
enzymes, it is unlikely that caffeine interferes with TAA phase
I biotransformation, because this is carried out mainly by
CYP2E2 [20,21]. On the other hand, coffee and caffeine, dit-
erpenes cafestol and kahweol and other compounds lead to a
wide spectrum of increases in phase II detoxiﬁcation enzymes
including sulfotransferase, UDP-glucuronosyl transferase and
glutathione transferase and peroxidase [42–44]. Glutathione is
the most abundant low molecular weight tripeptide thiol in liv-
ing cells and exists in reduced (GSH) and oxidized (GSSG)
states and their GSH/GSSG ratio has been used as a biomarker
of cellular toxicity [45]. The results of this study indicate that
conventional and decaffeinated coffee and caffeine treatments
can act as antioxidant agents by preventing hepatic TAA-
induced GSH depletion, a marker of the oxidative stress at the
cytosolic level [45]. This attenuated oxidative stress by coffee
beverages is not restricted to caffeine alone and could be due
to its antioxidant properties mediated by Nrf2-ARE pathway
stimulation of phase II enzymes related to detoxiﬁcation and
antioxidant defences [46].
GST-P expression is used as a potential marker for hepatic
pre-neoplastic and neoplastic lesions in male Wistar rats
[28,29]. Short- and medium-term TAA treatment can induce
the development of few PNL in the liver of non-initiated male
rats [47]. A signiﬁcant reduction in the number of GST-P-
positive PNL in the group receiving conventional coffee sug-
gests a potential protective effect against liver carcinogenesis,
as previously reported [21,47]. However, a suitable evaluation
of the protective efﬁcacy of coffee beverages and caffeine
against different stages of carcinogenesis might be better war-
ranted in other models of rat liver carcinogenesis [48,49].
In conclusion, the ﬁndings of this study indicate that treat-
ment with conventional and decaffeinated coffee and 0.1%
caffeine
was
hepatoprotective
against
TAA-induced
liver
injury in male Wistar rats. Groups receiving conventional
coffee
and
0.1%
caffeine
showed
a
more
pronounced
hepatoprotection than the group receiving decaffeinated cof-
fee, suggesting a special role of caffeine. Coffee is a com-
plex
chemical
mixture
that
may
contain
more
than
a
thousand different compounds, including carbohydrates, lip-
ids, nitrogenous compounds, vitamins, minerals, alkaloids,
phenolic compounds, many unknown molecules, collectively
called melanoidins, and more than 800 volatile compounds
mainly formed during the roasting process of coffee beans
[2]. Although caffeine is thermally stable to the roasting pro-
cess, the protective effects of coffee drinking on liver dam-
age
may
not
be
restricted
to
caffeine
and
additive
or
synergic effects among compounds might exist and the roast-
ing process appears to be critical for the chemopreventive
properties of coffee [2,50]. Thus, further studies are neces-
sary to evaluate the intrinsic mechanisms involved in the he-
patoprotective action of coffee beverages or their speciﬁc
compounds.
