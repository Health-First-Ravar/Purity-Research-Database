A B S T R A C T
The liver, which is essential for metabolism and detoxification, is vulnerable to various damaging factors, 
including viral infections, excessive alcohol consumption, and a diet high in fat and sugar. Coffee, one of the most 
widely consumed beverages worldwide, is valued not only for its flavor and aroma but also for its potential 
health benefits. In this context, the protective effects of its consumption against liver diseases have been 
extensively studied. Epidemiological, experimental, and clinical evidence suggests that regular coffee con­
sumption reduces the risk of liver disease and slows its progression to fibrosis, cirrhosis, and hepatocellular 
carcinoma. These benefits are attributed to its complex composition comprising various bioactive compounds 
such as caffeine, polyphenols, and diterpenes, which have anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, antifibrotic, and 
anticancer properties. Coffee mitigates liver damage and slows disease progression by modulating cellular re­
sponses, reducing oxidative stress, and restoring metabolic homeostasis. Several studies have demonstrated that 
coffee and its bioactive compounds regulate key pathways involved in inflammation, oxidative stress, and 
fibrosis while modulating hepatic lipid metabolism and insulin resistance. Furthermore, coffee can alter the 
composition of the gut microbiota, contributing to its hepatoprotective effects. Additionally, coffee consumption 
has been associated with lower liver enzyme levels, less liver structural damage in patients with various liver 
diseases, and reduced liver disease-related mortality. This narrative review explores the impact of coffee con­
sumption on liver health and discusses epidemiological evidence, cellular and molecular mechanisms, and 
clinical implications for the prevention, treatment, and management of liver diseases.

1. Introduction
The liver is essential for systemic homeostasis. As the main metabolic 
center of an organism, the liver plays a fundamental role in the pro­
cessing of macronutrients, detoxification of xenobiotics, and synthesis of 
essential biomolecules. It stores glucose in the form of glycogen and 
regulates its release through gluconeogenesis during fasting, ensuring 
stable blood glucose levels [1–3].
Hepatocytes are responsible for key processes in lipid metabolism, 
including the oxidation of fatty acids for the production of adenosine 
triphosphate, the synthesis of cholesterol and lipoproteins, and the 
formation of bile salts that are essential for lipid digestion and absorp­
tion. In addition, the liver is crucial for protein metabolism, producing 
most circulating plasma proteins, such as albumin and coagulation 
factors, and eliminating nitrogenous waste through the urea cycle. It 
also metabolizes heme in senescent erythrocytes into bilirubin, which is 
excreted in bile along with fat-soluble vitamins and minerals [1–3].
In addition to its metabolic functions, the liver regulates the blood 
volume, modulates immune responses, and maintains lipid and choles­
terol homeostasis. Its remarkable ability to detoxify xenobiotics (such as 
drugs, alcohol, and environmental pollutants) underscores its vulnera­
bility to various insults, such as exposure to toxic substances; viral in­
fections (e.g., hepatitis A, B, and C); and metabolic disorders such as 
diabetes, obesity, dyslipidemia, and autoimmune or genetic diseases. As 
a result, liver diseases have become a global health crisis, with a prev­
alence that is continuously increasing [1–3].
1.1. Liver damage, inflammation, fibrosis, and cirrhosis
Liver fibrosis and cirrhosis represent progressive stages of chronic 
liver damage leading to irreversible liver dysfunction and severe com­
plications, such as portal hypertension and hepatocellular carcinoma 
(HCC) [4]. Both conditions arise because of chronic inflammation trig­
gered by various insults to the liver, including viral infections, excessive 
alcohol consumption, high-fat and high-sugar diets, autoimmune and 
cholestatic diseases, and exposure to toxins and drugs [5]. Under­
standing the epidemiology and molecular mechanisms of these diseases 
is crucial for the development of effective prevention and treatment 
strategies.
In the context of chronic liver damage, nuclear factor kappa B (NF- 
κB) plays a central role in the establishment of an inflammatory 
microenvironment in the liver by regulating the transcription of proin­
flammatory genes, such as interleukins (IL)-1β and IL-18, as well as 
tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α) [6,7]. Persistent liver inflammation 
leads to the development of liver fibrosis, a pathology characterized by 
the excessive accumulation of extracellular matrix (ECM) components, 
particularly collagen [8]. The central event in this process is the acti­
vation of hepatic stellate cells (HSCs), which store vitamin A in their 
quiescent state but acquire a myofibroblast-like phenotype in response 
to inflammatory and profibrotic signals [8].
Under certain conditions, fibrosis can be partially alleviated through 
the apoptosis and senescence of activated HSCs, together with the 
degradation of the ECM by matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) and other 
antifibrotic factors. However, in advanced stages, the fibrinolytic 
response is insufficient, promoting its progression to cirrhosis [9]. Pro­
fibrotic cytokines, such as transforming growth factor-beta (TGF-β), 
platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF), TNF-α, and IL-1β, are among the 
main factors inducing HSC activation and are released in response to 
chronic hepatocellular injury and apoptosis. In addition, the activation 
of Toll-like receptors (TLRs) by damage-associated molecular patterns 
(DAMPs) promotes the production of inflammatory cytokines, whereas 
oxidative stress mediated by reactive oxygen species (ROS) amplifies 
proinflammatory and fibrogenic signals [9]. When fibrosis becomes 
extensive and disruptive, liver cirrhosis occurs, which is characterized 
by regenerative nodules formed by hepatocytes surrounded by collagen 
bands that damage the liver architecture. This pathological remodeling 
leads to portal hypertension, hepatocellular dysfunction with a reduc­
tion in the synthesis of essential proteins (albumin and coagulation 
factors, among others), and an increase in intrahepatic vascular resis­
tance, increasing the risk of gastroesophageal variceal bleeding. In 
addition, cirrhosis predisposes patients to the development of HCC 
through mutations and chronic cellular stress [8–11].
The burden of the disease varies significantly by region and etiology. 
For example, viral hepatitis is the main cause of cirrhosis in regions of 
high endemicity, such as Asia and Africa; alcohol-induced liver disease 
(ALD) is predominant in Eastern Europe and countries with high alcohol 
consumption; and the incidence of metabolic dysfunction-associated 
steatotic liver disease (MASLD) has increased rapidly due to the 
epidemic of obesity and type 2 diabetes, especially in North America and 
the Middle East [12–15].
1.2. Coffee
In recent years, increasing interest in dietary factors influencing liver 
health has revealed that coffee, one of the most widely consumed bev­
erages worldwide, may be a potential hepatoprotective agent. Coffee is 
produced from the roasted seeds of plants belonging to the genus Coffea 
and family Rubiaceae. The most widely cultivated and marketed species 
are Coffea arabica and Coffea canephora (robusta), which make up the 
majority of coffee plants grown for global coffee production. These 
plants grow in the tropical and subtropical regions of Latin America, 
Africa, and Southeast Asia, where the climatic conditions favor their 
cultivation [16].
From a chemical perspective, coffee is a complex mixture of bioac­
tive compounds that contribute to both its sensory profile and its 
physiological effects. Among the most representative compounds of 
coffee are caffeine, an alkaloid with central nervous system-stimulating 
properties; chlorogenic acids, a group of polyphenols; and diterpenes 
(cafestol and kahweol), lipids present in coffee beans. In addition, coffee 
contains hundreds of other compounds, including carbohydrates, lipids, 
amino acids, proteins, vitamins, and minerals [17,18]. Both individually 
and in combination, they exert diverse pharmacological effects, such as 
antioxidant, 
anti-inflammatory, 
antifibrotic, 
anticancer, 
and 
metabolism-regulating effects, which can attenuate liver injury and 
promote liver repair [19–21]. Epidemiological studies have consistently 
reported that regular coffee consumption is associated with a lower risk 
of liver diseases and their complications, such as fibrosis, cirrhosis, and 
HCC [22,23].
In this review, we use the term ‘high coffee consumption’ to refer to 
the intake of three or more cups per day, in line with definitions applied 
in large cohort studies and meta-analyses.
Emerging evidence has sparked increasing interest in elucidating the 
molecular mechanisms underlying these benefits. Preclinical and clin­
ical research suggests that the effects of coffee on liver function could be 
mediated by the attenuation of oxidative stress, reductions in proin­
flammatory and profibrogenic cytokine levels, increases in insulin 
sensitivity, and the regulation of gut‒liver axis dynamics [24–26]. These 
findings provide a solid basis for considering coffee as a potential dietary 
intervention for the prevention and treatment of liver diseases.
Given the central role of the liver in systemic homeostasis and its 
susceptibility to various insults, understanding how modifiable lifestyle 
factors, such as coffee consumption, affect the liver represents a prom­
ising avenue to mitigate the burden of liver diseases. This narrative re­
view aims to synthesize the available evidence on the impact of coffee 
consumption on liver diseases, the mechanisms underlying its hep­
atoprotective effect, and its clinical implications.
2. Relationships between liver diseases and coffee consumption
2.1. Viral hepatitis and its relationship with coffee consumption
Viral liver infections, particularly those caused by hepatitis B virus

(HBV) and hepatitis C virus (HCV), continue to be major causes of liver 
morbidity and mortality worldwide. The World Health Organization 
estimates that more than 254 million people are living with chronic 
hepatitis B, making it one of the infectious diseases with the largest 
burden worldwide [27]. Additionally, approximately 50 million people 
are infected with HCV, which often leads to chronic hepatitis and serious 
complications, including cirrhosis and HCC, if left untreated [27].
Chronic hepatitis, particularly hepatitis HCV and HBV infection, is a 
leading cause of liver transplantation worldwide and is strongly asso­
ciated with high rates of progression to advanced fibrosis, liver failure, 
and HCC. HCV infection, in particular, poses a significant public health 
challenge because of the lack of markers for early diagnosis and limited 
access to antiviral treatments in resource-limited settings [12,28].
In this context, recent research suggests that coffee consumption may 
have a protective effect on liver complications associated with these 
viral infections.
Several recent epidemiological studies have highlighted the inverse 
association between coffee consumption and the severity of liver dam­
age caused by viral hepatitis [22,23,29–35]. Compared with no coffee 
consumption, regular coffee consumption is associated with signifi­
cantly lower rates of progression to fibrosis and cirrhosis in individuals 
with chronic HCV infection [23]. Similarly, other analyses revealed that 
HBV-infected individuals who consumed more than two cups of coffee 
daily had a reduced incidence of HCC [22,29].
Moreover, coffee consumption has been linked to changes in 
biochemical markers of liver health in patients with viral hepatitis. 
Coffee intake correlates with decreased levels of alanine aminotrans­
ferase (ALT) and aspartate aminotransferase (AST) among HCV-positive 
individuals [23]. Likewise, epidemiological analyses support the po­
tential of coffee to reduce the viral load and mitigate hepatic inflam­
mation in patients with chronic hepatitis B and C [29].
Collectively, evidence from longitudinal studies and meta-analyses 
underscores the hepatoprotective effects of coffee consumption. For 
example, Kennedy et al. reported a dose-dependent inverse association 
between coffee intake and the risk of HCC in patients with chronic viral 
hepatitis [30]. Similarly, Barr´e et al. reported that regular coffee con­
sumption was linked to reduced levels of liver fibrosis biomarkers in 
individuals with chronic hepatitis B [31].
A study designed to analyze the relationship between coffee intake 
and the efficacy of antiviral treatment in patients infected with HCV 
yielded significant results [32]. The findings indicated that patients who 
consumed three or more cups of coffee per day were three times more 
likely to respond positively to treatment with peginterferon and riba­
virin than were those who did not consume coffee. This association 
remained consistent even after adjusting for various clinical, behavioral, 
and genetic factors, suggesting an effect independent of other known 
risk factors. The authors proposed that coffee could enhance the treat­
ment response through mechanisms associated with the signaling 
pathway mediated by the IL-28 protein encoded by the IL28B gene and 
the Janus kinase–signal transducer and activator of transcription (STAT) 
pathway. This pathway is particularly relevant, given that the com­
pound kahweol, which is found in coffee beverages, has been linked to it 
[32].
To date, no prospective studies have specifically evaluated the 
impact of coffee consumption on treatment outcomes in patients 
receiving direct-acting antivirals, such as sofosbuvir, velpatasvir, or 
grazoprevir. Most of the available evidence regarding clinical associa­
tions and mechanistic pathways has been derived from cohorts treated 
with interferon-based regimens, particularly interferon and ribavirin 
[32]. Although the hepatoprotective properties of coffee are well sup­
ported, currently, no direct evidence or formal reports describing the 
interactions between coffee consumption and modern antiviral thera­
pies are available.
However, some patients with chronic hepatitis C are either ineligible 
for antiviral therapy or choose to forego treatment owing to contrain­
dications, limited access, or concerns about side effects. In this context, 
adjunct strategies such as regular coffee consumption have improved 
biochemical parameters among individuals infected with the hepatitis C 
virus [33].
In a prospective cohort study conducted in a large Japanese popu­
lation, coffee consumption was associated with a significant reduction in 
the risk of developing liver cancer, both in the general population and in 
individuals infected with HCV and/or HBV [34].
Another study investigated whether the intake of polyphenol-rich 
foods, such as coffee, and daily chocolate consumption by human im­
munodeficiency virus (HIV)–HCV-coinfected individuals could alter 
liver enzyme levels [35]. The results revealed that high coffee con­
sumption, defined as the intake of three or more cups per day, and daily 
chocolate intake were associated with a lower likelihood of abnormal 
levels of the enzyme ALT. Furthermore, patients who reported high 
coffee consumption were less likely to have abnormal AST enzyme levels 
[35].
These biochemical improvements are associated primarily with 
caffeine and chlorogenic acids, two of the most abundant and bioactive 
compounds in coffee [36]. While their precise role in chronic viral 
hepatitis remains under investigation, preclinical studies suggest that 
they exert hepatoprotective effects by reducing oxidative stress, inhib­
iting proinflammatory signaling, and modulating hepatic metabolism 
[37,38]. Diterpenes such as kahweol and cafestol may also influence the 
expression of fibrogenic genes and cytokines [39–41]. However, direct 
clinical evidence linking individual coffee components to improvements 
in serum transaminase levels remains limited [39–41].
Individuals with chronic HBV infection are at high risk of liver dis­
ease progression, which is positively associated with metabolic disorders 
such as diabetes and hypertension. However, dyslipidemia may be 
inversely associated with liver disease progression, likely due to 
impaired hepatic lipid synthesis in advanced stages of liver damage; this 
finding does not suggest a protective effect of dyslipidemia but rather 
reflects hepatic dysfunction [42]. High coffee consumption has been 
associated with an increased risk of dyslipidemia and a lower risk of 
hypertension in HBV-infected patients; together, these effects are likely 
related to favorable clinical outcomes in this population [42].
Similarly, high coffee consumption was consistently associated with 
a lower risk of significant liver fibrosis, as assessed by three noninvasive 
markers, the AST/platelet ratio, the fibrosis-4 index, and the gamma- 
glutamyl transferase (GGT)/platelet ratio, in patients with chronic 
HBV infection [31].
However, some studies have not obtained evidence that coffee con­
sumption influences the progression of chronic hepatitis B in North 
American cohorts [43]. Although high cumulative consumption of cof­
fee throughout life is associated with a reduction in the risk of devel­
oping HCC in patients with chronic hepatitis B, this protective effect 
appears to be attenuated in patients with high viral replication [44].
The evidence suggests that coffee consumption may be beneficial in 
patients with chronic HBV infection. A population-based cohort study in 
Taiwan showed that regular coffee intake was associated with a signif­
icantly slower progression of liver fibrosis in hepatitis B e antigen- 
negative individuals over 5 years of follow-up [45]. Consistent with 
these findings, data from the ANRS CO22 Hepather cohort in France 
revealed that elevated coffee consumption was inversely associated with 
the levels of noninvasive biomarkers of liver fibrosis in patients 
receiving antiviral treatment for HBV [31].
Additional cross-sectional studies support these findings. For 
example, coffee intake of more than two cups per day was associated 
with significantly lower liver stiffness in patients with chronic HBV 
infection, as measured by transient elastography [46]. Furthermore, a 
case‒control study of chronic HBV infection carriers revealed that 
moderate coffee consumption significantly reduced the risk of HCC 
depending on the consumption level [47]. These observations are 
further supported by a comprehensive review of clinical data indicating 
that coffee may exert protective effects on various stages of HBV-related 
liver disease, including fibrosis, cirrhosis, and HCC (20).

2.2. Alcoholic liver disease and its relationship with coffee consumption
ALD is a leading cause of liver-related morbidity and mortality 
worldwide. It represents a spectrum of liver disorders induced by 
chronic and excessive alcohol consumption, ranging from alcoholic fatty 
liver disease to alcoholic hepatitis, progressive fibrosis, cirrhosis, and, in 
advanced cases, HCC [48]. Of these conditions, alcoholic hepatitis and 
cirrhosis are of particular concern, as they are associated with high 
short- and long-term mortality rates [48,49].
Alcohol metabolism in the liver generates toxic byproducts, such as 
acetaldehyde, which trigger direct cellular damage and promote chronic 
inflammation. Furthermore, excessive alcohol consumption alters the 
gut microbiota, which promotes bacterial translocation and inflamma­
tion through the gut‒liver axis [41]. These processes perpetuate a cycle 
of damage that exacerbates the progression of ALD.
ALD has a significant socioeconomic impact, as it primarily affects 
people of working age. According to recent data, alcohol consumption 
contributes to more than 50 % of deaths from liver disease in Europe and 
North America, placing a considerable burden on health systems 
[12,51]. Although alcohol abstinence is the mainstay of treatment, 
many people face psychological and social challenges that hinder 
adherence, highlighting the need to explore complementary strategies to 
reduce risk and mitigate disease progression.
Several epidemiological studies have documented an inverse asso­
ciation between coffee consumption and the development or progres­
sion of ALD. These investigations suggest that coffee may play a 
protective role in people who consume alcohol, reducing both the risk of 
significant liver damage and the severity of associated complications 
[23,52–63].
For example, Setiawan et al. reported an inverse association between 
regular coffee consumption and the development of advanced liver 
diseases, including ALD. This effect was attributed to bioactive com­
pounds in coffee, such as polyphenols and diterpenes, which exhibit 
antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties [52]. In a study by Saab 
et al., regular coffee consumption was associated with a reduction in the 
levels of liver damage markers in individuals who consumed alcohol 
chronically, suggesting that the progression to liver fibrosis or cirrhosis 
was slowed [23]. A meta-analysis by Liu et al. evaluated multiple 
observational studies and concluded that coffee intake is consistently 
associated with a lower risk of developing alcoholic cirrhosis, even when 
controlling for factors such as the amount of alcohol consumed [53]. 
That the study by Ruhl and Everhart suggested that coffee may modulate 
oxidative stress and systemic inflammation in patients with high alcohol 
consumption, thereby reducing hepatocellular damage and progression 
to advanced ALD [54]. These findings are consistent with more recent 
research suggesting that phenolic compounds and caffeine can reduce 
the levels of inflammatory biomarkers, such as TNF-α, which are 
implicated in the pathogenesis of ALD [55–57]. The study by Bravi et al.,

Several studies have evaluated the impacts of alcohol consumption 
and coffee intake on liver enzyme levels, which serve as indirect markers 
of liver damage [58–63]. In a study conducted in a population of 
Swedish women, the higher the daily alcohol consumption was, the 
higher the levels of the enzymes GGT and AST were. However, these 
effects were smaller or even nonexistent in women who consumed more 
than two cups of coffee per day [58].
In another study conducted in a Japanese population, the results 
revealed that the consumption of more than 3 cups of coffee was asso­
ciated with a reduction in GGT activity in men, particularly those with 
high alcohol consumption. In addition, coffee intake also showed an 
inverse relationship with aminotransferase activity, similar to that 
observed with GGT, which led the authors to suggest that coffee could 
exert a protective effect on alcohol-induced liver damage [59]. In a 
longitudinal study of French patients coinfected with HIV and HCV, the 
impact of coffee and alcohol consumption on advanced liver fibrosis was 
analyzed. The findings showed that higher coffee consumption was 
associated with a lower risk of advanced liver fibrosis, especially in 
patients with high alcohol consumption [60]. This result supports the 
hypothesis that coffee may protect against alcohol-associated liver 
damage. Furthermore, a previous study suggested that coffee con­
sumption may reduce the risk of alcoholic cirrhosis and other liver 
complications [60]. Coffee consumption was also associated with lower 
levels of the liver enzymes AST, ALT, and GGT, reinforcing the idea that 
coffee has beneficial effects on liver health, especially in patients with 
chronic liver disease [60]. In a cross-sectional study of 259 patients, the 
effects of different amounts and durations of coffee consumption on liver 
enzyme levels and liver tissue structure were analyzed in healthy in­
dividuals, patients with hepatic steatosis, patients with metabolic 
dysfunction-associated steatohepatitis (MASH), and individuals with a 
history of chronic alcohol consumption. The results revealed that both 
regular and long-term coffee consumption (for more than five years) 
were associated with significant decreases in ALT, AST, and GGT levels, 
alleviating liver structural damage, particularly in patients with alco­
holic steatosis. Furthermore, in patients with a history of alcoholism, 
coffee consumption partially normalized ALT and GGT levels. Although 
various coffee preparation techniques have been studied, the results 
suggest that the amount and duration of daily consumption have a more 
significant impact than the type of preparation used [61].
Taken together, these findings suggest that coffee could play an 
important role in inhibiting liver damage induced by excessive alcohol 
consumption (Fig. 1).
2.3. Metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease and its 
relationship with coffee consumption
The term MASLD has replaced the term nonalcoholic fatty liver 
disease (NAFLD), as it is considered a more accurate and inclusive term 
[64,65]. Different authors consider that the differences between NAFLD 
and MASLD are minimal [66], and thus for this review, we mention the 
findings of previous research on NAFLD under the new name MASLD. 
MASLD represents a broad spectrum of liver abnormalities, ranging from 
simple hepatic steatosis (fatty liver) to more advanced stages, including 
MASH, fibrosis, cirrhosis, and even HCC in some cases [67].
The global prevalence of MASLD is staggering, with an estimated 25 
% of the global population affected, making it the most common chronic 
liver disease worldwide [68]. This condition is intricately linked to 
increasing rates of obesity, insulin resistance, and other metabolic dis­
orders, which contribute to an increasing burden of liver-related 
morbidity and mortality [65–68].
Given the lack of effective pharmacological therapies for MASLD, 
particularly in more advanced stages such as MASH, lifestyle modifi­
cations, including diet and exercise, are the primary approaches for 
managing the disease [69]. In this context, emerging research suggests 
that coffee consumption may play roles in the prevention of and pro­
tection against the progression of MASLD.
The relationship between coffee consumption and MASLD has 
attracted increasing attention, with several studies suggesting that cof­
fee might play a protective role in the development and progression of 
hepatic fat accumulation and fibrosis [70–76]. As one of the most 
prevalent chronic liver diseases worldwide, MASLD is closely linked to 
metabolic dysfunction, particularly in individuals with obesity, insulin 
resistance, and type 2 diabetes. In this context, several epidemiological 
studies have provided substantial evidence supporting the hypothesis 
that coffee consumption might be inversely associated with MASLD 
development and progression and that the bioactive compounds in 
coffee may exert hepatoprotective effects [70–76].
For example, large-scale epidemiological studies have revealed a 
significant inverse relationship between coffee consumption and the 
prevalence of MASLD. A study by Kaur et al. indicated that increased 
coffee consumption was associated with a decreased risk of hepatic fat 
accumulation in individuals with metabolic risk factors, such as obesity 
and insulin resistance [70]. Similarly, Ebadi et al. reported that coffee 
consumption was associated with a 35 % decrease in the probability of 
liver fibrosis development in patients with MASLD, although no asso­
ciation was found between coffee consumption and the incidence or 
prevalence of MASLD [71]. On the other hand, regular coffee drinkers 
presented lower levels of liver enzymes, suggesting that coffee could 
mitigate inflammation and liver fibrosis in patients with MASLD [72].
Another relevant example is the systematic review conducted by 
Sewter et al., which aimed to investigate the relationship between coffee 
consumption and the progression of MASLD. The results suggested that 
the frequency of coffee consumption is inversely associated with the 
severity of liver fibrosis in people with MASLD. Similarly, compared 
with espresso coffee drinkers, regular filtered coffee drinkers were found 
to have a lower risk of developing liver fibrosis [72]. This finding was 
consistent with the results of another systematic review that investi­
gated the associations between coffee consumption and the incidence of 
MASLD in the general population, as well as the alleviation of liver 
fibrosis in patients with MASLD [73]. Most of the studies analyzed 
showed that individuals who regularly consumed coffee had a lower 
incidence of MASLD than those who did not consume it. Among patients 
with MASLD, coffee consumption was significantly associated with 
alleviated liver fibrosis [73]. Furthermore, another study revealed that 
coffee consumption was inversely correlated with hepatic fat accumu­
lation, liver enzyme levels, and inflammatory marker levels, suggesting 
that bioactive compounds in coffee, such as caffeine and polyphenols, 
might be key factors involved in reducing liver inflammation and stea­
tosis [61].
Additional research has shown that coffee consumption is beneficial 
not only for the prevention of MASLD but also for its management. Pa­
tients with MASLD who consumed moderate to high amounts of coffee 
had slower disease progression and were less likely to develop severe 
liver conditions such as fibrosis [73,74]. These results are consistent 
with other studies that documented a lower incidence of liver fibrosis in 
patients with MASLD who consumed coffee than in those who did not 
[75–78].
In a study that assessed MASLD using the fatty liver index and 
fibrosis using Fibroscan, increased consumption of both caffeine and 
decaffeinated coffee was associated with a decreased severity of MASLD 
in patients with type 2 diabetes and overweight or obesity [79]. 
Importantly, this study also revealed that noncaffeine coffee compo­

coumaric acid, were inversely associated with markers of liver fat and 
fibrosis, suggesting that these bioactive compounds, along with diter­
penes, may contribute to the attenuation of disease activity [79]. Simi­

developing liver cirrhosis and HCC in patients with MASLD [84–86]. 
One example is a study by Zheng et al., who showed that regular coffee 
drinkers had a significantly lower risk of developing HCC than non­

Taken together, these studies provide strong evidence for the pro­

its therapeutic effects on disease severity in patients with MASLD

2.4. Hepatocellular carcinoma and its relationship with coffee 
consumption
HCC is the most common primary liver neoplasm and one of the main 
causes of morbidity and mortality worldwide. It originates in hepato­
cytes, and its development is closely linked to chronic liver diseases, 
such as cirrhosis, chronic viral hepatitis, and MASLD. HCC is a highly 
aggressive tumor whose early diagnosis remains a challenge, since it is 
often asymptomatic in its initial stages, which delays the initiation of 
potentially curative treatments [87,88].
The incidence of HCC is particularly high in Asia and Africa. More­
over, according to the Global Cancer Observatory, this neoplasm is 
responsible for approximately 700,000 deaths annually, and its inci­
dence continues to increase, especially in regions with a high burden of 
chronic hepatitis [89,90]. In Western countries, the increases in obesity, 
type 2 diabetes, and MASLD rates have emerged as a determining factor 
in the increasing prevalence of HCC in recent decades [91–93].
A distinctive feature of HCC is its close relationship with liver 
cirrhosis, which is present in 80–90 % of HCC patients. Cirrhosis con­
stitutes a microenvironment conducive to carcinogenesis due to 
continuous cell regeneration and the chronic inflammatory state of the 
diseased liver [87,94]. The pathogenesis of HCC is multifactorial and 
involves persistent inflammation, oxidative stress, genomic instability, 
and the dysregulation of cell proliferation- and apoptosis-related path­
ways [95].
Early detection of HCC is essential, as it enables the early application 
of treatments with curative intent, such as liver resection, liver trans­
plantation, or radiofrequency ablation. However, most patients are 
diagnosed at advanced stages, which drastically reduces the number of 
therapeutic options and compromises long-term survival. Abdominal 
ultrasound is the recommended surveillance method in patients with 
cirrhosis and chronic hepatitis; however, its sensitivity is limited, 
particularly in individuals with fatty liver [96,97].
The therapeutic approach for treating HCC depends on the tumor 
stage and liver function. In the early stages of HCC, surgical resection or 
liver transplantation results in the best survival rates, but most patients 
are diagnosed at advanced stages of the disease in which the tumor is 
unresectable, requiring the use of systemic therapies. Given this sce­
nario, prevention plays a crucial role [96,97]. Several studies suggest 
that coffee consumption may be associated with a significant reduction 
in the risk of developing HCC, providing new possibilities for the pre­
vention and management of this devastating disease [55,98–104].
Regular coffee consumption is associated with a decreased risk of 
developing HCC, even after adjusting for confounders such as age, sex, 
alcohol consumption, and viral status [55,100]. A meta-analysis by Bravi 
et al., which included 16 observational studies, revealed a 40 % 
reduction in the risk of developing HCC in individuals who consumed at 
least two cups of coffee daily [55]. This protective effect showed a clear 
dose‒response pattern, indicating that the greater the amount of coffee 
consumed was, the greater the degree of risk reduction was [55]. 
Similarly, a more recent meta-analysis corroborated these findings, 
highlighting that both caffeinated and decaffeinated coffee have com­
parable benefits in reducing the risk of HCC [30].
Prospective studies have verified this inverse association between 
coffee consumption and the incidence of HCC. The European Prospec­
tive Investigation into Cancer and Nutrition study, which included more 
than 500,000 participants from various European nations, revealed that 
the consumption of three or more cups of coffee per day was correlated 
with a significant decrease in the risk of developing HCC; additionally, 
the levels of inflammatory markers such as IL-6 and liver biomarkers 
such as AST, GGT, and total bilirubin were lower in consumers than in 
nonconsumers [101].
The results of studies of individuals in Asia, a region with a high 
burden of HCC due to the prevalence of hepatitis B and C, coincide with 
those reported above. A Japanese study involving over 90,000 in­
dividuals followed for over 10 years reported that regular coffee 
drinkers had a 38 % lower risk of developing HCC than nondrinkers 
[102]. Similarly, a study conducted in South Korea revealed that coffee 
consumption was associated with a lower prevalence of MASLD [103].
The benefits of coffee are not limited to the general population; they 
have also been documented in individuals with chronic liver disease. In 
patients infected with HCV, coffee consumption was found to signifi­
cantly slow the progression to cirrhosis and HCC [104].
The relationship between coffee consumption and a decreased risk of 
HCC has been validated in multiple meta-analyses of international 
studies. A systematic review by Kennedy et al. concluded that regular 
coffee consumption is consistently associated with a reduction in mor­
tality from liver diseases, including HCC, even after adjusting for vari­
ables such as alcohol consumption, body mass index, and smoking [99]. 
Another meta-analysis by Larsson et al. revealed that coffee consumption 
is associated with a lower risk of liver cancer in both individuals with a 
history of liver disease and the general population [22].
In addition to liver-specific outcomes, several epidemiological 
studies and meta-analyses have reported inverse associations between 
coffee consumption and the incidence of other malignancies, including 
colorectal, postmenopausal breast, endometrial, and prostate cancers 
[105–108]. These observations further support the systemic anti- 
inflammatory, antioxidant, and metabolic regulatory effects of the 
bioactive compounds of coffee, reinforcing its potential role in chronic 
disease prevention.
Coffee is emerging as an accessible and promising intervention for 
the prevention of HCC, with studies supporting its potential to reduce 
the risk of this lethal form of liver cancer, suggesting that it represents a 
unique opportunity in the fight against a disease that continues to be a 
global public health challenge (Fig. 1).

coffee
Multiple molecular and cellular mechanisms through which coffee 
exerts its beneficial effects on liver health have been identified. These

3.1. Antioxidant properties
Oxidative stress is a central pathogenic mechanism in liver diseases 
and plays a crucial role in cellular damage and liver dysfunction 
[109–111]. In viral infections such as HCV and HBV infections, hepa­
tocytes generate increased amounts of ROS, which exacerbate liver 
damage [109–111]. Similarly, the excessive consumption of alcohol, 
fats, and sugars promotes the overproduction of ROS, contributing to the 
pathogenesis of ALD, as well as MASLD and its inflammatory form 
MASH [109–113].
Coffee contains various bioactive compounds with antioxidant 
properties [19–21]. Caffeine, one of its main components, has been 
shown to increase the activity of endogenous antioxidant enzymes such 
as superoxide dismutase (SOD), catalase (CAT), and glutathione perox­
idase (GPx), which neutralize ROS and protect against oxidative damage 
[114,115]. In addition, experimental evidence indicates that coffee and 
caffeine can modulate the activation of transcription factors related to 
the antioxidant response, such as nuclear factor-erythroid 2 related 
factor 2 (Nrf2), a key regulator of antioxidant gene expression 
[116,117]. Activation of this pathway reduces the accumulation of ROS 
in the liver, increasing the resistance of hepatocytes to oxidative stress.
Another group of antioxidant compounds present in coffee is 
chlorogenic acids, which not only neutralize ROS but also stimulate the 
activity of endogenous antioxidant enzymes such as SOD, CAT, and GPx, 
mitigating cellular damage and protecting hepatocytes from alcohol- 
induced injury [86,118,119]. Consistent with these findings, preclini­
cal studies have shown that the administration of coffee and its com­
pounds, such as caffeine and nicotinic acid, inhibits lipid peroxidation 
and the formation of oxidative damage products, such as malondialde­
hyde and 4-hydroxynonenal, in animal models of MASH, fibrosis, and 
cirrhosis induced by thioacetamide (TAA) and carbon tetrachloride 
(CCl4), as well as in models of secondary biliary cirrhosis [120–122]. 
These results highlight the potential of coffee as a modulator of oxida­
tive stress in various liver pathologies. Moreover, NADPH oxidase 
(NOX), one of the main sources of ROS in the fibrotic liver, represents a 
relevant therapeutic target in the management of oxidative stress [123]. 
Caffeine and coffee polyphenols have been shown to inhibit NOX ac­
tivity, reduce ROS production, and attenuate oxidative damage 
[124,125]. Furthermore, diterpenes found in coffee beans, such as 
cafestol and kahweol, induce the expression of phase II detoxifying en­
zymes, favoring the elimination of potential carcinogens in hepatocytes 
[126].
Taken together, these findings indicate that the bioactive compounds 
in coffee act synergistically through multiple molecular mechanisms to 
exert antioxidant and hepatoprotective effects. These mechanisms 
include the direct neutralization of ROS, induction of detoxifying 
enzyme systems, inhibition of pro-oxidant enzyme activity, and modu­
lation of key cellular processes. These actions contribute significantly to 
the prevention and management of various liver diseases (Fig. 3).

present in coffee beverages play crucial roles in modulating the in­
flammatory response [127–135]. Among these compounds, chlorogenic

compounds in various types of liver damage [120]. Similarly, coffee

systemic inflammatory markers, such as C-reactive protein and IL-6, two 
biomarkers widely used to assess generalized inflammation, in patients 
with atrial fibrillation [135]. These findings suggest that coffee not only 
acts locally in the liver but also systemically modulates the inflamma­

agement of chronic liver diseases. Through its bioactive compounds, 
such as caffeine and chlorogenic acids, coffee has significant potential to 
modulate liver inflammation. These compounds affect various cell 
signaling pathways involved in the activation of immune cells, the 
production of inflammatory cytokines, and the progression of liver 
fibrosis (Fig. 4).

the ECM and actively contribute to the progression of liver fibrosis

The hepatoprotective properties of coffee and caffeine have been

antifibrotic potential in treating different types of experimental liver

induced liver fibrosis. Caffeine is proposed to inhibit the cyclic adeno­

experimental study suggested that caffeine can modulate this signaling 
pathway independently of its action on A2AR [137]. Furthermore, the 
balance between apoptosis and autophagy is crucial for maintaining 
liver homeostasis [138]. Caffeine induces apoptosis in HSCs, the main 
ECM-producing cells, promoting the degradation of damaged organelles 
and preventing the accumulation of ECM components [138].
Several experimental studies in different liver injury models have 
highlighted the potential of caffeine to reduce hepatic collagen levels 
and fibrotic areas in the liver [38,139–143]. Caffeine prevents the pro­
gression of liver fibrosis by decreasing the expression of TGF-β, CTGF, 
and α-SMA and inhibiting the activation of MAPK and the phosphory­
lation of Smad3 [139]. Similarly, caffeine reduces the mRNA levels of 
these profibrogenic proteins. In zymography assays, caffeine has also 
been shown to modulate the activity of MMPs 2 and 9 [140]. Addi­
tionally, isochlorogenic acid, another compound present in coffee, 
modulates the expression of key proteins involved in fibrogenesis, such 
as TGF-β, by inhibiting the AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK)/

the transcription of genes related to inflammation and fibrosis [141]. 
Similarly, the coffee diterpene kahweol displays antifibrotic properties 
by interfering with TGF-β signaling, inhibiting HSC activation and pro­
liferation, and thereby reducing the synthesis of collagen and other ECM 
components [41]. Chlorogenic acids act by inhibiting PDGF, CTGF, and 
TGF-β signaling; limiting HSC migration and proliferation; and 
decreasing the expression of genes such as those encoding tissue in­

Decreased TGF-β levels could be linked to the attenuation of oxidative 
processes, resulting in decreased HSC activation and reduced ECM 
deposition. These findings suggest a possible role for nicotinic acid as an 
antifibrotic agent in liver injury [121].

3.4. Modulation of metabolism
Metabolic dysfunction and insulin resistance are common comor­
bidities in patients with chronic hepatitis. In particular, HCV infection is 
associated with various extrahepatic manifestations, including alter­
ations in glucose and lipid metabolism [143]. The strong association 
between HCV and diabetes mellitus is due to multiple mechanisms, as 
the virus can interfere with the insulin signaling pathway in hepatocytes 
and pancreatic cells [144]. Furthermore, insulin resistance has been 
identified as a key factor in the progression of chronic hepatitis C, 
contributing to the development of steatosis, the progression of fibrosis, 
and a reduced response to antiviral therapy [145]. Similarly, in MASLD, 
excessive triglyceride accumulation in hepatocytes results from an 
imbalance between the synthesis, storage, and oxidation of fatty acids. 
This metabolic alteration is associated with insulin resistance, hyper­
insulinemia, and chronic inflammation, favoring the progression to 
more advanced stages of liver damage, such as steatohepatitis and 
fibrosis [64,65].
Various bioactive compounds in coffee have been shown to have 
beneficial effects on hepatic metabolism. Chlorogenic acids, for 
example, increase insulin sensitivity by modulating the insulin signaling 
pathway in hepatocytes. In preclinical models, these compounds have 
been shown to reduce insulin resistance by regulating key proteins 
involved in signal transduction, such as insulin receptor substrate 1 and 
phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase, thus promoting glucose uptake and lipid 
homeostasis in the liver [146,147]. Furthermore, other coffee compo­
nents, 
such 
as 
caffeine, 
cafestol, 
and

also 
exert 
hepatoprotective effects by reducing lipid accumulation, increasing in­
sulin sensitivity, and exerting antioxidant effects [62]. The impact of 
these bioactive components on insulin sensitivity has direct implications 
for the prevention of hepatic steatosis, a common complication of 
chronic hepatitis, especially in patients with hepatitis C and MASLD 
[72,143–146].
A key mechanism of the hepatoprotection conferred by coffee is its 
effect on regulating lipid metabolism. Chronic alcohol consumption al­
ters this regulation, promoting the accumulation of triglycerides and the 
deposition of free lipids, contributing to hepatic steatosis, an early stage 
of ALD [148]. In contrast, coffee has been shown to beneficially 
modulate the sterol regulatory element binding protein 1c (SREBP-1c) 
signaling pathway through the activation of AMPK, a key energy sensor 
involved in the regulation of lipid and glucose metabolism [149,150]. In 
the context of MASLD, this modulation reduces lipid accumulation in 
hepatocytes and could counteract hepatic steatosis. Furthermore, 
caffeine decreases the expression of lipogenic factors such as SREBP-1c 
and acetyl-CoA carboxylase, both of which are essential for fatty acid 
synthesis. Reduced levels of these factors decrease lipogenesis and pre­
vent excessive fat accumulation in the liver [150].
Taken together, the metabolic effects of coffee and its bioactive 
compounds indicate their potential therapeutic roles in the prevention 
and treatment of hepatic metabolic disorders. However, although recent 
findings are promising, further studies are needed to clarify its mecha­
nisms of action and applicability in the treatment of metabolic liver 
diseases (Fig. 6).

antifibrotic properties, which could alter the metabolic profile and 
reduce liver damage [155]. Although these epigenetic mechanisms still

better understand coffee-induced hepatoprotection.
3.6. Inhibition of viral replication
The caffeine present in coffee can also exert a direct antiviral effect 
on the liver. Some studies suggest that caffeine may interfere with HCV 
replication, although the underlying mechanism has not yet been fully 
elucidated [156]. Additionally, research indicates that the consumption 
of at least three cups of coffee per day is associated with a reduction in 
circulating levels of HBV DNA, although further studies are needed to 
confirm these findings in clinical models [157].
Emerging evidence suggests that the bioactive components of coffee 
might influence cellular pathways relevant to viral replication. In 
particular, caffeine and chlorogenic acids have been shown to modulate 
intracellular signaling cascades such as the AMPK/mammalian target of 
rapamycin (mTOR), phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (PI3K)/protein ki­
nase B, and MAPK cascades [158,159], which are often exploited by 
hepatotropic viruses to support their replication [160,161].
For example, in the case of HCV, the induction of incomplete auto­
phagy facilitates viral persistence by preventing the formation of 
degradative autolysosomes [162]. By promoting complete autophagic 
flux through AMPK activation and mTOR complex 1 inhibition, caffeine 
may counteract this mechanism [158].
However, no direct evidence has shown that coffee or its compounds 
inhibit the replication of HBV or HCV in hepatocytes or in clinical set­
tings. Therefore, these mechanistic insights should be interpreted as 
hypothesis-generating rather than conclusive. Further experimental and 
clinical studies are needed to determine whether these effects translate 
into measurable antiviral activity in patients with chronic viral hepatitis.
Although mechanistic studies provide valuable insights into the 
hepatoprotective effects of coffee, most are based on in vitro systems or 
animal models, which may not fully replicate human physiology. The 
experimental doses often exceed those achievable through regular 
consumption, and species-specific differences in hepatic metabolism 
may limit clinical applicability [163]. Moreover, the bioavailability of 
key compounds such as chlorogenic acids is influenced by first-pass 
metabolism and gut microbial transformation, and their in vivo activ­
ity depends on brewing methods and individual variability [26,164]. 
These factors underscore the need for pharmacokinetic studies and 
clinical trials to validate the translational relevance of the preclinical 
findings.
Despite these translational limitations, in vitro and in vivo studies 
are essential for elucidating the molecular mechanisms underlying the 
hepatoprotective effects of coffee. These experimental models are useful 
for investigating specific pathways, such as antioxidant responses, the 
modulation of inflammatory mediators, fibrogenic signaling, and 
apoptosis regulation, which are difficult to assess in human studies 
because of confounding variables. Moreover, animal models provide an 
indispensable platform for exploring dose‒response relationships, 
identifying key bioactive compounds, and assessing potential toxicity 
before clinical trials can be justified. Thus, while clinical validation is 
necessary, mechanistic research constitutes a critical foundation for 
understanding how coffee components interact with hepatic cells and 
systems.
4. Conclusions, future perspectives and limitations
The reviewed epidemiological, clinical, and experimental evidence 
supports the protective effect of coffee on various liver diseases. Its 
regular consumption is associated with a lower incidence and progres­
sion of MASLD, ALD, chronic viral hepatitis, fibrosis, cirrhosis, and HCC 
(Table 1).
The bioactive compounds in coffee, such as caffeine, chlorogenic 
acids, 
diterpenes, 
and 
polyphenols, 
exert 
antioxidant, 
anti- 
inflammatory, antifibrotic, and hepatic metabolism-modulating ef­
fects, contributing to the attenuation of hepatocellular damage. These 
findings reinforce the consideration of coffee as a coadjuvant dietary 
strategy in the prevention and management of liver diseases, although 
its clinical impact should be interpreted with caution according to the 
risk profile of each patient.
Despite the accumulated evidence, some areas require further 
research. Longitudinal studies are needed to assess the long-term effects 
of coffee consumption on different liver disease phenotypes, as well as 
its interaction with dietary, pharmacological, and genetic factors. In 
addition, determining the effects of different coffee preparations and the 
optimal dose to obtain hepatoprotective benefits is crucial.
In particular, the dose–response relationship of the hepatoprotective 
effects of coffee deserves further investigation. While the available ev­
idence supports a dose-dependent hepatoprotective effect, defining an 
exact intake remains challenging due to variability in the coffee volume, 
composition, and preparation methods, and interindividual metabolic 
differences.
Numerous studies have demonstrated a dose-dependent relationship 
between coffee consumption and hepatoprotective outcomes, with 
benefits typically observed at intake levels of at least three cups per day 
[30,100]. However, the definition of one cup varies widely, and factors 
such as the coffee species, origin, roasting, and preparation significantly 
influence the concentration of bioactive compounds [165,166]. As a 
result, individuals consuming the same number of cups may ingest 
different doses of active constituents (Table 2). Future research should 
quantify coffee intake using standardized units (e.g., mg of caffeine or 
chlorogenic acids) and report the preparation methods to improve the 
comparability among different studies. Moreover, the minimum dura­
tion of consumption required to achieve liver benefits remains unclear; 
long-term habitual intake appears protective, but prospective studies are

The elucidation of epigenetic mechanisms and the modulation of the 
intestinal microbiota could open new therapeutic avenues. Likewise, the 
identification of specific biomarkers related to coffee metabolism would 
allow its consumption to be personalized based on the genetic and 
metabolic profile of each individual. Although recent data suggest a 
dose-dependent protective effect, specifying the ideal amount and fre­
quency is necessary to maximize hepatoprotection without adverse 
effects.
Given its low cost and high availability, coffee could represent a 
simple and viable dietary intervention in the management of liver dis­
eases, especially in high-risk populations. Likewise, coffee should not be 
considered in isolation but rather in the context of other modifiable 
lifestyle factors. Combining coffee consumption with healthy dietary 
patterns, regular physical exercise, and reduced alcohol consumption 
could enhance its hepatoprotective benefits and contribute to better 
overall liver health.
However, although the findings are promising, the available infor­
mation presents certain limitations. The methodological heterogeneity 
of the studies makes it difficult to compare the results and draw defin­
itive conclusions. Most epidemiological studies are observational, which 
prevents the establishment of direct causality between coffee con­
sumption and liver benefits. Additionally, confounding factors, such as 
diet, lifestyle, and the consumption of other hepatoprotective sub­
stances, may influence the findings.
Moreover, how coffee interacts with genetic predispositions, the 
intestinal microbiota, and other lifestyle factors is still uncertain. Ran­
domized controlled clinical trials evaluating not only the effects on the 
liver but also the possible side effects and their interactions with existing 
therapies are needed to overcome these limitations.
4.1. Adverse effects of coffee
While the hepatoprotective effects of coffee are supported by a 
growing body of epidemiological and experimental data, identifying 
potential risks, particularly excessive consumption, is critical. Caffeine is 
the primary component associated with adverse effects, including anx­
iety, insomnia, gastrointestinal discomfort, and tachyarrhythmia [167]. 
Although daily intake of up to 400  mg of caffeine (approximately four 
cups of coffee) is generally considered safe for most healthy adults, 
adverse effects may occur at lower doses in individuals with caffeine 
hypersensitivity, pre-existing cardiovascular disease, or anxiety disor­
ders [167]. Importantly, the estimated acute lethal dose of caffeine in 
adults ranges from 5 to 10 g, whether ingested orally or administered 
intravenously [168,169].
Unfiltered coffee preparations contain relatively high concentrations 
of diterpenes (cafestol and kahweol), which can increase serum low- 
density lipoprotein cholesterol levels and potentially contribute to dys­
lipidemia, especially when consumed in quantities exceeding five cups 
per day [170,171]. These considerations underscore the importance of 
tailoring recommendations based on individual risk profiles.
Not all studies report consistent benefits. For example, a North 
American cohort study did not show a significant association between 
coffee consumption and the progression of chronic hepatitis B [43]. This 
discrepancy may reflect disease-specific pathophysiology, variability in 
coffee consumption within the cohort, differences in the chemical pro­
file of the coffee consumed, ethnic diversity, and residual confounding 
by lifestyle factors [43]. These findings highlight the complexity of 
generalizing results across populations and emphasize the need for well- 
controlled prospective studies that account for these variables.
Moderate coffee consumption (2–4 cups per day) may be encouraged 
in individuals with MASLD, chronic hepatitis C, or compensated 
cirrhosis, provided that the patients have no contraindications. Obser­
vational and mechanistic studies support its potential role in attenuating 
liver fibrosis progression and reducing the risk of HCC in these pop­
ulations [46,71,76,104] (Table 2).

Special populations warrant particular attention in the context of 
coffee consumption. In pregnancy and lactation, caffeine intake should 
not exceed 200 mg/day, as caffeine crosses the placenta, accumulates in 
the fetus due to reduced clearance, and may contribute to low birth 
weight or neonatal irritability [167,168]. Patients with arrhythmias, 
anxiety, or insomnia are particularly sensitive to both dose and timing of 
caffeine; late-day or high-dose consumption increases the likelihood of 
palpitations, sympathetic activation, and sleep disturbances. In 
advanced liver diseases, including cirrhosis and portal hypertension, 
impaired hepatic metabolism prolongs caffeine half-life and may exac­
erbate hemodynamic instability [167,168].
Regarding drug interactions, caffeine is metabolized predominantly 
by cytochrome P450 1A2 and may be affected by agents that inhibit or 
induce this isoenzyme, including fluoroquinolones, fluvoxamine, and 
oral contraceptives [172]. Although these interactions are typically 
mild, clinicians should be aware of the potential for altered caffeine 
metabolism in patients with polypharmacy, particularly those with he­
patic dysfunction.
Coffee consumption represents a modifiable lifestyle factor with the 
potential to mitigate the progression of liver diseases and improve the 
clinical outcomes of diverse populations. Integrating mechanistic 
knowledge with clinical evidence will be crucial for defining its role in 
the prevention and management of these pathologies, with the ultimate 
goal of optimizing dietary and therapeutic strategies to reduce the global 
burden of liver disease. Overcoming these limitations will require more 
detailed clinical trials and preclinical studies to establish a foundation 
for future clinical applications.
