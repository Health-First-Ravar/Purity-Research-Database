Survey of imported green coffee beans for pesticide residues
RICHARD M. JACOBS† and NORMA J. YESS‡
†Food and Drug Administration, 50 UN Plaza Federal Office Building, San Francisco,
CA 94102, USA; ‡Food and Drug Administration, 200 C Street, SW, Washington, DC
20204, USA
(Received 31 December 1992; accepted 11 March 1993)
The US Food and Drug Administration carries out incidence/level monitoring in order to
acquire data on the presence and amounts of pesticide residues in particular
commodity/chemical combinations. In the survey reported here, imported green coffee
beans were analysed for a variety of pesticide chemicals. A total of 60 green coffee samples
were collected from 21 countries that are major exporters of coffee to the United States.
The samples were analysed for organochlorine/organophosphorus, N-methyl carbamate,
benomyl group and EBDC residues. Four samples had detectable residues: chlorpyrifos,
0.01, 0.02 and 0.04 ppm and pirimiphos-methyl, 0.01 ppm. The majority (93%) of the
green coffee samples analysed in this survey had no detectable pesticide residues.
Keywords: pesticide residues, coffee beans, contaminant monitoring
Introduction
The US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) is responsible for enforcing
tolerances for pesticide residues on foods. These tolerances, the maximum amounts
of a residue that are permitted in or on food, are set by the US Environmental
Protection Agency (EPA). In addition to its regulatory monitoring, FDA carries
out incidence and level monitoring in order to acquire data on the presence and
amounts of pesticide residues in particular commodity/chemical combinations. For
example, in 1991, FDA carried out three surveys under this approach, covering
aquaculture products, milk and processed foods (FDA 1992). Incidence and level
monitoring may be carried out by all FDA laboratories, by several or by a single
laboratory, depending on the targeted crop/analyte. In the survey reported here,
green coffee beans were analysed by FDA's San Francisco District for a variety of
pesticide chemicals.
Green coffee beans are imported into the United States from many South and
Central American, Asian and African countries. The Battelle World Agrochemical
Data Bank, a commercial source of foreign pesticide usage data, indicates that a
wide variety of insecticides and fungicides may be used on coffee crops worldwide.
FDA has analysed some coffee beans over the past several years using multiresidue
methods (MRMs) capable of detecting many of the pesticides listed by Battelle,
including a number of organochlorine and organophosphorus pesticides. Only a
few low-level quantities of some persistent chemicals, such as BHC, DDT and
dieldrin, have been found.
The agency wished to analyse coffee beans from a planned, broad range of
important exporting countries and to monitor them for pesticides that are not

While FDA monitors and enforces EPA tolerances on raw agricultural

FDA conducted a survey of imported green coffee beans. Residues of malathion

all residue levels had decreased to below the limit of quantitation (LQ) of the
methods. It is assumed that roasting would eliminate or degrade many of the
residues that persisted through the growing and drying stages of current coffee
production. However, to confirm that assumption, the present assignment directed
that if substantial amounts of residues were found, coffee beans from the same lot
would be roasted and reanalysed.
Collection and analysis of coffee beans
A total of 60 green coffee bean samples were collected from 21 different
countries that are major exporters of coffee to the United States. From one to four
samples were collected from each of the following countries: Brazil, Colombia,
Costa Rica, Dominican Republic, Ecuador, El Salvador, Ethiopia, Guatemala,
Honduras, India, Indonesia, Kenya, Mexico, Panama, Papua New Guinea, Peru,
Philippines, Thailand, Tanzania, Uganda and Yemen.
All test samples were analysed for the following pesticide residues:
Organochlorine/organophosphorus residues, using halogen- and phosphorus-
selective detectors (PAM I, 212.13b 1968 and revisions). General LQ is
0-01 ppm.
TV-Methyl carbamate residues (Sharp and Bramlett 1983) with extraction
according to Luke and Doose (1983). General LQ is 0-05 ppm.
Benomyl group (benomyl, thiophanate-methyl and carbendazim) (Walters and
Gilvydis 1988) modified as described by Gilvydis and Walters (1989). General
LQ is 0· 1 ppm.
EBDC residues (Keppel 1971); if positive, confirmation as described by Krause
(1989) or an alternative method for ethylenethiourea. Residues were reported
as 'zineb'. LQ is 0· 1 ppm.
All residues in this survey had their identities confirmed and were quantified.
Confirmation of identity was achieved either by chromatographing the extract on
a second column with different separation characteristics, or by using a different
detection mode, e.g. chlorine instead of phosphorus detection for chlorpyrifos.
Quantitation was achieved by comparing the detector response of the residue to that
of a known concentration of the standard that was nearly equivalent to the
concentration of the residue found in the test extract.

the survey. The range of fortification was from 0-10 to 4 ·0 ppm. Recoveries were

Results and discussion
Sixty samples of green coffee beans from 21 countries were analysed as noted
above. No residues of N-methyl carbamate pesticides, the benomyl group or
EBDCs were found. Organochlorine/organophosphorus residues were found in
four test samples: chlorpyrifos, 0-01, 0-02 and 0-04 ppm and pirimiphos-methyl,
0-01 ppm. Although there are no US EPA tolerances for these residues in green
coffee beans, the levels found were so low that regulatory action was no* deemed
warranted. For the same reason, study of residue fate during roasting of the coffee
beans would not have been informative and therefore was not conducted.
In conclusion, the majority (93%) of green coffee beans from 21 countries had
no detectable residues when analysed for several classes of pesticide chemicals.
