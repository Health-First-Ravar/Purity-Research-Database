ACID 
What happens during roasting 
Acetic Acid 
Increases during roasting up to (240°C) 464°F 
Butyric Acid 
Forms and degrade between 160°C - 260°C (320°F - 500°F). 
Citric Acid 
Decomposes starting around 176°C (350°F). It is less stable than malic acid and 
may lose 30-60%. 
Formic Acid 
High-temperature roasting causes the thermal breakdown of CGA and other 
compounds, forming formic acid. Forms  and degrades 175°C - 235°C (347°F - 
455°F).  Peaks around 218°C (425°F) then diminishes. 
Lactic Acid 
Degrades between 160°C and 260°C (320°F and 500°F). 
Malic Acid 
Decomposes starting around 190°C (375°F). It is more stable than citric acid and 
may lose 15-40%. 
Quinic Acid 
Roughly doubles during roasting (avg. 0.8g per 100g). Quinic acid forms as 
chlorogenic acids break down, typically between 175°C - 235°C (347°F - 455°F). 
Chlorogenic 
Acids (CGA) 
Declines starting at 149°C (300°F). 
Maillard and Caramelization 
Definitions 
The Maillard reaction is usually responsible for cooking raw foods and “browning” them to produce 
new flavors. It is a nonenzymatic browning reaction involving the carbonyl group of a sugar (a 
carbon atom double-bonded to an oxygen atom, defining the carbohydrate’s structure and 
reactivity—like aldehydes and ketones), reducing sugars and amino acids. The reaction breaks apart 
those compounds and forms new aromas, flavors, and compounds, including melanoidins. 
Caramelization is the browning of sugar.  
Application to 
roasting  
Temp. and Notes 
The temperature at which caramelization begins depends on the rate of temperature increase. 
Sucrose might begin caramelizing at 320°F/160°C when heat rises a few degrees per minute 
(roasting), but fast temperature rises may push sucrose caramelization to start above 356°F/180°C 
(Fernandes 2019).  
Maillard may begin around 280°F/140°C and usually is well underway by 330°F/165 °C.  
Specific sugars begin caramelizing at different temperatures. Fructose may start at 230°F/110°C and 
glucose at 320°F/160°C.

BROWNING 
300-356°F 
149-180°C 
The endothermic process 
continues. 
 
Some literature cites 
exothermic phase 
beginning at 170°C 
(Poisson L). 
Beans getting darker (melanoidins are 
forming) from darker yellows to 
variations of “cinnamon” and similar 
colors.  
Maillard reaction continues. Caramelization 
(all sugars, notably sucrose) starts around 
320°F/160°C. The faster the temperature 
rises, the higher the caramelization 
temperature is. Sucrose degradation may 
start at 360°F/180°C under a fast-rising heat 
application.  
 
Acrylamide and dozens of volatile compounds 
form as a by-product of Maillard. Trigonelline 
and CGA start to degrade. 
 
Some organic acids are partially lost, but 
others form in their place (e.g., some malic 
may form fumaric and maleic acids). Bean 
acidity overall increases (pH decreases), and 
carbohydrates degrade into carboxylic acids. 
The pH of coffee increases (Fernandes, F). 
 
Decisions: 
• What is the right speed/temperature to 
move through the Maillard reaction 
stage? 
• How quickly should I apply the heat to 
caramelize the sugars in the coffee?  
• Should I change the airflow or the flame 
intensity to increase the heat? 
 
Challenges:  
• Avoid baking. 
• Avoid scorching.  
• Avoid tipping. 
• Caramelize the sugars optimally.

FIRST CRACK 
356-410°F 
180-210°C 
At approximately 392°F / 
200°C, color, fragrance and 
taste compounds that 
make familiar “roasted 
coffee” notes emerge. 
 
First crack--exothermic. 
 
Exothermal reactions 
begin. Coffee beans start 
producing heat while being 
roasted, and this energy 
released by beans also 
becomes available to the 
beans.  
(Clarke, RJ and Macrae, R). 
Beans continue to get darker.  
 
First crack increases the beans' swelling. 
Expansion causes internal structures to 
rupture and release water, carbon dioxide 
(CO2) and some volatiles. In many small drum 
roasters, this might start as early as about 
380F°/ 194°C, but 395-401°F/201-205°C is 
typical.  
 
The surface of the beans has mottled colors 
and a rough surface. 
 
Structural carbohydrates begin to degrade in 
part by pyrolysis reactions: Cell walls of beans 
become more permeable.  
 
 
Some acids are forming (e.g., acetic acid can often be 
smelled at the first crack, and others are degrading). 
Perceived acidity declines as pH rises. 
 
The Strecker reaction converts amino acids into 
aldehydes (malty, honey-like aromas).  
Caramelization progresses, but if extended too long 
may produce bitterness. Byproducts of the Maillard 
reaction also produce bitterness. 
The CGA degrade more significantly, forming quinic 
and phenolic compounds. This can result in bitter 
compounds. 
Pyrolysis reactions (organic substances heated in a 
low-oxygen environment) begin.  
Decisions: 
• When I change heat or airflow, how is 
the smell or appearance of the bean 
impacted? 
• Do I roast faster or slower during the 
caramelization stage? 
• How do I mark first crack - by sound, 
smell, visual or a combination?  
• Do I turn the heat lower or off during 
first crack? If so, when do I put the heat 
back up? 
• How much acidity do I want in my 
finished coffee? How much CGA do I 
want to retain? 
• How much past first crack do I want to 
end the roast, or move into second 
crack? 
 
Challenges: 
Managing the heat and airflow during first 
crack.

Second crack may start at 
any point after first crack, 
depending on the intensity 
of the pyrolysis reaction. 
 
A more powerful 
exothermic phase typically 
follows a short 
endothermic phase. 
 
Above 480°F / 250°C, the 
pyrolysis exothermal effect 
drops. 
As roast gets darker, more high molecular 
weight melanoidins form.  
 
As second crack approaches, the bean surface 
smooths out and color becomes more even. 
As the roast gets darker, the lipids trapped 
inside the bean migrate to the surface from 
the internal CO2 pressure. 
 
Second crack releases CO2, aromatic and non-
aromatic compounds, as severe damage to 
cellulose fibers occurs.  
 
Bitterness increases as the roast increases. 
Chlorogenic acid lactones (the breakdown of 
chlorogenic acid) are more bitter. Volatile substances 
decrease the darker the roast.  
Extraction of soluble compounds during brewing is 
easier due to stretched cell fibers. 
Carcinogenic polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) 
form with carbonization. Pyrolysis of lipids, 
carbohydrates and amino acids form PAHs and smoke 
generated from heating these compounds, beginning 
to form as low as 392 F (200 C). Different PAHs form 
at different temperatures: lower molecular PAHs 
partially degrade and lead to the formation of higher 
molecular PAHs. To avoid the formation of 
carcinogenic PAHs, the roaster temperature should be 
lower than 240°C (464°F) for 15 minutes (Vegas, O), 
although these temperatures will degrade other 
compounds, too. 
Decisions: 
• When should I end the roast? 
• How much dark roast characteristics do I 
want to taste (roasted nuts, smokiness, 
etc.)? 
  
Challenges: 
Managing the balance of flavor without 
burning the coffee.
