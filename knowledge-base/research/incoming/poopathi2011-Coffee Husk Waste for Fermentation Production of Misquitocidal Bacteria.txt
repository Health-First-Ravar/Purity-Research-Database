ABSTRACT
Coffee husk waste (CHW) discarded as bio-organic waste, from coffee industries,
is rich in carbohydrates. The current study emphasizes the management of solid waste from
agro-industrial residues for the production of biopesticides (Bacillus sphaericus, and B. thurin-
giensis subsp. israelensis), to control disease transmitting mosquito vectors. An experimental
culture medium was prepared by extracting the Þltrates from coffee husk. A conventional culture
medium (NYSM) also was prepared. The studies revealed that the quantity of mosquitocidal toxins
produced from CHW is at par with NYSM. The bacteria produced in these media, were bioassayed
against mosquito vectors (Culex quinquefasciatus, Anopheles stephensi, and Aedes aegypti) and it
was found that the toxic effect was statistically comparable. Cost-effective analysis have revealed
that, production of biopesticides from CHW is highly economical. Therefore, the utilization of
CHW provides dual beneÞts of effective utilization of environmental waste and efÞcient pro-
duction of mosquitocidal toxins.

Each day, vast quantities of bio-organic wastes are
discharged from factories, Þsheries, poultries, and
food processing industries globally. Within the food
processing sectors, various liquids, sludge, and solid
biological and organic wastes require remediation
and alternative disposal methods that increasingly
are being investigated (Sim and Wu 2010). Degrad-
ing or handling these wastes directly without ac-
quiring additional beneÞts is costly and has led to
the development of programs to use bio-organic
wastes by means of simple fermentation technology
in mosquito vector control programs (Poopathi
2005). Recently, an organic waste treatment system
has been developed for using waste materials from
food processing industries (Pandey et al. 2000).
These recycling strategies enable the production of
biologically active substances such as soil improve-
ment agents and compost (Russ and Pittrof 2004). It
would be useful to manufacture and market a bio-
mass fermentation and treatment method capable of
reutilizing wastes produced by food bio-processing
industries into biologically active substances, ani-
mal feed, fertilizers, and fermentation agents. Such
methods would include a system for treating wastes
and a new fermentation agent that would be useful
in waste decomposition. Use of such methods would
reduce waste treatment costs, prevent environmen-
tal pollution, improve the soil condition, and yield
biologically active re-useable substances.
Considering the above applications, we have iden-
tiÞed new organic biological material i.e., the coffee
husk (coffee seed coat), discarded as waste material
after removing the pulp, from coffee processing in-
dustries. In the current study, the raw material, is rich
in nutrient sources (carbohydrate and proteins)
(Gouvea et al. 2009). In the present work, these ma-
terials, were bio-processed leading to the production
of bacterial biopesticides (Bacillus sphaericus, Bs and
B. thuringiensis subsp. israelensis, Bti) that are well
known biocontrol agents used in mosquito vector con-
trol programs (Delecluse et al. 1995, Poncet et al. 1995,
Poopathi 2005). The fermentation process using agro-
industrial residues is nevertheless, an easy to follow,
inexpensive technique, efÞcient enough to produce
effective bacterial toxins lethal to disease transmitting
mosquito vectors (Culex quinquefasciatus, Anopheles
stephensi, and Aedes aegypti). Ultimately, this new
approach would help to manage solid waste from the
environment, in a judicious manner.
It is understood that Bs and Bti are aerobic, rod-
shaped, endospore forming gram-positive soil bacteria
(Charles et al. 1997, Kumar et al. 2000, Poopathi et al.
2002). More than 50,000 isolates have been screened

Thus, it is a novel approach on the management of
solid waste from the environment.
Materials and Methods
Bacterial Strains. The known bacterial strains in
mosquito vector control program viz. B.thuringiensis
serovar israelensis H-14 (IPS-82) and Bacillus sphaeri-
cus (IAB-59) (Courtesy: Dr. J-F. Charles, Institute
Pasteur, Paris, France) were used.
Conventional Culture Medium (NYSM). The stan-
dard conventional medium (Nutrient Yeast Extracts
Mineral Salt Medium, NYSM) was used as reference:
glucose 5, peptone 5, NaCl 5, beef extract 3, yeast
extract 0.5, MgCl2 0.203, CaCl2 0.102, MnCl2 0.01
(gram/lit), pH 7.5.
Coffee Husk Waste (CHW). The agro-industrial
residues (coffee husk) produced as byproduct during
processing of pulp from coffee seeds were collected,
air-dried, powdered, and Þnally stored at 30C, until
further use. The major composition of CHW was the
protein,carbohydrate,lipid,ash,andmoisturecontent
at the rate of 7.0, 57.8, 0.3, 5.4, and 15.0%, respectively
(Gouvea et al. 2009). A small quantity of CHW (50
g/lit) was boiled in ordinary tap water for 30 min.
After cooling, the CHW was Þltered and the pH of the
Þltrate was adjusted (pH 7.8). The extract was dis-
pensed separately into three Erlenmeyer ßasks (ca-
pacity: 2 liter) for culturing Bs, Bti, and control (plain
medium without inoculation of Bs and Bti), respec-
tively. All bacterial culture media (conventional as
well as experimental) were autoclaved (at 120C/15
psi/20 min). Adequate replicates from each concen-
tration (three replicates/media/strain) were kept for
calculating mortality variations (if any).
Bacterial Inoculation. Precultures of Bti and Bs
were inoculated into experimental (CHW) and con-
ventional (NYSM) culture media and allowed to
grow under constant agitation (200 rpm at 30C).
Samples (vol.: 2.5 ml) from each culture medium
were drawn at every 6 h, up to a 72-h growth period.
The pH, culture density (turbidity at 650 nm) were
measured using digital pH meter (Genei, India) and
SP-75 UV-VIS spectrophotometer (Sanyo, United
Kingdom). The bacterial stages (vegetative to spo-
rulative stage) also were examined by LEICA DM
1000 LED microscope (Leica Microsystems, Wet-
zlar, Germany).
BacterialToxinSeparation.Bacterialsporesorcrys-
tals were puriÞed according to Charles et al. (1997).
The Bti and Bs spores or crystals from all replicates of
culture media, were harvested by centrifugation
(10,000  g/30 min/ 4C) using SORVALL Evolution
RC super speed centrifuge (Kendro, United States)
and the culture supernatants were discarded. The pel-
lets (cell mass) containing the spore or crystal toxin
were thoroughly washed (three times each with 0.1 M
NaCl and sterile distilled water). The toxins were
Þnally treated with protease inhibitor (phenyl methyl
sulfonyl ßuoride, 1 mM, Sigma) and resuspended in
sterile distilled water. The spores or crystals of Bti and
Bs were sonicated (30% scale output, 25% duty cycle
for 4 min) and crystals were separated by discontin-
uous sucrose gradients (72 and 79% mass/vol). The
pure crystals were then solubilized in 50 mM NaOH
for 90 min at 30C. After neutralization, the solubilized
crystals were dialyzed against 20 mM Na2HPO4/
NaH2PO4, pH eight and protein concentrations were
determined (Lowry et al. 1951). The crystals contain-
ing mosquitocidal toxins (Bti: 134, 125, 67, 25 kDa; Bs:
51, 42 kDa) were determined qualitatively by SDS-
PAGE (sodium dodecyl sulfate polyacrylamide gel
electrophoresis) (Lammeli 1970).
Toxicity Assays. Cx. quinquefasciatus, An. stephensi,
and Ae. aegypti mosquito larvae were procured from
the Division of Mosquito Rearing and Colonization,
Vector Control Research Centre, Pondicherry, India
for use in the current study. They are the vectors of
Þlariasis, malaria, and dengue respectively. Toxicity
assays (bioassays) for Bti and Bs were carried out as
per WHO (WHO 1982, 1985) and Poopathi et al.
(1999). A stock solution was prepared (100 mg/lit)
using Bti/Bs toxins and homogenized, using glass
beads, in a mechanical shaker. From this stock, serial
dilutions were made to obtain seven dosages within
the limit (Bti: 0.001Ð0.05 mg/lit; Bs: 0.05Ð0.20 mg/lit).
Toxicity tests were conducted in disposable wax
coated paper cups (350-ml capacity) with three rep-
licates of each concentration. Test media were pre-
pared by adding appropriate volumes of Bti/Bs in 300
ml of water, into which 20 early third-instar larvae
from respective mosquito species were introduced
separately. Food supplement (dog biscuit and yeast
mixture, 2:1) was provided for larvae under Bs treat-
ment, whereas no food was given to Bti treated larvae,
as per WHO recommendation (WHO 1982, 1985).
Toxicity assays were conducted at room temperature
(28C)andlarvalmortalitywasmonitoredafter24and
48 h for Bti and Bs treatment, respectively. Moribund
larvae (if any) in the replicates were counted as dead.
Treatment mortality was corrected for control mor-
tality (Abbott 1925).

data of Bti/Bs cultured from test (CHW) and control
(NYSM) medium were subjected to students ÔtÕ (P 
0.05). The lethal concentration values (LC50 and
LC90) were calculated by regression analysis by using
SPSS software package ASSAY (Courtesy: Dr. C.F.

AsshowninFig.1aand1b,itwasgenerallyobserved
that in both culture media (CHW and NYSM), after
a lag phase of about an hour, there was a rapid mul-
tiplication of bacterial cells and maturation of spores.
The culture density increased with increasing culture
time and reached a plateau (range, 1.5Ð3.3). This pro-
cess of multiplication lasted until 48th hour, followed
by cell lysis, which released the delta-endotoxin (crys-
tal toxins) into the media. The maximum growth and
release of endotoxins was completed at 72 h of growth.
Thus, the overall growth and production of Bs and Bti
spore/crystal toxins in the experimental culture media
(CHW) was comparable to that of the conventional
medium (NYSM).
Fig. 1a and 1b further indicated that a signiÞcant
level of spores or crystals (delta endotoxin) was
released, in both culture media, between 60 and 72 h
of fermentation process (peak growth period),
showing that the bacteria was able to grow and use
the nutrients contained in the coffee husk medium.
Growth pattern studies (optical density at 650 nm)
for both Bs and Bti have shown that the range for
NYSM was 1.15Ð2.68, whereas for CHW, it was 1.18Ð
2.70, indicating a similar growth rate for both media
(for Bs and Bti P  0.801 and 0.977, respectively).
Microscopic observation of Bs and Bti obtained from
CHW and NYSM, after 72 h growth period revealed
that, the sporulation rate of the former medium was
as good as the latter (Data not shown).
The protein concentration, an indication of toxin
production from both the media were quantiÞed and

of toxins (Fig. 2a and 2b) between the media, which

Discussion
Coffee husks have been discarded in bulk as resi-
dues from agro-industries, especially from coffee man-
ufacturing industries globally. They normally accu-
mulate carbohydrates (carbon and nitrogen) and are
used for fertilizer (Pandey et al. 2000). The current
study mainly focuses on the utilization of extract from
CHW, by Bs and Bti, for the production of mosquito-
cidal toxins. Biochemical studies have indicated that
the mosquitocidal spore or crystal toxins produced
from experimental culture medium (CHW) are sim-

Toxins produced from CHW had similar effect like
NYSM followed by general mode of action as de-
scribed (Poopathi et al. 2002, Charles et al. 1997).
The production of this new culture medium
(CHW) is very economical, compared with that of
the conventional medium, as CHW is plentifully
available as industrial bio-organic waste. Further
studies need to be carried out on the optimal fer-
mentation conditions for maximization of Bs and Bti
production, as it was extensively studied with Btk
