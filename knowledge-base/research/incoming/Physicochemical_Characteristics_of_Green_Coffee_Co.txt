Physicochemical Characteristics of Green Coffee:
Comparison of Graded and Defective Beans
K. RAMALAKSHMI, I.R. KUBRA, AND L.J.M. RAO
ABSTRACT: Defective (triage) coffee beans are beans rejected after separating the graded ones according to the size
and color. These coffee beans represent about 15% to 20% of coffee production in India but are not utilized for
beverages since these affect the quality of coffee brew. In the present study, physical characteristics such as bean
density, brightness, titratable acidity, pH, moisture, and total soluble solids and also chemical composition, namely,
caffeine, chlorogenic acids, lipids, sucrose, total polyphenols, and proteins, were evaluated in defective as well as in
graded green coffee beans. The physical parameters such as weight, density, and brightness of defective coffee beans
werelowcomparedtothegradedbeans,whichisduetothepresenceofimmature,broken,bleached,andblackbeans.
Caffeine content was low in triage beans compared to graded beans. Chlorogenic acids, one of the composition in
coffee responsible for antioxidant activity, was found to be intact (marginally high in some cases) in defective coffee
beans. Hence, triage coffee beans can be evaluated as a source of antioxidant or radical scavenging conserve for food
systems.

polyphenols, proteins, substandard, sucrose, triage
Introduction
C
offee is one of the world’s most widely consumed beverages.
According to the latest estimate of the Intl. Coffee Organiza-
tion (ICO), total coffee production in the crop year is approximately
102 million bags (each bag equivalent to 60 kg) while India’s cof-
fee production contributes 4.50 million bags. The quality of coffee
used for beverages is directly related to the chemical composition
of the roasted beans, which in turn is mainly affected by the com-
position of the green beans and postharvest processing conditions
(namely, drying, storage, roasting, and grinding). The quality of cof-
fee is commonly evaluated according to criteria such as bean size,
color, shape, roasting characteristics, processing method, crop year,
flavor or cup quality, and presence of defects (The Wealth of India
1950; Varnam and Sutherland 1994). The terms “defect” or “triage”
or “substandard” are employed with reference to the presence of de-
fective beans (namely, black or brown, immature, insect-damaged,
broken, and so on). In general, coffee is graded based on the size
of the coffee bean and percentage of imperfections. Apart from size
specification, a defect count standard based on the “percentage of
defects by weight” was specified in respect of each of the grades
of coffee (Anonymous 1982). The defective coffee obtained after
grading according to the size and color is termed as “substandard”
or “triage,” which contain the defects such as blacks, dark brown
beans, and damaged beans such as bleached beans (spongy), spot-
ted beans (more than a quarter of a bean surface), insect damaged
beans, sours, bits, and greens (immature beans).
The defective coffee beans represent about 15% to 20% of cof-
fee production on weight basis. Nearly 1.2 to 1.5 million tonnes are
generated in the coffee industry every year and are rejected in the
international market as these defective beans produce undesirable

Vasconcelos and others 2006). These studies were not made with
reference to the Indian coffee beans, which are different from other
geographical regions with regard to various physical and chemical
characteristics. Therefore, the present study focused on the physical
properties and chemical composition of the defective coffee beans
in comparison to nondefective beans from India and the results are
presented here.
Materials and Methods
Samples
Two varieties of Arabica and Robusta processed by dry and wet
methods were selected for the studies and were procured (Maruthi
coffee curing works, Mysore, India). Grading (Ramalakshmi and
Raghavan 2003) was carried out after removing the extraneous mat-
ter such as twigs, stones, and wood pieces. Arabica plantation (AP),
processed by wet method, were graded in the sieve with the aperture
size, 6.65 mm, whereas Arabica cherry (dry method, AC), Robusta
parchment (wet method, RP), and Robusta cherry (dry method, RC)
were graded with the sieve of aperture 5.50 mm as per the guide-
lines of the Indian Coffee Board (Anonymous 1982). The rejects or
defective beans (APD, ACD, RPD, RCD) are collected separately from
each variety. The electronic color sorter rejects from AP, AC, RP, and
RC were added to the respective rejects (APD, ACD, RPD, RCD) and

Physical attributes
Graded and defective coffee beans of the above mentioned va-
rieties were analyzed for the following physical parameters. All the
analyses were carried out in triplicate and statistical analysis was
applied.
Bean density and brightness. A known number (for example,
100) of coffee beans from each sample were weighed and average
bean density was evaluated as the ratio between the weight of the
100 beans and the volume occupied by the beans (Dutra and others
2001). Green coffee beans were placed in a Petri dish and color (per-
centage of reflectance) was determined by placing it over the sensor
unit of the Photovolt 575 (U.S.A.) reflection meter (Ramalakshmi
and others 2000).
Titratable acidity and pH. Titratable acidity of coffee bean was
determined by mixing green coffee powder (10 g) with 75 mL of 80%
ethanol for 16 h. An aliquot of the filtrate was titrated against sodium
hydroxide solution (of known strength) using phenolphthalein as
indicator. The volume of sodium hydroxide required for neutraliza-
tion was noted and titratable acidity was calculated (AOAC 2000).
Green coffee brew pH was recorded by preparing extractives with
green coffee powder (3 g) in 50 mL hot water (Mazzafera, 1999). The
extract was cooled to room temperature and pH of the extractive
was measured using a pH meter (Control Dynamics—APX-175E/C,
Bangalore, India).
Moisture and total soluble solids. Moisture content of coffee
samples was determined in the hot air oven at a temperature of
105 ± 2 ◦C for 48 h (Mazzafera 1999). Total soluble solids of the
powdered coffee sample was determined by refluxing coffee powder
(2 g) with hot water (200 mL) for 1 h and made up to 500 mL. An
aliquot (50 mL) was evaporated to dryness, followed by heating in a
hot air oven at 105 ± 2 ◦C to get concurrent weights and the amount
of total soluble solids was calculated (AOAC 2000).

Results and Discussion
Physical qualities
Results of studies on physical attributes of Indian coffee beans
are presented in Table 1. The weight of 100 seeds of defective coffee
beans was less compared to the graded beans, which is due to the
presence of broken and cut beans. Density of the graded beans (657
to 710 kg/m3) was higher than that of the defective coffee beans
(520 to 650 kg/m3) in all the varieties, which confirms the earlier
report (Franca and others 2005). As expected the density of Arabica
beans processed by both wet and dry methods (AP, AC) is lesser

beans, which is associated with the highest acidity. This could be at-
tributed to fermentation within the fruit during the drying process
or fermentation of the black bean or beans harvested at an inap-
propriate developmental stage. Titratable acidity and pH values are
matching with the literature reported values (Mazzafera 1999).
Moisture levels (5.52% to 7.38%) of the beans in the present study
are marginally lower than the levels reported (Clarke 1985; Maz-
zafera 1999). However, defective coffee beans did not show much
variation in moisture levels, when compared to graded beans in the
respective category. Total soluble solids content was in the range of
29% to 34% for graded and 26% to 32% for defective coffee beans
(Table 1). The decrease in total soluble solids of defective beans is
expected, as quantities of carbohydrates (for example, sucrose), caf-
feine, and polyphenols are found to be lower in defective compared
to graded. Robusta variety gave more soluble solids than Arabica
variety processed by both the methods.
Chemical qualities
The chemical characteristics of defective coffee in comparison
with graded coffee beans are presented in Figure 1 to 6.
Caffeine content was slightly lower in defective coffee beans (Fig-
ure 1). But significant changes were not found, as caffeine content
is not much affected by environmental, agricultural, and posthar-
vest practices. Caffeine content was in the range of 0.8% to 1.8%,
which is very much matching with the literature value of 0.9% to
1.4% (Macrae 1985; Franca and others 2005). Robusta coffee beans
contain more caffeine than Arabica beans (Figure 1). No differences
in caffeine contents were encountered among the defective beans.
Higher caffeine contents were reported in the endosperm of imma-
ture fruits and in the whole immature fruit (Mazzafera and others
1991). However, the minor contribution of caffeine to beverage bit-
terness led to conclude that this alkaloid is not responsible for any
change in beverage quality.

of compounds, representing 6% to 10% dry matter of the coffee
beans. Chlorogenic acid (Figure 2) is one of the key components
in coffee responsible for determining the beverage quality as well as
its antioxidant activity and in turn for health benefits. Chlorogenic
acid content was found to be marginally more in defective coffee
beans (6.83% to 8.80%) compared to graded coffee beans (6.35% to
8.32%). No reports were found for total chlorogenic acid content
from the defective beans. However, Franca and others (2005) es-
timated the quantities of 5-caffeoyl quinic acid (one of the major
isomer in chlorogenic acids) from water extracts of defective cof-
fee beans and reported in the range of 1.83% to 3.34%. Chlorogenic
acids from defective coffee beans perhaps could be utilized as nat-
ural antioxidants and may help to add the benefits to the coffee
growers/manufacturers. The relationship between chemical com-
position of immature cherries and chlorogenic acid was studied in
detail by Clifford and Kazi (1987), who suggested that immature cof-

to 5%. Though there was not significant difference in sucrose con-

showed slightly lower sucrose content, which could be attributed to
the presence of immature black beans since the degree of ripening
is related to the accumulation of sucrose in green coffee beans. It
was reported that good beverage was associated with coffee beans
of high sucrose content (Mazzafera 1999). Also it was observed that
dry processed coffee showed more sucrose content than the wet
processed coffee in both the varieties.

Conclusions
F
our classes of each graded and defective coffee beans (from 2
varieties),alongwiththecommerciallyavailabledefectivegreen
coffee beans, were analyzed for physical and chemical attributes. In
general the values for Robusta variety are higher than the Arabica
variety between the 2 varieties. There were marginal differences in
the physical and chemical parameters among the graded and de-
fective coffee beans. The quantities of all the chemical compounds
are marginally higher in graded beans compared to defective beans
except for the chlorogenic acid content. In particular, chlorogenic
acid, one of the components in coffee responsible for the antioxi-
dant activity, was found to be slightly high in defective coffee beans.
Since the defective coffee beans are not suitable for making cof-
fee beverage, the presence of phenolic compounds and chlorogenic
acids in reasonable quantities makes them suitable as a source of
natural antioxidant/oxygen scavenger/free radical scavenger con-
serve/compounds. These conserves/compounds can be tried for
preservation in different food systems.
