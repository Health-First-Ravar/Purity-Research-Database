Monoamine oxidase inhibitory activity in tobacco particulate matter:
Are harman and norharman the only physiologically relevant
inhibitors?

A B S T R A C T
Monoamine oxidase inhibition is signiﬁcant in smokers, but it is still unclear how the inhibition that is
seen in the brains and bodies of smokers is brought about. Our aim was to test the contribution of the
harman and norharman in tobacco smoke to MAO-A inhibition from tobacco smoke preparations, as part
of a re-examination of harman and norharman as the cause of the inhibition of MAO-A inhibition in the
brain.
Tobacco smoke particulate matter and cigarette smoke particulate matter were prepared and the
amounts of harman and norharman measured. The results were compared with the total monoamine
oxidase-A inhibitory activity.
At a nicotine concentration of 0.6 mM (a “physiological” concentration in blood) the total monoamine
oxidase-A inhibitory activity measured in these samples was sufﬁcient to inhibit the enzyme by
approximately 10%. Of this inhibitory activity, only a small proportion of the total was found to be due to
harman and norharman.
These results show that harman and norharman provide only a moderate contribution to the total
monoamine oxidase-A inhibitory activity of tobacco smoke, perhaps under 10%. This suggests that other
inhibitors (either known or unknown) may be more signiﬁcant contributors to total inhibitory activity
than has yet been established, and deserve closer examination.

1. Introduction
It is well established that monoamine oxidase (MAO) activity is
reduced in the brains and bodies of long-term smokers. The
average reduction in brain MAO-A is 30% (MAO-B, 40%) in
established smokers (Fowler et al., 1996, 2003). MAO activity
recovers slowly over the course of a few weeks when smokers stop
smoking (Fowler et al., 2003; Rose et al., 2001) and this time course
is believed to imply that the activity is irreversibly inhibited by
smoke components (Rose et al., 2001; Fowler et al., 2003; Lewis
et al., 2007; Yu and Boulton, 1987). MAO enzymes metabolise
dopamine (Lewis et al., 2007; van Amsterdam et al., 2006) the
reinforcement stimulus instigated by nicotine and most closely
associated with addiction (Le Foll and Goldberg, 2009; Pontieri
et al., 1996). Impairment of MAO activity is thus predicted to have
an impact on addiction to tobacco (reviewed in Hogg, 2016).
Indeed, MAO inhibitors have enhanced responses to nicotine in rat
self-administration tests (Kapelewski et al., 2011; Villegier et al.,
2011; Lotﬁpour et al., 2011; Smith et al., 2016). MAO inhibitors have
been trialled for smoking cessation (George and Weinberger,
2008).
Although the fact of MAO activity reduction in smokers and its
potential to affect the brain’s responses to nicotine is clear, it is less
clear how this reduction in activity is brought about. A variety of
MAO inhibitors have been found in tobacco smoke. The most
notable are harman and norharman, b-carbolines which are
reversible inhibitors of MAO-A and -B. Smoking is the major source
of human exposure to harman and norharman (Pfau and Skog,

Nevertheless, there are challenges to this view. Harman and
norharman are both reversible inhibitors (Lewis et al., 2007). Their
half-life in blood is reported to be close to one hour (Herraiz and
Chaparro, 2005) or less (Fekkes and Bode, 1993). In contrast,
nicotine has a half-life of closer to two hours (Benowitz et al., 2009)
and should be cleared from the body more slowly than these MAO
inhibitors. The concentration of nicotine in arterial blood can reach
100 ng/mL (0.6 mM) in smokers but it is more usually around half of
this. The concentrations of harman and norharman measured in
tobacco smoke, compared to the concentration of nicotine, suggest
that blood concentrations after smoking should be in the low
nanomolar range (Brennan et al., 2013, 2015; Lewis et al., 2012),
and this is indeed what is observed (van Amsterdam et al., 2006;
Rommelspacher et al., 2002). Yet the MAO-B activity in blood
platelets will be inhibited by 50% only if norharman reaches low
micromolar concentrations (Ki; 1.12 mM). Similarly the most potent
inhibitor of MAO-A, harman, has a Ki of 55 nM (Herraiz and
Chaparro, 2005). Can these reversible inhibitors, harman and
norharman, reach the concentrations required for 30–40% inhibi-
tion of MAO-A and -B activity?
Attempting to explain this discrepancy, Rommelspacher et al.
have found evidence for a longer half-life for norharman in
platelets (Rommelspacher et al., 2002) suggesting that it may be
possible for its concentration (and inhibitory effects) to build up
over time in speciﬁc compartments in the body. Partitioning effects
may be occurring in the brain as well (Rommelspacher et al., 2002;
Fekkes and Bode, 1993). However, there is as yet no direct evidence
that harman and/or norharman in tobacco smoke cause the MAO
inhibition seen in smokers, and no evidence for accumulation of
either harman or norharman in the brain to the mid nanomolar or
low micromolar concentrations required to cause the observed
inhibition of enzyme activity.
In this paper we report the results of experiments aimed at
determining the proportion of the total MAO-A inhibitory activity
in a preparation of tobacco smoke which is attributable to harman
and norharman. This is a ﬁrst step towards re-examining the
signiﬁcance of harman and norharman in causing the reduction of
MAO-A activity experienced by smokers.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Preparation of monoamine oxidase inhibitors
Tobacco smoke preparations (tobacco particulate matter; TPM)
were prepared as previously described (Lewis et al., 2012) using
particulate matter collected from the smoke under ISO 3308
conditions (LabStat international, Kitchener, Canada). The tobacco
and cigarettes samples used were each purchased commercially
within New Zealand and were chosen from commonly used
brands. The tobacco was Drum, rolled into cigarettes containing 1 g
of tobacco, and the cigarettes were Holiday Red. Filters with TPM
were extracted with ethanol, and the ethanol extracts used directly
in enzyme inhibition assays (Lewis et al., 2012). TPM prepared in
this way will lack the more volatile components of tobacco smoke
(e.g carbon monoxide and ammonia). Furthermore small particles
insoluble in ethanol (e.g. partially burnt plant material) largely stay
on the ﬁlter.
Harman and norharman were obtained from Sigma Aldrich (St
Louis, MO) and made up in ethanol to a stock concentration of
100 mM. For concentration: response curves inhibitors were
diluted in ethanol and 5 mL of inhibitor in ethanol (or ethanol
alone) added to the assay mix.
2.2. Monoamine oxidase inhibition assays
Human recombinant monoamine oxidase A (MAO-A) was
obtained from Sigma Aldrich, aliquoted and stored at 80 C until
use. Enzyme inhibition assays were performed essentially as
previously described (Lewis et al., 2012; Lewis, 2010) but with
minor modiﬁcations. The kynuramine substrate was used at100
mM and the ﬁnal ethanol concentration was 5% (v/v) in each well.
Finally, incubations were for 15 min only, before the reaction was
stopped by addition of sodium hydroxide.
Blank wells containing buffer and inhibitor only, at the same
concentration as in the assay, were included to allow removal of
ﬂuorescence due to the sample itself, prior to calculation of the
inhibition of MAO-A activity caused by the sample. Controls with
no ethanol or inhibitor, and for the intrinsic ﬂuorescence of the
substrate were also included. Harman, at close to its IC50
concentration, was used as positive control when TPM samples
were being analysed, to ensure assay consistency.
All assays were performed in triplicate and repeated at least
once. Prism 5 (GraphPad Software Inc) was used for ﬁtting a
sigmoidal concentration: response curve of log [inhibitor concen-
tration] against relative ﬂuorescence units. The% inhibition at
speciﬁc nicotine or inhibitor concentrations (see Tables 1 and 2)
was obtained by interpolation from the ﬁtted curve.
2.3. Nicotine, harman, and norharman analyses
Nicotine concentrations were determined by gas chromatogra-
phy-mass spectrometry as previously described (Ambrose et al.,
2007).
Harman and norharman concentrations were assessed by liquid
chromatography- mass spectrometry using an Agilent 1200 liquid
chromatograph coupled to an Agilent 6410 triple quadrupole mass
spectrometer ﬁtted with an Agilent Multimode ion source and run
in ESI mode. Samples in ethanol were diluted 50- to 300-fold in
acetonitrile before separation on a Zorbax SB-C18 column run at
35 C under isocratic buffer conditions (50% water/acetonitrile
0.02% in formic acid). A small amount of deuterated harman (D4
harman, BDG Synthesis) was added to each sample and standard
and was used to correct for ionisation suppression. Five standards
of each analyte were run separately (0.1–10 ppb in acetonitrile) and
the concentrations of harman and norharman in the samples were
assessed by comparison with the standard curves. Mass spectro-
metric detection was in positive ion mode (harman m/z 183,
norharman m/z 169, with subsequent determination of daughter
ions at m/z 154 (harman) and 145 (norharman) as well as m/z 115
for both analytes).
3. Results
3.1. Inhibition of MAO-A by ethanol
Addition of ethanol to 5% reduced the enzyme activity by 19.1–

3.2. Inhibition of MAO-A by tobacco smoke
Both of the tobacco smoke samples inhibited MAO-A, as
expected (Fig. 1A). Half maximal inhibition of MAO-A was achieved
by addition of 0.053 mL of the tobacco smoke sample (5 mL of a 1/94
dilution; 95% conﬁdence limits 0.036–0.079 mL) and 0.13 mL of the
cigarette smoke sample (5 mL of a 1/39 dilution; 95% conﬁdence
limits 0.11–0.15 mL).

3.3. Inhibition of MAO-A by harman and norharman
Harman and norharman also strongly inhibited MAO-A, with
half-maximal inhibition (IC50) values of 0.32 mM (95% conﬁdence
limits 0.26–0.39 mM) and 4.7 mM (95% conﬁdence limits 3.8–
5.7 mM) under these assay conditions (Fig. 1B).
3.4. Results of instrumental analysis
The concentrations of nicotine, harman and norharman in the
sample of tobacco TPM were 11 mM, 15.9 mM, and 13.9 mM
respectively, while for the cigarette TPM, the concentrations were
8.8 mM, 13.9 mM, and 27 mM respectively.
Table 1 shows the nicotine, harman and norharman concen-
trations determined for the two tobacco smoke samples tested,
when diluted to a physiologically relevant concentration for
nicotine of 0.6 mM, together with the total MAO-A inhibitory
activity of these samples.
Direct measurement of the MAO-A inhibition achieved by the
two TPMs at these dilutions showed that tobacco TPM inhibited
MAO-A by 9%, and cigarette TPM by 5%, in good agreement with the
results expected from the ﬁtted curve.
Table 2 shows the nicotine, harman and norharman concen-
trations determined for the two tobacco smoke samples tested,
when they were diluted somewhat less, to achieve half-maximal
MAO-A inhibition.
These amounts of harman and norharman in each of the TPM
samples, diluted to their IC50 concentration, were conﬁrmed by
direct instrumental analysis.
The inhibitory activity of mixtures of harman and norharman at
the IC50 concentrations of each TPM were also tested in the
standard inhibition assay and found to inhibit MAO-A by 7% for
tobacco TPM (value expected by interpolation from ﬁtted curve 4%
(95% conﬁdence limit 0–10%)) and by 9% for cigarette TPM (value
expected by interpolation from ﬁtted curve 7% (95% conﬁdence
limit 0–12%)).
4. Discussion
In comparison to the harman/norharman standard curves
(Fig. 1B) the TPM samples diluted to their IC50 concentration
contained less than 1/15th of the harman and 1/120th of the
norharman required for 50% inhibition of MAO-A activity (320 nM
and 4700 nM respectively). Overall, the concentrations of harman
plus norharman in these two tobacco smoke samples tested were
approximately 1/20th to 1/40th of 
that required to cause
half-maximal inhibition of MAO-A (Table 2).
The inhibition achieved with harman and norharman was in
good agreement with literature values (Lewis et al., 2007;
Herraiz and Chaparro, 2005; Rommelspacher et al., 2002).
Further, the concentrations of harman and norharman mea-
sured in the TPM were in agreement with the literature and
with our previous work (Rommelspacher et al., 2002; van
Amsterdam et al., 2006; Herraiz and Chaparro, 2005; Lewis
et al., 2012; Brennan et al., 2015, 2013). However, the total MAO
inhibition attributable to harman and norharman was far less
than would be expected if these were the major inhibitors
present in tobacco smoke. At face value it appears that harman
and norharman together make up perhaps 5%, and less than 10%
of the total MAO-A inhibitory activity in these samples of
tobacco smoke, suggesting that other inhibitors in the smoke
might be equally or more signiﬁcant.
The ratio of total MAO-A inhibition to nicotine is also notable. As
found previously (Lewis et al., 2012), assuming that uptake of

This suggestion matches the observations of Rommelspacher
et al. (Rommelspacher et al., 2002) who demonstrated an
approximately 10% inhibition of platelet MAO-B activity in vivo
following smoking. Harman is relatively inactive against MAO-B so
that norharman becomes the dominant inhibitor of the two, for
MAO-B (Herraiz and Chaparro, 2005). In these experiments plasma
norharman concentrations were measured as being under 1 nM
after smoking, in agreement with the results in Table 1 for the
concentrations of norharman when the nicotine concentration
reaches 0.6 mM. Assuming uptake of nicotine, harman and
norharman are equally efﬁcient, these results suggest a likely
blood concentration for harman of under 1 nM and under 2 nM for
norharman, once nicotine has reached 0.6 mM. This is three- to
ﬁve-hundred-fold less than the reported Kis for norharman and
MAO-A or -B, and 50- to 200-fold less than the reported Ki for
harman and MAO-A (Herraiz and Chaparro, 2005; Rommelspacher
et al., 2002).
Ki is a measure of the half-maximal inhibitor concentration that
removes the effect of competition with substrate. It is lower than
the IC50 measured for MAO-A in these experiments, so that more
inhibitor is required to meet the IC50 concentration than the Ki
concentration. The degree of MAO-A inhibition experienced in vivo
is likely to be somewhere between the Ki and IC50 ﬁgures
suggesting that, in reality, higher concentrations of harman and
norharman would be required for effective inhibition of MAO
enzymes than are suggested by the Ki values.
Partition of norharman into platelets, and an extension of the
half-life of norharman are suggested as reasons for the discrepancy
between the observed plasma concentration of norharman and the
MAO-B inhibitory effect of the smoke. The inﬂuence of other
inhibitors is a further factor which should be considered.
Similar reasoning is required to explain the discrepancy
between harman and norharman concentrations in plasma of
smokers and MAO-A inhibition. If inhibition of MAO-A is caused by
direct action of these inhibitors on the enzyme, then either the
enzyme must be irreversibly inhibited (at a rate faster than the
enzyme can regenerate), the inhibitor must have a half-life in the
body longer than that of nicotine so that its concentration can build
up, or some partitioning effects must be at work. For harman and
norharman alone to produce this effect, their concentrations
would need to build up by at least 10-fold (harman) and 100-fold
(norharman) in order to produce the observed inhibition of MAO-A
activity by direct inhibition.
Other inhibitors known to be in tobacco smoke include a
benzoquinone, 
2-naphthylamine,

potential (Sari and Khalil, 2015), all are thought to be reversible
inhibitors 
of 
relatively

towards 
MAO-A 
(van
Amsterdam et al., 2006; Lewis et al., 2007; Sari and Khalil,
2015). Their contributions to the total MAO-A inhibitory activity in
tobacco smoke have not been reported. No irreversible inhibitors
of MAO-A enzymes have been reported from tobacco smoke.
A limitation of this study is that it was only carried out using
MAO-A, and that it involved only two samples of TPM. Previous
work has shown that such samples do vary from batch to batch,
between tobacco types, and from brand to brand (Lewis et al.,
2012; Brennan et al., 2015). The samples used in this study
contained slightly higher harman/norharman concentrations than
found in the samples used in Lewis et al. (2012) (1.43–5.53 nM
harman at the EC50 of MAO-A for each TPM sample; n = 4,
unpublished data). The results reported here are thus unlikely to be
anomalous or unusual.
A further limitation is that TPM is a preparation of the
particulates in tobacco smoke, and does not fully represent the
more volatile or gaseous components, such as acetaldehyde,
ammonia, or carbon monoxide. Nevertheless, this work shows
clearly that there are signiﬁcant gaps in our understanding of the
causes of MAO inhibition in smokers.
5. Conclusion
This paper points to the possibility that unknown or poorly
characterised MAO-A inhibitors in tobacco smoke could be more
pharmacologically signiﬁcant than previously thought. Further
work to characterise the MAO-A inhibitors in tobacco smoke, their
pharmacokinetics, and their behavioural effects is required.
If the MAO-A and -B inhibitory activity in tobacco smoke helps
to support tobacco dependency, as seems likely, then determining
which tobacco smoke components contribute to MAO inhibition is
important for understanding the pharmacological basis of tobacco
dependence and improving our ability to help people to stop
smoking.
