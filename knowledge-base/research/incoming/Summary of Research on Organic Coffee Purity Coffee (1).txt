PURITY COFFEE 
SUMMARY OF RESEARCH ON ORGANIC COFFEE 
JANUARY 2018 
 
From a review of the available information on organics and pesticides in coffee, there is enough 
uncertainty on this topic for consumers to care deeply about the chemicals used in the production of 
their coffee. The US government does not have maximum residue limits (MRLs) for all the pesticides 
used on coffee and this leaves consumers unprotected unless they chose to purchase organics. 
Coffee-growing countries have different laws and monitoring systems for pesticide use. These 
laws may be similar or very different to the countries which later import the coffee. This gap is one 
which is typically safeguarded by local regulators setting maximum tolerances for residues upon import, 
which they regularly monitor. Whether or not individual coffee farmers abide by local law, many coffee-
growing countries use pesticides banned in the US and Europe. There is an abundance of evidence that 
coffee farmworkers use a variety of pesticides and herbicides that are detrimental to the environment 
and the human health of farm workers and communities within the watershed and local ecosystem.

A recent search of the Global MRL database listed 42 pesticides as used on coffee. That list does 
not capture all globally banned chemicals which may still exist in the environment or could be used 
illegally. There are multiple harmful pesticides with no set MRLs for coffee, which leaves the question of 
whether goverments are aware that those chemicals are utilized in coffee production.  If there are no 
MRLs listed for coffee, that essentially means that there is no set limit for pesticide residue and 
therefore they are not being monitored, leaving the population unprotected. Specifically, on the list 
above of highly-hazardous chemicals, Abamectin, Cypermethrin, Dichlorvos, Disulfoton, Endosulfan, 
Haloxyfop, parathion-methyl, Permethrin, and Propiconazole do not have coffee MRLs despite the fact 
that they have been documented in coffee production. This is a serious safety gap. 
Some of these chemicals are organophosphates, which are among them most widely used 
insecticides in the world. They are the most acutely toxic of all pesticides to humans, vertebrate animals, 
and insects. They act by deactivating an enzyme called Cholinesterase, which is essential for healthy 
nerve function. Others on this list have been proven to be toxic to bees, or be identified as possibly 
carcinogenic. Finally, a few of them are suspected of being endocrine (hormone) disruptors. Despite the 
importance of the human endocrine system, there are still aspects of these substances that we do not 
undersand. Regulators cannot agree on what is and what is not an endocrine disrupting chemical, and

thus for now they remain “suspected” with little protections implemented for consumers. Overall, these 
are highly-toxic substances which in many cases are banned in the US and Europe but are still being 
used to grow coffee. 
The use of chemicals in farming grows every year, worldwide. The Food and Agriculture 
Organization (FAO) of the United Nations monitors these statistics around the globe. Their most recent 
data on pesticide use (2012) lists China, Brazil, and the United States as the top three countries for total 
pesticide use. While the US has stayed fairly stable over the past twenty years, China has expanded its 
total pesticide use over 200% since 1991 and Brazil almost 800% since 1990. Notably, Brazil has applied 
this amount of pesticides on the roughly the same agricultural land area, as opposed to China, which has 
grown its permanent crops land area by over 200% in that period. This effectively means that Brazil has 
been intensifying the amount of pesticides used in agriculture per acre of land, therefore all crops, 
including coffee, are grown with almost 8 times more pesticides than just a few decades ago. Relatedly, 
the most recent FAO STAT information (2015) on countries which use the most chemical fertilizers list 
China, the United states, India, and Indonesia as the top users by total weight on NPK-type fertilizers. 
Another safeguard of organic certification lies in protecting against chemical fertilizers. Synthetic 
fertilizers are simply not allowed for in organic agriculture. 
Organic certification and any other certification programs of which organic certification is also 
mandatory (i.e. Bird friendly or Demeter) are essentially the only way to know the coffee production is 
being monitored and verified and that no pesticides (legal or illegal) are being used in production. Utz 
and Rainforest alliance certifications also provide very strict monitoring and rules regarding safe and 
legal chemical use, and may present a good alternative to organic certification, where available. 
The question is whether the chemicals they use make their way into your morning cup. The 
general industry assumption is that at peak temperatures (above 450 degrees) the roasting process will 
most likely burn off any residual chemicals or render then inert. It is unlikely these result in toxic 
residues in the cup of coffee, but we simply do not have the completed research to prove that 
definitively for each chemical. There are very few published studies that have documented residues in 
green coffee, let alone after roasting or in the beverage. Even if these pesticides do not end up as 
residue on the coffee, their use still has real human consequences. The fate of pesticides and other 
chemicals used in the agricultural production of coffee is still unclear. The safest choice for consumers is 
simply to purchase organic coffee. 
 
Compelling reasons to buy organic coffee: 
• 
The organic certification program is one of the only monitored and validated ways to know that 
illegal pesticides are not being used on coffee plants. 
• 
There are many pesticides banned by the US, EU, and Japan that are used during the production 
of coffee. 
• 
There is not enough publicly available scientific research to know that all chemicals used in the 
production of coffee do not make their way into the coffee cup. More research is needed to 
determine the fate of these chemicals after processing, roasting, and brewing coffee. 
• 
The health and safety of all people involved in coffee production is important to consumers, 
regardless of their country of origin. 
• 
The far-reaching detrimental effects of pesticides, known and unknown, provide enough 
evidence to choose to purchase only organic coffee.
