INFLUENCE OF MOISTURE AND pH
ON GROWTH AND SURVIVAL OF FUSARIUM OXYSPORUM
F.SP. BLABIDIS IN SOIL
By J. J. ORITSEJAFOR
Plant Pathology Division, Nigerian Institute for Oil Palm Research (NIFOR), Benin City, Nigeria
The effect of soil moisture and pH on growth and survival of three isolates of Fusarium
oxysporum f.sp. elaeidis in a sandy loam was studied. Survival of the fungus decreased with
increase in soil moisture. The highest survival of the pathogen was recorded at the lowest
levelof soil moisture tested (15% mc; 0'033 MPa), while it could not be isolated from infested
soil submerged under water for 12 weeks. Fluctuating moisture levels under outdoor
conditions were unfavourable to the fungus. Soil pH values of 5-7 were most favourable for
growth and survival of the fungus. Lysis of chlamydospore germlings was most pronounced
at pH 3 and the fungus could not be isolated after 12 weeks from infested soils maintained
at this value of soil pH.
Fusarium oxysporum Schlecht. f.sp. elaeidis Toovey
causes vascular wilt of the oil palm (Elaeis guineensis
jacq.). The disease is widespread in oil palm-
growing areas of the tropics (Wardlaw, 1948;
Prendergast,
1957;
Renard,
1976).
Initially
thought to be a disease of adult palms only, it is
now
known
to
also
affect
nursery
palms
(Aderungboye, 1976). The pathogen is known to
survive
in the
soil in the
dormant state
as
chlamydospores.
Relatively little is known about the effect of soil
environment on vascular wilt of the oil palm or F.
oxysporum f.sp. elaeidis. The success of flood-
fallowing to reclaim soil infested with the Panama
disease fungus (Stover, 1954, 1955) has focused
attention on the soil moisture relationships of
root-infecting fungi. Soil moisture is one factor
which can be controlled through irrigation and
flood-fallowing practices. Park (1958) reported that
he could not isolate F. oxysporum from Nigerian
soils supporting wilted oil palms, 24 weeks after
submergence in water. Turner (1982) states that
irrigation during the dry season may reduce the
incidence of the disease incited by the fungus.
Work in this Institute has revealed that field
populations of F. oxysporum are lowest during the
rainy season (July to September). There is no
report of the influence of pH on the survival of F.
oxysporum f.sp. elaeidis in the soil. Stover (1953a)
reported
that
F.
oxysporum
f.sp.
cubense
(E. F. Smith) Snyder & Hansen appeared to have
different pH optima in different soils. It may be
possible to manipulate soil moisture content and
soil pH to the detriment of the fungus in nursery
soils. Accordingly, the experiments reported here
were undertaken to obtain information on the effect
of different soil moisture and pH values on the
growth and survival of F. oxysporum f.sp. elaeidis.

and survival
The results of soil dilution platings after 1,4,8, 12
and
16 weeks of incubation of infested soils
maintained at the various levels of soil moisture for
each of the isolates of F. oxysporum f.sp, elaeidis are
presented in Fig. 1. Despite variations in the
number of pathogen propagules recorded between
isolates, there was a remarkable similarity in the
behaviour of all isolates at different levels of soil
moisture. For all three isolates, there was an initial
rise in pathogen population within the first 7 d of
incubation
at
the
lower
moisture
levels
(75- 15% me). This was however followed by a

The

more

Apparently, the lower soil moisture levels were
most favourable to the growth and survival of the
pathogen. With increase in soil moisture, there was

analysis of data collected at the 16th week showed
that survival of the pathogen was significantly
higher at the lowest moisture levels for all isolates
(T able 1). The fungus did not survive 12 weeks
under submerged conditions, and in some cases
could not be isolated after 8 weeks of incubation of
infested soils under this condition.

DISCUSSION
The changes in the population of F. oxysporum
f.sp. elaeidis when artificially introduced into soil
were monitored in this study. This was necessitated
by the fact that only a proportion of the naturally
occurring strains of F. oxysporum in oil palm
plantation soils are pathogenic to the crop (Locke,
1972). However, attempts were made to ensure that
levels of the pathogen used were comparable to
those existing in natural field soils.
It is evident from this study that moisture is an
important factor in the survival of F. oxysporum
f.sp. elaeidis in the soil. The results showed that
survival of the pathogen was significantly higher at
the lowest levels of soil moisture (Table 1). Stover
(1953b) and
Sequeria (1962) reported similar
results for F. oxysporum f.sp. cubense. Newcombe
(1960), using buried baits, detected only very little
activity for F. oxysporum f.sp. cubense after 12 weeks
of submergence of infested soils in water. Park
(1958)
could
not
isolate
F.
oxysporum
from
Nigerian soils supporting wilted oil palms 24 weeks
after submergence in water in the laboratory. In
the present investigation, F. oxysporum f.sp. elaeidis
could not be isolated from infested soils submerged
in water for 12 weeks.
Newcombe
(1960)
suggested that the
high
carbon dioxide content of soils is the main factor
involved in the elimination of the fungus under
conditions of submergence. Stover (1954) provided
evidence to show that factors other than carbon
dioxide could be involved in fungus elimination
from submerged soils. He showed that such factors
as soil temperature, oxygen absorption and a
constant food base are important in the survival of
F. oxysporum f.sp. cubense in submerged soils. Most
fungi are tolerant of high carbon dioxide content
(20 % or more) and low oxygen tensions where an
ample
nutrient
source
is present.
But
when
nutrients are limited carbon dioxide exerts much
greater effects (Brown, 1922).
It was observed that there was always an initial
increase in the population of F. oxysporum f.sp.
elaeidis in freshly infested soils particularly at the
lower levels of soil moisture (75-15 % me), Carry-
over of nutrients in the inoculum with which soils
were infested, and which perhaps became available
to the fungus on increasing soil moisture levels,
may be responsible for the initial increases in the
population of the
pathogen in infested soils.
However, at 100 % saturation and under conditions
of submergence, the available food could not be
utilized by
the
fungus
and
a decline
in its
population began almost immediately. At the lower
soil moisture levels, probably due to the exhaustion
of the available nutrients, the population began to
decline at a rate related to the soil moisture content;
slower rates of decline and larger populations were
maintained at lower levels of soil moisture. The
decline in the initially high population of the
pathogen could also be attributed to a tendency to

The results also indicate that F. oxysporum f.sp.
elaeidis can tolerate a wide range of soil pH.
Chlamydospores of the pathogen germinated at all
levels of soil pH investigated (Table 2). However,
at soil pH 3 germination of chlamydospores was
accompanied by lysis of germ-tubes (Fig. 3). This
occurrence may explain the sharp recession in the
population
and
complete
elimination
of
the
pathogen by the 12th week from soils maintained at
pH 3 (Fig. 4). Limited lysis of germ-tubes at soil
pH 4-7 on the other hand, and a tendency to form
new resting structures, may explain the gradual
decline in the initially high population and longer
survival of the pathogen in soils maintained at these
levels of pH. Apparently, the survival of the
pathogen in the soil is favoured by soil pH values
of 5-7, although good growth and survival can also
occur at a soil pH value of 4. Stover (1953a, b)
reported that F. oxysporum f.sp. cubense appeared
to have different pH optima in different soils, and
light-textured soils with acid reaction favoured the
fungus in contrast to alkaline heavy-textured soils
which were ' resistant' to disease spread. It is
pertinent to mention that major soils supporting oil
palms in Nigeria are acid sands.
