A chlorogenic acid-induced increase in
GLP-1 production may mediate the impact of
heavy coffee consumption on diabetes risk
Mark F. McCarty*
NutriGuard Research, 1051 Hermes Ave., Encinitas, CA 92024, USA
Received 15 March 2004; accepted 18 March 2004
Summary
Recent prospective epidemiology links heavy coffee consumption to a substantial reduction in risk for type
2 diabetes. Yet there is no evidence that coffee improves insulin sensitivity and, at least in acute studies, caffeine has
a negative impact in this regard. Thus, it is reasonable to suspect that coffee inﬂuences the risk for beta cell “failure”
that precipitates diabetes in subjects who are already insulin resistant. Indeed, there is recent evidence that coffee
increases production of the incretin hormone glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1), possibly owing to an inhibitory effect of
chlorogenic acid (CGA – the chief polyphenol in coffee) on glucose absorption. GLP-1 acts on beta cells, via cAMP-
dependent mechanisms, to promote the synthesis and activity of the transcription factor IDX-1, crucial for maintaining
the responsiveness of beta cells to an increase in plasma glucose. Conversely, the “glucolipotoxicity” thought to
initiate and sustain beta cell dysfunction in diabetics can suppress expression of this transcription factor. The
increased production of GLP-1 associated with frequent coffee consumption could thus be expected to counteract the
adverse impact of chronic free fatty acid overexposure on beta cell function in overweight insulin resistant subjects.
CGA’s putative impact on glucose absorption may reﬂect the ability of this compound to inhibit glucose-6-phosphate
translocase 1, now known to play a role in intestinal glucose transport. Delayed glucose absorption may itself protect
beta cells by limiting postprandial hyperglycemia – though, owing to countervailing effects of caffeine on plasma
glucose, and a paucity of relevant research studies, it is still unclear whether coffee ingestion blunts the postprandial
rise in plasma glucose. More generally, diets high in “lente carbohydrate”, or administration of nutraceuticals/
pharmaceuticals which slow the absorption of dietary carbohydrate, should help preserve efﬁcient beta cell function
by boosting GLP-1 production, as well as by blunting the glucotoxic impact of postprandial hyperglycemia on beta cell
function.

Why does coffee reduce diabetes risk?
Four recent prospective studies have linked heavy
regular use of coffee to decreased risk for type 2
diabetes, including a Harvard analysis that follows
up over 120,000 men and women for over a decade
[1–4]. The effect is robust and dose-dependent –

Caffeine seems unlikely to mediate this beneﬁt,
since acute studies indicate that caffeine tends to
impair insulin sensitivity by promoting epinephrine
secretion [5–7]. Moreover, as noted, apparent
beneﬁt has been observed in those who use de-
caffeinated coffee. Therefore, it is suspected that
the chief interesting organic compound in coffee,
other than caffeine – chlorogenic acid (CGA) –
may be responsible. Coffee drinkers are said to
ingest 500–1000 mg of CGA daily, and the con-
centration of this compound in coffee can be as
high as 2.5 mM [8,9].
These ﬁndings are surprising in that no previous
epidemiology or clinical research (at least that I am
aware of) has linked heavy coffee consumption to
superior insulin sensitivity or reversal of insulin
resistance syndrome. In contrast, a favorable im-
pact of moderate regular alcohol consumption on
insulin sensitivity has been documented in many
previous reports [10–13]. While it is conceivable
that this simply reﬂects a failure to look for such an
effect, another credible possibility is that the dia-
betes-preventive impact of coffee reﬂects, not an
impact on insulin sensitivity, but rather on the risk
for beta cell decompensation that precipitates
overt diabetes in subjects who are already insulin
resistant. Indeed, it is notable that, whereas cof-
fee emerges as relatively neutral with respect to its
impact on coronary risk in most prospective epi-
demiology, it is not associated with the marked
reduction in such risk that might be expected if
coffee were ameliorating insulin resistance syn-
drome (again, in marked contrast to the ﬁndings
with alcohol) [14,15]. Thus, it is reasonable to
suspect that beta cell function is the target of the
protective inﬂuence of coffee on diabetes risk.
Chlorogenic acid modulates incretin
production
How might CGA inﬂuence beta cell function? There
is recent evidence that CGA can inﬂuence incretin
production [9]. When subjects ingested sweetened
coffee – either caffeinated or decaffeinated – or a
comparable intake of sugar in water, the sub-
sequent rise in serum levels of glucose-dependent
insulinotrophic peptide (GIP) was suppressed, but
the rise in glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) was
enhanced. The same effect is seen following
acarbose administration, and likely reﬂects a re-
tardation of glucose absorption [16–19]; glucose
absorption in the proximal intestine provokes GIP
secretion, whereas glucose absorption more dis-
tally triggers release of GLP-1. Moreover, there is
reason to believe that CGA can slow glucose ab-
sorption; in vitro, at a concentration of 1 mM, CGA
was found to inhibit glucose uptake by brush border
membrane vesicles isolated from rat intestine, by
about 80% [20].
GLP-1, which boosts cAMP production in beta
cells by activating a membrane receptor, is known
to have a trophic effect on beta cell function that
ampliﬁes glucose-dependent insulin secretion and
can reverse the characteristic beta cell dysfunction
seen in type 2 diabetics – namely, a failure to in-
crease insulin secretion appropriately in response to
a rise in plasma glucose [21–23]. Indeed, modiﬁed
forms of GLP-1 with substantially longer plasma
half-lives are now showing promise as treatments
for type 2 diabetes [23–25]. One key effect of GLP-1
on beta cells is to increase expression, nuclear
translocation, and activity of the homeobox tran-
scription factor IDX-1 (a.k.a. PDX-1), which is cru-
cial for the proper differentiation and function of
beta cells [26–29]. In particular, IDX-1 stimulates
transcription of glucokinase and GLUT-2, of essen-
tial importance to appropriate glucose responsive-
ness in beta cells [30–33]. The “glucolipotoxicity”
which provokes and sustains beta cell dysfunction in
type 2 diabetics [34–38] is most likely associated
with
a
decrease
in
the
expression
of
IDX-1
[31,39–42]. It is therefore reasonable to suspect
that a sustained increase in the production of GLP-
1, by inducing IDX-1 in beta cells, would tend to
offset the down-regulatory impact of excess free
fatty acids and glucose on this transcription factor,
and thus possibly prevent or retard the onset of beta
cell “failure” in insulin-resistant subjects at risk for
diabetes.
In light of these considerations, it is proposed
that high regular intakes of CGA, by delaying ab-
sorption of meal glucose and thereby boosting
production of GLP-1, may tend to prevent or delay
onset of diabetes in at-risk subjects by offsetting
the harmful effects of free fatty acid overexposure
and postprandial hyperglycemia on beta cell func-
tion. Indeed, acarbose, which likewise delays car-
bohydrate absorption and boosts GLP-1 production,
has been demonstrated to decrease risk for type 2
diabetes in glucose intolerant subjects [43,44].
This hypothesis is all the more attractive in light
of evidence that CGA is virtually undetectable
in the plasma of coffee drinkers; its hydrolysis

How CGA slows glucose absorption
Furthermore, there are now theoretical grounds
for predicting that CGA can slow glucose absorp-
tion. The classical model of intestinal glucose ab-
sorption holds that glucose is actively transported
through the apical membrane of enterocytes by a
sodium gradient-driven glucose transporter, and
then exits passively to the interstitial space via
GLUT2 transporters in the basolateral membrane.
However, intestinal glucose absorption has re-
cently been found to be virtually normal in mice
and humans that fail to express a functionally
competent GLUT2 [49,50]. This has revealed the
existence of a second prominent route for glucose
absorption. After transport through the apical
membrane, a high proportion of glucose is phos-
phorylated by hexokinase. The resulting glucose-6-
phosphate, if not oxidized for fuel, is eventually
transported into the lumen of the endoplasmic
reticulum, where it is hydrolyzed by glucose-6-
phosphatase to once again yield free glucose; this
glucose can then reach the interstitial space either
by exocytosis of microsomes, or, after exiting the
endoplasmic reticulum, via GLUT2 transporters
[49,50].
The glucose-6-phosphate in enterocytes enters
the lumen of the endoplasmic reticulum via facili-
tated transport mediated by glucose-6-phosphate
translocase 1. A patient genetically deﬁcient in this
transporter has been shown to experience mild
carbohydrate malabsorption [50]. It is thus of
considerable interest that CGA has been shown to
inhibit the activity of this transporter in hepato-
cytes [51]. The fact that this transporter plays a
crucial role in hepatic glucose output has given rise
to speculation that CGA could beneﬁt diabetics by
quelling excessive hepatic glucose production.
Since it seems unlikely that much intact CGA
reaches the liver after oral administration, this
hope is unlikely to be realized. Nonetheless, in
light of evidence that about one third of an oral
dose of CGA is taken up intact by enterocytes, CGA
may indeed have the ability to inhibit this trans-
porter in enterocytes, thereby slowing glucose
absorption. The possibility remains that CGA could
inﬂuence glucose absorption by other mechanisms
as well – Welsch and colleagues have suggested
that CGA diminishes the sodium gradient that
drives apical glucose transport [20] – but inhibition
of glucose-6-phosphate translocase 1 seems likely
to contribute to CGA’s impact on glucose absorp-
tion, and may indeed prove to be the only way in
which CGA inﬂuences this process.
A skeptic might reasonably ask, if CGA can slow
glucose absorption, why has not a favorable impact
of coffee on the glycemic index of meals been well
established in previous research? Possibly because
the epinephrine-mediated pro-lipolytic, pro-glyco-
genolytic actions of caffeine have an acute coun-
tervailing effect on insulin sensitivity and hepatic
glucose output that can mask the impact of CGA on
glycemic index. Thus, oral glucose tolerance has
been found to be poorer after caffeinated coffee is
ingested than after decaffeinated coffee [9,52],
and administration of caffeine per se has an acute
adverse effect on glucose tolerance mediated by
increased epinephrine secretion [53,54]. Several
studies have concluded that pre-administration of
coffee has a detrimental impact on intravenous
glucose tolerance [55–57]. Unfortunately, surpris-
ingly few studies have examined oral glucose tol-
erance following coffee ingestion. One such study,
published over 30 years ago, observed that, when
5g of instant coffee was co-administered with an
oral glucose tolerance test, 30-min and 1-h plasma
glucose levels were signiﬁcantly lower than after a
comparable glucose tolerance test without coffee
[58] – a ﬁnding evidently consistent with a role for
CGA in slowing glucose absorption. On the other
hand, a more recent study, using a much smaller
bolus of glucose, found that glucose excursions
were signiﬁcantly higher after ingesting coffee [9];
systemic effects of caffeine may have been more

If indeed coffee ingested with meals has a no-
tably favorable effect on the oral glucose tolerance
of caffeine-tolerant chronic coffee drinkers, then a
portion of coffee’s diabetes-preventive potential
may stem from a blunting of postprandial hyper-
glycemia – thereby limiting the glucotoxicity that
promotes beta cell failure. Not unlikely, such an
effect contributes to the documented diabetes-
preventive activity of acarbose [43,44].
The hypothesis that increased production of
GLP-1 mediates much of the favorable impact of
heavy coffee ingestion on beta cell function, can
be challenged by noting that an increase in GLP-1
production is countered by a decrease in GIP pro-
duction. Like GLP-1, GIP has trophic effects on
beta cells mediated in part by increased production
of cAMP [21]. cAMP [21]; However, whereas GLP-1
or its derivatives have been found to be thera-

diabetes, sensitivity to GIP is lost. Thus, GLP-1 may
be inherently more useful for diabetes prevention
or treatment. Further clariﬁcation of this matter is
needed.
Chlorogenic acid – another strategy
for achieving “Lente Carbohydrate?”
A corollary of this hypothesis is that administration
of supplemental CGA with meals (without caffeine)
should lower the glycemic index of meals while
promoting GLP-1 production and reducing risk for
diabetes. The impact of CGA on glycemic index and
GLP-1 would of course be readily testable in short-
term studies. An extract of green coffee beans rich
in CGA (55%) is now commercially available in the
US, and would lend itself to such studies.
If such studies demonstrate that CGA can indeed
retard carbohydrate absorption in vivo, CGA will
expand the roster of strategies that can be used to
achieve this goal. Aside from choosing low-glyce-
mic-index starchy foods (characterized by such
features as a high amylose content, structural in-
tactness, and the presence of soluble ﬁber or amy-
lase inhibitory factors) [60], absorption of dietary
carbohydrate can be delayed by co-administration
of alpha-glucosidase inhibitors (e.g. acarbose, mi-
glitol), legume-derived amylase inhibitors, and sol-
uble ﬁber (glucomannan may be of particular utility
in this regard) [61–64]. Recent studies demon-
strating that acarbose therapy can reduce risk not
only for diabetes [43,44], but also for coronary
events and hypertension [65], strongly suggest that
diets high in “lente carbohydrate” [66] have a very
considerable contribution to make to prevention of
both diabetes and vascular disease. Conversely,
recent epidemiology has linked high-glycemic-load
diets to increased risk for diabetes, coronary dis-
ease, and various types of cancer [67–76]. Evi-
dently, nutraceuticals and pharmaceuticals which
can delay the absorption of dietary carbohydrate
have a bright future in preventive medicine.
