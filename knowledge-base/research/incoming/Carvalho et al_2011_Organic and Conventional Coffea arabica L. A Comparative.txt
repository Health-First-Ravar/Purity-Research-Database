Organic and Conventional Coffea arabica L.: A Comparative
Study of the Chemical Composition and Physiological,
Biochemical and Toxicological Effects in Wistar Rats
Dayene do Carmo Carvalho & Maisa Ribeiro Pereira Lima Brigagão &
Marcelo Henrique dos Santos & Fernanda Borges Araújo de Paula &
Alexandre Giusti-Paiva & Luciana Azevedo
Published online: 27 April 2011
# Springer Science+Business Media, LLC 2011
Abstract Differentiation between organic and conventional
coffee has increased due to the growing demand and high
consumption of healthy foods that contain compounds with
antioxidant potential, which have been associated with the
reduction of chronic diseases. We used organic and
conventional coffee in powder 4% (w/w) and infusions
5%, 10% and 20% (w/v) incorporated in a commercial diet
to test in vivo. The levels of chlorogenic acid, caffeine and
trigonelline were determined by high performance liquid
chromatography. The body weight, weight gain, food
consumption, aberrant foci crypt, mucin depleted foci,
stress biomarkers protein carbonyl and malondialdehyde,
biochemical parameters and behavior of the rats were
compared between the experimental and control groups
within a framework of colon carcinogenesis. The organic
coffee showed higher levels of chlorogenic acid, caffeine
and trigonelline than conventional, however, this difference
did not significantly affect behavior. The infusions had an
antioxidant effect, reducing the levels of malondialdehyde;
however, the biochemical parameters of the serum were not
altered, and there was neither induction nor prevention of
preneoplasic lesions.

Introduction
Recently, organic food has gained popularity due to a
demand for healthy foods and greater attention to produc-
tion methods. The International Federation of Organic
Agriculture Movements (IFOAM) sets standards for organ-
ically grown food production, and it considers these food
sectors to be growing [1]. Organic coffee is defined as
having a higher added value and is treated with organic
fertilizers, bio-fertilizers or organic matter, unlike conven-
tional coffee production, which uses chemicals, pesticides
and chemical fertilizers [2]. Studies have demonstrated few
and inconsistent differences in the nutrient composition of
organically produced foods compared to foods produced by
conventional methods [1]. Coffee is a complex mixture of
compounds whose levels are dependent on many factors
such as cultivation, environmental factors, processing and
crop management. Among the components in coffee, many
may be considered bioactive: niacin, caffeine, chlorogenic
acid, trigonelline, kaweol, cafestol, tannins and quinidine
[3]. There is growing interest about the effects of these
compounds on health, and their use may be related to the
prevention of diseases such as cancer, hepatic function,
cardiovascular diseases like hypertension [4] and a decrease
of serum uric acid concentrations [5].
Coffee drinking, however, does not always have exclu-
sively beneficial results; it can have consequences such as
decrease bioavailability of nutrients in the diet [4] and acid
reflux symptoms caused by coffee’s acidic components [6].
Besides reducing the frequency of certain diseases, studies
indicate that coffee may reduce stress, which is associated
with preneoplasic lesions that lead to cancer development
and progression. Colorectal tissue is regularly exposed to a
variety of hazardous chemicals, and the reactive nitrogen
species or reactive oxygen species formed during the
metabolic transformation, has been thought to play an
important role in carcinogenesis [7]. The routinely studied
biomarkers of oxidative stress are products of lipid
peroxidation such as malondialdehyde or protein carbonyl
content [8].
Until now, studies on the effects of coffee in disease
prevention have been commonly performed with samples
from conventional management. This work aims to perform
a chemical analysis of the bioactive compounds that
support the effects of coffee on the modulation of colon
cancer, reduction of oxidative stress, change in biochemical
parameters and behavioral of rats to compare organic and
conventional management systems.
Materials and Methods
Coffee Samples and Chemicals
The samples of roasted ground coffee were of good quality,
of the Catuaí red variety 2008/2009 crop and medium-
roasted (200–215 °C), over a 15-minute roasting time. The
organic and conventional samples were provided by the
Associação de Pequenos Produtores de Poço Fundo, Minas
Gerais, Brazil and the organic coffee is certified (BCS-
OKO Garantie Master Certificates nº POCO-7569/07.08/
14291-BR). The fertilization was performed with castor
meal micro-nutrient to organic coffee, and fertilizing
containing nitrogen, phosphorus and potassium (20:5:20)
to conventional coffee.
All chemicals, solvents and standards were of analytical
grade: chlorogenic acid, caffeine, trigonelline, methanol, 1,2-
dimetylhydrazine, coomassie brilliant blue, thiobarbituric
acid, guanidine hydrochloride, 2,4-dinitrophenylhydrazine
and methylene blue 1% (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO,
USA), chloridric acid, phosphoric acid, sodium chloride,
formaldehyde, toluidine blue, acetic acid, ethilenediamine
acid, trichloroacetic acid, ethyl acetate and sodium hydroxide
(Merck, San Diego, CA, USA). The water was purified on a
compact Milli-Q (Millipore, Milford, MA, USA).
Analysis of Chlorogenic Acid, Caffeine and Trigonelline
by HPLC
Infusions were prepared by adding 10 g of coffee powder to
100 ml of water heated to 90 °C. The mixture was
homogenized and vacuum filtered with qualitative filter
paper, and the filtrate was lyophilized. To obtain a methanol
extract, 1 g of the coffee powder was refluxed with 4.0 ml
of methanol for 2 h (60.0±2.0 °C), filtered and exposed at
environmental temperature until the solvent evaporated
completely. Samples and standards (chlorogenic acids,
caffeine and trigonelline) were dissolved in methanol
(2.0 mg ml−1) (n=3). The analysis used a PROMINENCE
chromatograph SHIMADZU model coupled with a spec-
trophotometric detector UV/Vis model SPD-M20A, a
power injector SIL-20A and a C-18 column of 25 cm.
The mobile phase consisted of H2O/CH3COOH (95/5 v/v)
(A) and acetonitrile (B) using gradient elution from A/B:
0 min 95/5, 5 min 95/5, 10 min 87/13, with a flow rate
0.7 ml min−1. Detection of the UV/Vis signal was

Animals, Diets, Experimental Design and Open Field Test
The University Ethical Committee for Animal Research
approved the protocols used in this study (protocol no. 235/
09). Four-week-old male Wistar rats were obtained from
CEMIB (UNICAMP Campinas, SP, Brazil). The animals
were maintained at 22±2 °C, 55±10% humidity, with a 12-
h light-dark cycle. They were fed commercial Nuvilab CR-
1® (Nuvital Nutriente S/A, Colombo, PR, Brazil) supple-
mented with organic and conventional coffee, powder or
infusions, in the modified diets.
The infusions were prepared by adding 5 g, 10 g and
20 g of coffee to 100 ml of 90 °C water and vacuum
filtering them with qualitative paper filter, and then, they
were incorporated into the diet (100 ml infusion kg−1 of
commercial diet); the powdered coffee was mixed into the
commercial diet (40 g powder kg−1 of commercial diet).
The diets were offered to 140 animals randomly allocated
into 14 groups (n=10) for 12 weeks (Fig. 1). The proximate
analysis of the diets was performed following the Associ-
ation of Official Analytical Chemists [10]. To study the
protective effect of coffee, groups 1–9 were given four
injections of DMH (40 mg kg−1 b.w., subcutaneous) twice a
week for two weeks; to study the harmful effect of coffee,
groups 10–14 received injections of an EDTA solution
(1.5% w/v, DMH vehicle). At the end of the study, all
animals were anesthetized with ketamine and xylazine and
then sacrificed by withdrawing blood from the heart. At
necropsy, the liver and colon were removed from all
animals. The blood was centrifuged at 2,500 rpm for
10 min to obtain serum, and the liver was perfused with
0.9% (w/v) NaCl solution; all samples were stored at −18 °C.
The colon was removed, opened longitudinally and fixed flat
in buffered formalin for 24 h, then placed in 70% (v/v)
ethanol.
We evaluated, in open field test, the animals that
received only EDTA (1.5% w/v) to study the effects of
organic and conventional coffee on a rat that did not have
induced cancer. The monitoring of the animals was
performed at the 4th, 8th and 12th week. In the test, each
rat was placed in the center of the field and the number of
peripheral squares, the number of central squares which the
animal stepped in with all four paws, as well as the number
of surveys that the animal took in 5 min were monitored
[11].
Biochemical Analysis: Oxidative Stress in the Liver
and Biochemical Parameters in Serum
Protein concentrations were determined using the Bradford
assay [12]. For the analyses of oxidative stress, the perfused
liver was homogenized in phosphate buffer pH 7.0 and
centrifuged for 5 min at 3,500 rpm and the supernatant was
used for the determination of lipid peroxidation and assayed
for the presence of protein carbonyl [13, 14]. For the
biochemical parameters, the total serum levels of choles-
terol, glucose, triglycerides, creatinine, urea, aspartate
aminotransferase and alanine aminotransferase were deter-
mined by an enzymatic method based on the Reitman and
Frankel reaction [15].
Colon Processing, Classical ACF and MDF Analysis
For classical ACF, the colons were stained with 1% (w/v)
methylene blue for 1 min to determine the number of ACF/
colon. The number of aberrant crypts in each ACF was
determined by light microscopy at 40x magnification. After
destaining in 70% (v/v) ethanol with gentle shaking at room
temperature, the colons were re-stained with 0.025% (w/v)

Statistical Analysis
Evaluations of mean body weight, diet consumption,
centesimal composition, oxidative stress and biochemical
parameters were performed using one-way analysis of
variance (ANOVA/Tukey’s test). The total number of
ACF, number of AC per focus and the relationship between
crypt/ACF in the different groups were analyzed by the
Kruskal-Wallis test, and the open field was applied by
ANOVA (p=0.05) followed by the Newman-Keuls test
using the software BioEstat® 4.0 [11, 16]
Results and Discussion
The demand for studies with organic coffee has grown due to
the rarity of related papers and evidence that crop manage-
ment can change the chemical composition of food and,
therefore, its effects on health. In this paper we have analyzed
the effects of coffee, organic and conventional, to compare
different parameters such as behavior and potential colon
lesion-inducing/protector agents, as well as effects on oxida-
tive stress and biochemical parameters. To minimize the
influence of environmental factors in the analysis, both coffee
types were produced in the same geographic region and were
all harvested during the 2008/2009 season. Thus, the differ-
ences found can be attributed predominantly to fertilization
methods characteristic of each management.
Bioactive compounds, such as chlorogenic acid, caffeine
and trigonelline, were identified by comparison of sample
retention times (in minutes) to standards, and the amount of
compound present was quantified using an external
standard calibration. The retention times for the standards
of chlorogenic acids, caffeine and trigonelline, were 17.993,
21.084 and 3.967 min, respectively. The linearity of the
method was 1–30 μg ml−1 for chlorogenic acid, 10.0–
60.0 μg ml−1 for caffeine and 15–50 μg ml−1 for trigonel-
line. The limits of detection/quantification obtained were
of 0.62/2.11 μg g−1 for chlorogenic acid, 0.99/3.30 μg g−1
for caffeine and 4.05/13.51 μg g−1 for trigonelline,
respectively. The infusion and the methanol extracts of
organic coffee have higher concentrations of chlorogenic
acids, caffeine and trigonelline than the conventional ones,
which may be due to the style of management (Table 1). In
comparative studies between organic and conventional
products, organic rabbit meat, milk of cow, sheep and
goat, and basil showed higher levels of organic com-
pounds; opposing studies hypothesized that higher levels
of minerals in conventional coffee were influenced by the
fertilizer used [2, 17–19].
Among these organic compounds, caffeine supplied in
gavage exerted muscle relaxant activities and sedative
effects or psychostimulate effects [20]. However, when we
evaluated these possible nonspecific effects of coffee,
organic and conventional, by the open field test, neither
treatment changed the numbers of crossings nor rearings
when compared to the control group (data not shown) and
despite differences in the levels of caffeine between the
conventional and organic treatments (Table 1), furthermore,
these levels of caffeine present in both managements were
not enough behavioral changes in rats.
We carried out previous experiments in vivo to aid in
assessing the best form of application and the appropriate
concentrations of coffee to use. In this study, the animals
received infusions of coffee at 1, 2, 3, 6 and 12% (w/v) as
source of water and the same concentrations incorporated
into their commercial diet, while the control group received
only water and the commercial diet. Through the evaluation
of body-weight, body-weight gain, diet intake and water/
infusion volume intake, it was shown that animals treated
with infusion concentrations of 6% and 12% (w/v)
presented a reduction in the infusion and diet consumption,
and, consequently, body-weight loss. The groups that
ingested coffee infusions at concentrations of 1, 2 and 3%
(w/v) as source of water and diet had results indistinguish-
able from the control group. However, these concentrations
are not representative of the coffee infusions consumed by
the population, which contains 10% (w/v) concentration on
average. Furthermore, in previous experiments in vivo it
was necessary to renew the infusions daily due to the
oxidation of coffee and the rejection of the old infusion by
the rats, and the diets that incorporated by infusion allowed
for the application of higher concentrations of coffee, can
be stored for a longer period.
Thus, we decided to incorporate infusions and powder
coffee in the commercial diets aiming to facilitate the
procedures and to apply the levels of coffee equivalent to
the average consumption of the population (10% w/v), its
half (5% w/v) and its double (20% w/v), and to also
incorporate powder (4% w/w). The incorporate powder is
due to significance of fiber as a modulator of physiological
and pathological processes [21]. The composition of the
experimental diets was comparable to the commercial diet,
aside from containing organic or conventional coffee. The
average concentrations of carbohydrate, ash, lipids, proteins
and water were 45.55±0.55 g 100 g−1, 8.86±0.23 g
100 g−1, 4.98±0.35 g 100 g−1, 27.80±0.64 g 100 g−1,
11.89±0.35 g 100 g−1, respectively. These values were
found to fulfill the needs of the animals and are consistent
with those presented by Oliveira et al. [22].
During the experimental period, after the induction of
neoplasic lesions (4th week) there was no change in body-
weight gain or consumption of the daily diet (26.12±9.23 g

DMH can also be metabolized into a methyl free-radical
generating a hydroxyl-radical or hydrogen peroxide that can
induce oxidative damage in vulnerable targets (such as
membrane unsaturated fatty acids, protein thiols, and DNA
bases) resulting neoplasia, inflammation and aging [24].
These negative effects may be reduced by the defense
systems of the organism and the presence of antioxidants
[25, 26]. In this study, the liver was chosen to analyze
oxidative stress because it produces large amounts of oxygen
free-radicals during detoxification of xenobiotic and toxic
substances. The free radicals attack proteins causing oxida-
tion and produce protein carbonyls, which is a step in protein
oxidation. Furthermore, free radicals can cause lipidic
peroxidation processes and many secondary toxic oxidation
products are formed, e.g., malondialdehyde [8]. Thus, we
evaluated the oxidative damage by lipidic peroxidation
through the levels of MDA and protein oxidation through
the levels of PCO. The levels of protein carbonyls (Table 2)
did not show differences between the groups, verifying that
the DMH and coffee, organic and conventional, did not
produce stress beyond that which was generated spontane-
ously, with an average value of 3.55±1.83 nmol PCO/mg
protein. The MDA levels did not differ between the control
groups that received DMH and EDTA, with an average of
11.88±1.52 μmol/mg protein. Despite the generation of
oxidative damages by DMH, in the levels of oxidative stress
were the same as those generated spontaneously. These
results differ from Rajeshkumar and Kuttan [27], probably
due to the dose and the time of action of the DMH in the
90-day study. The above-mentioned authors administered a
total of 120 mg/kg b.w. of DMH between days 45 and 69 of
the experiment, prior to sacrificing the animals, in contrast to
the 160 mg/kg b.w. between days 73 and 82 in the present
study. According to Sengottuvelan et al. [28] DMH is able to
increase reactive oxygen metabolites in tumor cells in the
early stages, but in later stages of carcinogenesis (in these
studies, days 69 and 82) may diminish the lipidic perox-
idation of membranes, and reduce the production of lipidic
hydroperoxides. Thus, the utilization of DMH in later stages
of carcinogenesis limited the interpretation of the protective
effect against oxidative damage in this study, because the
timing was not sufficient to maintain detectable oxidative
damage.

Recent studies have shown that coffee intake in the form of
an infusion decreased levels of lipidic peroxidation [29],
and other experiments with rats show data similar to the
present study (i.e., an increase in the antioxidant levels after
administration of coffee extract from a carcinogen with N-
methylpyridinium-2-aldoxime and carbon tetrachloride)
[26, 30]. In addition to oxidative stress in the liver,
biochemical parameters were analyzed in serum. We
observed that neither coffee nor DMH produced modifica-
tions of the biochemical parameters of serum levels (p<
0.05); the average values in the serum of the animals were
236.21±29.14 mg/dl glucose, 34.98±3.47 mg/dl cholester-
ol, 62.35±4.11 mg/dl triglycerides, 0.56±0.05 mg/dl
creatinine, 48.39±1.99 mg/dl urea, 19.62±2.30 U RF/ml
AST and 69.54±11.45 U RF/ml ALT. Thus, neither coffee
nor DMH caused dysfunctional renal, hepatic or metabolic
effects, unlike the study performed by Duarte et al. [29],
which confirmed the ability of coffee to reduce some
parameters such as triglycerides and cholesterol.

The interception of free radicals generated in the cellular
metabolism by the bioactive compounds (as observed in the
analyses of MDA), suggests that coffee may act as antimuta-
genic or anticarcinogenic; however, the roles of these bioactive
compounds have not been well defined in process of the
carcinogenesis [8, 22, 23]. Therefore, we supplied organic
and conventional coffee from the beginning of the experi-
ment, including the induction period with DMH. All DMH-
initiated cancers developed classical ACF and mucin-negative
ACF (G1-9) (Fig. 2a and b), whereas non-classical ACF and
mucin-negative ACF were detected in the colons from non-
initiated groups (G10-14) (Table 3). For data analysis we
used the Kruskal-Wallis’s test, once that the data obtained
from morphological analysis of groups are independent and
not follow a normal distribution. Then, we evidenced that the
number of ACF, the total number of AC/colon and the ACF/
colon showed no significant difference in the groups treated
with organic or conventional coffee when compared to the
group receiving only DMH (G2-9 vs. G1 p<0.05).
The same result occurred with mucin-negative ACF
analysis carried out with induced groups (G1-9), showing
the preneoplasic lesions at a high stage (Table 3). Despite
the fact that coffee reduced levels of MDA (Table 2) and
contained different concentrations of chemical compounds
in each treatments (Table 1), the supplied dietary coffee of
both treatments did not suppress the development of ACF
or crypt multiplicity at the dose levels tested (Table 3), and
the results of mucin-negative ACF indicated that coffee was
not able to suppress the clonal expansion of DMH-initiated
cells in the colonic mucosa.
However, considering the presence of organic com-
pounds and fiber in the coffee, some studies with rats have
contradictory findings in long-term experiments. Rao et al.
[21] showed that the dried coffee beans at 1% (w/w)
incorporated into a diet did not differ in ACF number; the
same result was found in our study with dried coffee at 4%
(w/w), organic and conventional. However, at higher levels,
such as 10% (w/w), these authors observed a reduction in
the number of ACF. Thus, the fiber and the chemical
compounds contained in up to 4% (w/w) of the coffee did
not have the ability to inhibit colon carcinogenesis. In
contrast, treatment with caffeine at 0.065% (w/w), per-
formed by Wang et al. [31], reduced ACF development.
This level of caffeine is approximately four times greater
than the levels supplied to the animals in this study via
organic or conventional coffee, 0.0182 and 0.174% (w/w),
respectively (Table 1). The discrepancy in the effective dose
of caffeine between the studies implies that it is possible
that higher concentrations, or doses, of coffee are needed to
effectively reach the lumen of the colon and exert chemo-
preventive effects, suggesting that the degree of intestinal
induction may vary, depending on DMH dose and the
levels of coffee administered.
Conclusion
We report that the organic coffee showed higher concen-
trations of chlorogenic acid, caffeine and trigonelline than
conventional, but despite this difference, both treatments
did not cause behavioral changes. The rats treated with the
organic and conventional coffee in concentrations of 5%,
10% and 20% (w/v), but not the 4% (w/w) of the powder,
showed reduced levels of MDA indicating antioxidant
effects. However, there were no changes in the biochemical
parameters tested and none of the experimental groups
showed anticarcinogenic effects. The results obtained in
animal experiments showed that coffee, organic and
conventional, had beneficial effects on health, but a more
stringent screening is needed to be able to infer potential
beneficial effects for humans.
