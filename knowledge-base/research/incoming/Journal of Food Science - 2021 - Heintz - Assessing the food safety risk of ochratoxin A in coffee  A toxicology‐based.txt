Abstract: Under the Food Safety Modernization Act (FSMA) and preventive
controls (PCs) regulations, food manufacturers must consider whether PCs are
needed for potential hazards present in food. The mycotoxin ochratoxin A (OTA)
is considered a chemical hazard under FSMA. It is produced by several fungal
species and can be present in various agricultural commodities, including cof-
fee. OTA presents a unique scenario in food safety, because it is known to be a
potential risk; because heating may destroy it, but not completely; and because
the hazard profile suggests it is not acutely toxic at the occurrence levels in coffee,
although at high exposure levels, it is potentially nephrotoxic and carcinogenic in
animal models. In the absence of US compliance levels, it is important for the risk
assessor and risk manager to determine whether PCs are warranted. To address
this complex situation in the coffee industry, we combined food safety and toxi-
cology risk assessment principles to examine the available information on OTA
hazard and risk in coffee. Exposure and health-based benchmarks for OTA in
coffee, established by reviewing peer-reviewed literature, food recall databases,
and authoritative reviews, resulted in large margins-of-exposure for both single
and repeated exposure scenarios. Furthermore, no evidence was identified from
historical data to suggest OTA is acutely toxic in humans from coffee consump-
tion or other exposure sources. Therefore, findings from this assessment indicate
that no PC is warranted for US coffee manufactures, based on the low severity
and likelihood of risk according to margin-of-exposure estimates and historical
data.

INTRODUCTION
The US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and Food
Safety Modernization Act (FSMA) require that the food
industry help the FDA to improve the agency’s capacity
to prevent foodborne illness outbreaks and reduce food
risks for consumers. Under this law, food manufacturing
facilities are expected to prepare food safety plans. The
food safety plan identifies potential hazards and distin-
guishes those that require preventive controls to prevent
foodborne illness or injury (FSPCA, 2016). In particular,
under the current good manufacturing practice (CGMP),
hazard analysis (HA), and risk based preventive controls
for human food regulation (hereinafter referred to as PC
for Human Food), food manufacturers must have quali-
fied individuals in place to ensure that food safety plans
meet regulatory expectations. These individuals, desig-
nated as preventive control qualified individuals (PCQIs),
are usually part of a multi-disciplinary team that must per-
form a HA on their operations to determine whether any

hazards are associated with the product they manufacture
that could, without preventive controls (PCs), cause illness
or injury to the consumer. Putting PCs in place ensures that
safe products reach the marketplace; the HA is conducted
to determine whether a PC is required, and the nature
and role of the PC (FSPCA, 2016). “Preventive Controls are
defined as those risk-based, reasonably appropriate proce-
dures, practices and processes that a person knowledge-
able about the safe manufacturing, processing, packing or
holding of food would employ to significantly minimize or
prevent the hazards identified under the hazard analysis
that are consistent with the current scientific understand-
ing of safe food manufacturing, processing, packaging or
holding at the time of analysis” (21 CFR 117.3). PCs require
monitoring, corrective actions, and verification steps.
For a commodity such as coffee, it would be expected
that the PCQI and multi-disciplinary team would consider
mycotoxins such as ochratoxin A (OTA), because they are
considered chemical hazards under the HA step of the
food safety plan creation. OTA was first reported as poten-
tially being present in green coffee (Levi et al., 1974), and
is named from the mold Aspergillus ochraceus from which
it was originally isolated (Studer-Rohr et al., 1995). OTA
is also produced by some other Aspergillus and Penicil-
lium species of mold. Prior to harvest, coffee fruit may
become contaminated with OTA via introduction through
the flower or an insect invasion (e.g., coffee bore beetle
[ICO, 2009; FAO, 2006]). During harvest, coffee fruit that
remains on the ground or is held before processing may be
subject to conditions that favor mold growth and are likely
to become contaminated with OTA. Additional opportuni-
ties for contamination arise during processing, particularly
for wet processing, as well as during storage, transporta-
tion, and trading (ICO, 2009; FAO, 2006). Aspergillus is the
most probable mold species to produce OTA in coffee post-
harvest (Bucheli & Taniwaki, 2002; Suarez-Quiroz et al.,
2005), and it is generally recognized that molds associated
with OTA can be present in coffee at some level, even when
processors follow good agricultural practices (GAP) and
good handling practices (GHP) to minimize the likelihood
of mold growth. OTA has also been found in other agricul-
tural commodities, including cereals and cereal products,
pulses, cacao products, nuts, spices, beer, grape juice, dry-
vine fruits, and wine (EFSA, 2006, 2020; Walker & Larsen,
2005).
Under the HA steps, OTA warrants consideration,
because it is known to cause nephrotoxicity and carcino-
genicity (FAO/WHO, 1991) and has been classified by the
International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC, 1993)
monographs program in Group 2B (possibly carcinogenic
to humans). A number of authoritative bodies (EFSA,
2006, 2020; JECFA, 1995; Kuiper-Goodman et al., 2010)
have evaluated the potential for OTA to cause health effects
in humans. These authorities have not identified evidence
to suggest that OTA is acutely toxic; rather, adverse effects
have been observed primarily in repeated-dose studies in
rat, mouse, rabbit, and porcine models. The primary out-
come of concern relates to renal toxicity and carcinogenic-
ity following exposures to high doses of OTA in exper-
imental rodent studies (EFSA, 2020). This identification
of a potential hazard is the first step in the HA, but the
amount, likelihood of occurrence, and severity of hazard
are all also given consideration when deciding whether a
PC will be required to minimize the occurrence of the haz-
ard, as opposed to relying on foundational programs such
as good manufacturing practices (GMPs).
The supply chain for coffee (like many other agricul-
tural commodities) is quite complex from farm (origin)
to table. OTA contamination may occur on the farm, and
other than good agricultural practices, it is not practical,
nor is it required, to implement a PC at origin. The quality
requirements of the green coffee supply chain (e.g., mois-
ture control and organoleptic evaluation) work to reduce
defective green coffee beans and concurrently reduce OTA
levels. Implementing a PC at a food manufacturing site
is an option, but should not be used unless the hazard
evaluation determines a PC to be necessary. To determine
whether hazard control steps should be included in a food
safety plan, it is critical to differentiate between the “pres-
ence” and “risk” of a hazard, because implementing a PC
instead of relying on foundational programs is a signifi-
cant financial and time commitment on the part of the
food manufacturer. The HA is intended to focus the man-
ufacturer on the specific hazards in their entire process for
which a PC might be needed. The purpose of the HA is not
to produce PCs for every hazard identified; instead, the HA
is intended to produce scientifically based justifications for
an ingredient or processing step, regardless of whether a
PC is required. This approach is the most pragmatic and
cost-effective way to reduce food safety risks in the supply
chain, because it ensures that the manufacturer’s efforts
correlate with the magnitude of the food safety risks in
their supply chain.
One way coffee manufacturers lower the potential for
OTA to be present is to follow Codex Alimentarius Best
Practices, which suggests that moisture in green coffee
stay below 12.5% to reduce the likelihood of mold growth
(ICO, 2009). Most roasters also include organoleptic eval-
uation of green coffee, because moldy coffee can neg-
atively affect taste and aroma attributes. Quality cup-
ping practices further minimize the likelihood of OTA
presence in the finished product (ICO, 2005). A 2005
International Coffee Organization (ICO) guidance docu-
ment found that the removal of coffee beans with visual
defects or earthy/moldy aromas reduced the frequency of
detecting OTA at levels greater than 10 ppb (ICO, 2005).

All of the practices mentioned above are commonly used
in coffee roasters’ quality control programs. The imple-
mentation of a PC step would be significantly different
and more cumbersome than relying on quality control pro-
grams, which is the more common way that coffee roasters
currently control for OTA presence.
Compliance requirements vary by geographic region.
OTA levels are regulated by some countries, such as the
European Union (EU) and Vietnam, to a maximum level
of 5 µg/kg for roasted coffee (Annex of Commission Reg-
ulation [EC] No. 1881/2006; USDA, 2013). The US has no
regulation or recommended levels for OTA in food; how-
ever, the FDA compliance program monitors mycotoxins
in domestic and imported foods, including OTA. Lab find-
ings at or below 20 ppb (µg/kg) for OTA in food require no
further evaluation (FDA, 2016).
OTA has come to the attention of the US coffee indus-
try during recent FDA inspections of coffee facilities, when
suggestions were made regarding the need for a PC for
OTA. Interestingly, the FDA considers coffee to be a food
that cannot be consumed without application of the appro-
priate control on the roasting process (21 CFR 1.507 and
117.136, paragraph [a], subpart [1]). Requiring a PC for OTA
in coffee contradicts Section 117.136 of the HA and risk-
based PC and Section 1.507 of the Foreign Supplier Verifica-
tion Program (FSVP) rule, because these sections demon-
strate that the FDA does not consider coffee to have a
hazard present that requires a manufacturer/processor to
implement a PC. According to Section 1.507, green cof-
fee importers are exempt from supplier evaluation and
approval (21 CFR 1.505), in addition to foreign supplier ver-
ification activities (21 CFR 1.506), as raw commodities such
as green coffee are anticipated to undergo further process-
ing (e.g., roasting) once imported. Although hazard anal-
ysis for OTA is an important part of the food safety plan
for coffee, the regulations described above highlight that,
if OTA were considered by the FDA to require a PC, the
language (using coffee as an example) included in these
regulations (21 CFR 1.507 and 21 CFR 117.136) would not
have been written.
In light of recent inquiries, we use toxicological princi-
ples to assess the safety of OTA in coffee by determining the
likelihood of it occurring at levels that would cause acute
illness or injury to the consumer. Findings from this assess-
ment can be used to establish whether a PC for OTA risk
management is necessary in the US coffee industry.

MATERIALS AND METHODS
The potential hazard of OTA in coffee is already recog-
nized and well documented; therefore, expectations for
hazard analysis and PC determination, as set forth under
the PC for Human Food, were implemented. General prac-
tices stated in the FSPCA PCQI curriculum (Chapter 8;
FSPCA, 2016) are focused on hazard evaluation, so were
used as a guideline for our approach herein. These steps
were used to determine whether OTA has a likelihood of
being present in coffee at levels that would cause acute ill-
ness or injury to the consumer. Both exposure and hazard
data were identified, and safety was assessed by calculat-
ing the margin of exposure for risk management consider-
ations.
2.1
Identification of exposure and
occurrence data
Exposure and occurrence information were identified
using a variety of sources. The recent authoritative
review and risk assessment conducted by the Euro-
pean Food Safety Authority (EFSA) panel on Contami-
nants in the Food Chain (CONTAM) (2020)—Risk assess-
ment of ochratoxin A—was a primary source relied
upon for exposure and hazard information; as part of
this risk assessment, EFSA compiled exposure informa-
tion published through 2018, as well as analytical data
from European national authorities, research institutions,
academia, food business operators, and other stakehold-
ers on OTA occurrence levels in foodstuffs (including
coffee) (EFSA, 2006, 2020). Publicly available databases
were also reviewed for OTA occurrence and exposure data
in coffee. These databases included FDA recalls,1 import
alerts,2 reportable food registry,3 warning letters,4 Cen-
ters for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) foodborne
outbreaks,5 and Europe’s Rapid Alert System for Food
and Feed (RASFF).6 Additionally, we gathered informa-
tion from the public literature using targeted searches (July
23, 2020 and August 20, 2021) of PubMed and Google
Scholar, on OTA concentrations in green and roasted
coffee.

2.3
Calculation of risk
The studies evaluating the underlying mechanisms of
genotoxicity in EFSA’s (2020) risk assessment of OTA were
inconclusive as to whether OTA causes DNA damage,
either directly or indirectly; however, there is clear evi-
dence of carcinogenic effects (i.e., kidney tumors) of OTA
in both mice and rats (EFSA, 2006, 2020; Kumar et al.,
2012, 2013; NTP, 1989). As a result, EFSA (2020) deter-
mined that a margin-of-exposure (MOE) approach8 was
needed to characterize the risk of both non-neoplastic and
neoplastic effects of OTA, following earlier EFSA guid-
ance on substances that are both genotoxic and carcino-
genic (EFSA, 2005). Therefore, a similar approach was
used for this risk assessment, and MOEs for OTA exposure
resulting from consumption of coffee were calculated by
comparing exposure to health-based benchmarks. To fully
address the potential for acute effects or effects following
single exposures, two scenarios were evaluated: (1) acute
exposure effects (e.g., lethality, overtly toxic responses) and
(2) chronic effects that could hypothetically result from
acute exposures (i.e., carcinogenicity via direct genotoxic
mechanism).

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
3.1
Exposure assessment of OTA in
coffee
To determine the likelihood of OTA being present at harm-
ful levels in coffee, concentrations of OTA in green and
roasted coffee were identified from the peer-reviewed lit-
erature. From the studies identified, several quantified
the percent reduction under different roasting conditions
(Figure 1). These studies that measured OTA levels in both
green coffee and roasted coffee are included in Table 1.
While these data suggest that OTA concentrations in green
coffee beans are low according to US action levels, the stan-
dard process of roasting the coffee beans further reduces
the concentration of OTA (Figure 1). OTA concentrations
were shown to be decreased by upward of 70% to 90%
or more, with the percent reduction generally highest at
higher OTA concentrations in green coffee beans. The
Codex Alimentarius (ICO, 2009) reported that a 65%–100%
reduction can be achieved depending on the roasting pro-
cess.
Additionally, a number of peer-reviewed publications
were identified from the literature that characterize OTA
occurrence levels in coffee (outlined in Table 2). EFSA also
provided occurrence levels of OTA in coffee in the EU in
their recent risk assessment of OTA, with an upper-bound
occurrence level of 0.94 µg/kg (EFSA, 2020). Most rele-
vant to this safety assessment is a recent risk assessment
of human dietary exposure to OTA in the United States,
conducted by Mitchell et al. (2017), in which the upper
range of occurrence of OTA in coffee was estimated to be
4.8 µg/kg. The range of OTA concentrations reported in
coffee (1.7−4.8 µg/kg) by Mitchell et al. (2017) was based
on data from a variety of foodstuffs purchased around the
United States in 2012 and 2013.
In addition to literature searching, we reviewed pub-
licly available databases to determine whether any evi-
dence indicates historical OTA concentrations exceeding
action limits. For historical data from the United States,
databases were reviewed through the full extent of time
that they covered, which was 5 to 10 years depending on the
database. According to the FDA’s databases—including
recall notices, warning letters, import alerts, reportable
food registry—as well as the CDC’s foodborne outbreaks
database, OTA was never listed as the cause of a food safety
issue in the United States (Table 3). This finding is not sur-
prising, because FDA merely monitors OTA levels in foods
and has not prescribed an advisory level, action level, or
regulatory level (FDA, 2016).

Hazard characterization for scenario 1
EFSA (2020) concluded that acute toxicity was not a crit-

of acute effects of OTA in laboratory animals was deter-

LD50 values for rat neonate and pig were 3.9 and 1 mg/kg,
respectively, according to literature compiled by JECFA

The LC50 of 0.6 µM (or 0.246 mg/kg) in zebrafish larvae
reported in this study was not considered relevant to this
assessment, due to the use of a non-mammalian model
(Khezri et al., 2018).
3.2.2
Risk assessment for scenario 1
All estimates of exposure to OTA in coffee in the United
States are orders of magnitude lower than the LD50 values
identified and noted above. Specifically, the US 95th per-
centile exposure to OTA in coffee (0.12 ng/kg-day; Mitchell
et al., 2017) is equivalent to 0.00000012 mg/kg-day, result-
ing in an MOE greater than 1,000,000 when using the low-
est LD50 (0.2 mg/kg in dog; FAO/WHO, 1991), thus demon-
strating a lack of concern for lethality. For example, an
adult weighing 70 kg would have to consume over 410,000
servings of coffee, each made with 7 g of coffee contain-
ing an OTA level of 4.8 µg/kg (the upper range of OTA
occurrence in US coffee), to reach the lowest level associ-
ated with lethality of OTA in mammals (0.2 mg/kg in dog;
FAO/WHO, 1991). Moreover, when considering exposure
to OTA in coffee at the highest concentration measured by
RASFF (54.9 µg/kg, one-time occurrence), estimated to be
21.9 ng/kg-day (using 95th percentile US coffee consump-
tion rate), compared to the lowest LD50 (0.2 mg/kg in dog;
FAO/WHO, 1991), the resulting MOE is slightly less than
the safety threshold for genotoxic compounds of 10,000
(MOE = 9132). Despite this MOE being slightly under the
threshold for genotoxic compounds, it still demonstrates
a lack of concern for lethality, as RASFF incident data is
gathered from food-product evaluations before the product
even gets to the EU market (see Exposure Assessment sec-
tion above) and this higher OTA concentration occurred as
a single instance. No MOE was calculated for non-lethal
acute effects, because the single study available (Albassam

3.3
scenario 2: chronic effects from
single or repeated exposures
3.3.1
Hazard characterization for scenario 2
In conducting their risk assessment and determining
health-based comparison values for OTA, EFSA (2020)
identified both neoplastic and non-neoplastic effects on
the kidney as the most sensitive endpoints. When calcu-
lating the specific health-based benchmark for neoplastic
effects, EFSA (2020) did not rule out direct DNA damage.
Because direct DNA damage is fundamentally considered
a “one-hit” or single-event type of response, a linear
approach is used herein to determine a health-based
benchmark, rather than using a threshold-based value
(i.e., a non-linear relationship between exposure and
response). The resulting value of 14.5 µg/kg-day was calcu-
lated by EFSA (2020) based on the benchmark dose lower
confidence limit (BMDL10) from a two-year carcinogenic-
ity study in rats. This EFSA value is recognized as being
protective of carcinogenic effects that could result from
genotoxicity associated with acute or chronic exposure.
Other health-based benchmark values for OTA include
Health Canada’s proposed maximum limit of 4 ng/kg-day
(Kuiper-Goodman et al., 2010), which is also based on a
non-threshold approach (and is thus more comparable
to EFSA based on the manner in which it was derived),
or JECFA’s provisional tolerable weekly intake (PTWI)
of 100 ng/kg-week, which is based on a non-genotoxic,
threshold-based approach (and is thus less comparable to
EFSA). Both Health Canada’s and JECFA’s health-based
benchmark values contain built-in safety factors, which is
why both are lower than EFSA’s value.
Only one additional chronic study (20 months) for OTA
was identified in the independent literature search. Stoev
(2020) reported increased neoplastic effects in the kidney,
liver, and intestines of rats at 10 mg/kg-day (the only con-
centration tested) compared to controls. Two studies were
identified evaluating short-term (7 days) exposure effects
of OTA in rodents, in which authors reported nephro-
toxicity and proximal tubule epithelial cell injury in rats
(Zhang et al., 2021) and decreased fertility due to oxidative
stress and epigenetic changes in mice (Jia et al., 2020) at
concentrations greater than 1 mg/kg. Of all the studies
available that investigated the chronic effects of OTA, it
was determined that the two-year bioassay employed by
EFSA (2020) was the most conservative and was also the
highest in quality and relevance for the risk assessment
herein.

CONCLUSION
Independent review of the recent literature corroborates
EFSA’s (2020) conclusion that acute and chronic toxicity
were of no concern based on occurrence and consumer
exposure levels of OTA. Furthermore, our independent
literature search did not identify any new studies that
would affect EFSA’s determination. No evidence was iden-
tified to suggest that OTA is acutely toxic in humans from
consumption of coffee or other exposure sources. Large
MOE values were determined for each scenario evaluated,
demonstrating that measured levels of OTA in coffee pose
only a low (de minimis) risk to the consumer following
either single or repeated exposures. The conclusions of
this risk assessment can be used to support coffee industry
food safety plans, indicating that severity and likelihood
of risk are low and do not require a preventive control for
OTA.
