Coffee and Cardiovascular Diseases
Siamak Bidel and Jaakko Tuomilehto
9.1
INTRODUCTION
Cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) are the primary cause of death worldwide. An estimated
17.1 million people died from CVDs in 2004, representing 29% of all global deaths. Of these
deaths, an estimated 7.2 million were due to coronary heart disease (CHD), and 5.7 million
were due to stroke. By 2030, almost 23.6 million people will die from CVD, primarily from
heart disease and stroke. CVD is projected to remain the single leading cause of death [1].
Although age, gender, ethnicity, and genetic background are important risk factors for
CVD and cannot be altered, other risk factors that can predict the occurrence, severity, and
prognosis of CVD events can be prevented, treated, or modiﬁed. For example, hypertension,
abnormal blood lipid level, tobacco use, physical inactivity, type 2 diabetes, unhealthy diet,
and heavy alcohol consumption are important modiﬁable risk factors for heart disease and
stroke. The behavioral risk factors of unhealthy diet, physical inactivity, and tobacco use
have long been suspected to account for about 80% of CHD and cerebrovascular disease. For
instance, the risk of cardiac events decreases 50% in people who stop smoking, and the risk
of acute myocardial infarction, stroke, and peripheral vascular disease decreases signiﬁcantly
during the ﬁrst 2 years after smoking cessation. The effects of unhealthy diet and physical
inactivity can manifest as high blood pressure, elevated blood glucose and serum lipids, and
overweight and obesity [2].
The widespread use of coffee and high prevalence of CVD raise the question of whether
drinking coffee affects the function of the human cardiovascular system (CVS).
An association between coffee consumption and CVD risk has long been suspected.
Fueling the debate about the hazards of the coffee drinking include the slight rise in blood
pressure due to coffee, known effects of caffeine on heart rate, and cholesterol-elevating
effects of coffee diterpenes; however, published results are conﬂicting.

9.2.1
Caffeine
The alkaloid caffeine is an important component of coffee and is probably the most fre-
quently ingested pharmacologically active substance in the world [3]. Physiological effects
that appear early after ingestion include central nervous system stimulation and acute car-
diovascular effects, including increased blood pressure and circulating catecholamine levels,
arterial stiffness, and endothelium-dependent vasodilation [4–6]. Caffeine also appears to
increase metabolic rate and diuresis [7], which may be associated with the development of
CVD. Regarding potential beneﬁts of caffeine consumption on the CVS, to date, there is no
evidence that caffeine and/or its metabolites directly affect lipid metabolism or antioxidant
activity. Further, published studies have not shown that the caffeine content of coffee protects
against CVD, but it may under certain conditions be detrimental.
9.2.2
Diterpenes: kahweol & cafestol
The diterpenoid alcohols kahweol and cafestol are oils found in coffee. These diterpenes
are almost undetectable in ﬁltered coffee, but their concentration in unﬁltered coffee varies
between 0.2 and 18 mg/100 mL depending on the preparation method. Kahweol and cafestol
are thought to increase serum cholesterol by reducing low-density lipoprotein (LDL) recep-
tor activity, which causes extracellular accumulation of LDL and promotes atherosclerosis

Results of several population-based studies in countries in which consumption of boiled

9.2.3
Polyphenols
Studies have shown that coffee has the highest concentration of polyphenols among bever-
ages. CGA is the most abundant polyphenol in coffee [22,23]. For coffee drinkers, coffee is
the richest dietary source of CGA. Studies have evaluated the effect of CGA and its metabo-
lite caffeic acid on LDL oxidation [24,25]. For instance, the results of an experimental study
demonstrated that caffeic acid is able to inhibit in vitro LDL oxidative modiﬁcation [25].
In addition, the effect of a number of polyphenols, ﬂavonoids, and antioxidant vitamins on
the oxidation of atherogenic lipoproteins LDL and very low-density lipoprotein (VLDL)
were compared in an in vitro model of atherosclerosis. CGA exhibited moderate antioxidant
activity, which was stronger than that of gallic acid, a black tea component, and the vitamin
antioxidants. Coffee was found to have the strongest antioxidant effect, which may be due
to synergistic effects of the polyphenols CGA and catechin on LDL oxidation [26,27].
CGA has also been shown to prevent oxidative damage to human epithelial cells [28].
A study of cardiomyocytes showed that CGA and other hydroxycinnamic acids were not
cytotoxic, stabilized membranes, and improved the cell energy status [29]. Another study
reported that dihydrocaffeic acid, a metabolite of caffeic acid, protects endothelium cells
against oxidative stress [30].
9.3
EARLY, TRANSIENT, OR ACUTE EFFECTS
OF COFFEE CONSUMPTION ON CVD
The early effects of coffee consumption on CVD should be considered separately because
some adverse effects are transient and may be modiﬁed or tolerated during long-term con-
sumption. A study involving healthy participants showed that coffee acutely raises systolic
and diastolic blood pressure and slightly lowers the heart rate [31]. A similar response
has been observed with caffeine administration, and symptoms disappeared with decaf-
feinated coffee consumption; therefore, caffeine appears to be responsible for these effects
[4,32–34]. Caffeine exerts many of its effects by antagonizing adenosine receptors, thereby
preventing the vasodilatory effect of adenosine [35,36]. Caffeine levels achieved by drinking
2–3 cups coffee can affect vascular tone and induce vasoconstriction by inhibiting phos-
phodiesterases, with consequent increases in cyclic adenosine monophosphate and cyclic

9.3.1
Tolerance or modiﬁcation
Some of the acute adverse effects of coffee consumption on CVD may be modiﬁed or a
tolerance effect developed with long-term coffee consumption. One study showed that the
acute pressor effect and elevated levels of epinephrine and norepinephrine caused by caffeine
disappeared after 3 days of daily caffeine consumption [53]. In addition, an earlier trial found
that consuming large amounts of coffee caused only a small transient (5-day) rise in mean
blood pressure, and continuous heavy coffee consumption (8 cups/day for 4 weeks) did
not increase the risk of hypertension [54]. However, in more recent and better-designed
studies not all participants exhibit complete tolerance to the pressor effect of coffee [43,55].
Smits et al. found that the increase in blood pressure appeared to be inversely related to
the basal plasma caffeine concentration and suggested that differences in the half-life of
caffeine could account for individual variations in tolerance to acute effects of caffeine [34].
However, a recent study reported that baseline saliva caffeine concentration did not differ
between tolerant and nontolerant participants [55].

9.4
COFFEE METABOLISM AND CVD:
GENETIC INFLUENCES
The individual variation in response to the adverse effects of coffee should be taken into
account to better understand the relationship between coffee consumption and CVD and
CHD. For example, complete tolerance to the acute effects of coffee was observed in only
half the participants in several trials. This variation may be due to differences in coffee
metabolism and caffeine elimination; thus, some individuals are more susceptible to the
adverse effects of coffee [56]. A study by Cornelis et al. showed that coffee intake is
associated with an increased risk of nonfatal myocardial infarction only among individuals
with a certain cytochrome P450 1A2 allele (i.e., slow caffeine metabolizers), suggesting the
role of caffeine in this association.
In another study of middle-aged Finnish men, Happonen et al. found a relationship
between consuming caffeine-containing coffee and the incidence of fatal or nonfatal CHD
events, which is highly dependent on catechol-O-methyltransferase (COMT) genotype [57].
In men who were either homozygous for the high-activity allele or heterozygous, heavy
coffee intake did not increase the incidence of acute coronary events. However, in men who
were homozygous for the low-activity COMT allele, heavy coffee intake (6.5 cups/day)
was associated with a 2-fold higher incidence of acute coronary events.
Some studies also reported individual differences in the association between coffee con-
sumption and plasma cholesterol and homocysteine concentrations; the genes involved have
been identiﬁed [58–60]. Weggemans et al. studied the effect of the apolipoprotein E (APOE)
polymorphism on the responses of serum cholesterol and lipoproteins to dietary treatments.
Their results indicated that the apoprotein E genotype inﬂuences the response of serum
cholesterol to dietary saturated fat and cafestol in opposite directions; however, a study by
Strandhagen et al. could not conﬁrm these results [60]. Strandhagen et al. also showed that
the homocysteine-increasing effect of coffee is more pronounced in individuals homozygous
for the 677TT allele of methylenetetrahydrofolate reductase (Figure 9.3) [61].

9.5.1
Coffee and CHD
Since the ﬁrst paper on the association between coffee and CHD was published in 1963, it has
been the subject of many subsequent studies; however, the potential involvement of coffee
consumptioninCHDremains unclear. Numerous case-control studies havereportedapositive
association between coffee consumption and CHD [62]. In contrast, more recent and better-
designed case-control and cohort studies reported a negative association or no association
[62,63]. Two earlier meta-analyses, which included both case-control and prospective cohort
studies, reported a positive association for case-control studies but not for cohort studies
[64,65]. A subsequent meta-analysis by Kawachi et al. also reported a positive association
only among case-control studies [66]. In the most recent meta-analysis, Soﬁet al. found the
same positive association between coffee consumption and CHD for case-control studies and
no association for prospective studies [62]. Since the publication of this last meta-analysis in
2007, a large prospective study performed in the United States found no detrimental effect

9.5.1.1
Coffee consumption, blood pressure, and hypertension

[31,68–71]. Intake of 200–250 mg caffeine (equivalent to 2–3 cups of coffee) is associated
with an early elevation of systolic blood pressure (by 3–14 mm Hg) and diastolic blood
pressure (4–13 mm Hg) [31]. The most recent meta-analysis of 16 randomized control trials
that assessed coffee and caffeine intake (duration 7–84 days) revealed that regular coffee and
caffeine intake is associated with blood pressure elevation (2.0 mm Hg systolic; 0.7 mm Hg
diastolic); however, the increase in blood pressure produced by caffeine tablet ingestion was
two to three times higher than that produced by regular coffee consumption despite equal

Although the pressor response to acute coffee intake has been well described and doc-

with the risk of hypertension among women only [75].

insufﬁcient evidence to show that long-term coffee consumption causes hypertension.
9.5.1.2
Coffee intake and risk of type 2 diabetes
The effects of coffee consumption on type 2 diabetes will be discussed in another chapter of
this book and will therefore be reviewed only brieﬂy here.
Type 2 diabetes is a well-known risk factor for CVD and CHD. Habitual coffee con-
sumption has been reported to lower the risk of type 2 diabetes. The ﬁrst report to show
a strong inverse association between coffee consumption and type 2 diabetes was a Dutch
study published by van Dam and Feskens [76]. Since, then several large prospective studies
from various countries have reported similar results [77,78]. Two meta-analyses published
in 2005 and 2009 demonstrated that habitual coffee consumption is associated with a sub-
stantially lower risk of type 2 diabetes [79,80]. The later meta-analysis, which also evaluated
the effects of decaffeinated coffee and tea, found the same inverse association [80]. Higher
coffee consumption was also associated with a lower CVD mortality rate in a cohort study
of Finnish patients with type 2 diabetes [81].

9.5.1.3
Coffee and atherosclerosis
Atherosclerosis is a common condition that can cause CHD, heart attack, organ damage, and
stroke. Although the effect of coffee consumption on metabolic risk factors for CVD, CHD,
and incident events has been a topic of many studies, few have evaluated the association
between coffee or caffeine intake and atherosclerosis progression as a potential risk factor
for CVD and CHD events [82–84].
A study by van Woudenbergh et al. examined the association of coffee intake with coronary
calciﬁcation, a marker of coronary atherosclerosis, in a population-based cohort of Dutch
people. They detected an inverse relationship between coffee consumption and coronary
calciﬁcation in women but not in men [83]. A recent study evaluated the relationship between
habitual consumption of coffee, decaffeinated coffee, and caffeine during young adulthood
with subsequent atherosclerosis development and progression, but observed no substantial
association between coffee/caffeine intake and coronary and carotid atherosclerosis [82].
Two important factors involved in the process of atherosclerosis are elevated LDL level,
which damages the arterial wall, and the body’s immune system, which responds to the
damage.
The cholesterol-raising effect of coffee diterpenes was discussed earlier in this chapter.
A review of observational studies reveals that the cholesterol levels of populations who
consume boiled unﬁltered coffee differs from that of populations who consume ﬁltered coffee.
The low concentrations of diterpenes in ﬁltered coffee indicates that paper ﬁlters remove
hypercholesterolemic factors; however, for individuals who normally drank 4 cups of ﬁltered
coffee/day, coffee abstinence for 6 weeks lowered cholesterol levels [85]. Similarly, another
recent study showed that coffee abstention for 3 weeks decreased total serum cholesterol by
0.22–0.36 mmol/L, and drinking 600 mL (about 4 cups) of ﬁltered coffee/day for 4 weeks
raised total serum cholesterol by 0.15–0.25 mmol/L [86]. Therefore, the cholesterol-raising
action of coffee does not appear to be completely lost by ﬁltering.
Several studies have investigated the effect of coffee on inﬂammation. Recent cross-
sectional study by Williams et al. showed that higher caffeinated coffee consumption is
associated with lower levels of inﬂammatory markers including C-reactive protein (CRP)
and E-selectin [87]. Another cross-sectional study of the same study population found an in-
verse association between decaffeinated coffee consumption and concentrations of E-selectin
and CRP among healthy women [88]. However, Zampelas et al. reported that moderate con-
sumption of regular and unﬁltered coffee was associated with higher plasma concentrations
of interleukin 6, CRP, and tumor necrosis factor- [89]. Many of the participants in the
latter study reported drinking unﬁltered coffee; therefore, the cholesterol-raising effect of
unﬁltered coffee may have induced inﬂammation as well.
9.5.1.4
Coffee consumption and plasma homocysteine
Plasma homocysteine concentration is a strong risk factor for CVD and CHD; however,
decreasing the level of homocysteine with vitamin B12 or folic acid did not reduce the risk
of CVD or CHD [90–95]. The ﬁrst study to investigate the effect of coffee consumption on
plasma homocysteine level reported a positive dose-dependent relationship between coffee
consumption and plasma homocysteine. The increase in mean total homocysteine concen-
tration between participants with the lowest coffee consumption (0 cups) and those with the
highest coffee consumption (≥9 cups) was 13.7% for men and 17.7% for women [96]. This
result was consistent with most [97,98] but not all published reports. In a cross sectional study

9.6
COFFEE CONSUMPTION AND HEART FAILURE
The association between coffee consumption and CHD, which is a leading cause of chronic
heart failure, was previously discussed in this chapter. Few studies have investigated the effect
of coffee consumption on developing heart failure. A recent statement from the American
Heart Association [103], which was based on results of a Swedish cohort study [104],
suggested that coffee consumption increased the risk of heart failure, but independent risk
attributions still needed to be conﬁrmed. A Swedish cohort study by Wilhelmson et al.
reported that men who consumed at least 5 cups of coffee/day had an odds ratio for heart
failure of 1.17 (95% conﬁdence interval, 1.05–1.30) compared with those who consumed less
than 5 cups of coffee/day; however, a recent study of another cohort of men from the same
population found no signiﬁcant association between coffee consumption and incident heart
failure [104,105]. The use of logistic regression in the ﬁrst study, which does not account for
the variable length of follow-up due to loss to follow-up or death from other causes, was an
important difference between these two studies.
The most recent study, a large prospective Finish cohort study, found no adverse effect of
coffee consumption on the risk of heart failure in men or women. In this study, consuming
a low to moderate amount of coffee was associated with a reduced risk of heart failure but
only among women [106].
9.7
COFFEE CONSUMPTION AND STROKE
Few studies have investigated the association between coffee consumption and stroke. An
early prospective study of 45,589 men (40–75 years old) showed that caffeinated coffee
and total caffeine intake did not appreciably increase stroke risk [107]. Another study using
prospective data from the Alpha-Tocopherol, Beta-Carotene Cancer Prevention Study, a
cohort study of 26,556 male Finnish smokers aged 50–69 years, found a signiﬁcant inverse
association between high coffee consumption and the risk of cerebral infarction among men,
independent of known cardiovascular risk factors. Consuming 9 cups of coffee/day reduced
the risk of cerebral infarction by 23% compared with participants who consumed less than
2 cups [108]. Results from a prospective cohort of 83,076 women in the Nurses’ Health
Study showed that long-term coffee consumption did not increase the risk of stroke; those
who drank moderate to high amounts of coffee had a lower stroke risk than those who did
not consume coffee [109]. The most recent prospective study of 34,670 women in Sweden
was found that low or no consumption of coffee was associated with an increased risk of

9.8 SUMMARY
Because millions of people drink coffee daily, and CVDs are common diseases that take
millions of lives every year, it is important to determine how CVDs are affected by habitual
coffee drinking. Results of studies presented in this chapter suggest that caffeine exerts a
transient effect on CVD, and diterpenes, which are almost eliminated by paper coffee ﬁlters,
may increase blood cholesterol. However, polyphenols, mainly CGA, are antioxidants that
appear to prevent CVD.
To date, there is insufﬁcient evidence to show that long-term consumption of coffee
increases the risk of CHD, hypertension, heart failure, and stroke. In addition, coffee drinking
may lower the risk of type 2 diabetes and atherosclerosis, which are risk factors for CVD
development and progression.
