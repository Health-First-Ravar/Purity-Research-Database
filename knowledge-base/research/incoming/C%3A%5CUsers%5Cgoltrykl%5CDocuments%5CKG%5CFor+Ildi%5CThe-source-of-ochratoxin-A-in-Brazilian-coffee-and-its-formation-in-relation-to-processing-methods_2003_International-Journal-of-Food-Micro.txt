The source of ochratoxin A in Brazilian coffee and its formation in
relation to processing methods

Abstract
A total of 408 Brazilian coffee samples was examined during the 1999 and 2000 coffee harvest seasons for the presence of
ochratoxin A (OA) and fungi with the potential to produce it. Samples came from four regions: Alta Paulista (western area of
Sa˜o Paulo State), Sorocabana (southwest Sa˜o Paulo State), Alta Mogiana (northeast Sa˜o Paulo State) and Cerrado Mineiro
(western area of Minas Gerais State). Cherries and beans were examined at different stages: immature, mature and overripe
cherries from trees, overripe cherries from the ground and beans during drying and storage on the farm. For mycological
studies, the cherries and beans were surface disinfected with chlorine, plated on Dichloran 18% Glycerol Agar at 25 jC for 5–7
days and analysed for the presence of Aspergillus ochraceus and closely related species, A. carbonarius and A. niger. More than
800 isolates of fungi belonging to these species were identified and studied for the ability to produce OA using the agar plug
technique and thin layer chromatography (TLC). A. niger was the species found most commonly (63% of isolates of these three
species), but only 3% of them produced OA. A. ochraceus also occurred commonly (31% of isolates), and 75% of those studied
were capable of OA production, a much higher percentage than reported elsewhere. A. carbonarius was found (6% of isolates)
only in Alta Paulista, the hottest region studied, and only from beans in the drying yard or in storage. However, 77% of the A.
carbonarius isolates were capable of producing OA. Average infection rates for cherries taken from trees were very low, but
were higher in fruit taken from the ground, from the drying yard and from storage, indicating infection by toxigenic species after
harvest. The average OA content in 135 samples of mature cherries from trees, overripe from trees, overripe from the ground,
drying yard and storage was 0.1, < 0.2, 1.6, 2.1 and 3.3 Ag/kg, respectively. Although individual OA levels varied widely, only
9 of the 135 samples analysed exceeded 5 Ag/kg OA, with one sample of poor quality dried coffee in excess of 100 Ag/kg OA.
The causes of high contamination were investigated on the farms concerned and several critical points were found, relating both
to local climatic conditions and the drying processes used.

1. Introduction
Ochratoxin A (OA) is believed to be produced in
nature by three main species of fungi, Aspergillus

ochraceus, A. carbonarius and Penicillium verruco-
sum, with a minor contribution by A. niger (JECFA
(Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee on Food Addi-
tives), 2001). OA production by A. ochraceus was
discovered by Van der Merwe et al. (1965), but this
species has been regarded as an uncommon fungus
except in long-stored food commodities (Pitt and
Hocking, 1997). P. verrucosum is believed to occur
only in cool temperate climates, and is mainly
associated with cereals (Pitt, 1987; Pitt and Hock-
ing, 1997). A. carbonarius and A. niger were
described as sources of OA within the last 10 years
(Abarca et al., 1994; Te´ren et al., 1996; Heenan et
al., 1998). Although the presence of OA in green
coffee beans has been reported by several authors in
concentrations between 0.2 and 360 Ag/kg (Levi et
al., 1974; Levi, 1980; Studer-Rohr et al., 1994), the
source of OA in coffee remains poorly understood.
Although it is commonly assumed that the major
source of OA in coffee is A. ochraceus, little solid
evidence of this exists. Moreover, the recent dis-
covery that A. carbonarius also commonly has the
potential to produce OA has prompted a more
thorough investigation of this question. A. carbo-
narius and A. niger have been isolated from coffee
by several authors (Te´ren et al., 1997; Nakajima et
al., 1997; Joosten et al., 2001; Urbano et al., 2001),
indicating that these two species are also potential
sources of OA in coffee.
The issue of time of invasion of coffee by
toxigenic fungi has not been adequately researched
also. This is of great importance in understanding the
problem of OA in coffee and developing control
strategies. Experience with other crops has shown
that if invasion occurs preharvest, control will be
much difficult than if it occurs postharvest, i.e.
during drying and storage. Postharvest problems
can be expected to relate to unfavourable climates
for drying, poor drying practice or quality control, or
inadequate storage conditions.
This work was carried out in order to study: (i) the
distribution of fungi with the potential to produce OA
in coffee beans throughout the harvest and drying
process; (ii) to evaluate the presence of OA in Brazil-
ian coffee beans; (iii) to investigate the relationship
between the presence of OA in coffee and local
climatic conditions; (iv) the influence of processing
practices on OA production.
2. Material and methods
2.1. Coffee-producing regions
Four regions producing coffee in Brazil were
selected for study, characterized by differing climatic
conditions during harvesting and the quality of test
beverages made from dried coffee beans. The regions
were as follows: (i) Alta Paulista, a western area in
Sa˜o Paulo State, a hot, rainy region with an average of
21 jC during the growing and harvest season and 47.1
mm/month rainfall, a relatively low altitude, below
800 m, and an average beverage quality; (ii) Soroca-
bana in south western Sa˜o Paulo State, a relatively
cold, rainy region with an average of 17.7 jC and 64.9
mm/month rainfall, also of low altitude, below 800 m,
and generally producing low-quality beverage; (iii)
Alta Mogiana in north east Sa˜o Paulo State, a temper-
ate, moderate rainfall region with an average of 19.5
jC and 29.5 mm/month rainfall, with a relatively high
altitude of 800–1000 m, and a generally good-quality
beverage; (iv) Cerrado Mineiro, in the western part of
Minas Gerais State, a temperate, dry region with an
average of 19.1 jC and 14.8 mm/month rainfall, with
a high altitude above 1100 m, and producing good-
quality beverage. Two to three representative farms
were selected for study from each region.
2.2. Sampling
During the 1999 and 2000 harvest periods, coffee
samples (Coffea arabica) were collected from the
selected farms at different stages of maturation and
processing: cherries, including immature and overripe
fruits from trees, overripe cherries from the soil, all
types of cherries from the drying yard and from
storage in barns. Three samples of 500 g from each
stage were taken at a single sampling time and kept
inside an icebox during transport from the farm to the
laboratory. The period from sampling to mycological
analysis did not exceed 24 h.
2.3. Mycological analysis
Subsamples of coffee cherries or beans were sur-
face disinfected with 0.4% chlorine solution for 1 min
(Pitt and Hocking, 1997), and then a total of 50
cherries or beans was plated directly (10 particles

per plate) onto Dichloran 18% Glycerol agar (Hock-
ing and Pitt, 1980). The plates were incubated at 25
jC for 5–7 days, then inspected for colony growth
visually and with the aid of a stereomicroscope.
Representative colonies of Aspergillus species with
the potential to produce OA were isolated in pure
culture. Although Penicillium species were also fre-
quently present, no attempt was made to identify
these.
2.4. Identification of fungi
Aspergillus isolates were grown on standard iden-
tification media, Czapek yeast extract agar and malt
extract agar, and identified according to Klich and Pitt
(1988). Numbers of isolates identified as A. ochraceus
or closely related species, A, carbonarius and A. niger,
were counted for each sample, and percentages infec-
tion of the coffee fruit calculated.
2.5. Test for OA production by isolated fungi
The isolates identified as A. ochraceus or closely
related species, A. niger and A. carbonarius, were
grown on yeast extract 15% sucrose agar at 25 jC for
7 days and evaluated for the production of OA by the
agar plug technique, which tests small samples from
petri dishes by thin layer chromatography (TLC)
(Filtenborg et al., 1983). If isolates were negative
for OA production by this technique, the colony and
yeast extract sucrose medium from a petri dish were
extracted with chloroform (50 ml) in a Stomacher for
3 min (Taniwaki, 1995). Extracts were filtered and
concentrated in a water bath at 60 jC to near dryness
and then dried under a stream of N2. Residues were
resuspended in chloroform and spotted on TLC plates
which were developed in Toluene/ethyl acetate/formic
acid (5:4:1) and visualised under UV light at 365 nm.
An OA standard (Sigma, St. Louis, USA) was used
for comparison.
2.6. Analysis of OA in coffee
Coffee samples were analyzed for OA according to
Pittet et al. (1996). The samples were extracted with a
solution of methanol/3% sodium bicarbonate (50:50).
The extracts were filtered and diluted with phosphate-
buffered saline and applied to an immunoaffinity
column (Vicam, Watertown, MA, USA) containing a
monoclonal antibody specific for OA. After washing,
the OA was eluted with HPLC grade methanol and
quantified by reverse-phase HPLC using a fluores-
cence detector. The mobile phase used was acetroni-
trile/4 mM sodium acetate with 0.5% acetic acid
solution (42:58). The flow rate was 1 ml/min. The
equipment used was a Shimadzu LC-10VP system
(Shimadzu, Japan) set at 330 nm excitation and 470
nm emission. The HPLC was fitted with a ODS
Hypersil (5 Am, 25  4.6 mm) pre-column and
Supelcosilk LC-18 (5 Am, 250  4.6 mm) column
(Supelco, USA). The detection limit of this method
was 0.2 Ag/kg OA.

3. Results and discussion
In total, 408 samples of coffee beans from the 1999
and 2000 harvests were examined. The percentage
infection by A. ochraceus (and possibly related spe-
cies), A. carbonarius and A. niger, from the four
regions studied, is shown in Table 1. The presence
of these species was low in fruit obtained from trees,
but higher in samples from the ground (over ripe
fruit), from the drying yard and from storage.
From all samples analysed, 872 isolates of A.
ochraceus or closely related species, A. niger and A.
carbonarius, were obtained (Tables 2 and 3). A. niger
was the most common species found (549 isolates,
63% of the three species). A. ochraceus, perhaps
including an unknown percentage of closely related
species, was also common (269 isolates, 31%). A.
carbonarius was much less common (54 isolates,
6%), and was found in only one region, Alta Paulista,
which is a relatively hot area for growing coffee.
Average infection rates of coffee cherries with these
fungi at various harvest and drying stages are given in
Table 2. It can be seen that infection rates overall were
low. On average, A. ochraceus infected less than 0.6%
of fruit on the tree. Rates increased during drying and
averaged 2% in storage. Infection by A. carbonarius,
averaging 0.5%, was found only in samples from the
drying yard and in storage.
When the 872 isolates were tested for the ability to
produce OA in culture, it was found that most A.
ochraceus and A. carbonarius isolates were capable
of toxin production (Table 3). The percentage of A.
carbonarius isolates which produced OA (77%) was
lower than that found by Heenan et al. (1998), who
reported 90% capability for isolates taken from wash
water associated with processing dried vine fruits in
Australia. However, the percentage of A. ochraceus
isolates producing OA (75%) is much higher than has
been reported previously, e.g. 30%, by Ciegler (1972)
and 16%, by Chourasia (1995). This finding may be
due to the use of only fresh isolates, or perhaps to the
presence of a more frequently toxigenic species
closely related to A. ochraceus, but not taxonomically
distinguished from it in this study. The perception that
only occasional isolates of A. niger are potential OA
producers was confirmed, as only 6 of more than 500
isolates (3%) was found to have this capacity, in
agreement with Heenan et al. (1998), who reported
2% of 115 A. niger isolates as being able to produce
OA.

with ground, equipment or drying yard surfaces. It
seems likely that damage to the skin of the fruit often
initiates infection. The much higher infection rates in
some samples during drying and storage indicate a real
potential for OA production. Although A. carbonarius
infection was uncommon, infection rates of fruit in
samples from the drying yard in one case was 26% and
from storage was up to 12% (Table 4). Again, these
infection rates indicate a high potential to produce OA.

serious potential for OA production because the per-
centages of toxigenic isolates is so low, but do empha-
sise the need to distinguish between A. carbonarius and
A. niger in studies on the occurrence of OA in foods.

potentially capable of OA production was reflected in
the figures for ochratoxin assays. The range of OA
detected in the coffee samples from different regions
is presented in Table 5. Samples containing OA higher
than 1 Ag/kg were found in three of the four regions
studied. However, only 10 of 135 coffee samples
contained OA levels of 5 Ag/kg or more.
Means and ranges of OA found in 135 samples
from the various stages of harvesting and processing
are given in Table 6. Samples taken from fruit on the
tree contained negligible amounts of OA, almost
always below the limit of detection. Mean levels of
OA from fruit from the ground, from the drying yard
and from storage were all below 4 Ag/kg, again
indicating overall acceptability. However, a few sam-
ples from fruit taken from the ground or during drying
and storage contained much higher and often unac-
ceptable levels. This indicates a lack of quality control
during drying on some farms.

Case 3. This sample, from a second farm in Alta

Case 4. On this farm, one sample from storage
contained 9.6 Ag/kg OA, although A. ochraceus was
not found in it. The storage barn was built very close
to the coffee dryer, which was found to be injecting
hot, humid air into the coffee in the barn. The
temperature of the coffee at the top of the stack was
higher than 50 jC, which caused a warm humid
condition for fungal growth and OA production.
Cases 5 and 6. From a farm in Sorocabana, two
coffee samples from the soil and from storage
contained excessive levels of OA (6 and 8 Ag/kg,
respectively). The agricultural practice on this farm
was poor, as coffee was routinely collected from the
soil by raking. The source of OA was probably A.
ochraceus, although it was found only at a low-
percentage infection.
Cases 7 and 8. This farm in Sorocabana was badly
affected by the climate conditions of this region as
well as poor practice. One sample of coffee from
storage contained 109 Ag/kg OA. The beans from
which this sample was taken had been segregated as
‘‘floaters’’ during processing. The cause of such a
high contamination was investigated with the farmer
and several critical points were found: the farm was
located in a basin frequently affected by a heavy mist
during drying; the coffee was turned over only
infrequently during drying; in many cases the coffee
layer was very thick during drying; the barn showed
signs of moisture infiltration from the ceiling and
floor; birds had access to the storage barn. All of these
factors favoured growth of A. ochraceus and produc-
tion of OA in the coffee. Indeed, 36% of the beans in
this sample contained A. ochraceus. A second sample
from this farm, taken from fruit raked up from soil,
had 12% infection by A. ochraceus and 37 Ag/kg OA.
Cases 9 and 10. Two samples from a farm in
Mogiana showed excessive OA concentrations. One,
from the 1999 harvest, came from storage, and
contained 10 Ag/kg OA, while the second, from the
2000 harvest, was from the drying yard, and contained
48 Ag/kg OA. These figures were surprising because
Mogiana is known as a region with generally good
coffee due to its high altitude, temperate climate and
reasonable rainfall. However, when the cause of the
high OA levels was investigated, it was found that the
drying yard was very small for the amount of dried
coffee produced, so that before drying, the coffee was
often heaped up in the yard, waiting to be spread out.
Mist was also a problem because the farm is located in
a basin. The time required to dry was therefore often
excessive, allowing time for fungal growth.

4. Conclusions
In a study of fungi with the potential to produce
OA in Brazilian coffee, the major fungus responsible,
was found to be A. ochraceus. Of 269 isolates of this
species, 75% were found to be capable of toxin
production, a higher percentage than has been
reported previously. A. carbonarius was also found,
though much less commonly, but it also had the
potential to produce OA in coffee, as most isolates
examined were also toxigenic.
Few cherries on the coffee trees were infected with
these species, indicating that infection mostly
occurred after harvest, and the fungal sources were
likely to be soil, equipment and drying yard surfaces.
Variability in infection rates of these toxigenic species
was reflected in a wide variability in OA levels in
samples from the drying yard and storage.
A. niger was more common than A. ochraceus or
A. carbonarius, but only a low percentage of isolates
appear capable of producing OA, so this species is
probably a relatively unimportant source of OA in
coffee.
Ochratoxin A was found in some samples, but
occurrence was sporadic. Only 7% of 135 samples
had OA levels higher than 5 Ag/kg, and in all cases,
this was found to involve local environmental con-
ditions and poor harvesting, drying and storage prac-
tice. The solution to the problem of OA in dried coffee
appears to lie in improvements to agricultural practice.
