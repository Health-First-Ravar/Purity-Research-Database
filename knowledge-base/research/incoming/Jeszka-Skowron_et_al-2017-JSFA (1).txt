Abstract
BACKGROUND: The quality of co’¼Ćee depends not only on the contents of healthy compounds but also on its contamination with
microorganisms that can produce mycotoxins during development, harvesting, preparation, transport and storage.
RESULTS: The antioxidant activity of green co’¼Ćee brews measured in this study by ABTS, DPPH and FolinŌĆōCiocalteu assays
showed that co’¼Ćee extracts from Robusta beans possessed higher activity in all assays than extracts from Arabica beans. The
occurrence of ochratoxin A and a’¼éatoxins (B1, B2, G1 and G2) in green co’¼Ćee beans was studied using liquid chromatogra-
phy/mass spectrometry. Apart from mycotoxins, the content of ergosterol as a marker indicating fungal occurrence was also
determined. Among a’¼éatoxins, a’¼éatoxin B1 was the dominant mycotoxin in co’¼Ćee bean samples, with the highest level at
17.45 ng gŌłÆ1. Ochratoxin A was detected in four samples at levels ranging from 1.27 to 4.34 ng gŌłÆ1, and fungi potentially pro-
ducing this toxin, namely Aspergillusoryzae, Alternaria sp., Aspergillusfoetidus, Aspergillustamarii and Penicilliumcitrinum, were
isolated.
CONCLUSION: Steaming and deca’¼Ćeination of co’¼Ćee beans increased antioxidant activities of brews in comparison with those
prepared from unprocessed beans. Although toxins can be quanti’¼üed in green co’¼Ćee beans and novel fungi were isolated, their

INTRODUCTION
Co’¼Ćee is one of the most popular beverages in the world. Co’¼Ćea
arabica and Co’¼Ćea canephora var. robusta are the most important
species of co’¼Ćee. Arabica species come mainly from South Amer-

while Robusta species come mainly from South Asia (India and
Vietnam) and the lowlands of Central and West Africa.1,2 Co’¼Ćee
can be considered as a functional food owing to the presence
of some active compounds. The most important are chloro-
genic acids and ca’¼Ćeine, which reduce the incidence of cancer,
diabetes and liver diseases, protect against ParkinsonŌĆÖs disease
and thus decrease mortality risk.3,4 Green co’¼Ćee bean extract
shows health-promoting e’¼Ćects by reducing visceral fat and body
weight.5,6 These e’¼Ćects are observed owing to the content of
bioactive compounds7ŌĆō11 that show antioxidant activity.8,9,12
The quality of co’¼Ćee depends not only on the contents of
healthy compounds but also on its contamination with toxic
substances.13 Co’¼Ćee cherries (and then beans) can be a’¼Ćected
by microorganisms (e.g. fungi) that can produce toxins during
development, harvesting, preparation, transport and storage.14
Mycotoxins are produced by fungi of various genera, including
Fusarium, Trichoderma, Alternaria, Penicillium, Aspergillus and
Stachybotrys, under di’¼Ćerent growth conditions, with the level
of production depending on the substrate, temperature and
humidity.15 Approximately 25% of global food and feed crop
production is a’¼Ćected by fungi.14 They are associated with many
diseases such as nephropathy and immunosuppression, which
are caused by ochratoxins.16 A’¼éatoxins are hepatotoxic and show
carcinogenic e’¼Ćects.17 In the European Union, the legal limit for
ochratoxin A in roasted co’¼Ćee beans is 5 ng gŌłÆ1.18
Therefore there is a need for a quick and cheap method of esti-
mation of the quality of co’¼Ćee. Ergosterol could be one indicator
of potential fungal and mycotoxin contamination. Saxena et al.19
revealed that HPTLC measurement of ergosterol on long-grain
white rice can be a useful method to detect fungal activity and
ochratoxin production. However, another study showed a lack of
correlation between ergosterol content and mycotoxin produc-
tion in cereal products.20

CONCLUSIONS

ABTS, DPPH and F-C assays than Arabica. Steaming of Robusta cof-

DPPH assays in comparison with unprocessed co’¼Ćee. On the other
hand, roasting of beans decreased the antioxidant activity.
Potentially ochratoxigenic fungi A. oryzae, Alternaria sp., A.
foetidus, A. tamarii and P. citrinum were isolated. Aspergillus niger
infected 89% of Robusta beans. Robusta green co’¼Ćee beans origi-

The content of ergosterol as a fungal marker was studied. Its
presence was con’¼ürmed in every tested sample. Nevertheless,
no correlation between ergosterol and mycotoxin contents was
found. Thus this marker cannot be used to assess contamination
with mycotoxins.
