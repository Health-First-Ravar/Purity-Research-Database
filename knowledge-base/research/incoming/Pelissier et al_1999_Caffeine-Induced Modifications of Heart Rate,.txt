PELISSIER, A.-L., M. GANTENBEIN AND B. BRUGUEROLLE. Caffeine-induced modifications of heart rate, tem-
perature, and motor activity circadian rhythms in rats. PHYSIOL BEHAV 67(1) 81–88, 1999.—Even if the effects of caffeine
on some physiological parameters are well known, its influence on circadian rhythmicity had not yet been investigated. This
possible influence is of particular importance, introducing a possible bias in chronopharmacological studies. Thus, the aim of
this study was to evaluate the effects of repeated caffeine administration on the circadian rhythms of heart rate “H,” body
temperature “T,” and motor activity “A” in unrestrained rats maintained under controlled conditions (LD 12:12, light from
0600 to 1800 h) by using radiotelemetry transmitters. The study was divided into three 7-day observation spans: a 1-week con-
trol span “P1,” a 1-week treatment span “P2,” and a 1-week recovery span “P3.” P1 was performed for assessing baseline
measurements of H, T, and A. During P2, four rats received caffeine (25 mg/kg) at 0900 h, while four rats received saline in
the same conditions every day of the observation span. H, T, and A were continuously monitored and plotted every 10 min.
For P1, P2, and P3, a power spectrum analysis (Fourier transform) was applied to determine the dominant period of rhyth-
micity. If H, T, and A circadian rhythms were detected, the characteristics of these rhythms, i.e., mesors, amplitudes and ac-
rophases, were determined by cosinor analysis, expressed as means 6 SEM and compared by analysis of variance. Our results
indicated that caffeine did not suppress the circadian rhythmicity of H, T, and A, but significantly increased mesors and de-
creased amplitudes of the three rhythms and advanced acrophases of temperature and activity compared to the control
group.

CAFFEINE is a widely consumed psychoactive substance
found in a variety of beverages such as coffee, tea, and thera-
peutic drugs (2). Studies conducted in humans show that caf-
feine produces subjective and behavioral effects that are
those of typical stimulant drugs. Low and intermediate doses
delay sleep, increase arousal, alertness, concentration, and
performance on cognitive and motor task, while high doses
cause nervousness and adverse anxiogenic effects (13,20).
Cardiovascular and respiratory effects of caffeine have been
also well documented. Low and intermediate doses produce a
positive inotropic effect on the myocardium and a positive
chronotropic effect at the sinoatrial node, causing transient
increases in heart rate, force of contraction, and heart work.
High doses induce tachycardia and ventricular arrhythmia
(7,25). Also, caffeine decreases airway resistance in the lung
and induces respiratory stimulation (7). In rats, injections
induce a dose-dependent hyperthermia (11,22,28), produce
tachycardia at large doses (25), increase motor activity, and
produce stimulant-like discriminative stimulus effects (13,18).
The physiological effects of caffeine are thought to be medi-
ated primarily through competitive blockade of adenosine
receptors (7). Various findings suggest an involvement of
dopamine systems in the central effects of caffeine, and caf-
feine has also been proposed to indirectly enhance dopami-
nergic activity by competitive antagonism of adenosine recep-
tors that are colocalized and functionally interact with
dopamine receptors (7).
Nevertheless, most of the work had focused on the time
span immediately following caffeine injections, and its influ-
ence on circadian rhythmicity has not yet been investigated.

This possible influence is of particular importance, introduc-
ing a possible bias in chronopharmacological studies particu-
larly devoted to psychoactive or cardiovascular drugs. Thus,
the aim of this study was to evaluate the effect of caffeine on
the circadian rhythmicity of heart rate and motor activity.
Elsewhere, temperature rhythm is considered as a marker
rhythm, and its drug-induced perturbations may be a good re-
flection of these drug effects on the circadian pacemaker.
Before measuring the chronopharmacological effects of
caffeine, i.e., the influence of the hour of administration of
caffeine on circadian rhythms, we aimed to study the effects
of caffeine administered during the rest period, where rats are
supposed to be more susceptible to psychostimulants drugs.
Otherwise, to assess the long-term effects of caffeine upon
circadian rhythms, the possible modifications induced by re-
peated caffeine injections, on heart rate, locomotor activity,
and temperature circadian rhythms were investigated in rats
and compared to saline injections. Such a protocol allows tak-
ing into account the possible effects of stress induced by injec-
tion and/or handling.
MATERIALS AND METHODS
Animals
For a minimum of 3 weeks before use, eight Wistar AF
IOPS adult male rats from IFFA-CREDO (St. Germain-sur-
l’Arbresle, France), mean weight 275 g and 10 weeks old,
were housed in individual transparent polypropylene cages
(40 3 30 3 30 cm3) under controlled environmental condi-
tions, i.e., relative humidity (50–55%), temperature (24 6
18C), and synchronization by a light–dark cycle [LD 12:12;
light from 0600 to 1800 h; dark from 1800 to 0600 h). Light
was provided by two 60-W fluorescent tubes, and lighting in-
tensity was around 300 lx at cage level. Food and water were
available ad lib. Changes occurring one time per week on an
irregular schedule. The experiments were conducted during
November 1997, in accordance with the internationally ac-
cepted principles concerning the care and use of laboratory
animals (6) after acceptance of our animal experimentation
ethical committee (Commission Consultative d’Ethique Ani-
male, Centre de Formation et de Recherches Expérimentales
Médico-Chirurgicales, Faculté de Médecine de Marseille,
France).
Telemetry System
H, T, and A were measured by means of radiotelemetry.
The surgical implantation of the transmitter (model TA11-
CTA-F40®, Data Sciences, St. Paul, MN) was performed un-
der ketamine hydrochloride KETALAR® (100 mg/kg body
weight i.p.) general anesthesia. Just before implantation, cali-
bration values of each transmitter were entered into the
Dataquest III® data acquisition system (Data Sciences). Trans-
mitters were implanted as described in details by Kramer et
al. (21). Shortly, under complete anesthesia, a 2-cm incision
was made in the peritoneum under aseptic conditions, and the
sterilized telemetry sender was implanted into the abdominal
cavity and sutured to the abdominal wall. ECG leads were ex-
tented subcutaneously to the right axilla and to the left lower
rib area and sutured to muscle tissue there. After closure, ani-
mals were monitored until they recovered from anaesthesia,
and were then returned to their home cage. Signals from the
transmitter were received by an antenna mounted in a re-
ceiver board (model CTR86®, Data Sciences) placed under
the animal’s cage. Data of H (beats/min, i.e., bpm), T (8C),
and A (counts) were continuously monitored, collected as de-
scribed by Meerlo et al. (23) every 10 min over a 21-day pe-
riod, and processed by a PC with a specialized recording and
analysis system (Dataquest III®, Data Sciences).
Drugs
Caffeine-free base (CAFFEINE MERAM®, France) was
dissolved in saline (0.9%) and injected subcutaneously in the
interscapular region at a dose of 25 mg/kg. This dose was cho-
sen according to the recommendations for interspecies com-
parison (4). Animals of the control group were injected with
saline in the same conditions.
Experimental Protocol
After a recovery period from surgical implantation and/or
anesthesia, the study was divided into three 7-day observation
spans: the first week consisted in a control span “P1” for base-
line measurements of H, T, and A circadian rhythms. This pe-
riod was only characterized by daily handling and weighing of
animals at 0900 h. The second week consisted in a treatment
span “P2”: four animals received caffeine (group “C”) at 0900 h,
while four animals received saline (group “S”) at 0900 h in the
same conditions during the 7 days of the treatment span. The
third week consisted in a recovery span “P3.” Animals were
also weighed during P2 and P3 at 0900 h.
Data Analysis
H, T, and A were continuously monitored and plotted ev-
ery 10 min. Data were analyzed by two methods using
Dataquest III® software. First, to determine the dominant pe-
riod of the three rhythms of P1, P2, and P3, a power spectrum
analysis (Fourier Transform) was applied to 30-min average
data intervals. Power spectrum analysis allowed examination
of the spectral content of the raw telemetered data series. The
computed power spectrum showed peaks at the frequency
corresponding to the period of the variation in the data series.
Then, to assess the circadian variations of H, T, and A, single
cosinor analysis was applied to individual data for P1, P2, and
P3, and a rhythm was considered to be significantly detected
if p , 0.05 (8). The circadian rhythm characteristics of H, T,
and A, i.e., MESOR (Midline Estimating Statistic Of Rhythm
corresponding to the mean level that is equal to the 24-h aver-
age), amplitude (half of the peak-to-through difference of the
fitted cosine function), and acrophase (the crest time of
rhythm given in degrees, where 360 degrees corresponded to
a 24-h cycle and the starting time of 0000 was assessed as 08)
were estimated by the linear method of least squares (24) and
expressed as means 6 SEM. For each rat, the gain in body
weight [D-(D-1)] was calculated for each day of the protocol
and expressed as means 6 SEM.
A three-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used for
statistical analysis (Statview II® program). The three factors
analyzed were the observation span (P1, P2, or P3), the day of
the observation span (D1, D2 . . . . D7), and the treatment
(caffeine—C or saline—S). If no interaction related to the
days within an observation span was detected, the compari-
sons between P1, P2, and P3 for C and S and the comparisons
between C and S for P1, P2, and P3 were done by a one-way
ANOVA followed, if a significant difference was found, by a
Fisher’s PLSD (Protected Last Significant Difference) test for
multiple comparisons. The weight changes of the two groups
(C and S) were analyzed using the same proceedings.

RESULTS
Heart Rate Circadian Rhythm
Fourier analysis showed that H circadian rhythm was sig-
nificantly detected for all rats during P1, P2, and P3, excepted
for one rat of the caffeine group during P2. As the Fourier
transform confirmed that the circadian rhythms were vali-
dated, the characteristics of the rhythms, i.e., mesors, ampli-
tudes, and acrophases were determined by cosinor analysis
using a 24-h period. As the applied three-way ANOVA did
not detect any interactions related to the day (D1 to D7) and
the observation span (P1, P2, or P3) of the protocol, ANOVA
one-way was performed in order to compare: 1) mesors, am-
plitudes, and acrophases of C and S over the three observa-
tion spans of the protocol (P1 versus P2, P1 versus P3, and P2
versus P3 for C and for S); 2)mesors, amplitudes, and ac-
rophases between C and S for each observation span of the
protocol (C versus S for P1, P2, and P3). As shown in Fig. 1,
the mesor of the caffeine group was significantly increased
during P2 compared to P1 and P3, while the mesor of the con-
trol group was not significantly modified whatever the obser-
vation span. Furthermore, the mesor of the caffeine group
was significantly higher compared to the control group during
P2, while the values of the two groups were not significantly
different during P1 and P3, (F(5, 141) 5 78.08, p , 0.05. The
amplitudes of the two groups were significantly decreased
during P2 compared to P1 and P3, and this decrease was sig-
nificantly more important for the caffeine group than for the
control group, while the values of the two groups were not
significantly different during P1 and P3, F(5, 141) 5 22.76,
p , 0.05. The acrophases of the two groups were not
modified whatever the observation span, F(5, 141) 5 1.39,
p . 0.05.
Body Temperature Circadian Rhythm
Fourier analysis showed that T circadian rhythm was sig-
nificantly detected for all rats during P1, P2, and P3, excepted
for one rat of the caffeine group during P2, and cosinor analy-
sis was applied to determine the characteristics of the rhythms.
As the applied three-way ANOVA did not detect any interac-
tions related to the day (D1 to D7) and the observation span
(P1, P2, or P3) of the protocol, ANOVA one-way was per-
formed. As shown in Fig. 2, the mesor of the caffeine group
was significantly increased during P2 compared to P1 and P3,
while the mesor of the control group was not significantly
modified whatever the observation span. Furthermore, the
mesor of the caffeine group was significantly increased com-
pared to the control group during P2, while the values of the
two groups were not significantly different during P1 and P3,
F(5, 141) 5 5.51, p , 0.05. The amplitudes of the two groups
were significantly decreased during P2 compared to P1 and
P3, and this decrease was significantly more important for the
caffeine group than for the control group, while the values of
the two groups were not significantly different during P1 and
P3, F(5, 141) 5 41.68, p , 0.05. The acrophase of the caffeine
group was phase advanced during P2 compared to P1 and P3,
while the acrophase of the control group was not modified
whatever the observation span. The acrophases of the two
groups were significantly different during P2, but not during
P1 and P3, F(5, 141) 5 5.36, p , 0.05.

DISCUSSION
The present study had demonstrated that: 1) repeated caf-
feine administration did not suppress the circadian rhythmic-
ity of H, T and A excepted for one animal; 2) repeated caf-
feine administration modified the mesors, amplitudes, and
acrophases of the three rhythms and induced a decrase in the
weight gain; and 3) all the observed modifications were signif-
icantly more important for the caffeine group than for the
control group (p , 0.05).
These data showed that caffeine perturbated the H, T, and
A circadian rhythms. The observed increases of H, T, and A
mesors did agree with previously studies reporting that caf-
feine injections induced tachycardia (7,25), hyperthermia
(22), and increased motor activity (18) for the time span im-
mediately following caffeine injections. The phase advance of
T and A rhythms during P2 could be explained by the hour of
administration. Indeed, caffeine, which is responsible for hy-
perthermia and hyperactivity, was injected at the moment of
the lowest values of temperature and activity of the 24-h
scale. These results did agree with previously studies using
theophylline (12).
The observed modifications of the control rats during P2
are certainly due to handling and/or injection stress. As dem-
onstrated by Gattermann and Weinandy (14) and Harper et
al. (17), the influence of this stress is of less importance com-
pared to surgical and social stress. Moreover, Diez–Noguera
et al. (9) described a stress-induced decrease of amplitude of
activity circadian rhythm without modifications of the others
parameters, i.e., mesors and acrophases. Finally Clement et
al. (5) have shown comparable results concerning tempera-
ture circadian rhythm. All these data totally agree with our
results. The handling and/or injection stress is supposed to be
the same between treated and control rats; thus, the signifi-
cantly observed differences between the two groups during
P2 are thought to be explained by the propers effects of caf-
feine on H, T, and A circadian rhythms, which is consistent
with previously reported studies (17,26).
Our results also document a weight decrease for the
treated and control rats. The caffeine-induced weight loss
may be due to an increasing energy expenditure as previously
mentioned, even if not totally elucidated (10). In control rats,
the weight loss could be explained by handling and/or injec-
tion stress according to Izumi et al. (19). Moreover, Szafar-
czyk et al. also explained the observed stress-induced weight
loss by the increasing neuronal activity of hypothalamic
paraventricular nuclei (27). Finally, one must notice that the
weight loss is significantly more pronounced for the caffeine
group compared to the control group, confirming the proper
effects of caffeine.
The observed modifications on the circadian rhythms
would suggest a caffeine influence on the circadian pace-
maker, which could be explained by modifications of the mel-
atonin circadian synthesis. The pineal hormone melatonin,
which is involved in the neurobiological time-keeping system,
is characterized by a production following a circadian rhythm,
i.e., with levels increasing during the night and decreasing
during the daytime (3). This melatonin production might be
affected by caffeine administration. According to Zatz and
Heath (30), pulses of caffeine on chick pineal cells in dis-
persed cell culture produced an acute enhancement of mela-
tonin output and a phase shift of the circadian pacemaker
with a light-like pattern. Wright et al. (29) demonstrated that
caffeine reduced nighttime melatonin levels and altered am-
plitude and acrophase of the temperature circadian rhythm in
sleep-deprived humans. They suggested that caffeine may act
by inhibiting the neuromodulator adenosine. Indeed, a review
of the literature concerning pineal function reported on the
existence of A2b adenosine receptors in the rat pineal (15),
and showed that adenosine increased melatonin levels in this
pineal gland (16). Because caffeine is an adenosine antago-
nist, it was suggested that caffeine administration might de-
crease melatonin levels (1).
The influence of the hour of administration will be investi-
gated in a future study by comparing nighttime and daytime
caffeine administration. Thus, for the first time, the chrono-
pharmacological variations induced by caffeine on the biolog-
ical rhythms will be assessed, while for a second time the pos-
sibility that the observed modifications might be induced by a
potential effect of caffeine on the rhythm of melatonin pro-
duction will be investigated.
