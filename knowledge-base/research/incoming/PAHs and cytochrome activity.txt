Inhibition of Human Cytochrome P450 1A1-, 1A2-, and
1B1-Mediated Activation of Procarcinogens to Genotoxic
Metabolites by Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons
Tsutomu Shimada* and F. Peter Guengerich
Department of Biochemistry and Center in Molecular Toxicology, Vanderbilt UniVersity School of Medicine,
606 Light Hall, 2215 Garland AVenue, NashVille, Tennessee 37232-0146
ReceiVed October 20, 2005
Many chemicals in the environment can cause cancer, and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs)
are among the most ubiquitous. Cancer risk assessments require consideration of these in complex mixtures.
PAHs require metabolic activation by cytochrome P450 (P450) enzymes, primarily 1A1, 1A2, and 1B1.
We determined if individual PAHs and other procarcinogens affect the activities of human P450s 1A1,
1A2, and 1B1 by measuring 7-ethoxyresorufin O-deethylation (EROD) activity and metabolic activation
of PAH dihydrodiols and 2-amino-3,5-dimethylimidazo[4,5-f]quinoline (MeIQ) to genotoxic metabolites
in a Salmonella typhimurium NM2009 system. Of 23 PAHs examined, benz[a]anthracene (B[a]A), benzo-
[b]fluoranthene, and 5-methylchrysene were the most potent inhibitors of P450 1A2- and 1B1-catalyzed
EROD activity, with IC50 values <10 nM. Other PAHs, e.g., dibenz[a,c]anthracene, dibenz[a,h]anthracene,
dibenz[a,j]acridine, and 3-methylcholanthrene, rather selectively inhibited P450 1B1, with IC50 values
<15 nM. Benzo[a]pyrene (B[a]P) and nine other PAHs also inhibited P450 1A2 as well as 1B1 with
IC50 values <150 nM. Parent PAH compounds were generally more potent than 10 dihydrodiol metabolites
of PAHs and 3- and 9-hydroxy B[a]P in inhibiting EROD activity. In addition, we found that three
selected PAHs (5-methylchrysene, B[a]P, and B[a]A) inhibited metabolic activation of 5-methylchrysene-
1,2-diol, (()-B[a]P-7,8-diol, dibenzo[a,l]pyrene-11,12-diol, and MeIQ to genotoxic metabolites catalyzed
by P450s 1A1, 1B1, and 1A2, respectively, in S. typhimurium NM2009. Thus, individual PAHs may
affect their own and metabolism of other carcinogens catalyzed by P450 1A1, 1A2, and 1B1, and these
phenomena cause alteration in their ability to transform cells when single or complex PAH mixtures are
ingested by mammals, influencing risk assessment.
Introduction
The history of carcinogenic polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons
(PAHs)1 is nearly 250 years old, going back to observations by
Hill (1) and Pott (2) in London with tobacco and soots.
Yamagiwa was able to produce tumors with PAH mixtures in
an experimental animal model in 1915 (3), and in 1933, Cook
et al. (4) succeeded in isolating benzo[a]pyrene (B[a]P), which
has been a prototype in PAH chemical carcinogenesis (5).
The Millers demonstrated the need for metabolic activation
of PAHs to generate reactive products that bind irreversibly to
cellular nucleophiles, first with proteins and then with DNA
(6, 7). The nature of the activation process remained unclear,
and an early area of interest was the so-called K-region epoxide
theory (8). Subsequently, work by Sims (9) and several others
implicated the bay-region diol epoxides, a concept developed
by Jerina, Conney, and others (10). Other potential contributing
pathways include one-electron oxidation and oxidation of
dihydrodiols to o-quinones (11-13). The source of most of these
reactive PAH products in human tissues is the family 1 P450
enzymes, i.e., 1A1, 1A2, and 1B1 (14, 15). Several N2-guanyl
and N6-adenyl DNA adducts have been characterized, and
recently, structures have been reported for two PAH-adducted
oligonucleotides bound to DNA polymerases, providing impor-
tant information on how these adducts may cause mutations
(16, 17).
Considerable concern exists about the regulation of PAH
emissions and risk assessment related to several diseases,
particularly respiratory disease and cancer (18, 19). One
important consideration is that PAHs are always found in
complex mixtures, not as single components (20-22). The
scientific evaluation of mixtures, including PAHs, is a difficult
problem (23, 24). One approach (additive) is to treat risk as the
sum of that expected from each of the individual PAHs involved.
However, mechanisms underlying interactions ultimately need
to be established in order to define the true risks. Studies on
PAH mixtures have been done, although much of the focus has
been on the induction of P450s (25-28).
In the course of our continuing studies with PAHs and human
P450s, we found that the parent PAHs are very strong inhibitors
of the human P450s that are generally agreed to be most
extensively involved in the activation of PAHs and heterocyclic
amines, the family 1 P450s.

Materials and Methods
Chemicals. B[a]P-7,8-diols [(()-, (+)- and (-)-], 3- and
9-hydroxy-B[a]P, benz[a]anthracene (B[a]A)-1,2-diol, B[a]A-trans-
3,4-diol, B[a]A-cis-5,6-diol, and B[a]A-8,9-diol were purchased
from the National Cancer Institute Chemical Carcinogen Repository/
Midwest Research Institute (Kansas City, MO). B[a]P and B[a]A
were purchased from Sigma Chemical Co. (St. Louis, MO) and
Aldrich Chemical Co. (Milwaukee, WI), respectively. 7,12-Dim-
ethylbenz[a]anthracene (DMBA), DMBA-trans-3,4-diol, DMBA-
cis-5,6-diol, chrysene-1,2-diol, 5-methyl (Me) chrysene-1,2-diol,
5,6-dimethylchrysene-1,2-diol, dibenzo[a,l]pyrene (DB[a,l]P)-11,-
12-diol, benzo[g]chrysene-11,12-diol, and benzo[c]phenanthrene-
3,4-diol were kindly donated by Dr. S. S. Hecht (University of
Minnesota, Minneapolis, MN). 7-Ethoxyresorufin and resorufin
were purchased from Sigma. Other chemicals and reagents used in
this study were obtained from sources described previously or were
of the highest qualities commercially available (14, 29, 30).
Enzymes. A variant of P450 1B1*3 (Arg48Ala119Val432Asn453)
was used in this study. Bacterial “bicistronic” P450 1A1, 1A2, and
1B1 systems were prepared as described (31). Briefly, plasmids
for the expression of P450 1A1, 1A2, or 1B1 plus human NADPH-
P450 reductase (using a single promoter) were introduced into
Escherichia coli DH5R cells by a heat shock procedure, and the
transformants were selected in Luria-Bertani medium containing
100 µg ampicillin/mL. Bacterial membranes were prepared and
suspended in 10 mM Tris-Cl buffer (pH 7.4) containing 1.0 mM
EDTA and 20% glycerol, v/v (32). Yields of P450, as determined
by the original spectral method (33), ranged between 40 and 250
nmol/L medium.
Enzyme Assays. Standard incubation mixtures for the assay of
7-ethoxyresorufin O-deethylation (EROD) consisted of human P450
enzymes (usually membranes containing 10 pmol for P450) with a
substrate concentration of 2.5 µM in a final volume of 0.5 mL of
100 mM potassium phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) containing an
NADPH-generating system consisting of 0.5 mM NADP+, 5 mM
glucose 6-phosphate, and 0.5 units of yeast glucose 6-phosphate
dehydrogenase/mL (34, 35). The formation of resorufin from
7-ethoxyresorufin was determined fluorimetrically as described (36).
All inhibitors and 7-ethoxyresorufin were dissolved as stock
solutions (1 mM) in (CH3)2SO and diluted to working solutions;
the final concentration of organic solvent was e1% (v/v) in all
cases.
P450-dependent activation of procarcinogens to reactive products
that cause induction of umu gene expression in tester strain
Salmonella typhimurium NM2009 was determined as described
previously (14, 29). Standard incubation mixtures contained P450
(10 pmol) and 2.5 µM 5-Me chrysene-1,2-diol, (()-B[a]P-7,8-diol,
DB[a,l]P-11,12-diol, and 2-amino-3,5-dimethylimidazo[4,5-f]quino-
line (MeIQ), in a final volume of 1.0 mL of 100 mM potassium
phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) containing the NADPH-generating system
and 0.75 mL of bacterial suspension. The induction of umu gene
expression was monitored by measuring â-galactosidase activity
using o-nitrophenyl-â-D-galactopyranoside as a substrate and is
presented as units of â-galactosidase activity/min/nmol P450 (14,
29).
Kinetic parameters for inhibition of EROD activities and
activation of procarcinogens to genotoxic products in S. typhimu-
rium NM2009 by PAHs and other compounds were estimated by
nonlinear regression analysis using the program KaleidaGraph
(Synergy Software, Reading, PA) or GraphPad Prism (Graphpad,
San Diego, CA).
Results
Inhibition of P450 1A1, 1A2, and 1B1 EROD Activity by
5-Me Chrysene, Dibenz[a,h]anthracene (DB[a,h]A), and
B[a]P. We examined inhibition of EROD activities in bicistronic
E. coli membranes expressing P450 1A1, 1A2, and 1B1 (plus
human NADPH-P450 reductase) by the carcinogenic PAHs
5-Me chrysene, DB[a,h]A, and B[a]P (Figure 1). IC50 values
were determined to be 135, >1000, and 350 nM for the
inhibition of EROD by 5-Me chrysene, DB[a,h]A, and B[a]P,
respectively, in the P450 1A1 system. When P450 1A2 was
used, IC50 values of 6.0, >1000, and 140 nM, respectively, were
estimated. IC50 values for inhibition of P450 1B1 were 8.0, 5.2,
and 31 nM, respectively.

Inhibition of EROD Activities by PAH Diols and Other
PAH Metabolites, Heterocyclic Arylamines, and Mycotoxins.
Ten PAH diols, 3-OH-B[a]P, 9-OH-B[a]P, 6-aminochrysene,
2-nitrochrysene, 6-nitrochrysene, 7 heterocyclic arylamines, and
aflatoxin B1 and sterigmatocystin were examined for inhibition
of EROD activities of P450 1A1, 1A2, and 1B1 (Table 2).
B[a]P-4,5-diol, 3-OH-B[a]P, and 9-OH-B[a]P inhibited the three
P450s with IC50 values similar to or less than the parent PAH
B[a]P did. Other PAH diols examined were less active in
inhibiting these P450s when compared with the parent PAHs.
6-Aminochrysene inhibited P450 1A1-, 1A2, and 1B1-dependent
EROD activities with IC50 values of 110, 100, and 45 nM,
respectively (Table 2). 2-Nitrochrysene and 6-nitrochrysene were
weak inhibitors. 3-Amino-1,4-dimethyl-5H-pyrido[4,3-b]indole
(Trp-P-1) and 3-amino-1-methyl-5H-pyrido[4,3-b]indole (Trp-
P-2) inhibited P450 1B1 with IC50 values of 55 and 200 nM,
respectively, but six other heterocyclic arylamines did not
significantly inhibit the EROD activities of P450s (Table 2).
Aflatoxin B1 and sterigmatocystin were weak inhibitors of the
reactions catalyzed by all three P450s.
Effects of Substrate Concentration on Inhibition of EROD
Activity by B[a]P, 5-Me Chrysene, rNF, and Galangin. The
EROD Km (µM) and kcat (nmol/min/nmol P450) values were
determined to be 1.5 and 65 for P450 1A1, 0.61 and 11 for
P450 1A2, and 0.54 and 29 for P450 1B1, respectively (Figure
2 and Table 3). When 0.8 µM B[a]P, 0.16 µM RNF, and 0.16
µM galangin were added to the incubation mixture containing
P450 1A1, three chemicals caused decreases in EROD kcat values
from 60 to 17-26 min-1 but did not increase the Km values
(Figure 2 and Table 3). Similarly, P450 1A1, 1A2, and 1B1
EROD activities were inhibited by B[a]P, 5-Me chrysene, RNF,
and galangin, with decreases in kcat values without increases in
Km values (Figure 2 and Table 3).

Discussion
Urban atmosphere, diesel exhaust particles, tobacco smoke
condensates, and broiled foods contain many types of carcino-
gens, and PAHs have been recognized to be the major ones
(20, 22, 38). Most of the carcinogenicity studies have been done
using single chemicals in experimental animals, and B[a]P, DB-
[a,h]A, DMBA, and 3-MC are reported to be potent carcinogens
(4, 20). Complex mixtures of PAHs and other chemicals have
been studied to determine if these mixtures cause increased or
decreased capacities to form PAH-DNA adducts in vitro and
tumor formation in vivo, as compared with single chemicals
(27, 28, 39-45). The results reported to date are complex; in
some cases, the carcinogenic activities and DNA-binding
capacities are additive (20, 40-42, 46-48) but in other cases
decreased when compared with individual carcinogens (20, 22,
25, 40, 46, 49-52). The complex mixtures contain different
types of chemicals that cause alterations in the biological
activities of PAHs (20, 22, 25). Because most of the PAHs
require metabolic activation by P450s (and other enzymes) to
exert their carcinogenic potentials, the nature of enzyme
inhibitors and inducers in the mixtures has been studied
extensively (15, 53-56). In general, the chemical inhibitors may
be more important than the chemical inducers with respect to
their low dose effects in modifying carcinogen metabolism and
action (Figure 4) (37, 46, 58, 59).
Numerous inhibitors of P450 1A1, 1A2, and 1B1 have been
reported. These include flavonoids (RNF and galangin), 2,4,3′,5′-
tetramethoxystilbene, naturally occurring coumarins (bergamot-
tin, imperatorin, and isopimpinellin), vinylic and acetylenic PAH
compounds [e.g., 1-ethynylpyrene and 1-(1-propynyl)pyrene],
and synthetic organoselenium compounds (34, 37, 46, 58, 60-
63). In vivo studies have shown that some of these inhibitors
are able to suppress the tumor formation induced by PAHs and
other carcinogens in experimental animals (61, 64-66).
Several carcinogenic PAHs were found to be potent inhibitors
of P450 1A2 and 1B1, with IC50 values <150 nM. These include
B[a]A, B[a]P, benzo[b]fluoranthene, benzo[j]fluoranthene, ben-
zo[k]fluoranthene, chrysene, dibenz[a,c]anthracene, dibenz[a,j]-
acridine, DB[a,h]P, 3-MC, and 5-Me chrysene. Interestingly,
B[a]A, benzo[b]fluoranthene, and 5-Me chrysene inhibited P450
1A2- and 1B1-dependent EROD activities with IC50 values <10
nM. DB[a,h]A is rather unique because it inhibited P450 1B1-
mediated EROD activities with an IC50 of 5.2 nM, while it did
not inhibit P450 1A1 and 1A2 activities even at 1000 nM. DB-
[a,h]A has been shown to induce tumors in Sprague-Dawley
rats and NMRI mice (67, 68) and has been proposed to be
transformed to a bis-diol epoxide, which acts as a DNA-binding
species in the mouse (69). Which P450 enzymes are involved
in this activation step(s) remains unclear.
The IC50 values obtained with P450 1A1-dependent EROD
activities were always higher than those with P450 1A2 and
1B1. Our results also showed that B[e]P and fluoranthene, which
have been reported to be weak carcinogens or noncarcinogens
(20, 22), very strongly inhibited P450 1A2 and 1B1 but not
P450 1A1. Conversely, a potent carcinogen DB[a,l]P did not
show significant inhibition of EROD activities by P450s, except
that the former PAH inhibited P450 1A1-dependent EROD
activity with an IC50 of 330 nM. These results imply that the
carcinogenic potential of PAHs is not directly related to the
inhibitory activities for P450s 1A1, 1A2, and 1B1. Our results
also showed that B[a]P, 5-Me chrysene, RNF, and galangin
inhibit EROD activities of P450 1A1, 1A2, and 1B1 in a
noncompetitive manner (Figure 2 and Table 3). These chemicals
decreased P450 1A1, 1A2, and 1B1 EROD kcat values without
increases in Km values, suggesting that PAHs bind to the sites
distinct from substrate binding sites.
5-Me chrysene, B[a]P, and B[a]A were selected to determine
if these chemicals inhibit metabolic activation of potential
procarcinogens 5-Me chrysene-1,2-diol, (()-B[a]P-7,8-diol, and
DB[a,l]P-11,12-diol and MeIQ catalyzed by three P450s in S.
typhimurium NM2009. 5-Me chrysene itself inhibited the
activation of 5-Me chrysene-1,2-diol to active metabolites
catalyzed by P450 1A1 and 1B1, as well as those of (()-B[a]P-
7,8-diol and DB[a,l]P-11,12-diol. Similarly, B[a]P inhibited the
activation of (()-B[a]P-7,8-diol, 5-Me chrysene-1,2-diol, and
DB[a,l]P-11,12-diol to active metabolites by P450 1A1 and 1B1.
Activation of MeIQ by P450 1A2 was inhibited by three PAHs.
The results indicated that PAHs are able to alter their own
metabolism by P450s as well as those of other environmental
carcinogens (Figure 3).
IC50 values obtained with inhibition of P450-dependent
activation of PAH diols and MeIQ by PAHs and RNF and
galangin were higher than those determined with EROD
activities. 5-Me chrysene, for example, inhibited P450 1A1
EROD with an IC50 of 135 nM, although it inhibited P450 1A1-
dependent activation of 5-Me chrysene-1,2-diol, (()-B[a]P-7,8-
diol, and DB[a,l]P-11,12-diol with IC50 values of 8.1, 18, and

Metabolites of PAHs (e.g., 3-OH and 9-OH B[a]P and 10
dihydrodiol derivatives of PAHs) were rather weak inhibitors
of P450-dependent EROD activities as compared with the parent
compounds. Vinylic and acetylenic PAHs and other compounds
have been shown to be mechanism-based inhibitors of P450s
(34, 37, 70). Whether PAHs require metabolism to exert P450
inhibition remains unclear at present.
6-Aminochrysene and Trp-P-1 and Trp-P-2 inhibited P450
1B1-dependent EROD more strongly than P450 1A1 and 1A2.
Other heterocyclic amines, 2- and 6-nitrochrysene, aflatoxin B1,
and sterigmatocystin were relatively weak in inhibiting these
three P450s.
In conclusion, our current studies showed that 14 of 23 PAHs
tested were found to be potent inhibitors of P450 1A2 and 1B1
and in several cases P450 1A1. Some of these PAHs were not
so potent in inhibiting P450 1A1 as compared with P450 1A2
and 1B1. Among the 14 chemicals, B[e]P and fluoranthene have
been reported to be weak carcinogens or noncarcinogens, while
others have been reported to be potent carcinogens in experi-
mental animals and possibly in humans. Because P450s 1A1,
1A2, and 1B1 are the major enzymes that metabolize these
PAHs, it is suggested that these PAHs affect the metabolism of
other carcinogens present in the environment. Our studies
showed that the three potent P450 inhibitors 5-Me chrysene,
B[a]A, and B[a]P strongly decreased the activation of 5-Me
chrysene-1,2-diol, (()-B[a]P-7,8-diol, and DB[a,l]P-11,12-diol
to genotoxic metabolites catalyzed by P450 1A1 and 1A2 and
of MeIQ by P450 1A2 in S. typhimurium NM2009. Thus,
individual PAHs may affect their own and other carcinogen
metabolism catalyzed by P450s 1A1, 1A2, and 1B1 (Figure 3),
and these phenomena cause alterations in their ability to
transform cells when single or complex PAH mixtures are
ingested by mammals, influencing risk assessment.
