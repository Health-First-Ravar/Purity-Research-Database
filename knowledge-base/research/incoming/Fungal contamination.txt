ABSTRACT
Studies on the microbiology of coffee cherries and beans have shown that the predominant
toxigenic fungal genera (Aspergillus and Penicillium) are natural coffee contaminants. The aim of
this study was to investigate the distribution of fungi in Coffea arabica L. (Arabica coffee) and
Coffea canephora L. var. robusta (Robusta coffee) green coffee samples obtained from different
sources at the pre-roasting stage. Twenty-eight green coffee samples from different countries of
origin (Brazil, Timor, Honduras, Angola, Vietnam, Costa Rica, Colombia, Guatemala, Nicaragua,
India, and Uganda) were evaluated. The fungal load in the contaminated samples ranged from 0
to 12330 colony forming units (CFU)/g, of which approximately 67% presented contamination
levels below 1500 CFU/g, while 11% exhibited intermediate contamination levels between 1500
and 3000 CFU/g. Contamination levels higher than 3000 CFU/g were found in 22% of contami-
nated coffee samples. Fifteen different fungi were isolated by culture-based methods and
Aspergillus species belonging to different sections (complexes). The predominant Aspergillus
section detected was Nigri (39%), followed by Aspergillus section Circumdati (29%). Molecular
analysis detected the presence of Aspergillus sections Fumigati and Circumdati. The% coffee
samples where Aspergillus species were identified by culture-based methods were 96%. Data
demonstrated that green coffee beans samples were contaminated with toxigenic fungal species.
Since mycotoxins may be resistant to the roasting process, this suggests possible exposure to
mycotoxins through consumption of coffee. Further studies need to be conducted to provide
information on critical points of coffee processing, such that fungal contamination may be
reduced or eliminated and thus exposure to fungi and mycotoxins through coffee handling and
consumption be prevented.
Introduction
According to the International Coffee Organization,
global coffee production is rising (1.4% in 2015/2016
compared to 2014/2015), driven by an increase in
total demand (2.1% per annum in the last 4 years),
resulting in higher export rates (growth rate of 3.1%
since 2010). Two methods may be used for coffee
processing. 1) The “wet” method is more frequently
used for Arabica coffee in Colombia, Central
America and Hawaii, which involves hand picking
of mature cherries from bushes, mechanical depulp-
ing, and a final water wash (24 to 48 hr) step to
remove the mucilage layer (fermentation). The fer-
mented beans are then dried to a final 12% moisture
content. 2) The “dry” or natural method of fermen-
tation is generally used for Robusta coffee in Brazil
and Ethiopia, which consists of stripping by hand or
machine mature coffee cherries from bushes, and
spread them on the ground for sun drying during
the course of 10–25 days. After fermentation a dry
cherry is left, free of mucilage and pulp but still
surrounded by dry skins from which beans are
mechanically separated and stored at 11–12% final
moisture content (Silva et al., 2000).
Similar to other crops, coffee cherries and beans
are exposed to contamination and consequent
colonization by microorganisms during different
phases of plant development, harvesting, trans-
port, and storage. Microbial action is critical for

as crop and product handling (Batista et al.,
2003). Chemical control methods such as copper-
based fungicides used prophylactically act by kill-
ing spores or other pathogen propagules on the
plant surface to protect perennial cash crops, while
systemic fungicides, such as triazoles, enter plant
tissues and destroy the pathogen during or after
infection, and thus are redistributed to the plant to
some extent. Generally, systemic fungicides are
more expensive than protectants, but the lower
doses offset costs. A major drawback with most
systemic fungicides is that many fungal pathogens
have developed resistance to them (Kano et al.,
2014; O’Neill, 2015).
Microbiological studies demonstrated that toxi-
genic fungal genera such as Aspergillus and
Penicillium are natural contaminants of coffee
cherries and beans and are present from the field
to the warehouse (Mislivec et al., 1983; Nakajima
et al., 1997; Silva et al., 2000; Rezende et al., 2013).
Among Aspergillus genus, the designations actually
represent sections (or complexes) of closely related
species (also referred to as cryptic species) that
cannot be clearly distinguished morphologically
(Lamoth, 2016). Aspergillus sections Circumdati
(International
Commission
on
Microbiological
Specifications
for
Foods—ICMSF,

and
Nigri (Joosten et al., 2001), and Penicillium verru-
cosum (Pitt, 1987) are well-known ochratoxin A
(OTA) producers, and their presence in coffee
beans constitutes a potential source for coffee con-
tamination with OTA.
Ochratoxin A (OTA) is one of the most reported
mycotoxin present in coffee beans and roasted coffee
(Pitt et al., 2000; WHO, 2001), and several adverse
health effects were reported including hepatotoxi-
city, nephrotoxicity, teratogenicity, and carcinogeni-
city (IARC, 1993; Pitt et al., 2000; WHO, 2001; Clark
& Snedeker, 2006). In fact, OTA is the only myco-
toxin monitored at termination of the coffee process
production, with maximal OTA levels being 5 μg /kg
(for roasted coffee beans and ground roasted coffee)
and 10 μg /kg (for soluble coffee) (EU–SCF, 1998).
Coffee production phase in which invasion by
toxigenic fungi occurs has not been adequately
identified which is of importance for understand-
ing issues associated with OTA presence in coffee
and for the development of preventive and control
strategies to eliminate this mycotoxin. Experience
with other crops demonstrated that control of
OTA is more challenging when fungal invasion
occurs during pre-harvest compared to post-har-
vest such as during drying and storage (Taniwaki
et al., 2003). The quality of pre-harvest coffee
depends upon the combination of the botanical
variety, topographical and climatic conditions,
and cropping conditions (ITC, 2002). If fungal
infection with toxigenic strains occurs during cul-
tivation, it becomes difficult to obtain mycotoxin-
free coffee beans (Bucheli and Taniwaki, 2002).
Post-harvest problems might result from unfavor-
able conditions for drying, poor drying practices
or quality control, or inadequate storage condi-
tions (Taniwaki et al., 2003).
The skin (husk) of coffee beans is a significant
source of OTA; thus, cleaning and standardization
methods are effective in reducing mycotoxin levels.
During industrial processing of converting coffee
beans into roasted coffee and soluble coffee, a
decrease of up to 90% in OTA levels may occur
(Viani, 2002). Nevertheless, OTA destruction in the
roasting stage depends upon the combination of
time and temperature. Since each country applies
different roasting conditions, significant differences
in concentrations of OTA occur across Europe with
respect to contamination levels (Stegen et al., 1997).
This variability may also be related to varying analy-
tical conditions or even heterogeneity in distribution
of toxins (Tsubouchi et al., 1988; Micco et al., 1989;
Urbano et al., 2001).
In addition to OTA, other mycotoxins were
detected in coffee beans. Nakajima et al. (1997)
found aflatoxin B1 (AFB1) contamination mainly in
samples of green coffee beans imported from African
and Asian countries. Interestingly, the samples that
presented AFB1 also contained OTA contamination.
In Spain, García-Moraleja et al., (2015) noted levels
of contamination by aflatoxins B1, B2, G1 and G2,
OTA, fumonisins B1 and B2, sterigmatocystin, niva-
lenol, deoxynivalenol, 3-acetyldeoxynivalenol, 15-
acetyldeoxynivalenol, diacetoxyscirpenol, neosola-
niol, and HT-2 and T-2 toxins in different samples
of ready-to-drink coffee. The occurrence of myco-
toxins in the samples ranged from 4% (fumonisins)
to 43% (deoxynivalenol), and mean concentrations
ranged from 0.69 g/kg (AFB2) to 196.2 g/kg

The presence of fumonisin B2 (Noonim et al., 2009)
and AFB1 (Nakajima et al., 1997) in coffee may be
associated, similar to OTA, with the roasting pro-

Based on the presence of mycobiota in coffee
beans and the influence of environmental factors
on growth and production of mycotoxins, preven-
tion and control strategies need to be developed to
avoid fungal dissemination and production of
mycotoxins (Noonim et al., 2009). The issue of
occupational burden cannot be underestimated,
since workers in coffee processing units display
an increased risk of being exposed to these myco-
toxins. Previous studies performed in processing
units (coffee silos, coffee haulage companies, and
coffee
manufacturing
companies)
found
that
workers in these settings are exposed simulta-
neously to fungi and mycotoxins (Brera et al.,
2002; Sakwari et al., 2011).
The objective of this study was to determine the
distribution of fungi in green coffee beans from
samples of Coffea arabica L. (Arabica coffee) and
Coffea canephora L. var. robusta (Robusta coffee)
varieties from different global origins at the pre-
roasting stage. In addition, it is the first approach
combining culture-based methods and molecular
tools to detect the prevalence of toxigenic strains
of Aspergillus genera in the Portuguese green cof-
fee beans industry.
Materials and methods
Sampling collection and preparation
Twenty-eight samples of approximately 200 g of
green coffee beans from Coffea arabica (Arabica
coffee) and Coffea canephora var. robusta (Robusta
coffee) from different countries of origin (Brazil,
Timor, Honduras, Angola, Vietnam, Costa Rica,
Colombia,
Guatemala,
Nicaragua,
India,
and
Uganda) were examined (Table 1). Samples were
collected from the warehouse of a coffee roasting
industry
where
coffee
beans
were
previously
packed in bags after drying and sent to our
group for microbiological evaluation of the green
beans. The coffee beans were transported by
different means and stored under various condi-
tions at the pre-roasting stage. The methodology
used to prepare coffee samples was adapted from
Joosten et al. (2001). Briefly, 20 g of green coffee
beans from each sample was suspended in 180 ml
distilled water and homogenized for 20 min at 392
g; 100 ml washed supernatant was stored at −20°C
for molecular analysis.
Fungal identification and detection
Culture-based methods
The washed supernatant (100 µl) was plated in 2%
malt extract agar (MEA) with chloramphenicol
(0.05 g/L) media and dichloran glycerol (DG18)
agar-based media, for morphological identification
of mycobiota. Inoculated plates were incubated at
27°C for 5 to 7 days. After incubation, quantitative
colony forming units (CFU/g) and qualitative
results were obtained with identification of the
isolated fungal species. For species identification,
microscopic mounts were performed using tease
mount or Scotch tape mount and lactophenol cot-
ton blue mount procedures. Morphological identi-
fication
was
achieved
through
macro-
and
microscopic characteristics according to De Hoog
and colleagues (2000).

Molecular detection by real-time polymerase chain
reaction
Five milliliter washed supernatant was centrifuged
at 2500 g for 10 min, supernatant was removed,
and DNA then extracted using the ZR Fungal/
Bacterial DNA MiniPrep Kit (Zymo Research)
according to the recommendations of the manu-
facturer. Molecular identification of the different
species/strains was achieved by Real-Time PCR
(RT-PCR) using the Rotor-Gene 6000 qPCR
Detection System (Corbett). Reactions included
1× iQ Supermix (Bio-Rad), 0.5 μM of each primer
(Table 2), and 0.375 μM of TaqMan probe in a
total volume 20 μl. Amplification followed a three-
step PCR: 40 cycles with denaturation at 95°C for
30 sec, annealing at 52°C for 30 sec, and extension
at 72°C for 30 sec. A non-template control was
used for each PCR reaction. As positive controls
for the species, DNA samples were obtained from
reference strains from the Mycology Laboratory
from the National Institute of Health Doctor
Ricardo Jorge (INSA).
Statistical analysis
Statistical software SPSS V22 was used for statis-
tical analysis. Data analysis was performed using
univariate descriptive statistics with frequency (n;
%); median and graphical representations are in
accordance with the nature of the data. Results
were considered significant at the 5% significance
level. To compare total fungal and Aspergillus spp.
load in Arabica and Robusta coffee green samples,
Mann–Whitney U-test was used.
Results
Fungal load
The % fungal contamination in the analyzed green
coffee samples was 64.3%. Sixty–four percent (9/14)
of fungi was present in Arabica coffee, whereas 56%
(5/9) was detected in Robusta coffee. The fungal
load in the contaminated samples ranged from 0
to 12330 CFU/g, of which approximately 66.7% (9)
presented contamination levels below 1500 CFU/g,
and 11.1% (4) displayed intermediate contamina-
tion
levels
between
1500
and
3000
CFU/g.
Contamination levels higher than 3000 CFU/g
were found in 22.2% (5) of the contaminated coffee
samples. Within the coffee samples, no significant
differences were found with Robusta (1215 CFU/g)
compared to Arabica (1170 CFU/g) coffee.
Fungal identification
The fungal diversity present in the coffee samples was
characterized based upon the morphological features
of the colonies. Fifteen different fungi genus/sections/
species were isolated (Table 3), with the most preva-
lent being Aspergillus section Nigri (39.1%), followed
by Aspergillus section Circumdati (29.3%), Penicillium
sp. (9.9%), and Aspergillus section Aspergilli (9.9%). It
was not possible to count two coffee samples from
Vietnam and a third sample from Timor. In addition
to
these
prevalent
fungi,
Aspergillus
sections
Versicolores, Candidi, Flavi, and Fumigati were also
isolated (Table 3). Lower amounts of the species
Syncephalastrum sp., Mucor sp., Aureobasidium sp.,
Leichtimia sp., and Acremonium sp. were also
identified.

Discussion
The susceptibility of coffee crops to various fun-
gal diseases is a considerable challenge for coffee
producers, who frequently resort to fungicides to
control foliage and berry diseases. Fungi produce
severe damage to coffee beans and are poten-
tially detrimental to both organoleptic and sani-
tary quality of the coffee. In addition, coffee
colonization with toxigenic fungal species is an
important indicator of a potential contamination
with mycotoxins. In fact, although coffee beans
are chemically and thermally treated during the
transformation process, several studies reported
the presence of mycotoxins in the final product
(Studer-Rohr et al., 1995; Batista et al., 2009;
Vanesa & Pácin, 2013).
Fungal contamination assessed by conventional
methods (64.3%) was lower than reported in other
studies with fungal burden ranging from 71.34 to
98% (Ilic et al., 2007; Noonim et al., 2008; Alvindia
& Acda, 2010; Gamboa-Gaitan, 2012; Nganou
et al., 2014; Alvindia & Guzman, 2016). With
molecular tools, it was possible to detect fungal
contamination in 96.4% of the coffee samples,
suggesting a higher fungal incidence within the
samples analyzed (Table 5). Although no signifi-
cant differences were found among coffee varieties
regarding total fungal load and Aspergillus counts,
Arabica green coffee contained a higher number of
contaminated samples, whereas Robusta green cof-
fee contained a greater fungal load mean (CFU/g).
This might be due to the generally wet processing
of Arabica coffee (Alvindia & Acda, 2010), which

Over fermentation or prolonged fermentations
due to lack of sun and high humidity provide
opportunities for spoilage as under these condi-
tions fungal growth is promoted. With dry fer-
mentations, typical from Robusta coffee increased
contamination might occur during re-spreading
on the ground and with rain as the ground tem-
perature is high and conditions are largely aerobic
(Silva et al., 2000). Since these two types of coffee
beans displayed similar fungal species, the differ-
ences observed in average mold counts indicated
higher extent of fungal contamination (Noonim
et al., 2008).
Considering the five samples of decaffeinated
coffee, two of these presented no apparent signs
of contamination, while in the other three contam-
ination levels were lower than 1500 CFU/g. This
observation may be due to processes required for
coffee decaffeination through solvent extraction
with known toxicity toward fungi (Oda et al.,
2014) and found to decrease OTA contamination
levels in coffee (Heilmann et al., 1999).
Regarding coffee mycobiota, it is well known
that fungal contamination is highly influenced by
processing practices, such as fermentation in wet
process (water quality and equipment used) or
drying process (time period, cleanliness of equip-
ment, and environment) (Noonim et al., 2009). All
these variables, as well as green coffee beans trans-
portation and storage (Parani & Eyeni, 2012),
affect fungal burden and mycotoxins production
and, consequently, coffee contamination. In the
culture-based
approach,
the
most
prevalent
Aspergillus section was Nigri, which is in agree-
ment with previous findings (Sartori et al., 2006;
Noonim et al., 2008; 2009; Culliao & Barcelo, 2015;
Alvindia & Guzman, 2016). Section Circumdati,
the second most prevalent section in this study,
was also identified in other studies (Batista et al.,
2003; Silva et al., 2000; Suárez-Quiroz et al., 2004;
Taniwaki et al., 2003; Noonim et al., 2008). Finally,
although less abundant, several additional species
within the Aspergillus genera were isolated in our
study. These include the sections Flavi (Batista
et al., 2001, 2003; Mislivec, et al., 1983; Silva
et al., 2000), Fumigati (Bucheli et al., 1998;
Suárez-Quiroz et al., 2004), Aspergilli (Eurotium
herbariorum) (Mislivec et al., 1983), Versicolores
(Batista et al., 2001, 2003; Mislivec et al., 1983)
and Candidi (Kuntawee & Akarapisan, 2015).
The lower quantity distribution of Penicillium gen-
era may be attributed by the fact that most
Penicillium species generally do not penetrate
into the bean as deeply as Aspergillus. In addition,
most Penicillum species do not grow well at ele-
vated temperatures (Mislivec et al., 1983).
Molecular tools confirmed that 10 samples were
co-contaminated, and this phenomenon was recently
demonstrated by Culliao and Barcelo (2015). The use
of DNA markers for easy and fast detection of fungal
species may be useful to complement conventional
methods, which require fungal cultivation and exper-
tise in taxonomy (Sartori et al., 2006; Viegas et al.,
2012, 2014). Molecular tools generated results in 48
hr, saving time and effort in comparison with culture-
based methods (Sartori et al., 2006). In addition,
detecting not only the viable but also the non-viable
fungal burden might provide a more complete profile
of the mycotoxins that are present (Viegas et al., 2012,
2014).
DNA from the Aspergillus sections Fumigati and
Circumdati was amplified by qPCR in 25 samples
where the presence of one of the fungal species was
not previously identified by conventional methods.
However, in five samples, it was possible to obtain
fungal isolates from species that were not detected by
molecular methods. This might be due to the presence
of PCR inhibitors in the examined coffee samples
(Yang & Rothman, 2004; McDevitt et al., 2007).
Using conventional methodologies, a wide distribu-
tion of fungal species was found. This approach com-
bining both methods is useful to overcome culture-
based and molecular methods limitations (Viegas
et al., 2012, 2014).
Recently, in addition to the fungal species of
Aspergillus section Circumdati that was reported to
produce OTA (Dalcero et al., 2002; Battilani et al.,
2003; Frisvad et al., 2004), Aspergillus section Nigri
was also found to generate OTA (Abarca et al., 1994;
Téren et al., 1996). Both Aspergillus sections were
predominant in our study and need to be taken into
consideration when analyzing the toxigenic potential
of samples.
The initial quality of the fruits harvested, the pre-
sence of OTA-producing fungi, as well as the

conditions at the processing unit, contributes to OTA
formation in coffee (Bucheli & Taniwaki, 2002). The
dominance of Aspergillus section Nigri in coffee beans
might be due to climate change and likely to produce
migration of OTA-producing Aspergillus section
Nigri to cooler areas in which these species were not
previously present (Paterson & Lima, 2010). The
expected increase in global temperature in the com-
ing decades may expose coffee beans grown in cooler
areas to a greater risk of contamination by OTA due
to the invasion of Nigri section (Alvindia & Guzman,
2016).
Conclusions
Data demonstrated that fungal contamination occurs
extensively in green coffee beans. Since the fungi
species found are producers of mycotoxins, data sug-
gest a potential contamination of coffee with myco-
toxins. This is due to the fact that mycotoxins can be
resistant to the roasting process. Therefore, preven-
tion of fungal contamination of coffee beans may
contribute to eliminate/control of contamination by
mycotoxins. Future studies need to be developed to
understand how different roasting conditions contri-
bute to reduce mycotoxins presence.
