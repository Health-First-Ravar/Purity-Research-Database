Simple Summary
Coffee silverskin is a natural by-product of coffee roasting that contains bioactive and
prebiotic compounds. This study investigated the effects of dietary coffee silverskin sup-
plementation on growth performance, carcass traits, and gastrointestinal health in broiler
chickens. The results suggest that coffee silverskin is a promising functional feed ingredient
that can improve broiler growth performance and gut health.
Abstract
Coffee silverskin (CSS) remains a neglected poultry additive; investigating its bioactive
potential is essential for optimizing agricultural productivity and enhancing food security
via advanced nutrition. This study analyzed how dietary CSS supplementation influences
broiler growth, slaughter characteristics, meat quality, cecal microbial ecology, and intesti-
nal histomorphology. A total of 400 one-day-old male Ross 308 broilers were randomly
assigned to four dietary treatments with 10 replicates of 10 birds each. Experimental treat-
ments consisted of a negative control (NC, basal diet) and CSS-supplemented groups (0.5,
1.0, and 2.0 g/kg, designated CSS0.5, CSS1.0, and CSS2.0, respectively). All data were
subjected to one-way ANOVA using the procedure of SPSS 23.0. Coffee silverskin supple-
mentation, specifically at 1.0–2.0 g/kg, optimized broiler growth performance, significantly
elevating body weight and average daily gain (p < 0.05). While carcass yield improved in a
dose-dependent manner, fundamental meat quality—pH and shear force—remained stable.
Crucially, CSS inclusion reconfigured cecal ecology, selectively suppressing Escherichia coli
and Salmonella sp. while enhancing Lactobacillus populations (p < 0.001). This microbial shift
mirrored histological gains, notably enhanced villus height and villus height-to-crypt ratios
(p < 0.05). These findings demonstrate that CSS, particularly at 1.0–2.0 g/kg, enhances
broiler performance, carcass quality and gut health.

1. Introduction
The environmental burden of industrial waste has emerged as a critical global chal-
lenge, with particular attention being paid to sustainable waste management solutions
across various sectors [1]. Within the food production industry, coffee processing gener-
ates substantial by-products, with global coffee silverskin (CSS) production estimated at
approximately 1.8 million metric tons annually [2]. This thin epidermal layer, discarded
during bean roasting, represents both an environmental liability and an untapped resource
rich in bioactive compounds [3].
Recent investigations have revealed that CSS contains significant quantities of phenolic
compounds, caffeine, and chlorogenic acids—bioactive molecules with demonstrated health
benefits, including antidiabetic, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties [4,5]. The
substantial dietary fiber content [3,6] and prebiotic potential [7] of CSS have prompted
research into its applications as a functional food additive [8]. Furthermore, CSS demon-
strates promising lipid-modulating effects, including the ability to improve high-density
lipoprotein cholesterol profiles [9] and regulate adipocyte metabolism [10].
The livestock industry faces mounting pressure to identify sustainable alternatives to
antibiotic growth promoters, particularly in poultry production [11]. Current research indi-
cates that dietary antioxidants and fiber sources can significantly improve intestinal health
and disease resistance in poultry [12,13]. While preliminary studies in aquaculture systems
have demonstrated CSS’s efficacy in enhancing growth performance and immune param-
eters in tilapia [7,14], research examining its application in terrestrial livestock remains
notably scarce.
This study systematically investigates the effects of dietary CSS supplementation
on Ross 308 broiler chickens, specifically evaluating growth performance parameters,
carcass characteristics and meat quality, cecal microbiota composition, and small intestinal
morphology to address critical knowledge gaps in sustainable poultry nutrition.
Our findings contribute to the growing body of research on sustainable agricultural
practices by evaluating the potential of CSS as both a functional feed additive and a means of
valorizing coffee processing by-products. The results may inform strategies for improving
poultry production efficiency while addressing environmental concerns associated with
industrial food waste.
2. Materials and Methods
2.1. Preparation of Coffee Silverskin
Coffee silverskin used in this study was obtained from Saenchai Estate. Briefly, the
CSS samples were dried at 60 ◦C for 48 h and subsequently ground using a grinder (Cutting
Mill SM 100, Retsch, Haan, Germany) into a powder, which passed through a 1 mm sieve,
producing the CSS powder. Proximate analysis of CSS (Table 1) was conducted according
to AOAC [15]. The bioactive compounds in CSS, including total phenolic compounds,
2,2-diphenyl-1-picrylhydrazyl (DPPH), total flavonoid compounds, caffeine, epicatechin,
and gallic acid, were determined according to Nhu-Trang et al. [16], and the results are
presented in Table 2.

2.2. Animals and Experimental Design
A total of 400 one-day-old male Ross 308 broilers were randomly allocated to 4 groups
of 10 replicates each, with 10 birds per replicate. The negative control (NC) group was
fed a basal diet, and 3 CSS test groups were fed the basal diet plus 0.5, 1.0, and 2.0 g/kg
(CSS0.5, CSS1.0, and CSS2.0, respectively) for 35 d. The chicks were fed starter diets
from day 1 to day 14 and finisher diets from day 15 to day 35, based on the nutrient
requirements for broilers according to NRC [17] (Table 3), and proximate analysis of the
basal diets was conducted according to AOAC [15]. The chicks were raised in floor pens
(100 cm × 100 cm × 80 cm), provided with supplemental heat and ventilation, and given
free access to feed and water. The lighting program was provided continuously for the
first week. The duration was gradually reduced (1 h per d) to 18 h by day 13, which was
maintained throughout the study. The initial temperature was 32 ◦C, which was gradually
lowered by 2 to 3 ◦C per week until reaching 25 ◦C.

was recorded daily throughout the experimental period.

2.4. Slaughter Traits
At day 35, the birds were randomly weighed and slaughtered (10 broilers per treat-
ment). After defeathering, the carcass (without feathers, blood, and internal organs) was
weighed to determine carcass percentage, which was expressed as a percentage of live body
weight. Breast and thigh muscles were weighed separately. Internal organs, including
the liver, spleen, heart, proventriculus, gizzard, small intestine, cecum, and abdominal
fat, were excised, weighed and calculated as a percentage of live weight. The meat parts
(breast and thigh) were promptly placed in separate sealable plastic bags and kept at 4 ◦C
for subsequent meat quality analysis [18]. At 24 h post-slaughter, the pH values were
measured using a portable pH meter and the breast and thigh meats were measured for
color parameters using a Minolta Chroma Meter (Model CR-410, Minolta Camera Co., Ltd.,
Osaka, Japan) [19]. According to Qu et al.’s method [20], shear force and cooking loss were
evaluated. Meat samples, each weighing 50–60 g, were gently dried using tissue paper
to remove surface moisture before their weights were recorded. Individual samples were
placed in heat-resistant bags, sealed, and cooked in an 80 ◦C water bath for 20 min. The
samples were removed and cooled at room temperature for 30–35 min and gently dried to
remove excess liquid. Cooking loss was calculated as (raw meat weight −cooked meat

2.5. Cecal Microbial
At the end of the trial (10 broilers per treatment), cecal digesta were collected and
analyzed for bacterial content following a modified procedure by Qiu et al. [21]. Cecal
digesta (1 g) was mixed with 9 mL of 0.1% peptone solution in sterile sampling tubes
using a vortex mixer (LMS Co., Ltd., Bunkyo-ku, Tokyo, Japan). Serial dilutions were
prepared, and the samples were plated onto specific agar media: Lactobacillus Man Rogosa
Sharpe (MRS) Agar (Himedia Laboratories, Maharashtra, India) for isolating and culturing
Lactobacillus sp., Xylose-Lysine Deoxycholate (XLD) Agar for Salmonella sp., and Eosin
Methylene Blue (EMB) Agar for Escherichia coli. The XLD and EMB agar plates were
incubated for 24 h, while the Lactobacillus MRS agar plates were incubated for 48 h at
37 ◦C. Bacterial colonies were counted immediately after incubation, and the results were
presented as log10 colony-forming units per gram (cfu/g) of cecal content.
2.6. Small Intestine Histology Measurements
The small intestines (duodenum, jejunum, and ileum) were collected from the birds
(10 birds per treatment) after slaughter in 1 cm sections and cleaned with phosphate-
buffered saline before being preserved in a 10% formaldehyde solution for 24 h. The
samples were then processed through a standardized protocol that included dehydration,
clearing, and embedding in paraffin wax [22]. Using a rotary microtome, the tissues
embedded in paraffin were sectioned into slices (4 µm thick) and positioned on glass slides.
The sections were deparaffinized and stained with hematoxylin and eosin (H&E). Then, the
tissue samples were mounted using a coverslip for observation. Histological examination
was conducted with a compound microscope (A1 Zeiss Axio Scope, Carl Zeiss, Göttingen,
Germany) at 10× magnification. Measurements of villus height (VH), crypt depth (CD),
and villus height to crypt depth ratio (VH:CD) were taken using Motic Images Plus 2.0
software (Motic China Group Co., Xiamen, China) [23].
2.7. Statistical Analysis
The experimental data were analyzed by one-way ANOVA using SPSS 23.0 software
(SPSS Inc., Chicago, IL, USA), followed by Duncan’s multiple range test. The results are
expressed as treatment means with the pooled standard error of the mean (SEM). Significant
differences were accepted at p < 0.05. The effects of different levels of CSS supplementation
were analyzed using linear and quadratic contrasts.
3. Results
3.1. Growth Performance
The effects of CSS on growth performance are presented in Table 4. By day 14,
birds receiving CSS at 0.5 and 1.0 g/kg (CSS0.5 and CSS1.0, respectively) demonstrated
significantly higher body weight and ADG compared to the negative control (NC; p = 0.009),
a trend that persisted through day 35, with all CSS groups maintaining superior growth
performance (p = 0.005). ADFI varied by treatment and phase (p < 0.001), with CSS0.5
consistently eliciting the highest ADFI. Feed conversion ratio outcomes showed phase-
dependent differences, with CSS1.0 and CSS2.0 exhibiting efficiency, and CSS2.0 achieving
the best overall FCR (p = 0.006). Linear and quadratic contrasts indicated dose-dependent
responses for growth performance parameters, including body weight and ADG at 35 d
(p < 0.05). Mortality did not differ significantly among the treatment groups.

3.2. Slaughter Performance and Meat Quality
The effects of CSS on the slaughter performance of broiler chickens are shown in
Table 5. CSS supplementation led to an increase in final live weight (p = 0.004), with CSS1.0
achieving the highest value and significantly exceeding NC, a pattern mirrored in defeather
and carcass weights (p = 0.003 and p = 0.008, respectively), while CSS1.0 notably improved
carcass percentage (p = 0.046). Likewise, there was a linear and quadratic improvement in
live weight, defeather weight, and carcass weight in broilers that received CSS (p < 0.05).
Additionally, CSS enhanced meat composition by increasing breast and thigh meat yields
(p = 0.012 and p = 0.043), and although most visceral organ weights remained unchanged
(p > 0.05), the CSS groups tended to exhibit lower gizzard weights and reduced abdomi-
nal fat.
A comprehensive evaluation of meat quality parameters—including physicochemical
properties, instrumental color, and texture—revealed no significant differences across
treatment groups (p > 0.05), indicating that CSS supplementation at varying inclusion
levels did not affect post-mortem pH, color stability, cooking loss, or shear force, thereby
preserving overall meat quality characteristics (Table 6). Supplementation with CSS showed
no significant linear or quadratic effects on physicochemical properties, instrumental color,
and texture (p > 0.05). However, a linear decrease was observed in breast meat color (b*)
with increasing dietary CSS concentration.

3.3. Cecal Microbiota
The effects of CSS on cecal bacterial microbiota are shown in Table 7. The CSS2.0 exhib-
ited the most pronounced reduction in pathogenic bacteria, with E. coli counts significantly
lower compared to the NC. Coffee silverskin supplementation showed a dose-dependent
inhibitory effect on E. coli, with CSS2.0 demonstrating superior efficacy among CSS treat-
ments (p < 0.001). A similar pattern was observed for Salmonella sp., where CSS achieved
significant suppression compared to NC (p < 0.001). Coffee silverskin supplementation
significantly enhanced Lactobacillus sp. populations in a dose-dependent manner (p < 0.001).
The highest CSS doses (CSS1.0 and CSS2.0) promoted the most substantial increases, ex-
ceeding those of the NC. This stimulatory effect was particularly notable at inclusion levels

4. Discussion
Coffee silverskin is a by-product of coffee roasting that has gained increasing atten-
tion due to its richness in bioactive compounds [3]. Coffee silverskin contains phenolic
compounds, caffeine, and chlorogenic acids, which have been associated with antioxidant,
anti-inflammatory, and lipid-modulating effects [5,10]. In poultry production, the search
for natural feed ingredients has highlighted the potential role of dietary antioxidants in
improving growth performance and gut health.
The significant peak in ADFI observed at the 0.5 g/kg inclusion level (91.57 g/d;
p < 0.001) suggests a sensory optimum where aromatic volatiles from the coffee roasting
process act as an appetitive stimulus. Conversely, the numerical reduction in intake at
the 2.0 g/kg level (81.10 g/d) may reflect a dose-dependent increase in bitter-tasting
alkaloids, such as caffeine (1440.40 mg/100 g in raw CSS), which—while not aversive—
may subtly temper the voluntary intake stimulation compared to lower inclusion rates.
Dietary supplementation with 2.0 g/kg of CSS improved broiler growth performance by
increasing body weight, average daily gain, and feed efficiency. These findings indicate
that CSS may serve as a functional feed additive with potential applications in livestock
production [7,14]. The growth-promoting effects are attributable to the rich bioactive profile
of CSS, particularly its polyphenols (e.g., flavonoids, gallic acid) [24]. These compounds
likely enhance nutrient utilization by modulating digestive enzymes [25], improving gut
morphology [26], and beneficially shifting the gut microbiota [27]. Our findings align
with reports on other phenolic-rich additives, such as grape seed extract [28] dandelion
extract [29], and coffee cherry pulp extract [30], that similarly enhance poultry growth. The
dose-dependent response suggests an optimal inclusion level between 1.0 and 2.0 g/kg,
although further investigation is required to refine this across different production phases
and to account for source-specific variability in bioactive composition [31], as reported for
other by-products like grape pomace [32].
Dietary CSS at 1.0–2.0 g/kg improved broiler carcass characteristics, increasing de-
feathered weight, carcass yield, and total meat percentage. Notably, there was an increased
breast meat yield at the 2.0 g/kg inclusion level compared with the NC group, an effect on
lean mass deposition consistent with reports on grape seed extract and resveratrol [28,33,34].
Crucially, these gains in carcass yield did not compromise meat quality, as pH, color, and
drip loss remained stable across all treatments. This stability presents a significant ad-
vantage over other polyphenol sources like grape pomace and eucalyptus leaves, which
have been reported to alter fat distribution and meat pH [35,36]. The favorable outcome
may be associated with the unique bioactive profile of CSS [37], which appears to preserve
myofibrillar integrity [38] without affecting organ weights—a safety profile also observed
for additives like gallic acid [39].
Dietary CSS beneficially modulated the cecal microbiota in broilers, suppressing
pathogenic E. coli and Salmonella sp. while promoting the growth of Lactobacillus sp.
This effect may contribute indirectly through modulation of the gut microbial ecosystem.
The enrichment of beneficial bacteria such as Lactobacillus sp. may inhibit pathogenic
microorganisms via competitive exclusion, production of organic acids, and improvement
of gut environmental conditions due to the phenolic compounds in CSS, which disrupt
bacterial cell membrane integrity, leading to ion leakage and impaired virulence [40]. This
mechanism—involving ATP depletion and inhibition of DNA gyrase [25]—is consistent
with the action of other flavonoid-rich botanicals like thyme, oregano, and grape seed
extract [41–45]. By fostering a healthier microbial balance, CSS may consequently enhance
the intestine’s absorptive capacity.
Dietary CSS enhanced small intestinal morphology, increasing VH and VH:CD—a
key indicator of absorptive capacity. These architectural improvements, particularly at

1.0–2.0 g/kg CSS, are consistent with a conserved mechanism for plant-derived polyphe-
nols [40,46]. Our results corroborate findings on grape pomace, which induced similar
duodenal and jejunal adaptations [32], and align with the enhanced VH:CD ratios reported
for guava and dandelion extracts [29,40,47]. The segment-specific variability we observed
may reflect regional differences in polyphenol bioavailability or nutrient transporter ex-
pression [48].
Coffee silverskin improves intestinal morphology through two synergistic mecha-
nisms: direct epithelial stimulation and indirect microbial modulation. First, its phenolic
compounds, such as chlorogenic acid, likely promote enterocyte turnover via ERK/NF-κB
signaling, a pathway also activated by grape seed polyphenols [48,49]. Second, CSS shifts
the gut microbiota by suppressing pathogens like E. coli while enriching for beneficial
Lactobacillus, which in turn mitigates intestinal inflammation and preserves epithelial
integrity [40,46,50]. The different jejunal effects observed between CSS and tannin-rich
grape pomace [51] likely stem from their distinct polyphenolic profiles, particularly the
predominance of caffeine derivatives in CSS.
5. Conclusions
Dietary supplementation with CSS is an effective strategy to improve broiler produc-
tion efficiency. At levels of 0.5–2.0 g/kg, CSS enhanced growth performance, improved
carcass yield, and beneficially modulated the cecal microbiota by suppressing pathogens
while promoting Lactobacillus sp. Crucially, these benefits were achieved without compro-
mising meat quality parameters. Therefore, CSS represents a promising and sustainable
functional feed additive for the poultry industry, with future research needed to optimize
dosage across different production phases.
