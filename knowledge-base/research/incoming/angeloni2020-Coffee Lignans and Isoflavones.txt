A B S T R A C T
Green coﬀee, the raw material of roasted coﬀee and coﬀee beverages, is one of the most widely traded com-
modities worldwide. There is a dearth of studies on its content in phytoestrogens such as isoﬂavones and lignans.
Previously, we developed an eﬃcient method for the simultaneous quantiﬁcation of 6 isoﬂavones (daidzin,
genistin, daidzein, genistein, formononetin and biochanin A) and 3 lignans (secoisolariciresinol, matairesinol
and lariciresinol) in green coﬀee by using HPLC-MS/MS. Several extraction processes were evaluated and the
best performing, base hydrolysis followed by enzymatic digestion, was validated and used to analyse 25 diﬀerent
coﬀee samples, 1 Coﬀea canephora and 24 Coﬀea arabica, from diﬀerent countries. Lignans (total content:
286.5–8131.8 µg kg−1) were found in higher concentration than isoﬂavones (total content: 3.4–300.0 µg kg−1)
and
the
most

were
secoisolariciresinol
(172.6–5714.1
µg
kg−1)
and
lariciresinol
(113.9–2417.7 µg kg−1). Notably, the Ethiopian coﬀee samples contained the highest levels of these compounds.
1. Introduction
Coﬀee is one of the most important agricultural products in inter-
national trade. In 2018, 168 million of green coﬀee bags of 60 kg were
produced. Brazil and Vietnam are the main coﬀee producers, ac-
counting for 54% of worldwide production (International Coﬀee
Organization, 2018). Green coﬀee beans, the starting raw material for
roasted coﬀee and coﬀee beverages, are obtained from the seeds of
coﬀee fruits after a series of processes to remove the outer layers of
coﬀee cherries (the skin, pulp, mucilage and parchment). At the end of
any processing method, coﬀee beans are dried to a ﬁnal water content
of 10.0–12.0% (de Melo Pereira et al., 2018).
The
main
constituents
of

coﬀee
are
carbohydrates
(55.0–65.5%), lipids (10.0–18.0%), nitrogen-containing compounds
(11.0–15.0%),

(0.8–4.0%),

(6.7–9.2%) and minerals (3.0–5.4%). Other compounds that are found
in lower percentages are non-volatile aliphatic acids (citric, malic and
quinic acids) and phenols (Mussatto, Machado, Martins, & Teixeira,
2011). In addition, coﬀee is a valuable source of phytoestrogens, also
called “dietary estrogens.” The scaﬀold of this group of polyphenolic
compounds is structurally similar to 17-β-estradiol, thus capable of
inﬂuencing the estrogenic activities (Landete et al., 2016). The most
studied and best-known phytoestrogens are isoﬂavones, also considered
as a subclass of ﬂavonoids. They occur in the Leguminosae family, the

in alfalfa, red clover and beans (Zaheer & Humayoun Akhtar, 2017).
Lignans, another important class of phytoestrogens, are characterized
by dimerization of two phenylpropanoid units, and are found in such
foods as ﬂaxseeds, sesame seeds, cereals, fruits and vegetables; they
occur as aglycons (secoisolariciresinol, SECO, lariciresinol, LARI and
matairesinol, MAT), glycosidic forms (e.g., secoisolariciresinol diglu-
coside) or oligomer forms (Landete, 2012). Isoﬂavones and lignans
have not only phytoestrogen functions but also biological ones. They
possess antioxidant and anti-inﬂammatory properties, and also serve to
lower cholesterol levels and reduce the risk of contracting such chronic
diseases as prostate and breast cancer (Zaheer et al., 2017; Landete,
2012).
Isoﬂavone and lignan quantiﬁcation is usually conducted using a
high performance liquid chromatography-diode-array detector (HPLC-
DAD), high performance liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectro-
metry (HPLC-MS/MS), and gas chromatography-mass spectrometry
(GC–MS) (Mazur et al., 1998; Alves, Almeida, Casal, & Oliveira, 2010;
Thompson, Boucher, Liu, Cotterchio, & Kreiger, 2006). Various sample
preparation techniques and extraction processes can be used, such as
acid and base hydrolysis, enzymatic digestion, organic solvent extrac-
tion, and solid-phase extraction (SPE). Mazur et al. (1998), pioneered
phytoestrogen quantitation in ground coﬀee, found SECO in quantities
ranging

concentration of 0.503 mg kg−1 and only traces of SECO and genistein
(≤0.250 mg kg−1, their limit of detection), while they did not detect
any MAT, formononetin or biochanin A. In a study of coﬀee brews,
Thompson et al. (2006) detected daidzein (1 μg L−1), formononetin
(2 μg L−1), genistein (1 μg L−1), SECO (34–47 μg L−1), LARI (9–11 μg
L−1) and MAT (1–2 μg L−1). Kuhnle et al. (2008) analysed coﬀee
beverages reporting a SECO concentration of 100–160 μg L−1 whereas

tively. Caprioli et al. (2016) and Angeloni et al. (2018) reported the use
of HPLC-MS/MS to quantify, the ﬁrst, isoﬂavones in espresso and
ground coﬀees; the second lignans in espresso coﬀees. Caprioli et al.
(2016) detected biochanin A (0.59 to 3.95 μg L−1) and formononetin
(0.36 to 4.27 μg kg−1), whereas Angeloni et al. (2018) quantiﬁed SECO
(27.9–52.0 μg L−1) and LARI (5.3–27.8 μg L−1) content. A recent study
(Angeloni et al., 2019) reported the use of enzymatic hydrolysis cou-
pled with HPLC-MS/MS analysis for the quantiﬁcation of lignans in
ground coﬀee. They found that SECO (84.4–257.8 µg kg−1) was the
lignan in highest concentration followed by LARI (26.1–91.5 µg kg−1).
Summarising, diﬀerent studies have quantiﬁed isoﬂavones and lig-
nans in coﬀee powder and beverages but to the best of our knowledge,
none have quantiﬁed them in green coﬀee. Therefore, we sought to
develop a simple and eﬃcient method for quantifying three lignans
(SECO, LARI and MAT) and six isoﬂavones, two of them in glycosidic
form, (daidzin and genistin), and four aglycones (daidzein, genistein,
formononetin and biochanin A) in green coﬀee beans. HPLC-MS/MS
triple quadrupole, operating in multiple reaction monitoring (MRM)
mode, was chosen to separate, identify and quantify the target com-
pounds. Diﬀerent extraction processes were evaluated, and the recovery
and quantitative data were examined, as well. The best process was
chosen, validated and then applied to 25 green coﬀee samples from
many diﬀerent countries.
2. Material and methods
2.1. Chemicals and reagents
Isoﬂavones, namely, daidzin (analytical standard, C21H20O9, mole-
cular weight 416.38, CAS No 552-66-9), genistin (analytical standard,
≥98%, C21H20O10, molecular weight 432.38, CAS No 529-59-9),
daidzein (analytical standard, C15H10O4, molecular weight 254.24, CAS
No 486-66-8), genistein (analytical standard, C15H10O5, molecular
weight 270.24, CAS No 446-72-0), formononetin (analytical standard,
C16H12O4, molecular weight 268.26, CAS No 485-72-3) and biochanin
A (analytical standard, C16H12O5, molecular weight 284.26, CAS No
491-80-5) and lignans, namely, lariciresinol (LARI, ≥95.0%, C20H24O6,
molecular weight 360.40, CAS No 27003-73-2), matairesinol (MAT,
≥95.0%, C20H22O6, molecular weight 358.39, CAS No 580-72-3) and
secoisolariciresinol (SECO, ≥95.0%, C20H26O6, molecular weight
362.42, CAS No 29388-59-8) were purchased from Sigma Aldrich (St.
Louis, MO, USA). Individual stock solutions of each isoﬂavone and
lignan, at a concentration of 1000 mg L−1, were prepared by dissolving
pure standard compounds in HPLC grade methanol and storing them in
glass-stoppered bottles at 4 °C. Afterwards, standard working solutions
at various concentrations were prepared daily by appropriate dilution
of the stock solution with methanol. HPLC-grade acetonitrile and me-
thanol were supplied by Sigma-Aldrich (Milano, Italy). HPLC-grade
formic acid (99%) was obtained from Merck (Darmstadt, Germany).
Analytical-grade hydrochloric acid (37%) was obtained from Carlo Erba
Reagents (Milan, Italy). Ascorbic acid 99% was purchased from Sigma
Aldrich (Steinheim, Germany). All other chemicals were analytical
grade. Taka-diastase from Aspergillus oryzae (~100 U/mg) and clara-
diastase (≥35 U/mg) were purchased from Sigma Aldrich (St. Louis,
MO, USA). Standard Reference Materials® (SRM) 3238 ‘Soy-Containing
Solid Oral Dosage Form was purchased from Sigma Aldrich (Germany).
Deionized water (> 18 MΩ cm resistivity) was further puriﬁed using a
Milli-Q SP Reagent Water System (Millipore, Bedford, MA, USA). All
solvents and solutions were ﬁltered through a 0.2 µm polyamide ﬁlter
from Sartorius Stedim (Goettingen, Germany). Before HPLC analysis, all
samples were ﬁltered with Phenex™RC 4 mm 0.2 µm syringeless ﬁlter,
Phenomenex (Castelmaggiore, BO, Italy).
2.2. Coﬀee samples
Illycaﬀè SpA (Trieste, Italy) supplied twenty-four diﬀerent wet-
processed Coﬀea arabica green coﬀee samples having diﬀerent geo-
graphic origins, i.e., Rwanda (three commercial lots: samples 1, 2 and
3), Costa Rica (three commercial lots: samples 1, 2 and 3), Brazil (four
commercial lots: samples 1, 2, 3 and 4), Ethiopia (four commercial lots:
samples 1, 2, 3 and 4), India (four commercial lots: samples 1, 2, 3 and
4), Colombia (three commercial lots: samples 1, 2 and 3) and Nicaragua
(three commercial lots: samples 1, 2 and 3), Simonelli Group S.p.A.
(Belforte del Chienti, Italy) provided one 100% C. canephora (robusta)
green coﬀee sample from Bali (Indonesia).
2.3. Sample preparation and extraction procedures
Coﬀee samples were ground in an Ariete Blendy 570 grinder
(Florence, Italy). Homogeneous portions of 100% C. canephora sample
were used for development and optimization of the method.
We evaluated diﬀerent extraction procedures, namely, acid and
base hydrolysis, enzymatic digestions, organic solvent extraction and a
combination of these, as following described, and chose the best one for
lignan and isoﬂavone quantiﬁcation in diﬀerent green coﬀee samples.
The extraction conditions were developed and optimized taking as
point of departure previous article in ground and espresso coﬀee

2.3.1. Acid and base hydrolysis
Three diﬀerent extraction processes, namely acid hydrolysis, base
hydrolysis and base hydrolysis in MeOH, were chosen for their likely
capacity to break glycosidic and ester bonds (Mortensen et al., 2009;
Smeds and et al., 2007). We avoided to test very strong acid hydrolysis
since in previous works (Angeloni et al., 2019; Angeloni et al., 2018;
Caprioli et al., 2016) unsatisfactory recovery levels and lower extrac-
tion eﬃciencies were obtained. Acid hydrolysis (Method 1) was per-
formed by adding 6 mL of HCl 0.1 M and 60 mg of ascorbic acid (as
antioxidant) to 0.5 g of green coﬀee powder. The hydrolysis was per-
formed while keeping the sample at a controlled temperature of 80 °C
for 30 min in a water bath under magnetic stirring. The sample was
cooled at room temperature, its pH was adjusted to 5–6 by adding KOH
4 M, and then it was stored overnight at −18 °C to precipitate soluble
polysaccharide and protein fraction. Later, the sample was thawed at
room temperature and centrifuged at 5000 rpm for 10 min. Finally,
before HPLC-MS/MS injection, an aliquot of supernatant was collected,
centrifuged at 13,000 rpm for 15 min and ﬁltered with a 0.2 µm syr-
ingeless ﬁlter. Base hydrolysis (Method 2) was carried out by adding
3 mL of KOH 0.1 M and 30 mg of ascorbic acid to 0.3 g of green coﬀee
powder and keeping the sample at a controlled temperature of 80 °C for
30 min in a water bath under magnetic stirring. Afterwards, the sample
was cooled at room temperature, and the pH was brought to 5–6 by
adding HCl 2 M. After storage overnight at −18 °C, the sample was
melted, then centrifuged at 5000 rpm for 10 min, and an aliquot of
supernatant was collected and centrifuged at 13,000 rpm for 15 min.
Before analysis, the sample was ﬁltered with a 0.2 µm syringeless ﬁlter.
With the purpose of enhancing the extraction yield, we carried out
the same process of Method 2 but used KOH 0.1 M dissolved in MeOH
(Method 3) instead of water.
2.3.2. Enzymatic digestions
Two diﬀerent types of enzymes, a) taka-diastase, an α-amylase en-
zyme derived from Aspergillus oryzae, and b) clara-diastase, a mixture of
enzymes that containing α-amylase, cellulase, invertase, peptidase,
phosphatase and sulfatase, were evaluated for their ability to extract
the compounds of interest from the coﬀee matrix. The choice of en-
zymes and digestion conditions were deﬁned on the base of previous
studies (Angeloni et al., 2019; Angeloni et al., 2018). Hydrolysis with
taka-diastase (Method 4) was performed by adding 4 mL of enzyme
mixture at 2% (w/v) and 40 mg of ascorbic acid to 0.5 g of green coﬀee
powder, followed by digestion in a water bath at a controlled tem-
perature of 37 °C for 2 h, under magnetic stirring. After cooling at room
temperature, the sample was stored overnight at −18 °C and, after-
wards, was melted at room temperature and centrifuged at 5,000 rpm
for 10 min. Before HPLC analysis, an aliquot of the liquid part was
centrifuged at 13,000 rpm for 15 min and ﬁltered with a 0.2 µm syr-
ingeless ﬁlter.
Hydrolysis with clara-diastase (Method 5) was carried out adding
2 mL of enzyme at 5% (w/v) together with 30 mg of ascorbic acid to
0.3 g of green coﬀee powder, after which the sample was kept in a
water bath at a controlled temperature of 37 °C for 2 h, under magnetic
stirring. After cooling at room temperature, 1 mL of MeOH was added
and the sample was vortexed for 1 min. Afterwards, the sample was
stored overnight at −18 °C and, after melting at room temperature, it
was centrifuged at 5,000 for 10 min. Before injection into the HPLC-
MS/MS, an aliquot of supernatant was collected, centrifuged at
13,000 rpm for 15 min and ﬁltered with 0.2 µm syringeless ﬁlter.
2.3.3. Methanolic extraction
The methanolic process (Method 6) was structured in the same
manner as acid hydrolysis. Brieﬂy, 4 mL of a methanol:water solution
(85:15) together with 40 mg of ascorbic acid were added to 0.5 g of
green coﬀee powder and the sample was kept in a water bath at a
controlled temperature of 80 °C, for 30 min under magnetic stirring.
After storing overnight at −18 °C, the sample was kept at room tem-
perature to thaw it and then centrifuged at 5000 rpm for 10 min. Before
quantiﬁcation through HPLC-MS/MS, an aliquot of supernatant was
collected, centrifuged at 13,000 rpm for 15 min and ﬁltered with a
0.2 µm syringeless ﬁlter.
2.3.4. Combination processes
Two other processes, Method 7 and Method 8, combined the two
extractions previously tested. Method 7 was performed by adding 2 mL
of 0.1 M KOH dissolved in MeOH and 30 mg of ascorbic acid to 0.3 g of
green coﬀee powder and keeping the sample in a water bath at a
controlled temperature of 80 °C for 30 min. After cooling at room
temperature, the pH was adjusted to 6 by adding 2 M HCl and enzy-
matic digestion was carried out by adding 1 mL of clara-diastase 10%
(w/v) and then keeping the sample at 37 °C for 2 h. Afterwards, the
sample was centrifuged at 5000 rpm for 10 min and stored overnight at
−18 °C. Finally, after melting, an aliquot of supernatant was collected,
centrifuged at 13,000 rpm for 15 min, ﬁltered with a 0.2 µm syringeless
ﬁlter and injected into the HPLC.
Method 8 involved acid hydrolysis followed by enzymatic digestion.
The acid extraction was carried out adding 2 mL of 0.1 M HCl together
with 30 mg of ascorbic acid to 0.3 g of green coﬀee powder and keeping
the sample at 80 °C for 30 min. After cooling, the pH was raised to 6 by
adding 2 M KOH and after that, enzymatic digestion was performed, as
described above for Method 7.

2.4. HPLC-ESI-MS/MS
HPLC-MS/MS studies were performed using an Agilent 1290 Inﬁnity
series and a Triple Quadrupole 6420 from Agilent Technology (Santa
Clara, CA) equipped with an electrospray ionization (ESI) source op-
erating in negative and positive ionization mode as the instrument al-
lowed to perform the run with continuous positive/negative polarity
switching. The separation of target compounds was achieved on a
Kinetex C18 analytical column (50 mm × 2.10 mm i.d., 2.6 μm) from
Phenomenex (Castel Maggiore, Bologna, Italy). The mobile phase for
HPLC-MS/MS analyses was a mixture of 85% water (A) and 15% HPLC-
grade acetonitrile (B), both with 0.1% formic acid. The separation was
obtained with 0.4 mL/min with this gradient elution: 0 min (15% B),
5 min (40% B), 8 min (15% B) and then constant until the end of the
run (10 min). All solvents and solutions were ﬁltered through a 0.2 µm
polyamide ﬁlter from Sartorius Stedim (Goettingen, Germany). The
injection volume was 2 µL. The temperature of the column was 30 °C
and the temperature of the drying gas in the ionization source was
350 °C. The gas ﬂow was 11 L/min, the nebulizer pressure was 50 psi
and the capillary voltage was 4000 V. Detection was performed in the
“multiple reaction monitoring” (MRM) mode. The MRM peak areas
were integrated for quantiﬁcation. To enhance the sensitivity, the ac-
quisition time was divided into three periods. The most abundant
product ion was used for quantitation, and other two product ions were
used for qualiﬁcation. The selected ion transitions and the mass spec-
trometer parameters are reported in Table 1.
2.5. Principal component analysis (PCA)
Chemical diﬀerences among the various coﬀee samples, in terms of
lignan and isoﬂavone contents, were analysed by PCA using the sta-
tistical software STATISTICA v.7.1 (Stat Soft Italia S.r.L., Vigonza,
Italy). For the purpose, a covariance matrix composed of 25 coﬀee
samples and 8 variables (200 data) was built and eigenvalues were
calculated.

3. Results and discussion
3.1. Extraction procedures
The extraction procedure is considered one of the most important
stages of sample preparation in food analysis and should be developed
and optimized for each analyte in each food matrix (Mortensen et al.,
2009). Therefore, we evaluated the diﬀerent extraction methods – acid
(Method 1) and base hydrolysis (Methods 2 and 3), enzymatic diges-
tions (Methods 4 and 5), methanolic extraction (Method 6) and com-
bination processes (Methods 7 and 8) – for their ability to release bound
and free compounds from the coﬀee matrix. For each method, recovery
(R%) levels were calculated by spiking the matrix (C. canephora green
coﬀee) with authentic standard of analytes at two levels: 500 and
1000 µg kg−1 for lignans and 50 and 100 µg kg−1 for isoﬂavones. The
spiking levels were selected according to phytoestrogen content found
in espresso and ground coﬀee (Angeloni et al., 2019; Angeloni et al.,
2018; Caprioli et al., 2016) and also in green coﬀee used for method
validation.. Diﬀerent recovery experiments were performed by spiking
the matrix before (pre-spike) and after (post-spike) the extraction pro-
cess. Recoveries were expressed as percent of ratio between pre-spike
and post-spike samples; in this manner only the recovery contribution
was evaluated unhooking the data from the inﬂuence of matrix. Re-
covery was used to evaluate the best performing procedure together
with the extraction eﬃciency in term of total phytoestrogens content.
Table 2 shows the recoveries at one spiking level (500 µg kg−1 for
lignans and 50 µg kg−1 for isoﬂavones) since similar results were ob-
tained with the higher spiking level (1000 µg kg−1 for lignans and
100 µg kg−1 for isoﬂavones). Considering that isoﬂavones in soy pro-
ducts have been found to occur in an unconjugated form (or aglycone)
or in a conjugated form (glycoside, acetyl-glucoside and malonyl-glu-
coside) (Ren, Kuhn, Wegner, & Chen, 2001), and that lignans in ﬂax-
seed occur mainly in oligomer forms as glutaryl ester-linked oligomers
(Ford, Huang, Wang, Davin, & Lewis, 2001), we assumed that these
compounds might be present in green coﬀee beans in these forms as
well, and reasoned that acid or base hydrolysis might be useful for
breaking glycosidic and ester bonds. Thus, we evaluated the extraction
eﬃciency in acid and base conditions.
Method 1 (acid hydrolysis) showed good recoveries for secoisolar-
iciresinol (94%), matairesinol (86%), daidzin (77%) and genistin
(72%), but lower levels for biochanin A (28%), daidzein (53%), gen-
istein (54%) and formononetin (41%) and a total lack of lariciresinol,
probably because under acid conditions lariciresinol rearranges to
(+)-cyclolariciresinol (cLARI) (Angeloni et al., 2018; Smeds et al.,
2007).
Method 2 (hydrolysis in water) revealed low recovery levels for all
compounds, in contrast to base hydrolysis in MeOH (Method 3). The
latter process showed good recoveries for lariciresinol (75%), secoiso-
lariciresinol (104%), biochanin A (78%), genistein (79%) and for-
mononetin (82%) and slightly lower ones for matairesinol (68%),
daidzin (68%), genistin (61%) and daidzein (73%).

3.2. Optimization and validation of the HPLC-MS/MS analytical method
A new analytical method for simultaneous quantitation of three
lignans (secoisolariciresinol, lariciresinol and matairesinol) and six
isoﬂavones (daidzin, daidzein, genistin, genistein, formononetin and
biochanin A) was developed by using HPLC-MS/MS triple quadrupole.
For isoﬂavones in glycosidic form, the precursor ions were protonated
molecules [M + H]+ in positive polarity, whereas for the aglyconic
form and lignans the precursor ions were deprotonated molecules
[M−H]−in negative polarity (Caprioli et al., 2016; Angeloni et al.,
2018). The chromatographic separation was characterized by good
resolution and short run time, with the target phytoestrogens eluted
within 6 min (Table 1). As an example, Fig. 1 shows the HPLC-MS/MS
chromatogram of a standard mixture of the nine isoﬂavones and lignans
plotted as overlapped multiple reaction monitoring (MRM) transition of
each monitored compounds. Fig. 2 supplementary materials shows
HPLC-MS/MS chromatograms of analytes in a green coﬀee sample
(Rwanda 1) plotted as MRM transition. The studied validation para-
meters for the developed method were linearity, limit of detection
(LOD), limit of quantiﬁcation (LOQ), repeatability, accuracy, precision,
speciﬁcity and matrix eﬀects (Fig. 3 supplementary materials, Table 3,
Tables 2–4 supplementary materials).
Linearity was tested by injecting seven diﬀerent concentrations of
the three lignans and six isoﬂavones, in triplicate, then plotting and
calculating calibration curves with the respective determination coef-
ﬁcients (R2). All analytes showed good linearity, since the R2 equalled
or exceeded 0.9952.
LODs and LOQs were calculated by injecting gradually lower con-
centrations of standard compounds and measuring the signal-to-noise
ratio (SNR), using MassHunter Software from Agilent Technology
(Santa Clara, CA). The standard concentration with SNR = 3 was as-
signed to LOD, while that with SNR = 10 to LOQ. The method revealed
good sensitivity for both isoﬂavones and lignans, since the LODs ranged
from 0.1 to 5.0 µg L−1 and from 1.5 to 15.0 µg L−1, respectively, levels
lower than those reported by Kuhnle et al. (2008) and Horn-Ross et al.
(2000) but similar to the results of Alves et al. (2010) and Caprioli et al.
(2016).
Method repeatability was tested by injecting 5 replicates for each
standard concentration over the course of ﬁve days. The intra-day re-
peatability or run‐to‐run precision and inter-day repeatability or day‐-
to‐day precision were expressed by Relative Standard Deviation (RSD)
percent. The RSD ranged from 0.4 to 4.2% for run-to-run precision and
from 4.6 to 10.4% for day-to-day precision. Repeatability was also
evaluated by measuring the RSDs of recovery replicates studied for each

3.2.1. Accuracy and precision
The accuracy is deﬁned as the closeness of the agreement between
the result of a measurement and a true value of the measurand (Bipm,
Ifcc, & Iso, 2017). Several approaches have been suggested to evaluate
the accuracy of an analytical method and the main strategies currently
used are: a) measure the analyte in a particular reference materials and
comparing the results with the certiﬁed value; b) measure the analyte in
blank samples spiked with known analytical concentrations and de-
termining the percentage of recovery; c) compare the results from the
method under validation with those from a reference method; d) de-
termine the analytical concentration in the sample by means of the
standard addition technique (Araujo, 2009). In the current work ac-
curacy has been studies via the ﬁrst two approaches. As certiﬁed value
has been used that reported from a soy-product reference material
namely Standard Reference Materials® (SRM) 3238 Soy-Containing
Solid Oral Dosage Form. For recovery studies a green coﬀee matrix (C.
canephora) has been employed because it was not available the blank
matrix of green coﬀee. In the ﬁrst approach, accuracy was determined
by comparing the measured isoﬂavone contents applying the current
method with the reference values of the SRM 3238. Three homogeneous
portions of 300 mg for each packet of the unit of SRM 3238 (a unit of
SRM 3238 consists of 5 packets) were extracted following Method 7,
obtaining at the end 15 replicates (n = 15). Table 2 supplementary
materials reports measured isoﬂavone contents in the SRM 3238 and
the certiﬁed values. The measured values of isoﬂavones using the cur-
rent method was in agreement with data reported by SRM 3238. In
detail, as it is showed in Fig. 3 supplementary materials the con-
centration of daidzin (12556 ± 1968), genistin (13098 ± 832) and
genistein (116 ± 9) overlapped with the uncertainty interval on the
certiﬁed values. In the second approach, accuracy was calculated via
spike-recovery studies and it was determined using percentage relative
errors at three diﬀerent concentrations by comparing the true value of
the analyte in the sample (true concentration) with the values obtained
by analysis (measured concentration). As it can be seen from Table 3
supplementary materials, the % relative error for the standards are
between (−1.53)–(−14.76)% for the 9 diﬀerent analytes. The lowest
% relative error is (−1.53)% obtained for genistein at concentration
level of 700 µg kg−1, meanwhile the highest % relative error is
(−14.76)% obtained for SECO at concentration level of 100 µg kg−1.
The measure of precision usually is expressed in terms of impreci-
sion and computed as standard deviation of the test result. The preci-
sion of this method was determined by monitoring the RSD % of the
three replicates. As it can be seen from Table 3 supplementary mate-
rials, all the RSD % obtained are between 2.2 and 6.9% for the nine
analytes. The lowest RSD was 2.2% for the standard of daidzein at
concentration level of 700 µg kg−1.
3.2.2. Matrix eﬀects
The matrix eﬀect was evaluated via signal suppression/enhance-
ment (SSE). SSE (%) was calculated by dividing the slope of the matrix-
matched calibration curve by the slope of the calibration curve (in
solvent)
and
multiplying
by
100
(Sulyok,
Berthiller,
Krska,
&
Schuhmacher, 2006). In this manner was possible to have a more
truthful situation in real samples in a wide concentration range. The
matrix-matched calibration curve was prepared by adding certain
amounts of standards to extracted samples prepared by following the
chosen method, i.e., Method 7, and subtracting, for each concentration,
the content of analytes measured in the blank extract. The slope of the
calibration curve and matrix-matched calibration curve, both prepared
with seven concentrations of analytes (5–10,000 µg L−1), the respective
determination coeﬃcients (R2) and the SSE (%) for each analyte are
shown in Table 4 supplementary materials. An SSE (%) of 100 means no
matrix eﬀect, while values < 100 mean signal suppression and va-
lues > 100 indicate signal enhancement (Sagratini et al., 2012). Slight
signal enhancement was found for formononetin (SSE = 111.6%),
while other compounds showed marked signal suppression (from 22.7
to 75.9%). Those values could be due to the presence of interferences
that can reduce the ionization process by aﬀecting the analyte droplet
formation or evaporation as well as the amount of the analyte ions
formed in the gas phase that reaches the detector (Gosetti, Mazzucco,
Zampieri, & Gennaro, 2010). Matrix eﬀects results were taken into
account for the quantiﬁcation of lignans and isoﬂavones in 25 green
coﬀee samples; thus the measured concentration of each analyte was
corrected according (increased or decreased) to the obtained SSE%.
3.3. Green coﬀee samples
The new analytical method was applied to 24 diﬀerent 100% C.
arabica green coﬀees from diﬀerent geographical origins (Rwanda,
Costa Rica, Brazil, Ethiopia, India, Colombia and Nicaragua). Table 4
displays the concentration, expressed in µg kg−1, of the target phy-
toestrogens in the coﬀee samples. Quantitative analysis was performed
by comparing sample areas with those of a standard mixture. The
highest phytoestrogen concentration was found in the Ethiopian sam-
ples: sample 1 (8137.1 µg kg−1), followed by samples 4, 3 and 2
(7117.1 µg kg−1, 5128.2 µg kg−1 and 3703.8 µg kg−1, respectively).
SECO (172.6–5714.1 µg kg−1, with an average of 1335.1 µg kg−1) and
LARI (113.9–2417.7 µg kg−1, with an average of 615.1 µg kg−1) were
the most abundant compounds under study in green coﬀee. These two
lignans occur in various foodstuﬀs such as ﬂaxseed, soy products, cer-
eals, legumes, roasted coﬀee beans and coﬀee beverages (Thompson
et al., 2006; Milder, Arts, van de Putte, Venema, & Hollman, 2005;
Kuhnle et al., 2008). Our results on lignan content in green coﬀee are
similar to those reported by Mazur et al. (1998) who found a SECO
concentration from 3.93 to 7.16 mg kg−1 in roasted coﬀee. Lower levels
of lignans in coﬀee beverages were reported by Thompson et al. (2006)
and by Angeloni et al. (2018). The former used a SPE coupled with
GC–MS analysis and identiﬁed the following contents: SECO, from 34 to
47 µg L−1; LARI, from 9 to 11 µg L−1; MAT, from 1 to 2 µg L−1. The
latter used a HPLC-MS/MS triple quadrupole instrument and reported
concentrations for SECO and LARI from 27.9 to 52.0 μg L−1 and from
5.3 to 27.8 μg L−1, respectively.

3.4. PCA
PCA scores and loading plots of the various coﬀee samples analysed
are reported in Fig. 2. The variability of data was mostly explained by
the ﬁrst principal component (98.59%), which was mainly inﬂuenced
by the content of the two lignans SECO (values of eigenvectors: 1485;
62) and LARI (values of eigenvectors: 546; −170). Most of the samples
were close to each other in the score plot (central part of the plot), as
they were composed of similar amounts of phytoestrogens. However,
the Ethiopian coﬀee samples diﬀered signiﬁcantly from the others, with
higher contents of SECO and LARI, indicating that they are a good
source of lignans.
4. Conclusions
For the ﬁrst time, an analytical method for the simultaneous
quantitation of three lignans (secoisolariciresinol, matairesinol and
lariciresinol) and six isoﬂavones (genistin, daidzin, daidzein, genistein,
biochanin A and formononetin), in green coﬀee, has been developed.
The extraction eﬃciency of diﬀerent procedures was evaluated, and
recovery and total concentration of target compounds were studied as
well. The best performing process was Method 7, a double extraction
composed of base hydrolysis in MeOH and enzymatic digestion with
clara-diastase, since it showed good recovery levels, ranging from 74 to
94%, and the highest total concentration of all the compounds studied
(1193.4 µg kg−1). This method was validated and then applied to 25
diﬀerent green coﬀee samples. Lignans (286.5–8131.8 µg kg−1) were
more abundant than isoﬂavones (3.4–300.0 µg kg−1). This work pro-
vided new insight into two important phytonutrients (i.e. lignans and
isoﬂavones) of green coﬀee, and along with previous works (Angeloni
et al., 2019; Angeloni et al., 2018; Caprioli et al., 2016), allowed to
