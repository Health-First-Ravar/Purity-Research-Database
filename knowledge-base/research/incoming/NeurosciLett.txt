To  clarify  the  relationship
 between
 the  volatile
 compounds
 present
 in  roasted
 coffee
 beans
 and
 psycho-

 stress,
 we  investigated

 of  coffee  volatiles
 in
 mice
 using  a  variety
 of

 pharmacology
 methods.
 In  the  elevated
 plus-maze

 to  coffee
 volatiles
 increased
the
 time
 spent
 in  and  the  number
 of  entries
 into  the  open  arms

 increasing
 spontaneous
 locomo-

 sleep

 was  prolonged

 exposure.

 effects
were
 detected
 in  the  open-ﬁeld
 or  forced-swim
 tests.  These
 results  suggest
 that  coffee
 volatiles
 lower
the
 arousal
 level  and
 exert  anti-anxiety-like,
 stress-reducing
 effects  in  mice.

1.
 Introduction
Coffee
 is

 popular

 worldwide

 annual

 four

or

 [9].  It
 was
 also
 reported
 that
 increased
 relaxation
 or

 We  chose

 was

 into
 several

 (20

 ×
 31
 cm
 ×
 13
 cm,
 ca.
 80
 g/case)
 in
 a
 windowless
 test
room
 (3.8
 m
 ×
 2.8
 m
 ×
 2.4

 with
 the

 at
25
 ±
 1 ◦C.
 The
 total
 amounts
 of

 powder

 400
 g
(Coffee400

 1200
 g
 (Coffee1200

 Control
 animals
were
 maintained
 in
 the
 room
 for
 corresponding
 periods
 of
 time
without
 exposure
 to
 coffee
 volatiles
 (Control

2.3.
 Experimental
 procedures
Immediately
 after
 placing
 the
 coffee
 powder
 in
 the
 test
 room,
the
 mice
 were
 exposed
 to
 coffee
 volatiles
 for
 4
 h
 (10:00–14:00).
Then,
 all
 behavioral
 experiments
 were
 conducted
 in
 the
 same
 test
room
 in
 the
 presence
 of
 coffee
 volatiles
 (14:00–16:00).
 In
 each
behavioral
 experiment,
 the
 mice
 were
 used
 only
 once
 to
 avoid
habituation.
 All
 animal
 behaviors
 were
 recorded
 with
 a
 digital
 video
camera
 (DCR-SR100,
 Sony,
 Japan)
 and
 analyzed
 after
 completion
 of
the
 experiments.
 The
 experiment
 and
 data
 analysis
 were
 conducted
by
 different
 individuals
 to
 avoid
 experimenter
 bias.
 Namely,
 the
person
 scoring
 the
 behavior
 was
 blind
 to
 the
 treatment
 groups.
2.4.
 Elevated

The

 was

 and

like

 (open)

 (30

 (enclosed)

(30

 ×

 cm
 ×

 The

 and

white

 The

 measured
 5
 cm
 ×
 5
 cm.
The

 was
 elevated
 50
 cm

 ﬂoor
 and

 of
the

 420
 lx.
 At

 for

 and

 and

 In

 and

 the

 [19], was

 An

deﬁned

 The

ﬂoor

 and

 In

test,

in
 0.5%

elevated

 [21,27].
2.5.

 test
Open-ﬁeld

 was
 evaluated

 bucket-shaped
chamber
 (75-cm
 ﬂoor

 35-cm

 87-cm

 The

 and
 ﬂoor
 were

 of
 plastic
 and

 The
inner

 (45

 ﬂoor

 The

 ﬂoor

 400
 lx.
 Mice
 were
 gently
put

 the

 of
 the
 ﬂoor.
 Behavior

 for
 5
 min,
and
 the

 of

 (locomotor

 and

ing

 measured.
 The
 maze
 ﬂoor
 was
 cleaned
 with
 cotton
 and
ethanol
 between

2.6.

 sleep

Four
 hours

 were
 exposed
 to
 coffee
 volatiles,
pentobarbital-Na
 (Nacalai

 [13]. Animals
 were
gently

 onto

 ﬂoor

 Sleeping

was

 period
 between
 the
 loss
 of
 the
 righting
 reﬂex
and
 its
 return.
2.7.

The

 plas-
tic
 cylinders
 (10-cm

 20
 cm

 Mice

 cylinder

 6
 min.
 Immo-
bility

 6-min
 test
 period
 and
during
 the
 ﬁnal
 3
 min

2.8.

Initially,

using

Data

4.
 Discussion
The
 elevated
 plus-maze
 is
 the
 most
 widely
 used
 of
 all
 currently
available
 animal
 models
 of
 anxiety.
 As
 shown
 in
 Fig.
 1,
 the
 time
spent
 in
 the
 open
 arms
 and
 the
 number
 of
 open-arm
 entries
 of
control
 mice
 was
 notably
 low
 (2.1
 and
 3.6%
 of
 the
 total
 number
of
 entries,
 respectively).
 This
 indicates
 that
 the
 mice
 experienced
high
 levels
 of
 anxiety
 and
 were
 suitable
 for
 evaluating
 the
 pre-
sumed
 anxiolytic
 substances
 [12].
 After
 exposure
 to
 coffee
 volatiles,
the
 time
 spent
 in
 the
 open
 arms
 and
 number
 of
 open-arm
 entries
increased
 in
 a
 dose-dependent
 manner.
 These
 results
 were
 simi-
lar
 to
 those
 obtained
 using
 an
 anxiolytic
 dose
 of
 diazepam
 [21,27].
However,
 there
 were
 no
 differences
 in
 the
 total
 number
 of
 arm
entries.
 In
 addition,
 exploratory
 locomotor
 behaviors
 remained
unchanged
 in
 the
 open-ﬁeld
 test.
 These
 results
 suggest
 that
 cof-
fee
 volatiles
 have
 a
 moderate
 anxiolytic
 effect
 without
 affecting
normal
 motor
 activities.
The
 duration
 of
 pentobarbital-induced
 sleep,
 a
 useful
 measure
for
 evaluating
 the
 arousal
 level
 of
 experimental
 animals
 [16], is
prolonged
 by
 a
 variety
 of
 anxiolytic
 and
 hypnotic
 agents
 related
to
 gamma-aminobutyric
 acidA (GABAA)-benzodiazepine
 receptors
[3,7].
 The
 serotonergic
 system
 is
 also
 linked
 to
 the
 regulation
 of
pentobarbital-induced
 sleep
 time
 [4,29].
 Our
 results
 indicate
 that
exposure
 to
 coffee
 volatiles
 extended
 sleeping
 time
 and
 suggest
that
 the
 volatiles
 lowered
 the
 arousal
 level
 in
 the
 animals
 by
strengthening
 the
 pharmacological
 action
 of
 pentobarbital.
Some
 
plant
 
and
 
fruit
 
volatiles
 
reportedly
 
exert
 
an
antidepressant-like
 
action,
 
as
 
well
 
as
 
anxiolytic
 
effects,
 
in
rodents
 [10,14,26].
 Based
 on
 these
 reports,
 we  investigated
the
 antidepressant
 potential
 of
 coffee
 volatiles.
 Contrary
 to
 our
expectations,
 coffee
 volatiles
 did
 not
 exert
 antidepressant-like
effects,
 as
 demonstrated
 by
 the
 absence
 of
 changes
 in
 immobility
time
 of
 the
 forced-swimming
 test.
These
 data
 suggest
