ABSTRACT
Phosphorus (P) is one of the most important minerals required for plant growth occupying a
strong position among soil macro nutrients. Soil P deficiency is often fulfilled by phosphate fertil-
izers. P deficiency in soils is due to less total P contents in the soil and fixation of added P from
chemical fertilizers as well as other organic sources like manures. The response of plant under P
stress or even when it is present in adequate amount is very mild. The basic constraint in the
availability of P is its solubilization as it gets fixed both in acidic and alkaline soil. Soil fixed P can
only be solubilized by phosphate solubilizing microorganisms (PSMs).These bacteria released dif-
ferent types of organic acids in the soil which make P soluble and available to plants. The poten-
tial of these PSMs to solubilize P varies and mainly depends upon mechanism adopted for
solubilization, their molecular genetics as well as their ability to release P in soil. The PSMs, having
all the characteristics of phytohormone production, nitrogen fixation, as well as, heavy metal
decontamination and creating salt stress tolerance in plants, are quite rare for sustainable agricul-
ture. Application of this environment friendly approach for increasing crop productivity as well as
its impact on soil and plant health is discussed in this review which will not only open new ave-
nues of research but also provide fruitful information about phosphate solubilizing microbes for
sustainable agriculture development.

Introduction
Phosphorus (P) is an important nutrient for plant growth
and development which plays a vital role in its metabolic
activities and various functions. P is the major yield limiting
plant nutrient in arid and semi arid soils (Ahmad et al.
2018). Being the part of organic molecules like adenosine
diphosphate (ADP) and adenosine triphosphate (ATP), P
helps in energy transfer and storage. P also plays an efficient
role in plant photosynthesis, respiration, formation of cell
membrane, glycolysis and enzymes activities (Wu et al.
2005) showing that the growth and development of all crops
are dependent upon P availability (Wyngaard et al. 2016).
The presence of P as an integral part of nucleotides, phos-
pholipids,
phosphoproteins,
and
coenzymes
shows
its
importance for life (Havlin et al. 2005). In plants, it
increases root development, empowers stem, improves seed
formation, increases crop maturity and nitrogen fixation.
In soils, there are 170 different phosphate supplying min-
erals. Despite a considerable quantity of total P stock in the
soil, only a very little amount (<1%) is dissolved in it
(B€unemann 2015). P reacts both in acidic and alkaline soil
conditions and is present in three different forms like

Soluble P (readily extractable with water or weak acids),
labile soil P (chemically reactive in soil and water) and non-
labile soil P (unreactive) are also different forms of P in the
soil. The forms of P; primary orthophosphate (H2PO4)1
and secondary orthophosphate (HPO4)2 are necessary for
plant growth (Zhu et al. 2018). The primary orthophos-
phates are present in acidic soils while secondary orthophos-

plants is of great concern which creates stunted growth,
delay in crop maturity and seed formation.
P deficiency in soils is due to less total P contents in the
soil and fixation of added P from chemical fertilizers as well
as other organic sources like manures. There are many fac-
tors that create fluctuation of total P contents in the soil
which include particle size distribution, mineralogy of the
parent material, organic matter content and pH (Havlin
et al. 2005). However, soil P sorption and desorption cap-
acity, particle size distribution, metal oxides, and soil pH are
also the main factors regulating P dynamics in the soil (Li
et al. 2016). Zhang et al. (2002) reported that weathering
contributes to the transformations of P among different P

Dynamics and forms of P in soil
Plants uptake P from soil solution which is refilled by differ-
ent P pools in the soil like Al/Fe2 bound P or Ca bound P
(Saleque et al. 2004). As a result of the strong reaction of P
with soil colloid, diffusion role is minimized and mass flow
takes up the process (Hinsinger 2001). Soil pH is also
another factor which imparts great effects on P fixation and
on the availability of Fe, Al and Ca which assist in P fix-
ation. In alkaline soils, Ca2þ reacts with phosphate ions and
decreases P availability by forming different insoluble phos-
phate products. In acidic soil, precipitates of hydroxyl phos-
phates are formed by the reaction of Fe and Al with
phosphate ions which ultimately make the P unavailable to
the plants (Harris et al. 2006). The phosphate adsorption
capacity of soils is also very much affected by cation and
anions present on the exchange sites, however divalent cati-
ons (Ca2þ) are much attracted to P adsorption than mono-
valent (Naþ) cations in clays. In alkaline calcareous soils,
some of the applied P is taken up by the plants while most
of the P is fixed as calcium phosphate minerals (Sharif et al.
2011). The presence of clay minerals in soil has also an
important role for P supply (Mehdi et al. 2007). It is rather
difficult to estimate the proper kinetics involved in P fix-
ation or transformation because there may be numerous fac-
tors involved in it.
There are different forms of phosphate which exist in the
soil (i) solution P (ii) active P (iii) fixed P (Busman et al.
2009). A different form of P in the soil matrix is shown in

phates (Dash et al. 2017). Phyton is also the most prevalent
organophosphate in soil (a Ca–Mg salt of phytic acid).

P sources
Rock phosphate as P source
Rock phosphate is a term used for naturally occurring min-
erals embedded with a high concentration of P. The deposits
of rock phosphate (RP) in the world are in the form of sedi-
mentary and igneous rocks. Apatite is the main P bearing
mineral (Steven et al. 2013) which occurs as Hydroxyapatite
[Ca10 (PO4)6(OH)2] and Fluorapatite [Ca10(PO4)6F2] among
the world phosphate deposits (Tredwin et al. 2014). Large
deposits of rock phosphate are present in United States of
America, China and Morocco (Cordell et al. 2009), while
Sengul
et
al.
(2006)
reported
deposits
of
the
Upper
Cretaceous and Eocene ages of the Mediterranean phospho-
genic province in Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia, Egypt, Israel,
Jordan, Syria, Saudi Arabia, Turkey, and Iraq.
World rock phosphate reserves (Table 1) are 290 million
metric tons (Van-Kauwenbergh 2010). About 35 million
tons of rock phosphate reserves are present in the Hazara
area of Pakistan (Khan et al. 2012; Pakistan Mineral
Development Corporation 2006). These are widespread in
Tarnawai, Lagarban, Kakul, Thandiani areas in the form of
dolomite, orthoquartzites, and limestone (Javed et al. 2008).
The type of phosphate present in the rock phosphate is
insoluble in water. (Das 2005; Khan et al. 2012) which is
non exchangeable form. Plants can’t take up P directly from
it (Xuan et al. 2012). Rock phosphates are categorized on
the basis of total P2O5 contents in the minerals; low grade
ores, intermediate grade ores and high grade ores with
12–16% P2O5, 17–25% P2O5 and 26–35% P2O5 contents,
respectively (Sengul et al. 2006). Use of indigenous rock
phosphate is preferred rather importing costly fertilizers
(Van-Straaten 2002).
Direct application of RP to the soil depends on its
reactivity and dissolution in the soil. Dissolution of rock
phosphate depends upon different factors like soil pH, soil
cation exchange capacity, soil buffering capacity, soil texture,
P fixation capacity, calcium exchange capacity and microbial
community present in the soil (Barea et al. 2002; Bolland
et al. 2001; Sikora 2002). Yusdar et al. (2007) reported in
the soil incubation study that dissolution of rock phosphate
in acidic soils depends on soil texture (54%) > soil acidity
(43%) > fertilizer (3%) while fertilizer was not significant as
RP dissolution factor. The P retention capacity of soil pro-
vides a sink for H2PO4
 release from RP (Hanafi and Syers
1994). The presence of clay minerals is correlated with RP
dissolution and the rate of transformation of P nutrients in
acid soils (Kanabo and Gilkes 1988). Application of RP in
acid soil depends on its reactivity, composition of apatite
[Ca5(PO4)3(F,Cl,OH)] and its mesh size. Agronomic effi-
ciency can give a better understanding to assess a rock phos-
phate as a potential fertilizer for direct application. Most of
the RPs are reasonably suitable for direct use in acid soils
but unsatisfactory results with neutral to alkaline soils. The
alkaline calcareous soils of arid and semi-arid regions are
not suitable for RP direct application because of their high
pH, drought stress and low organic matter content (Al-
Oud 2011).

18% P2O5) and Nitrophos (NP ¼ 23% P2O5) are the com-
monly used chemical fertilizers as source of P in Pakistan
(Rashid 2005). Chemical fertilizers directly supply nutrients
to the soil with immediate effect to the plants. They are
quite high in nutrient contents and are easy to apply with
least labor cost. Nevertheless, the nutrient requirement of

Organic manures, composts, and P availability
Organic manures and composts are a good source of plant
available P (Nathan and Rhonda 2007). Due to the increas-
ing demand for safe and healthy food, environmental con-
cerns and sustainability of agriculture, the need for the use
of organic fertilizers is indispensable (Mehdi 2010). There
are plenty of organic manures like farmyard manure (FYM),
poultry manure, plant residues, organic wastes (sewage
sludge, biogenic waste, fruit and vegetable waste, de-inking
paper waste, etc.) which are added to the soil for nutrient
supply. In temperate zones of the world, soil degradation
can be improved by the addition of organic matter through
organic manures (Zayed and Motaal 2005). Increase in P
bioavailability in the soil is possible with the application of
different organic materials like composts, manures, rural
and urban biogenic wastes and crop residues (Gichangi
et al. 2009; Schefe et al. 2008; Takeda et al. 2009). Eghball
et al. (2002) and Sharpley and Moyer (2000) reported higher
P availability from manure and recorded 75, 90, and 91% P
availability from dairy manure, poultry litter, and swine
manure, respectively. Direct incorporation of fresh manure
(poultry litter and cow dung) may create pathogenicity
problem leading to diseases, carry bad odor and also con-
taminate water table (Chaudhry et al. 2013). Manures are
good to improve soil fertility but due to the low nutrient
status and high water contents, a huge quantity of organic
material for application to the soil is required which creates
an extra burden of labor and transportation expenses to the
poor farmer (Ibrahim et al. 2008). So, there is a need to
minimize the bulky nature of manures through decompos-
ition and humification process of composting.

properties, free of pathogens and phytotoxicity (Albrecht
et al. 2010) and due to high nutrient availability, it can be

Data pertaining to P availability from composts are scarce

Soil microbial biomass may also be useful to address the
problems of low P nutrition and least nutrient use efficiency
in the soils. Microbial biomass is a significant resource of P
in soil (Oberson et al. 2001). P fixation in the soils is pre-
vented through the mechanism of immobilization in which
microorganisms increase P bioavailability by keeping the P
in mineralizable form. Added or soil indigenous P is incor-
porated in microbes’ bodies and thus becomes temporarily
unavailable to the plants and stored in the organic form.
Contrary to that, the organic form of P is converted to inor-
ganic form with the help of microorganisms through the
continuous process of mineralization and immobilization.
Both the processes of mineralization or immobilization are
also dependent on the type of organic material added in the
soil. Immobilization of P will occur with the addition of
organic material having C/P ratio (>300) and the material
with low carbon: P (C/P) ratio (<200) will create P mineral-
ization (Brady and Weil 2008).
In soil, the exhaustion of carbon source creates microbial
turn over which occurs below 170 to 365 days depending
upon moisture and temperature of the medium and quality
and quantity of organic materials, (Kouno et al. 2001; Achat
et al. 2010). Microbial biomass carbon and P have different
turn over time. Mostly, microbial biomass P turnover is
faster than microbial biomass carbon because most part of
microbial carbon is located in the structural portion of
microbial biomass (cell wall) which takes a lot of time to be
decomposed. Microbial P exists mostly in nucleic acids and
a very small portion in cell walls (Oberson and Joner 2005).
The turnover time of microbial biomass carbon is 82 days
and microbial biomass P requires 37 days (Kouno et al.
2001). Microbial biomass in different soil ranges from
0.4–163 mg g1 soils (Achat et al. 2010).
Small amounts of P (1.4–4.7% of total soil P) are stored
in the microbial biomass (Malik et al. 2013). Garcıa-Gil
et al. (2000) applied municipal solid waste compost, cow
manure and mineral fertilizer to evaluate their effect on
enzyme activity and microbial biomass after 9 years of appli-
cation. It was recorded that municipal solid waste increased
microbial biomass and microbial activity which improve

Soil phosphatases are enzymes that catalyze the hydrolysis
of organic phosphate esters to orthophosphate, and thus
constitute an important link between biologically unavailable
and bio available P pools in the soil (Olander and Vitousek
2000; Saha et al. 2008). Phosphatase enzymes are categorized
as acid and alkaline phosphomonesterases depending on the
pH of soil. Acid phosphatases are found in low pH acid soils
whereas, alkaline phosphatases are found in alkaline soils
which take part to hydrolyze organic form of P to inorganic
P. Phosphatase enzymes play a vital role in hydrolyzing P

Meena and Biswas (2014) reported an increase in microbial

Conclusions
P availability in alkaline and acidic soils is a serious prob-
lem. To overcome the problem of P availability in soil,
inoculation of PSMs is the most promising and cost effective
tool. The PSMs are capable of P solubilization leading to its
increased availability to plants. Additionally, the PSMs also
help to restore the nutrient status and improve soil health.
The isolation and characterization of PSMs which are cap-
able of surviving in a variable environment are imperative.
The formulation of consortia by selecting compatible PGPRs
having variable characters is required to attain long term
benefits in agriculture productivity. Addition of organic
matter with bio inoculants is of paramount importance. One
of the ways in this regard is the selection of thermo tolerant
bacterial strains which have multifunctional plant growth
promoting characters. Bio inoculation of PSMs capable of P
solubilizing from low grade rock phosphate sources may act
as a workable option to attain good crop productivity. The
survival of PSMs in soil may be enhanced by creating com-
mercial products through carrier based inoculation with
greater shelf life. Characterization of efficient phosphate
solubilizing microbes through advanced techniques of meta-
genomics
are
required
to
be
explored
for
sustainable
agriculture.
