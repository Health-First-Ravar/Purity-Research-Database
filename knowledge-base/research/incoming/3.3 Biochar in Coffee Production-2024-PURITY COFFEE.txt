BIOCHAR-COFFEE LITERATURE REVIEW 
INTRODUCTION 
BIOCHAR HISTORY 
Since the dawn of civilization, agriculture has been in a constant state of evolution. The transition from 
nomadic hunting and gathering to settled farming marked a pivotal moment in human history. This shift 
not only changed how we lived but also how we interacted with the land around us. The success and 
strength of early civilizations often hinged on a crucial factor: the quality of their soil. Agricultural 
prosperity became the foundation upon which great societies were built. This was primarily due to 
favorable geolocation, climate conditions, or, in some cultures, blessings from the gods. However, in some 
cases, none of the above deemed the success of a civilization. Specific communities deliberately 
transformed and enhanced their soil to meet their needs. This early terraforming was not done through 
magic or divine intervention, but through biochar application.  
Biochar dates to 450 to 8000 BC, when ancient Amazonians enhanced the infertile soil of the Amazonian 
basin by creating “terra preta” or “Indian black earth” (Taylor, 2010). To this day, this “terra preta” is rich 
and full of minerals and nutrients. A mixture of charcoal, bones, broken pottery, manure, and compost 
was added to the soil to obtain this durable man-made soil.   
Similarly, on the other side of the world, early Japanese farms carried out a practice called “haigoe,” which 
consisted of farmers combining human waste with rice husk biochar and applying it to the fields before 
planting crops. (Tomczak, et al. 2020) 
This nutrient cycling method was also used by the Mayan civilisation who would mix charcoal with animal 
and human waste to reduce odor and the spread of germs, spreading it every so often in the fields to 
gradually improve soil fertility (Terra Preta, 2014). 
We can, therefore, see proof of forms of biochar present in our agricultural history, where soil 
management was centered around adding long-term wealth to the soil instead of only extracting wealth 
from the soil to grow plants. 
BIOCHAR AND COFFEE STATE OF USE AND HISTORY 
The use of biochar in coffee production started in the early 2000s. It is an area of interest that still requires 
significant research, but there is proof of coffee farms worldwide obtaining positive results from using 
biochar over the last 20 years. Most trials are in the Ithaka Institute for Carbon Intelligence & International 
Biochar Initiative paper (2018). Spent coffee grounds seem to have the most literature regarding coffee 
and biochar, but not necessarily for use on the coffee itself, but rather for the biochar feedstock  (what 
substances were used to create the biochar) for other agricultural products.

WHY USE BIOCHAR?  GENERAL APPLICATIONS 
However, with the impacts of global warming becoming increasingly apparent, interest in biochar has 
spiked as a potential solution to adapt farms to climate change, whether it is drought, flooding, or 
unsuitable soil; a way to transfer to more sustainable and regenerative methods; or a way to reduce the 
carbon footprint of a coffee farm and obtain carbon credits.  
Due to its characteristics and structure, biochar can increase the soil's nutrients, moisture, and diversity, 
resulting in heightened yields and improved crop immunity. Its stable structure enables carbon 
sequestration in the soil for centuries or millennia.   
 
A RECENT HISTORY OF FERTILISER USAGE 
After the Second World War, the world's agricultural practices shifted from small-scale production to a 
larger, more intensive scale to ensure that food security (especially in the West) would never again be 
threatened. This was achieved through the mass utilisation of synthetic fertilisers. In fact, according to 
World Bank data, “Fertiliser consumption has gone up from 71 kilograms per hectare of arable land in 
1976, to 136 kilograms in 2018. Due to their increasingly widespread use, fertilisers, especially nitrogen 
fertilisers, are in the top three expenses for farmers in the US, and farmers spent $24.4 billion on fertilisers 
in 2020”. However, up to 64% of the nitrogen fertiliser applied to coffee fields is lost due to leaching and 
volatilization with the plant only absorbing around 25%. This volatilization and leaching of synthetic 
fertilisers contributed to nitrous oxide being released into the atmosphere, causing atmospheric warming 
up to 200 times greater than carbon dioxide (CO2). Not to mention the pollution of the water table and 
surrounding aquatic environments.  
Fertiliser is a short-term replenishment of depleted nutrients in the soil. Due to the readily available 
synthetic nitrogen (N) introduced into the soil in fertiliser, plants reject symbiotic microbial relationships. 
This is one example of how fertilisers degrade the richness and biodiversity of the soil.  
Industrial fertilisers are becoming more costly as well. As of May 2022, fertiliser prices have risen 30% 
since the start of the year, following last year’s 80% surge. This is due to the inevitable and foreseeable 
decrease in the phosphorus supply on our earth. Projections show that these sources could run out in 50 
to 100 years. 
However, biochar can be used in agriculture as a soil amendment to solve problems caused by synthetic 
fertilisers and reduce our reliance upon these industrial compounds. Biochar and its combinations with 
other organic components will allow us to change our agricultural practices and mindset, adding long-
term wealth to the soil to grow our plants rather than extracting the wealth from the soil to grow our 
plants.

BIOCHAR: DEFINITIONS AND ATTRIBUTES 
DEFINITIONS:  WHAT EXACTLY IS BIOCHAR? 
 
“Biochar is carbonised organic material” (Draper, 2018, p.6).   
“It is a specific type of charcoal” (Carbon Gold, 2023).  
It is incredibly recalcitrant due to aromatic carbon structures caused by the 
anaerobic process that transforms biomass into biochar called pyrolysis. 
(Tomczyk et al., 2020).  
 “Biochar improves soil aggregation, enhances nutrient- and water-holding 
capacity, provides habitat for soil organisms, modulates microbial activity and 
biodiversity, and may stabilise soil organic carbon (SOC). Pyrolysis also yields 
biofuels in the form of combustible gases or oils. Biochar is thought to mitigate 
climate change by providing both renewable energy and a soil amendment that 
may significantly enhance net soil carbon (C) sequestration.” (Biochar and 
Sustainable Agriculture, 2018) 
The studies on biochar are extensive, ranging from its use in agriculture as a renewable resource to carbon 
credits and to a way of remediating soil and potentially purifying water. Many studies focus on how the 
temperature, feedstock, and pyrolysis duration influence biochar characteristics and its efficacy for 
specific uses.   
This literature review, however, is focused on coffee.  As we reference biochar on other crops, we do so 
with an eye on coffee and how the attributes may impact coffee production.

PYROLYSIS  
“Pyrolysis is an umbrella term for the many processes occurring during the 
thermal decomposition of organic materials at elevated temperatures in a 
sufficiently inert atmosphere. The processes lead to a de-volitation of the 
treated material where the material is split into a volatile (typically gaseous) 
reaction and a recalcitrant (typically solid) fraction. The word pyrolysis is 
composed of the Greek words for fire (pyro) and splitting (lysis).” (Thomsen, 
2022, p.8) 
Typically, pyrolysis occurs at a higher temperature than 350 °C; however, biochar production most 
commonly occurs around temperatures of 500-700 °C. Generally, for straw or woody feedstock, the 
product of the solid fraction (i.e., biochar) is between 15-40% of the end product of the pyrolysis. The 
volatile product (mix of hydrocarbon gases) is also around 15-40% of the overall end product, and the rest 
(around 30-70%) is bio-oil (a mixture of hydrocarbons). Volatile gas and bio-oil can be used as alternative 
and renewable sources of energy (Thomsen, 2022). 
Why must we submit biomass to a process of pyrolysis to obtain biochar?  
Most simply because the lack of oxygen reduces the oxidation of the carbon present in the biomass (Singh 
et al., 2020). The product residue has a higher carbon content, as carbon has not been released as carbon 
dioxide (CO2 ) (Tomczyk et al., 2020). In fact, 50% of the carbon of the biomass is retained in the end 
product of biochar, making carbon 50-90% of the overall weight of biochar (Shrestha et al., 2023). It is a 
stable reservoir for carbon separate from the atmosphere. Therefore, pyrolysis is not considered 
combustion but a thermal decomposition of organic material where the weak bond between molecules 
rearranges themselves to form new functional groups and characteristics.  e.g., carboxyl, lactone, lacteal, 
quinine, chromene, anhydride, phenol, ether, pyrone, pyridine, pyridine, and pyrrole (Mia et al., 2017; 
Tomczyk et al., 2020). 
Three major stages are considered during pyrolysis (Tomczyk et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2017).  
1. Pre-pyrolysis: (200 °C) 
Evaporation of moisture breaks bonds between the atoms within the molecules forming the 
biomass. These bonds rearrange, with the significant apparition of carboxyl (-COOH) and carbonyl 
(-CO) groups on the surface of the newly formed biochar particles. These function groups increase 
the CEC of a particle. 
2. Main pyrolysis: (200-500 °C) 
Decomposition of cellulose and hemicelluloses. More and more pores appear as a result.  
3. Formation of carbonaceous soil products (above 500 °C) 
      Degradation of lignin (discussed below) and other molecules with stronger chemical bonds.

THE MAIN ATTRIBUTES OF BIOCHAR  
PORES  
During pyrolysis, the dehydration of the organic matter in the feedstock causes longitudinal pores to form 
originating from the vascular bundles in the raw biomass (Lehmann et al., 2011). This causes biochar 
particles to have an extremely high specific surface area (SSA). Typically, the SSA of biochar made from 
coffee crop residues is 40.1 m2/g for coffee biochar produced at 500 °C (Al-Awadhi et al., 2022).  
The high specific surface area of biochar particles is what is responsible for the numerous beneficial 
attributes that are sought after in biochar: 
WATER ABSORPTION – SOLUTION FOR DROUGHT 
The porous composition of biochar, similar to that of a honeycomb, causes it to have the characteristics 
of a sponge. Research by Samsalee and Sothornvit (2021) in Thailand showed that biochar originating 
from spent coffee grounds had a water-holding capacity of 1.5 g water/g biochar. This is more efficient 
than rice husk biochar, which has only 1 g of water per 1g of biochar (Samsalee & Sothornvit, 2021).  
This heightened water-holding capacity is due to the many oxygen-containing functional groups on the 
surface of the biochar. These groups are hydrophilic, attracting H20 molecules through hydrogen bonding 
and dipole-dipole interactions (Fan et al., 2018). 
Coffee is a crop that has an extremely high water demand, with up to 1 m3 to 20 m3 of water required per 
ton of coffee produced (Tomczyk et al., 2020; Blinova et al.,2017). Biochar hydrophobic characteristics 
also enhance the water-holding capacity due to hydrophobic functional groups on its surface (Adhikari et 
al., 2023). This is a great solution for farmers struggling with drought as it retains water in the soil around 
the roots, reducing the need for irrigation and helping those with climatic, locational, or energy 
constraints. Furthermore, coffee is a crop that hates having waterlogged roots. The biochar acts as a 
sponge, absorbing the excess water from the plant's rhizosphere (Tomczyk et al., 2020). 
A paper by Situmeang et al. (2023b) also stated that after the use of biochar in the soil, there were fewer 
restrictions on root length in the coffee crop due to a heightened availability of water and nutrients in the 
rhizosphere (Kartika et al., 2021).

NUTRIENT ABSORPTION – SOLUTION FOR DEPLETED SOILS 
If we examine the chemistry of biochar, we can see that the functional groups of the biochar molecules 
are mostly those present below in Figure 1. However, the functional groups will change depending on the 
pyrolysis conditions.  The oxygen-containing functional groups, such as carboxyl and phenolic groups on 
the surface of biochar, can dissociate protons and carry negative charges, allowing them to attract and 
exchange cations such as calcium (Ca2+), magnesium (Mg2+), and potassium (K+) in the soil solution. All 
cations are essential for the growth and health of plants (Tomczyk et al., 2020). 
The negatively charged sites on biochar particles originate from carboxylate and phenolate functional 
groups, while the positive sites originate from oxonium groups (aromatic rings) (Tomczyk et al., 2020). 
 
 
Figure 1a. Biochar surface functional acidic groups (Tomczyk et al., 2020, p.197) 
 
 
Figure 1b. Biochar surface functional primary groups (Tomczyk et al., 2020, p.197) 
The surface functional groups of the biochar cause the heightened CEC (cation exchange capacity) 
characteristic that biochar contributes to the soil. For biochar, it is generally over 0.01 mol/kg. The highest 
CEC in coffee residues was 0.029 mol/kg at 450°C for spent coffee ground biochar. The CEC of a particle is 
its ability to attract and retain cations (positively charged ions) on the particle's surface. This is because 
the “surface groups can act as electron donors or electron acceptors, which leads to the formation of 
coexisting areas whose properties can range from acidic to basic and from hydrophilic to hydrophobic” 
(Amonette & Joseph, 2009; Tomczyk et al., 2020).  

 
Page | 10 
Coffee is primarily grown in tropical and humid environments around the equator. In these environments, 
fertiliser is the most inefficient due to heightened evaporation and leaching. “Roberts (2008) reported 
application efficiencies ranging between 20% and 40% for nitrogen, 10% and 30% for phosphorus, and 
between 20% and 60% for potassium. These low application efficiencies result in high costs for the 
producer and potential environmental contamination due to leaching of agrochemicals in nearby water 
bodies.” (Perret et al., 2012) 
We are interested in the high CEC content in biochar for two reasons. There is potential to: 
1) Increase fertiliser efficiency by absorbing more applied nutrients and slowing their release into 
the soil. The CEC of soil is one of the main factors determining a fertilizer's efficiency, whether 
synthetic or biological (Perret et al., 2012). The performance of an applied soil amendment is 
increased when applied with biochar, allowing farmers to reduce their reliance on synthetic 
fertilisers and turn towards soil amending practices such as compost and biological fertiliser. For 
those accustomed to conventional agriculture, these practices are often less effective than 
synthetic fertilisers.  
2) Amend and increase the fertility of depleted soil naturally. “In Africa, cropland showed a decrease 
in soil fertility with high depletion recorded for Nitrogen (22 kg/ha), phosphorus (2.5 kg/ha) and 
potassium (15 kg/ha) over the past 30 years in 37 countries” (Rogers et al., 2022. p.256). As we 
know, plants require cations in the soil to grow, such as ammonium, calcium, magnesium, and 
potassium. Biochar attracts and increases the levels of advantageous micronutrients, so when 
applied in the vicinity of the roots of a plant, it acts as a reservoir of essential minerals needed for 
the plant's growth and health.  
  
SOIL BIODIVERSITY 
Furthermore, revisiting the discussion in the introduction, the abundant use of synthetic fertilisers in 
agricultural products has decreased the amount of symbiotic microbial relationships in the soil. Biochar 
pores and the heightened moisture content are optimal for microbes to multiply and live. This is essential 
for soil diversity as they increase nutrition for plants, such as nitrogen from mycorrhizae fungi, and the 
microbes also serve as nutrition for protozoa, mites, nematodes and other soil biota (Tomczyk et al., 2020; 
Briones, 2014). 
Biochar improves soil structure, increases soil porosity and decreases soil aggregates. This allows more 
aeration and oxygenation of soil, consequently increasing the soil microbiota and, therefore, the 
nitrification process.  
Furthermore, due to the elevated CEC in the soil from biochar application, the soil can retain more (NH4+) 
ammonium ions. This provides a steady supply of nitrifying bacteria, ensuring a constant but steady 
release of nitrogen for the plant (Premalatha et al., 2023b).

SOIL PH 
Biochar has a very high to neutral pH from around 6 to 9 on the pH scale (Reed et al., 2017). This 
characteristic of pH is due to the formation of carbonates and the contents of inorganic alkalis  (Tomczyk 
et al., 2020; Ding et al., 2014). The ability of biochar particles to absorb the hydrogen ions and 
decarboxylation processes are the main factors in soil acidity neutralization. The biochar particles in soil 
are subject to gradual oxidation, leading to the production of functional groups containing oxygen (Rogers 
et al., 2022). As the pyrolysis temperature increases, more functional surface groups disappear due to 
dehydration (such as the acidic carboxyl group). The carboxyl group is an acidic functional group. 
Therefore, more and more basic functional groups are formed, causing the alkaline character of the 
biochar (Tomczyk et al., 2020; Al Wabel et al., 2013). 
According to FAO estimates, only 24% (3.90 Mha) of the earth's surface is potentially arable, most of which 
(2500 Mha) is composed of acidic soils (Rogers et al., 2022). Biochar reduces the acidity of an area of soil 
in the same way that ash is used as a liming agent; however, the difference here is that the effects will be 
observed for much longer than lime or ash.  
Al-Awadhi (2022b) found during trials on spent coffee grounds that coffee residue biochar was mildly 
alkaline with a pH of 7.5 to 10.6, increasing with pyrolysis temperature. In contrast, biochar from spent 
coffee grounds had a more acidic pH of 5.54.  
In summary, coffee biochar has more basic surface chemistry, higher aromaticity, a larger surface area, 
and a more developed porous structure than biochar from other common feedstocks like rice husk and 
corn cob. These unique properties make coffee biochar suitable for CO2 capture and soil amendment 
applications. 
 
CARBON SEQUESTRATION 
Globally over 3.5 billion tons of waste from agricultural processing is dumped or burnt each year. This 
causes a significant release of carbon dioxide (CO2 ), methane (𝐶𝐻₄) and nitrous oxide (N₂O), which has a 
21- to 310-times worse impact on global warming than CO2 alone (Waste and Recycling, 2024). 
Biochar has high thermal stability due to the aromatic carbon structure formed after high temperatures 
during pyrolysis. The unstable CO2 in biomass is converted into a stable material resistant to 
decomposition for over 150 years (Perret et al., 2012). Moreover, the denitrification process produces 
nitrous oxide and methane-oxidizing bacteria, which prevent the production of 𝐶𝐻₄ in the soil. These are 
equally promoted by adding biochar (Maeda et al., 2020; Efosa et al., 2023). Therefore, biochar is a way 
to absorb and store carbon in the soil and reduce the carbon, methane, and nitrous oxide emissions that 
would have otherwise been released into the atmosphere. 
Coffee cultivation has a relatively high carbon footprint (Van Rikxoort et al., 2014). A Costa Rican study 
calculated and studied carbon emissions. In a shade-grown polyculture coffee farm, 1.93 kg of CO2 
emissions were emitted for every kg of green coffee produced. Out of these emissions, 95% resulted from 

 
Page | 12 
the use of chemical fertilisers, highlighting once again that agriculture must reduce its reliance on 
synthetic fertilisers.  
This can be achieved with the help of biochar.  
However, interest in biochar has also spiked due to its effective use to sequester large amounts of stable 
carbon in the soil. It is incredibly efficient as it takes advantage of a natural process: how trees absorb and 
capture carbon in biochar.   
In the 1990s, a new form of trading arose: carbon credit trading. Carbon offsets and credits are a trading 
mechanism that allows entities to compensate for their greenhouse gas emissions by supporting projects 
that reduce, avoid, or remove emissions elsewhere. A carbon credit is a transferable financial instrument 
representing a reduction, avoidance, or removal of one ton of carbon dioxide or its equivalent. Since the 
1990s, it has seen dramatic growth over the last decade, specifically in the last few years. It is estimated 
to be valued at $50 billion by 2023 (The Spaventa Group, 2024). 
Biochar production and application can be used to buy and sell carbon credits, offsetting your carbon 
emissions and, ultimately, becoming carbon neutral. Net-Zero, a French biotech company, and ECOM, a 
global coffee trader, have started a project to use biochar to promote sustainable coffee production and 
the generation of carbon credits. They are installing a biochar factory in Brazil, where they expect to 
produce 4000 tons of biochar annually, removing 6000 tons of CO2 (ecomtrading.com, 2024). 
NH Investment & Securities Co., one of South Korea’s largest securities firms, is tapping into agricultural 
carbon offset credits as the sector becomes popular for corporate emitters. They estimated that biochar 
can sequester as much as 2 Gt of CO2 annually by 2050 for $30–$120 per ton of CO2. Biochar carbon credits 
are usually priced at $3 to $20+ per Mt CO2e. However, some biochar projects have sold credits for $110/t 
CO2e. In December 2023, a deal was closed between NH and 4EN, a Seoul-based biochar producer. NH 
and 4EN biochar agreement is forecast to generate 167,000 tons of carbon credits by 2030.  
Pyreg is a company based in Germany since 2009 that provides technology to remove carbon from the 
atmosphere using biochar crafted explicitly for the carbon trading market. They are based in various 
locations worldwide, including Austria, China, Germany, Sweden, Switzerland, and the USA, and are 
currently removing around 30,000 tonnes of C02 per year (Our Technology—PYREG GmbH, 2024).

HOW THE PARAMETERS OF PYROLYSIS AFFECT THE MAIN ATTRIBUTES OF BIOCHAR  
Before the biochar production, the nature of the chosen feedstock and the time and temperature of 
pyrolysis must be considered. These parameters significantly influence the structure and the 
physicochemical properties of the end residual biochar product.   
FEEDSTOCK  
The feedstock should be considered depending on the intended use of biochar. Firstly, it dramatically 
impacts the carbon content contained within the biochar. Carbonization is enhanced through the 
decomposition of lignocellulosic vascular bundles, causing the formation of aromatic C==C structures 
(Situ, G. et al., 2022). Therefore, more woody and vegetative biochar will have a higher carbon content 
than manure-based biochar. This means that if the intention of the biochar is carbon sequestration and 
carbon trading, it is more advisable to use a wood-based biochar.  
In a 2011 case study in Kenya, several crop residue wastes were transformed into biochar to analyze and 
compare their respective macro- and micronutrients.  
 
Figure 2. Macro and micronutrients of biochar are produced from different crop residues. (Draper, 2018, p.13) 
As we can see, coffee leaves had the highest percentage of nitrogen and magnesium among the other 
crops studied. As well as significantly higher amounts of iron, copper and calcium. When added to the soil, 
all these nutrients will be amended into the soil for use by the plant without being degraded, evaporated, 
or leached away (Draper, 2018; Alvum-Toll et al., 2011). 
 
Coffee production creates different byproducts with different properties.

When pruning and leaf litter are considered, the total weight of coffee residue waste exceeds that of the 
coffee cherry harvest (Draper, 2018). The farmer could valorise all this waste by turning it into biochar. 
Most importantly, as mentioned earlier, turning lignocellulosic waste into biochar reduces CO2 emissions.  
 
COFFEE PULP/HUSK  
 
WET PROCESSING OF COFFEE CHERRIES  
When coffee cherries are harvested, the pulp, the outer protective layer of the two beans, is left behind. 
Most often, this deskinning is carried out using a wet processing method. The wet pulp is exceptionally 
acidic with a high caffeine and tannin content. When left to decompose, it emits high volumes of CO2, 
nitrous oxide and methane into the atmosphere. It is not uncommon for producers to add coffee pulp to 
the soil to increase the amount of carbon and nutrients. However, by carbonising pulp, the emissions from 
the decomposition of this pulp are next to zero. Carbonization also prevents an increase of insects and 
pathogens in the areas where the coffee pulp would be decomposing.  
Biochar made from the coffee pulp at 500°C using a Pyreg reactor produces biochar with a relatively high 
potassium content and a high CEC compared to biochar made from other feedstocks. Therefore, the 
coffee farmer has a waste-valuing soil amendment.  
 
 

 
Page | 15 
DRY PROCESSING OF COFFEE CHERRIES 
Another method of removing the skins of coffee cherries is dry processing. This is when the cherries are 
sun-dried and then de-hulled. This waste is referred to as husks or hulls, which are often piled in mounds 
and burnt. This method also causes significant air pollution. It is estimated that for every ton of green 
coffee produced, 180 kg of husks are also produced (Blinova et al., 2017).  
It should be noted that husks from dry processing would be more suitable for transformation into biochar 
than pulp from wet processing due to their lower moisture content (below 14%). With wet pulp, the 
biomass must go through a drying process pre-pyrolysis. 
Coffee husk is rich in organic matter, with high carbon organic coffee husk up to 50.83%, which is more 
than 20% lignin; 1.27% total nitrogen; 2.46% Potassium; 0.06% Phosphorus; and a ratio of 
Carbon/Nitrogen of 40.02 (Situmeang et al., 2023b; Dzung et al., 2013). 
Equally, Gunarathne et al. (2020) stated that: “Biochar prepared in an enclosed space by pyrolysis at 500°C 
can contribute to the enhancement of physicochemical and biochemical reactions to reclaim acid salt-
contaminated soil” (Situmeang et al., 2023b, p.678) concerning coffee husks.

As mentioned earlier, the levels of lignocellulosic matter in the feedstock determine the pores and specific 
surface area of biochar.  
Lignin is a complex aromatic polymer that contributes significantly to biochar's thermal stability and final 
carbon content. It is a critical structural component in the support tissues of most plants, particularly in 
wood and bark. Feedstocks with a high lignin content have increased mesopores and micropores and, 
therefore, a high specific surface area due to a prolonged decomposition process. These pores, however, 
are irregular and variable. There is also more formation of aromatic ring clusters due to the elevated 
temperatures that lignin requires in order to decompose. This makes the biochar from a lignin-rich 
feedstock have a large absorption capacity due to its many pores and high SSA.  Furthermore, it has 
greater thermal stability (Alves et al., 2024).  
A high lignin content also results in a higher biochar yield due to lignin’s thermal resistance to degradation, 
resulting in a higher proportion of solid carbonaceous material (Amalina et al., 2022). 
Cellulose is a polysaccharide that decomposes at a lower temperature than lignin. This means that the 
pores are larger and more uniform. Feedstocks with high cellulose content typically have a lower fixed 

 
Page | 16 
carbon content but can have a higher crystallinity and specific surface when delignified. The crystallinity 
characteristic of biochar increases its mechanical strength and stability (Ban et al., 2023). There is also a 
pattern of lower yield with feedstocks with a high cellulose content. This is because cellulose starts to 
degrade at 229°C to 344°C into volatile compounds (Alves et al., 2024c). 
One final remark is that lignin has the broadest range of decomposition temperatures, making it the most 
thermally stable. Decomposition can occur from 160°C to 900°C.  
 
MUCILAGE  
Coffee mucilage is a sweet and sticky solution containing mostly polysaccharides found within the coffee 
cherry. During the fermentation stage, microorganisms break down the mucilage. It has a very high 
moisture content but a very low carbon content and is, therefore, not suitable as a feedstock. However, 
if it were used as a way to activate biochar during the composting stage, it could be a valuable innovation 
in coffee production.  
 
PARCHMENT  
Between drying and grading, the parchment, which contributes to 12% of the dry weight of the cherry, is 
removed (Drape, 2018; Schweikle et al., 2018). The parchment has a low moisture content and an elevated 
lignin content, making it an ideal feedstock for biochar purposes.  
 
CHAFF 
During roasting, the chaff, also known as the silver skin, is the last residue produced during the coffee 
production line. For every 120 tons of roasted coffee, 1 ton of chaff is also produced. This residue is very 
high in potassium, calcium, magnesium, sulfur, and phosphorus minerals. It equally has a high 
hydrophobicity and a low pH (Draper, 2018). Lots of research has been carried out to find ways to valorize 
this residue, such as turning it into green energy. 
 
SPENT COFFEE GROUNDS SPG 
To prepare a beverage from 1 ton of coffee beans, around 0.65 tons of coffee grounds are discarded (Liza 
et al., 2021; Al-Awadhi et al., 2022). The majority of research into biochar made from coffee has been 
developed using spent coffee grounds as a feedstock.  
What is particularly interesting about spent coffee ground biochar is that it has a heightened specific 
surface area (SSA) in comparison to other feedstock biochar. For example, spent coffee grounds pyrolyzed 
at 500 °C  created a biochar with a SSA of 40.1 𝑚2/𝑔 ( Islam et al., 2022) and at 300°C a SSA of 42.050 

 
Page | 17 
𝑚2/𝑔  (Chen et al., 2021). This is significantly elevated in composition to a biochar made from a selection 
of wood residues, which at 500°C created biochar with a SSA of 14.54 𝑚2/𝑔  (Kalina et al., 2022).  
Due to this elevated surface area, spent coffee ground biochar has been found to have increased 
purification and extraction capabilities compared to other forms of biochar. This is because the biochar's 
CEC is increased, causing more cations and anions absorbed from the surrounding area to be absorbed 
into it. 
Research has been developed to investigate the phytoextraction capabilities regarding purifying polluted 
soils and wastewater.  
In fact, research from Islam et al. (2022) proved that spent coffee grounds made into biochar at a 
temperature of 500°C  have been found to have the highest silver adsorption capacity of 49.0 mg/g with 
99.9% silver removal efficiency. This is because the surface of biochar is typically negatively charged, which 
allows it to attract and bind positively charged silver ions through electrostatic forces. Furthermore, the 
carboxyl (-COOH) and hydroxyl (-OH) groups can form complexes with silver ions, effectively trapping 
them.  
Another aspect that aids in the phytoremediation capacity of SCG biochar is that an elevated pH helps 
immobilize heavy metals, which is achieved by adding biochar into the soil (Kim et al., 2014). 
Moreover, research has been developed by Manariolis et al. (2014) on spent coffee group biochar’s 
capacity to purify and filter wastewater. As mentioned previously, coffee processing has a high water 
demand, with a lot of this water being discarded with an elevated pH (around 4) and many tannins, 
caffeine and organic coffee waste.  
This wastewater, which contains high concentrations of tannins and caffeine, can have detrimental 
impacts on the surrounding environment. However, it is equally rich in advantageous nutrients.  
In fact, Pradhan et al. (2020) found SCG to be one of the most effective materials among various plant 
growth media for removing organics from greywater.  
Hypothetically, biochar could be used as a filtering system in coffee processing. The wastewater discarded 
after wet pulping, washing, and fermentation can be purified by biochar created on the farm. This biochar 
could then be considered activated and charged by the nutrients it has acquired from the wastewater, 
especially if it is water from washing the beans post-fermentation.

YIELD ANALYSIS 
Research carried out by Al-Awadhi et al. (2022) on spent coffee ground biochar found that yield decreases 
as the temperature increases, as is observed in Figure 5, with a 70% reduction in yield between 300 and 
600°C. 
  
  
Figure 5. Graph representing the effect of the temperature of pyrolysis on the yield of biochar (SPG)(Al-Awadhi et al., 2022,

The reason for this decrease in yield is primarily because as the temperature increases, it promotes a more 
complete thermal decomposition and volitation of the biomass components. For example, lignin requires 
more elevated temperatures to decompose than other organic compounds. Pyrolysis breaks down the 
complex organic molecules into more simple compounds, often volatile, rather than remaining as solid 
biochar (Yaashikaa et al., 2020). However, these volatile compounds are not lost. They are what become 
the bio-oil and syngas, which can be used as green energy. For example, fast pyrolysis around 500°C can 
yield 60-70% bio-oil, with only 15-25% biochar (What Is Pyrolysis? 2021). 
Also, trials carried out by Domingues et al. (2020) investigated the CEC of weathered soils using coffee 
husk biochar. They found that “350°C was selected to create biochar with functional groups capable of 
generating negative charges; 450°C was chosen as an intermediate temperature that completely degrades 
hemicellulose and cellulose and still preserves some organic functional groups. In contrast, 750°C 
stabilizes C through aromatization and strongly reduces the functional groups that bolster CEC. With these 
assumptions, the contrasting effects of the pyrolysis temperature on CEC could be evaluated.” (p.3)

BIOCHAR PRODUCTION AND PYROLYSIS 
 
To obtain biochar, biomass must be subject to high temperatures in an inert atmosphere to prevent 
combustion; in other words, pyrolysis. This process, however, is a natural process occurring in nature all 
around the world. The first example is during forest fires, where temperatures often reach 500°C, and 
there is little oxygen due to the rapid combustion. This is why post-forest fire charcoal and biochar-type 
substances can be found in the soil. Another example is volcanic activity; here, there are very high 
temperatures from the magma and lava flow, and limited oxygen in underground magma chambers or 
within lava flows. Finally, lightning strikes cause rapid localised pyrolysis of the vegetation it hits.  
Below are the man-made structures ranging from small to large scale that have managed to capture and 
reproduce this pyrolysis process found in nature:

THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN BIOCHAR AND ASH  
There is common confusion about the difference between biochar and ash. Ash is an agricultural 
amendment that originates from slash-and-burn practices. Similarly to ash, these practices boost fertility 
for the next season before the soil reverts to normal each time there is a decrease in soil diversity.  
The difference in the nature and structure of biochar and ash originated from the different  parameters 
of thermal decomposition in which the biomass is subject to:  
- 
Biochar is produced after the thermal decomposition of a feedstock at temperatures between 
300-800°C in an inert atmosphere in the absence of oxygen. Biochar is, therefore, not created as 
a by-product of combustion (Smołka-Danielowska & Jabłońska, 2021). 
- 
Ash is produced as a by-product of combustion. During complete combustion, the biomass is 
burned in an oxygenated environment above temperatures of 500°C. During this chemical 
reaction, the carbon contained within the biomass is oxidized. This carbon leaves in the form of 
CO2, leaving behind the inorganic material (Smołka-Danielowska & Jabłońska, 2021). 
This is the first major differentiation between biochar and ash. Ash has a lower final carbon content as the 
carbon initially present is oxidised. Therefore, there are no or little aromatic C compounds in the 
composition of ash, giving it no carbon sequestration properties. Furthermore, this absence of aromatic 
C compounds is the reason why ash is significantly less stable than biochar (Tomczyk et al., 2020). Due to 
this lack of stability, it does not have the same characteristics as biochar in that it is able to stay in the soil 
for long periods while retaining and slowly releasing the nutrients it contains into the soil solution (Saha 
et al., 2013). 
Ash is rich in calcium, potassium, phosphorus, and magnesium, with a typical NPK ratio of 0-1-3. However, 
due to the lack of stability of ash, these nutrients are easily leached away. This means that ash needs to 
be applied at higher rates, similar to the other conventional liming methods, unlike biochar (Saha et 
al.,2013). 
The complete combustion results in ash having a less porous structure to biochar due to little organic 
material being left behind. This process leads to the formation of dense, crystalline structures with 
minimal porosity. The structure of ash, in fact, is predominantly composed of minerals such as alumino-

 
Page | 31 
silicates, calcium silicates, and iron oxides, which do not contribute to the formation of a porous structure 
(Linderoth et al., 2020), unlike the hemicellulose, cellulose, and lignin contained in biochar. We can see 
clearly below this difference in the nature of these two particles:  
This absence of porosity impacts the capacity of ash to retain water and nutrients in the soil in the same 
way that biochar does (An Introduction to Biochars and Their Uses in Agriculture,2019). In fact, fly ash,  
 
Figure 12. Comparison in structure of (left) biochar (Howard. L) and (right) ash (Scimat photos) 
which is commonly used as an agricultural amendment, has a low CEC of 12-20 meq/100g to 159 
meq/100g (Henmi, 1987) compared to biochar, which has a CEC that ranges from 27.80 to 292.63 
meq/100g (Ngo et al., 2023). Note: The CEC is expressed in milliequivalents per 100 grams (meq/100g) of 
soil. 
Ash is used in agriculture as a liming agent to raise soil pH. It usually has a very high pH of 10-13 due to 
the complete combustion of the biomass, resulting in ash-rich oxides (Reed et al., 2017). In fact, one study 
reported wood ash having a pH of 12.8 compared to 8.2 for biochar produced from the same feedstock 
(Reed et al., 2017). 
Ash use is an effective method practiced worldwide because it immediately adjusts the soil's pH and 
nutrient availability. However, like conventional and synthetic soil amendments, this is a “short-term fix” 
that will need to be reapplied multiple times without contributing any new or extra wealth to the soil's 
qualities and causing long-term detrimental impacts on the soil's capacity to self-regulate (Saha et al., 
2013). 
The use of ash and biochar as soil amendments targets very similar soil problems. Biochar, however, 
provides a long-term solution that also aims to regenerate the soil so that it can thrive without synthetic 
soil amendments each season.

ACTIVATION  
Biochar's nature is very similar to that of a sponge. It can hold and retain water and nutrients for long 
periods. The activation of biochar is essential if biochar is used for agricultural purposes. Firstly, because 
of this sponge-like capacity, there is a risk that un-activated biochar would initially deplete the natural 
surrounding nutrients in the soil and compete for nutrients with the surrounding plants, initially causing 
a decrease in the plant's health (biochar6.com). 
When the biochar is activated, the anion/cation exchange sites on the surface of the particles are filled, 
allowing nutrients in the soil solution to be absorbed and exchanged with those kept in the biochar. 
Without this step, there can be a negative phenomenon where the nutrients are locked in the biochar.  
Furthermore, at this step, the farmer can tailor the biochar according to the particular depleted nutrients 
in their soil. Different activation methods can also optimise biochar for specific applications, whether 
improving soil structure, water retention, or contaminant remediation.  
 
COMPOST ACTIVATION  
Mixing biochar with compost is one of the most common ways to activate biochar.  
Mixing biochar with compost improves its quality and activity. This process is called co-composed biochar 
(COMBI) (Antonangelo et al., 2021). 
The porous quality of the biochar provides a habitat for the microorganisms to live, increasing the 
moisture content of the compost and increasing aeration. The biochar also promotes microbial activity, 
balancing the pH and absorption due to its surface properties, ammonia (NH3 ), ammonium (NH₄⁺), and 
hydrogen sulfide (H₂S), which would otherwise cause inhibitory effects on microbial activities during 
compositing (Antonangelo et al., 2021). 
These factors all speed up the composting time and decrease the need for the compost to be rotated as 
often (Sanchez et al., 2017; Draper, 2018).  
Furthermore, during the fermentation of vegetative waste, especially coffee skins, there is a significant 
gas emission of CO2, N2O, CH4, and NH3 (Antonangelo et al., 2021). The addition of biochar reduces the 
emission of these gases. The reduction in CH4 emission is because suitable growing conditions are created 
for methanotrophs (consuming CH4) while undesirable for methanogens (producing CH4) (Vandecasteele 
et al., 2016). The increased absorption of NO3- makes the substrate less available for denitrifying bacteria, 
reducing the creation of N2O gases (López-Cano et al., 2016; Vandecasteele et al., 2016). 
Composting also changes the physicochemical properties of biochar. It is activated with the absorbed 
nutrients from the compost and incorporated with beneficial microbes. Sanchez et al. (2017), Draper 
(2018), and Antonangelo et al. (2021) reported that the functional groups and the CEC of the particles 
were enhanced (Khan et al., 2016; Prost et al., 2013). It was also noted, however, that migration of 
phosphorus (P), potassium (K), calcium (Ca), and magnesium (Mg) from composting materials to biochar 

 
Page | 33 
was much easier than that of heavy metals such as manganese (Mn), copper (Cu), zinc (Zn), and 
molybdenum (Mo), regardless of their concentrations (Khan et al., 2016).  
“Overall, COMBI has been seen to increase C, N, and P in soils and stabilise soil aggregates, as well as 
stimulate microorganisms” (Forján et al., 2018; Mackie et al., 2015; Novak et al., 2019; Oustriere et al.,

to improve soil properties (Sudita et al., 2021), reduce land degradation (Karim et al., 2020), and restore

THE IMPORTANCE OF BIOCHAR ACTIVATION WITH MICROORGANISMS. 
Research on the impact of biochar on heavy metals in soils, specifically lead, cadmium, and arsenic, 
showed two key findings:  1. Biochar alone does little to impact the levels of heavy metals, and 2. Biochar 
combined with certain bacterial species can effectively immobilize the metals in the soils.  
Based on the results of El-Naggar et. al. (2021), none of the biochar they applied had positive effects on 
reducing the mobilization of arsenic, cadmium, lead or zinc. They also commented that using pristine 
biochar, as they did,  to remediate soils with high levels of multiple contaminants is not recommended. 
Optimizing biochar properties by increasing surface area and functionality, and providing active sites for 
capturing potentially toxic elements is essential before it can be effectively used for multi-contaminant 
soil remediation. 
On the other hand, a study by Ji et. al. (2021) confirmed that mixed bacteria-loaded biochar is a viable 
technology for remediating heavy metal contamination in site soils. They loaded biochar with two types 
of bacteria combinations they called BCM and BCB (including mixes of Bacillus amyloliquefaciens, Bacillus 
cereus, Bacillus velezensis, and others). Results showed higher immobilization effects than raw biochar, 
indicating a favorable synergistic effect between biochar and mixed bacteria.  
Other studies have shown how important certain bacteria are in mitigating fusarium and leaf rust, and 
further field trials should be done with biochar and these bacteria to determine the effectiveness of 
combinations of them.

 APPLICATION RATIOS  
The literature contains many different propositions about the application ratios of biochar. What follows 
is a summary of the ratio quantities that appeared the most often and had the most advantageous effects 
on the plant itself.  
Generally, applying 10 to 20 tonnes of biochar per hectare is advised for best results. (Gao et al., 2021; 
Sudita et al., 2021). The research of Situmeang et al. (2023b) showed that “Utilisation of coffee husk waste 
as biochar with an application of 10 t ha-¹ gave the highest yield and significantly increased plant height, 
number of leaves, leaf area, wet weight, and dry weight of biomass per plant compared to no biochar. In 
the application of 10 tons of biochar per hectare, there was an increase of 13.22%, 28.65%, and 15.81% 
compared to no biochar, respectively in plant height, wet weight, and the highest dry weight of the total 
biomass per plant”.  
One of biochar's most significant attributes is its soil stability. However, many farmers decide to apply 
biochar multiple times to the soil.  
Francisco Javier Tamayo stated that biochar will impact the plants more if it is applied and introduced into 
the soil before the crop is planted. However, if the plant has already established roots in the soil, for 
example, with trials with stumped plants, it is better to apply them to the topsoil of the plant. Biochar, for 
the first couple of years, will only have a significant impact on the lateral roots of the plant, but over time, 
it will come into contact with more and more roots.  Another method is injecting the biochar so that it has 
contact with the plant's deeper root systems. However, this requires specific technology. 
This is why multiple biochar applications per year for crops are helpful. “Biochar loaded with beneficial 
microorganisms and nutrients increase the cationic exchange and moisture in the soil. This can be done 
once a year or once every few years but the benefit of doing so more often is to change the structure of 
the soil at a faster pace, increasing the production yield. In the end, the goal is to create a soil structured 
in a way that retains water, facilitates cationic exchange, and jeeps microorganisms and nutrients in the 
soil longer, while also supporting the weight of the plant.” 
However, is it possible to apply too much biochar?  
If the biochar is loaded, the issues that can occur are smaller than those of unactivated biochar. If a 
considerable quantity of unactivated biochar is added to the soil, it will absorb and trap a large amount of 
nutrients and moisture, inhibiting it from reaching the plants. If a large amount of activated biochar is 
incorporated, the amount of nutrients it will take from the soil will be much less in comparison and will 
not impact the plant's nutrition, as noted by coffee producer and biochar researcher Francisco Tamayo.

CASE STUDY 2 - HOW BIOCHAR CAN IMPROVE RESISTANCE AGAINST DISEASES. 
Using case studies, we will investigate the influence of biochar on the CEC, moisture, microbial activity, 
and pH, which influence plant resistance to diseases.   
LEAF RUST  
The B4SS project based in Peru conducts research to investigate biochar's influence on coffee yield and 
quality. They made biochar from green waste from a landfill. Their results showed that coffee bushes with 
biochar additions had greater resistance against roya fungus, commonly known as leaf rust (Draper, 2018).  

 
Page | 42 
 
Figure 13. Coffee leaf from a plant grown with biochar (left) and coffee leaf from a plant grown without biochar (right) in San Ramon, Peru. 
(Draper, 2018) 
Among other causes, nutrient management can prevent the spread of this fungus and its infection in a 
plant. Lack of nitrogen and phosphorus can increase susceptibility to leaf rust spread and infection. 
However, an excess of potassium can promote leaf rust spread (Draper, 2018). 
How does biochar help mitigate the spread and disinfection of leaf rust in coffee? This is because biochar 
increases and improves mineral nutrition for plants due to its increases in the CEC of the plant's 
rhizosphere.  We also know that biochar carries much nitrogen, increasing the plant's nitrogen uptake. 
This allows more nitrogen-based defense compounds to be built, increasing the plant's immunity to fight 
against the roya fungus.  
Furthermore, as we know, biochar increases soil microbial activity by promoting more positive microbes 
such as mycorrhizae and rhizobium, creating more competition and suppressing the spread of the roya 
fungus bacteria.  
 
FUSARIUM 
Although there are no concrete studies investigating the effect of biochar on fusarium, research has 
shown that fusarium thrives in more acidic soils, more specifically from a pH of 5-7 (ORITSEJAFOR & Plant 
Pathology Division, Nigerian Institute for Oil Palm Research (NIFOR), Benin City, Nigeria, 1986). Biochar 
can be used to raise the pH of soils over a long period to a general pH range of 6-9.  

 
Page | 43 
Cruz et al. (2019) investigated the correlation of soil pH to fusarium abundance in a soya bean crop. They 
found that no fusarium was found in the soil analysis above a pH of 8. Therefore, we could hypothesise 
that biochar, by creating a more alkaline soil, can reduce the apparition and spread of fusarium by 
reducing the optimal conditions for it to thrive.  
Also, microorganisms, such as those in the Trichoderma family, mycoparasites that attack and kill 
phytopathogens by coiling and destroying their hyphae, should be considered.  In combination with 
biochar made with caffeine-containing compounds, an even greater impact of microorganisms fighting 
fusarium has been shown by Sugiyama et. al. (2016). 
Not only would the absence of fusarium have a beneficial impact on the farmer because of the heightened 
health and immunity of the coffee plant, but also, in a paper discussing coffee and health by Ildi Revi, the 
fusarium’s presence in the coffee plant has an impact on the molecules present in the end product, 
impacting the end consumer:  
“Deoxynivalenol is a type of mycotoxin produced by Fusarium species of fungi, 
particularly Fusarium graminearum, but in coffee it is caused by F. xylarioides and is 
called “coffee wilt disease”. Species within the Fusarium genus are notorious for 
inhabiting soil and causing various crop diseases that have significant economic impacts, 
affecting a wide range of crops, particularly wheat, corn and oats. These fungi harm 
plants, humans, and animals, leading to various illnesses. A particularly hazardous trait 
of these fungi is their production of toxic fumonisins and trichothecenes, which can lead 
to a variety of symptoms, including nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, skin irritation, and in 
severe cases, immunosuppression and even death. Due to its potential health risks, there 
are regulatory limits for DON in food products to minimize exposure” (Revi, 2024) 
Another study researching the antifungal properties of biochar was carried out by Sehar (2017), who 
found that an “application of 4% woody biochar per ml of water was able to significantly reduce 
populations of the parasitic nematode Pratylenchus coffee in Petri dishes.”

was incorporated into the agricultural cycle.  
To evaluate how the biochar affected the soil, they took samples from two different sections of the soil and then 
evaluated the pH and the mineral composition: 
- 
the first category was top-soil samples from 0-20 cm deep (Dahal et al., 2018)

CONCLUSION  
 
Agriculture has been changing and evolving constantly since 12,000 years ago when humans shifted from hunting 
and gathering to settlements with domesticated plants. Each revolution significantly influenced our societies and 
relationship with food and its production. Since then, the intensity of farming has only increased, ending with the 
Green Revolution in the 1950s, where high performance and quantity were valued above all to ensure food security. 
This last revolution has resulted in dramatic consequences for the environment, notably soil depletion and pollution.  
At the moment, we are faced with a dilemma. Soil infertility is caused by a loss of biodiversity, rising temperatures, 
water shortages, and increased pathogens and pests due to disrupted ecosystems. All these factors require more 
energy and chemicals to solve; however, these solutions paradoxically may exacerbate the problems. 
 By 2050, we need to increase our food production capacity by 70%, but only 5% of available land is left to expand. 
In addition, the IPCC report shows that 800 gigatons of C02 must be removed for our planet to achieve net zero and 
keep global warming below 1.5 degrees.  
Biochar has the potential to respond to the many problems that agriculture and our planet are facing.  
Modern agriculture is experiencing a significant shift towards less intensive, more sustainable practices. This 
movement echoes historical farming methods that were inherently more harmonious with surrounding ecosystems 
and natural processes. For example, the use of biochar in agriculture emulates the ancient Amazonian practices of 
“terra preta.” Here, the notion of farming is entirely different. Contrary to depleting the soil resources to farm, there 
is also a thought of investing in the land's future productivity, adding long-term wealth to the soil.
