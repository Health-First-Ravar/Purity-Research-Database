Abstract 
20 
We developed a colorimetric sensor array (CSA) that is sensitive to highly contributory volatile compounds of 
21 
coffee aroma for discrimination of coffee samples roasted to different roast degrees. Strecker aldehydes and α-
22 
diketones were significantly higher for the medium roast than the other roast degrees. The development of 
23 
several sulfur compounds was pronounced in the medium-dark and dark roasts, except for dimethyl sulfide, 
24 
which was only detected in the light roast. The CSA method coupled with principal component analysis or 
25 
hierarchical cluster analysis successfully distinguished the roasted coffee samples according to roast degree. 
26 
Partial least squares regression results showed that the CSA responses were well-correlated with the 
27 
concentrations of volatile compounds in the coefficient of determination (rp
2) range of 0.686–0.955. These 
28 
results demonstrate that the CSA rapidly responded to coffee aroma compounds and was capable of predicting 
29 
coffee aroma development.

1. Introduction 
36 
The ultimate purpose of roasting coffee beans is to impart desired flavors for consumers by causing various 
37 
physical and chemical changes in the coffee beans. The color and characteristic flavor of coffee beans are 
38 
developed via a complex series of reactions involving precursors in green beans, such as the Maillard reaction, 
39 
Strecker degradation, breakdown of amino acids, and interactions between intermediate decomposition products 
40 
(Baggenstoss, Poisson, Kaegi, Perren, & Escher, 2008; Buffo & Cardelli-Freire, 2004; Wang & Lim, 2014). 
41 
Numerous studies have focused on roasting (e.g., roast degree and roasting time and temperature profiles) 
42 
because of the significant impact of roasting on the formation of several hundreds of chemical compounds 
43 
associated with coffee flavor that are directly linked to coffee quality (Baggenstoss, Poisson, Kaegi, Perren, & 
44 
Escher, 2008; Gloess, Vietri, Wieland, Smrke, Schönbächler, et al., 2014; Santos, Viegas, Páscoa, Ferreira, 
45 
Rangel, & Lopes, 2016; Sunarharum, Williams, & Smyth, 2014). Flavor is a combination of aroma, taste, 
46 
texture, and mouthfeel and chemesthesis or trigeminal sensations; among these, aroma is the most important 
47 
component, allowing consumers to recognize coffee flavors (Sunarharum, Williams, & Smyth, 2014). In this 
48 
regard, Bhumiratana, Adhikari, and Chambers (2011) found that a light roast gives sweet, cocoa, and nutty 
49 
aromas, whereas a dark roast produces burnt/acrid, ashy/sooty, sour, pungent, coffee, and roasted aromas. 
50 
Bhumiratana, Adhikari, and Chambers (2011); Schenker, Heinemann, Huber, Pompizzi, Perren, and Escher 
51 
(2002) reported that a more complex aroma profile was exhibited at a medium degree of roast, for which the 
52 
highest increase in the concentration of aroma compounds was observed. A previous study by Baggenstoss, 
53 
Poisson, Kaegi, Perren, and Escher (2008) showed that rapid roasting (high temperature-short time roasting) led 
54 
to considerable differences in aroma formation compared to slow roasting (low temperature-long time roasting). 
55 
Thus, it is important to develop an optimal roasting profile to satisfy consumer preferences while maintaining 
56 
the quality of coffee products. In addition, monitoring the quality of coffee product is required to meet consumer 
57 
expectations; a rapid analytical tool is needed to detect coffee flavor development during roasting and identify 
58 
different coffee products with different roasting profiles (Dorfner, Ferge, Yeretzian, Kettrup, & Zimmermann, 
59 
2004; Gloess, Vietri, Wieland, et al., 2014).  
60 
More than 800 volatile compounds have been identified in roasted coffees (Buffo & Cardelli-Freire, 2004; 
61 
Czerny, Mayer, & Grosch, 1999). Despite the complexity of coffee flavor, previous investigations revealed that 
62 

  
4 
 
as few as 20–30 volatile compounds are predominantly responsible for the characteristic coffee aroma (Blank, 
63 
Sen, & Grosch, 1992; Czerny, Mayer, & Grosch, 1999; Semmelroch, Laskawy, Blank, & Grosch, 1995). Sulfur 
64 
compounds such as sulfides and thiols, particularly 2-furfurylthiol, are the most important aroma compounds in 
65 
coffee because of their extremely low odor thresholds and concentrations, resulting in a greater olfactory 
66 
contribution to coffee flavor (Baggenstoss, Poisson, Kaegi, Perren, & Escher, 2008; Dulsat-Serra, Quintanilla-
67 
Casas, & Vichi, 2016; Sunarharum, Williams, & Smyth, 2014). Strecker aldehydes, such as methylpropanal, 2-
68 
methylbutanal, and 3-methylbutanal, were found to be potent compounds affecting coffee aroma (Czerny, Mayer, 
69 
& Grosch, 1999; Holscher & Steinhar, 1992). Czerny, Mayer, and Grosch (1999) through sensory analyses 
70 
determined that these Strecker aldehydes are responsible for malty notes. α-Dicarbonyls such as 2,3-butanedione 
71 
and 2,3-pentanedione have been proposed as indices for aroma freshness of coffee brews by Pérez-Martínez, 
72 
Sopelana, De Pena, and Cid (2008), who reported that decreases in these compounds in coffee brews was 
73 
dependent on storage period, which significantly affected several sensory notes.  
74 
The volatile compounds responsible for coffee aroma are typically investigated using various forms of 
75 
headspace sampling methods, mainly solid-phase microextraction (SPME) and gas chromatography (GC) 
76 
coupled to either mass spectrometry (MS) or flame ionization detector (FID) (Cheong, Tong, Ong, Liu, Curran, 
77 
& Yu, 2013; Lee, Cheong, Curran, Yu, & Liu, 2015; Yang, Liu, Liu, Degn, Munchow, & Fisk, 2016). GC-based 
78 
methods are the commonly used techniques, but component-by-component analysis has important limitations 
79 
and must be conducted by a skilled operator; the operations are also cumbersome and time-consuming. A 
80 
sensor-based method may overcome these limitations. In several studies, an electronic nose (E-nose) system was 
81 
used either to monitor and predict coffee roasting or to differentiate different coffee samples (Gardner, Shurmer, 
82 
& Tan, 1992; Romani, Cevoli, Fabbri, Alessandrini, & Dalla Rosa, 2012). Suslick, Feng, and Suslick (2010) 
83 
successfully applied a colorimetric sensor array (CSA) technique to differentiate between coffee samples. These 
84 
sensor-based methods are advantageous for discrimination of similar complex mixtures such as coffee because 
85 
they are faster, easier to use, and less costly compared to conventional analytical methods. Moreover, the CSA 
86 
technique is a promising analytical tool based on strong chemical interactions between a sensor and analytes, 
87 
including Brønsted and Lewis acid-base, hydrogen bonding, dipolar, and π–π interactions, rather than weak 
88 
interactions, including van der Waals and physical adsorption, on which E-nose is exclusively based (Askim, 
89 
Mahmoudi, & Suslick, 2013). In addition, the CSA technique successfully discriminated complex food matrices 
90 

  
5 
 
in beverages such as beers (Zhang, Bailey, & Suslick, 2006), soft drinks (Zhang & Suslick, 2007), and green 
91 
teas (Huo, Wu, Yang, Fa, Luo, & Hou, 2014). CSA-based methods have also shown potential for recognizing 
92 
changes in the volatile compositions of foodstuffs, such as those in vinegar during fermentation (Guan, Zhao, 
93 
Cai, Lin, Yao, & Sun, 2014), and in fish (Zaragozá, Fuentes, Fernández-Segovia, Vivancos, Rizo, Ros-Lis, et al., 
94 
2013), pork (Huang, Zou, Shi, Guo, Zhao, Zhang, et al., 2014), and cooked chicken during storage (Kim, Li, 
95 
Lim, Kang, & Park, 2016).  
96 
The aim of this study was to develop a simple and rapid CSA-based method to differentiate between coffee 
97 
samples roasted to different roast degrees. The array was fabricated using 2,4-dinitrophenylydrazine (DNPH) as 
98 
an aldehyde- and ketone-specific sensing material and 5,5′-dithiobis(2-nitrobenzoic acid) (DTNB) as a thiol- 
99 
and sulfide-specific sensing material so that the sensor array would be more sensitive to important coffee aroma 
100 
compounds, such as Strecker aldehydes, α-diketones, thiols, and sulfides, than to other chemical species in 
101 
roasted coffee. We demonstrated the potential of the CSA method to detect important volatile compounds known 
102 
as ‘key odorants’ by determining the good relationships between GC and CSA methods using partial least square 
103 
regression (PLSR).

2. Materials and methods 
106 
2.1. Sample preparation 
107 
Arabica green coffee beans, which were harvested in the Chiapas of Mexico at an altitude of 1300–1600 m and 
108 
processed by the washed method, were purchased from an importer. Coffee roasting was conducted using a 
109 
Hottop roaster (Model KN-8828P; Chang Yue Industrial Co., Tainan, Taiwan). Approximately 250 g green beans 
110 
were poured into the roasting chamber at 75 °C, as recommended by the manufacturer, and roasted using the 
111 
automatic roasting program. Once the temperature of the roasting chamber on the display reached 187 °C (light 
112 
roast), 202 °C (medium roast), 217 °C (medium-dark roast), or 225 °C (dark roast), the roasted coffee beans 
113 
were ejected from the roasting chamber into the cooling tray for automatic cooling for 5 min. The roasted beans 
114 
were cooled for an additional 30 min at room temperature (approximately 25 °C) and finely ground using a 
115 
coffee grinder (Model Super Jolly Electronic; Mazzer Luigi S.R.L., Venice, Italy) immediately before each 
116 

  
6 
 
usage. Roasting degrees were established based on roasting progress: light roast, the point at the beginning of 
117 
the first crack; medium roast, the point at the peak of the first crack; medium-dark roast, the point at the 
118 
beginning of the second crack; dark roast, the point at the end of the second crack.  
119 
 
120 
2.2. Color values, weight loss, and moisture content of the samples 
121 
The L*, a*, and b* values of the samples were analyzed using a chromameter (Model CR-400; Konica Minolta 
122 
Sensing, Inc., Tokyo, Japan) (Wang & Lim, 2014). L* indicates the lightness (0, black and 100, white). a* is 
123 
red/green coordinate (+, red and −, green) and b* is yellow/blue coordinate (+, yellow and −, blue). 
124 
Approximately 12 g of each ground roasted coffee sample were placed in a Petri dish (ø = 60 mm) and covered 
125 
with the lid of a Petri dish to level the surface. After removing each lid, the L*, a*, and b* values of different 
126 
three areas on the surface in the Petri dish were measured and averaged. The mean for each sample was obtained 
127 
from triplicate measurements. The weight loss of each sample was measured immediately after cooling and was 
128 
expressed as the mean obtained from triplicate measurements. The moisture content of the samples was 
129 
determined using a moisture analyzer (Model MX-50; A&D Co., Ltd., Seoul, Korea). The values were measured 
130 
seven times for each sample and averaged.  
131 
 
132 
2.3. Determination of volatile compounds  
133 
2.3.1. Extraction of volatile compounds by headspace solid-phase microextraction (HS-
134 
SPME) 
135 
Approximately 2.0 g of ground roasted coffee sample were transferred into a 20-mL headspace-vial containing a 
136 
magnetic stir bar and then 6 mL of saturated sodium chloride solution and 10 µL of fluorobenzene solution (50 
137 
mg/L in methanol) as an internal standard were added. The vial was tightly capped with a silicon septum and 
138 
allowed to equilibrate for 90 min at room temperature under constant agitation on a magnetic stirrer (Model MS-
139 
300HS; Misung Scientific Co., Ltd., Seoul, Korea). Next, an 85-µm Carboxen/polydimethylsiloxane 
140 
(CAR/PDMS) SPME fiber (Supelco, Bellefonte, PA) was exposed to the headspace of the vial for 20 min at 
141 

  
7 
 
room temperature under stirring. After extraction, the fiber was inserted into the injection port of the GC for 10 
142 
min at 230 °C (Akiyama, Murakami, Ohtani, Iwatsuki, Sotoyama, Wada, et al., 2003; Petisca, Pérez-Palacios, 
143 
Pinho, & Ferreira, 2014).  
144 
 
145 
2.3.2. Gas chromatography-flame ionization detection (GC-FID) 
146 
Analyses were performed on a model 7890 GC (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA) with a DB-WAX 
147 
column (60 m × 0.25 mm × 0.25 µm, Agilent Technologies). The flow of helium carrier gas was 1.0 mL/min and 
148 
split mode (10:1) was used. The oven temperature was maintained at 50 °C for 2 min and then increased at 
149 
5 °C/min until 230 °C. The detector was maintained at 250 °C.

To calculate the concentrations of the ten targeted volatile compounds, calibration curves were determined by

adding each mixed-solution of authentic volatile compounds of known concentration with the internal standard

to a deodorized coffee sample as a blank matrix (Du, Song, Baldwin, & Rouseff, 2015). The blank matrix was

obtained by pumping the volatile compounds in ground light-roasted coffee in a vacuum oven at room 
156 
temperature for 12 h. The mixed-authentic standard solutions, containing ranges of 233.5–7472.5 mg/L for 2-

methylpropanal, 601.6–9625.0 mg/L for 2,3-butanedione, 473.6–7577.5 mg/L for 3-methylbutanal, 238.6–

furfurylthiol, and 11.3–2901.6 mg/L for dimethyl trisulfide, were used to draw calibration curves considering

the actual concentrations in the coffee samples.

Approximately 2.0 g of the matrix and 6 mL of saturated NaCl solution were transferred into a 20-mL headspace 
163 
vial. Next, 10 µL of mixed-authentic standard solution and 10 µL of internal standard solution were added and 
164 
tightly capped with a silicon septum. For the blank test, there was no addition of a mixed-authentic standard 
165 
solution. As described in Section 2.3.1, analysis was conducted after volatile compound extraction after 90 min 
166 
of equilibration. The coefficient of determination (r2) for individual calibration curves was 0.9912–0.9995

2.4. Fabrication of CSA and data collection 
170 
2.4.1. Preparation of stock solutions 
171 
Stock solutions of (1) DNPH, (2) pH indicators, (3) polyethylene glycol (PEG)-400, and (4) DTNB were 
172 
prepared as follows:  
173 
(1) Approximately 1.0 g of DNPH was dissolved in a mixed solution of 3 mL of water and 10 mL of ethanol, 
174 
and then 5 mL of concentrated sulfuric acid were added dropwise during magnetic stirring. After 10 min of 
175 
stirring, the solution was filtered through a filter paper.  
176 
(2) Nine pH indicator solutions were prepared using thymol blue, cresol red, bromocresol purple, neutral red, 
177 
bromoxylenol blue, metanil yellow, bromothymol blue, bromophenol blue, and chlorophenol red. 
178 
Approximately 5 mg of each pH indicator were dissolved in 10 mL of ethanol.  
179 
(3) Approximately 2.0 g of PEG-400 were dissolved in ethanol and adjusted to a volume of 20 mL with ethanol.  
180 
(4) Approximately 5.0 mg of DTNB were dissolved in 5 mL of phosphate buffer (pH 7.0).  
181 
 
182 
2.4.2. Fabrication of CSA 
183 
Two classes of sensing materials were prepared.  
184 
The first class consisted of six sensing materials to detect carbonyl compounds, such as aldehydes and ketones. 
185 
One of the six pH indicator solutions, including thymol blue, cresol red, bromocresol purple, neutral red, 
186 
bromoxylenol blue, and metanil yellow, was added to a mixed solution of DNPH and PEG-400, so that the ratios 
187 
of pH indicator/DNPH/PEG-400 were 1:3:3.  
188 
The second class consisted of six sensing materials to detect sulfur compounds, such as thiols and sulfides. One 
189 
of the six pH indicator solutions, including bromothymol blue, cresol red, bromoxylenol blue, bromophenol blue, 
190 

  
9 
 
chlorophenol red, and metanil yellow, was added to a mixed solution of DTNB, PEG-400, and 0.1 N NaOH, so 
191 
that the ratios of pH indicator/DTNB/PEG-400/0.1 N NaOH were 1:3:3:1. To fabricate the 3 × 4 CSA, the 
192 
twelve sensing materials were printed on a polyvinylidene fluoride membrane using a micropipette. The array 
193 
was dried in a fume hood for 30 min and used for analysis. 
194 
 
195 
2.4.3. Data collection 
196 
As shown in Fig S1, an online monitoring system coupled with Color Analysis software was used (Kim, Li, Lim, 
197 
Kang, & Park, 2016). The CSA was placed in the reaction chamber and 10.0 g of sample were transferred to the 
198 
sample chamber. The sample was allowed to stand for 5 min under nitrogen gas flow at 300 mL/min towards A 
199 
to stabilize the array and equilibrate the headspace of the sample chamber. Next, two three-way valves were 
200 
concurrently turned towards B, and CSA images were immediately captured by a digital camera at 0.5-min 
201 
intervals for 4.5 min. Following analysis, all gas lines were purged with nitrogen gas at 300 mL/min for 
202 
subsequent analyses.  
203 
Color Analysis software was used to extract color values (R, red; G, green; and B, blue) from the captured CSA 
204 
images. Averaged color values were extracted from the center of each sensing spot in a collected image, and 
205 
then values of color change (∆R, Rf–Ri; ∆G, Gf–Gi; and ∆B, Bf–Bi) were obtained by subtracting the color 
206 
values of the initial image (Ri, Gi, and Bi) from the color values of the final images (Rf, Gf, and Bf). The reaction 
207 
time for CSA analysis was set to 4 min, at which the total Euclidean distances stabilized (Fig. S2).  
208 
To recognize color differences between samples with the naked eye, a color difference image of the array for 
209 
each sample was obtained by expanding the mean color change values in the spots of the first dye class from 0–
210 
20 to 0–255 and in the spots of the second dye class from 0–50 to 0–255 using PowerPoint version 2016 
211 
(Microsoft Co.). The mean color change values were obtained from six replicate CSA analyses.

2.5. Statistical analysis 
214 
One-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was conducted on the color values, weight loss, moisture content, and 
215 

  
10 
 
concentrations of volatile compounds, and Duncan’s multiple range test was applied when ANOVA revealed p < 
216 
0.05. The GC and CSA datasets (i.e., 4 sample groups × 6 replicates) were studied by principal component 
217 
analysis (PCA) and hierarchical analysis (HCA), not only to determine whether the samples could be classified 
218 
by different roast degrees but also to demonstrate the feasibility of the CSA method. Each trial for the GC and 
219 
CSA methods provided 10-dimensional (concentrations of 10 compounds) and 36-dimensional vectors (3 color 
220 
components × 12 sensing materials), and all data for the vector were used for statistical analyses. Partial least 
221 
squares regression (PLSR) was used to investigate the correlation between the independent variables (CSA color 
222 
responses of the sensing materials of the first class) and dependent variables (concentrations of carbonyl 
223 
compounds) and between the independent variables (CSA color responses of the sensing materials of the second 
224 
class) and dependent variables (concentrations of sulfur compounds). To avoid overfitting, the total of 24 
225 
samples was divided into 2 subsets; 12 samples (3 of 6 replicates for each roast degree) were assigned to a 
226 
calibration for modelling. The other 12 samples (3 of 6 replicates for each roast degree) were assigned to a 
227 
prediction set, to test the performance of the final model. The robustness of each established model was 
228 
evaluated by determining the coefficient of determination (r2), root mean square error of calibration (RMSEC), 
229 
and root mean square error of prediction (RMSEP) for each model. Significant differences between RMSEC and 
230 
RMSEP were determined by independent-sample t-test. All data were used without any further treatments.

3. Results and discussion 
233 
3.1. Color values, weight loss, and moisture content of roasted coffee samples 
234 
Table 1 shows the color values, weight loss, and moisture content of the samples. These parameters have been 
235 
proposed as indicators for determining an optimal roast degree, although there is no accepted definition for this 
236 
determination (Yang, Liu, Liu, Degn, Munchow, & Fisk, 2016). The L*, a*, and b* values of the samples 
237 
significantly decreased with roast degree, whereas weight loss increased depending on roast degree. These 
238 
trends were also observed in previous studies (Cho, Park, Kim, Kim, & Han, 2014; Wang & Lim, 2014). The 
239 
moisture contents of the samples ranged from 2.14 ± 0.37% to 3.27 ± 0.18%. The content of the samples 
240 
significantly decreased according to roast degree, but there was no significant difference between the medium-
241 
dark-roasted sample and dark-roasted sample. In general, the moisture content of green coffee beans decreases 
242 

  
11 
 
from approximately 8–12% to 2–3% after roasting (Gloess, Vietri, Wieland, Smrke, Schönbächler, López, et al., 
243 
2014; Schenker, Heinemann, Huber, Pompizzi, Perren, & Escher, 2002). We observed a similar tendency in our 
244 
roasted coffee samples.  
245 
 
246 
3.2. Discrimination of coffee samples roasted to different degrees by HS-SPME-GC-FID  
247 
The concentrations of volatile compounds in the samples are shown in Table 2, and chromatographic results are 
248 
shown in Fig. S3 and Fig. S4. Quantified compounds are regarded as potent aroma compounds in roasted coffee, 
249 
and can be also used either as markers for aroma freshness of coffee or as indicators for distinguishing coffees 
250 
of different origin (Holscher & Steinhart, 1992; Mayer, Czerny, & Grosch, 1999; Pérez-Martínez, Sopelana, De 
251 
Pena, & Cid, 2008). Although sulfur compounds are present at low levels in roasted coffee, these compounds 
252 
play an important role in coffee aroma because of their very low odor thresholds (Sunarharum, Williams, & 
253 
Smyth, 2014). Dimethyl sulfide is a marker for the aroma freshness of roasted coffee and may be derived from 
254 
the thermal degradation of methionine or S-methylmethionine during roasting (Baggenstoss, Poisson, Kaegi, 
255 
Perren, & Escher, 2008). Dimethyl sulfide was detected in the light-roasted coffee sample at a concentration of 
256 
0.10 ± 0.01 mg/kg, but was not detected in further roasted coffee samples. Dimethyl disulfide is considered an 
257 
oxidation product of methanethiol, and may be converted to dimethyl sulfide and dimethyl trisulfide through 
258 
disproportionation (Baggenstoss, Poisson, Kaegi, Perren, & Escher, 2008; Baggenstoss, Poisson, Luethi, Perren, 
259 
& Escher, 2007). The concentration of dimethyl disulfide in the coffee samples continuously increased during 
260 
roasting from 0.08 ± 0.00 mg/kg to 0.39 ± 0.02 mg/kg. Dimethyl trisulfide in the samples increased from 0.34 ± 
261 
0.01 mg/kg in the light roast to 0.49 ± 0.03 mg/kg in the medium roast. The concentration slightly decreased in 
262 
the medium-dark roast, but increased again in the dark roast. This biphasic behavior was also observed in a 
263 
study by Baggenstoss, Poisson, Kaegi, Perren, and Escher (2008), who reported the biphasic development 
264 
pattern of dimethyl sulfide during roasting, regardless of the roasting process used.  
265 
2-Fufurylthiol is considered one of the most important compounds because of its high contribution to the roasted 
266 
character of coffee flavor (Baggenstoss, Poisson, Kaegi, Perren, & Escher, 2008; Mayer, Czerny, & Grosch, 
267 
1999). The concentration of 2-furfurylthiol gradually increased from 0.50 ± 0.00 mg/kg in the light roast to 0.81 
268 
± 0.05 mg/kg in the medium-dark roast, and then slightly decreased to 0.75 ± 0.02 mg/kg in the dark roast. This 
269 

  
12 
 
tendency is slightly different from the results of Baggenstoss, Poisson, Kaegi, Perren, and Escher (2008), who 
270 
reported that 2-furfurylthiol concentration increased constantly during the roasting process, and a decrease in 
271 
concentration was only observed following excessive roasting. They suggested that the continuous increase in 2-
272 
furfurylthiol was attributed to the precursor abundance. However, Mayer, Czerny, and Grosch (1999) showed 
273 
that the concentration of 2-furfurylthiol depends on the coffee bean variety. In the study, 2-furfurylthiol in the 
274 
two varieties continuously increased during roasting, whereas the concentration in the other coffee variety 
275 
increased from the light roast to the medium roast, but decreased in the dark roast. Thus, coffee variety may 
276 
contribute to the formation of 2-furfurylthiol because its precursors may rely on coffee variety.  
277 
Methional may be produced from the precursor methionine through Strecker degradation (Mottram, 2007). β-
278 
Elimination of the compound results in the formation of methanethiol, which is related to the aroma freshness of 
279 
roasted coffee and can be further oxidized to dimethyl disulfide (Dorfner, Ferge, Yeretzian, Kettrup, & 
280 
Zimmermann, 2004). Methional was not detected in light- and medium-roasted samples, but was detected at a 
281 
concentration of 1.62 ± 0.17 mg/kg in the medium-dark-roasted sample and 2.36 ± 0.17 in the dark-roasted 
282 
sample.  
283 
2-Methylpropanal, 2-methylbutanal, and 3-methylbutanal are also produced through Strecker degradation from 
284 
the corresponding precursors, valine, isoleucine, and leucine (Mottram, 2007). The concentrations of the 
285 
aldehydes were higher in the light- and medium-roasted samples than in medium-dark- and dark-roasted 
286 
samples. In the medium roast, the aldehydes reached individual maximum concentrations of 17.52 ± 3.28 mg/kg 
287 
for 2-methylpropanal, 19.79 ± 3.23 mg/kg for 2-methylbutanal, and 10.91 ± 1.67 mg/kg for 3-methylbutanal. 
288 
When the roasting process was extended, these concentrations decreased. α-Diketones, such as 2,3-butanedione 
289 
and 2,3-pentanedione, are formed by a reaction between amino acids (e.g., glycine and alanine) and sugars (e.g., 
290 
glucose and mannose) in coffee beans during roasting, as well as by various possible reaction pathways 
291 
(Baggenstoss, Poisson, Kaegi, Perren, & Escher, 2008; Gaffney, Abelmann, Pierce, Glynn, Henshaw, McCarthy, 
292 
et al., 2015). These compounds increased rapidly and reached individual maximum concentrations of 21.96 ± 
293 
2.27 mg/kg for 2,3-butanedione and 25.64 ± 2.96 mg/kg for 2,3-pentanedione in the medium roast. After further 
294 
roasting, these values gradually decreased. Holscher and Steinhart (1992) showed that 2-methylpropanal, 3-
295 
methylbutanal, and 2-methylbutanal decreased gradually during roasting, whereas 2,3-butanedione and 2,3-
296 
pentanedione decreased dramatically after an initial increase during roasting. In contrast, 3-methylbutanal, 2,3-
297 

  
13 
 
butanedione, and 2,3-pentanedione in the roasted coffee samples increased up to individual maximum points, 
298 
and then decreased during roasting in a previous study (Baggenstoss, Poisson, Kaegi, Perren, & Escher, 2008). 
299 
The development patterns in the latter study were similar to results obtained in our study, while the results of the 
300 
former study were not.  
301 
Fig. 1a shows a two-dimensional score plot using the first two principal components (PCs) obtained from PCA 
302 
using concentrations of volatile compounds. PC1 and PC2 account for 55.03% and 35.26% of total variance, 
303 
respectively. The samples closely gathered in the plot according to roast degree and no overlapping was 
304 
observed. PC1 separated the samples into three cluster groups, i.e., dark roast, medium-dark roast, and a group 
305 
of light and medium roasts. The light and medium roasted coffee samples were not separated by PC1 according 
306 
to roast degree, but could be separated by PC2. In contrast, an HCA with the concentrations of volatile 
307 
compounds clearly distinguished the samples with different roast degrees without any mis-clustering (Fig. S5). 
308 
The clustering pattern was similar to the separation by PC2 rather than that by PC1. The PCA loading plot 
309 
presented in Fig. 1b shows the compounds that affected the grouping. The medium-dark- and dark-roasted 
310 
samples were positioned to the left plane on the score plot, indicating that separation was primarily affected by 
311 
four sulfur compounds, including dimethyl trisulfide, 2-furfurylthiol, dimethyl disulfide, and methional. The two 
312 
roasted samples positioned in the left plane on the score plot had higher concentrations for the four sulfur 
313 
compounds except for dimethyl sulfide, compared to those in the right plane on the score plot. This might affect 
314 
the separation of medium-dark- and dark-roasted samples from other samples. In contrast, the medium-roasted 
315 
coffee samples were positioned in the upper-right area of the score plot and close to the location of five carbonyl 
316 
compounds on the loading plot. This indicates that separation of the medium-roasted coffee samples was 
317 
responsible for the five carbonyl compounds. The location of dimethyl sulfide suggests that the compound 
318 
functions as a classifier of light-roasted coffee samples.  
319 
The different patterns for the samples according to roast degree on the PCA score plot suggests the development 
320 
pattern of aroma compounds during roasting. For the light roast, most compounds except for dimethyl sulfide 
321 
were less prominent. For the medium roast, the development of carbonyl compounds was remarkable. During 
322 
the roasting process, most of the sulfur compounds were noticeably developed. Based on these results, the 
323 
development of sensory characteristics may be expected, although this development depends on the odor-active 
324 
value for each compound in the coffee and masking by other more potent aroma compounds. The three Strecker 
325 

  
14 
 
aldehydes, 2-methylpropanal, 2-methylbutanal, and 3-methylbutanal, impart malty odor notes in roasted coffees 
326 
(Czerny, Mayer, & Grosch, 1999). The two α-diketones, 2,3-butanedione and 2,3-pentanedion, impart buttery 
327 
odor notes and may significantly contribute to the aroma of coffee brews with the above Strecker aldehydes 
328 
(Blank, Sen, & Grosch, 1992; Mayer, Czerny, & Grosch, 2000). This indicates that in the medium roast, 
329 
malty/buttery odor note is more perceptible than in other roast degrees in our study. 2-Furfurylthiol gives potent 
330 
sulfurous/roasty odor notes and plays an important role in coffee aroma (Czerny, Mayer, & Grosch, 1999). A 
331 
cooked potato-like odor note in roasted coffees is likely generated by methional, and its high impact on the 
332 
aroma of coffee brews has been demonstrated in other studies (Czerny, Mayer, & Grosch, 1999; Mayer, Czerny, 
333 
& Grosch, 2000). Individual sulfur compounds can develop the overall coffee flavor when presented with other 
334 
sulfur compounds highly associated with coffee aroma (Mayer, Czerny, & Grosch, 2000). These results suggest 
335 
that in medium-dark and dark roasts, sulfurous/roasty odor notes may be easily perceptible compared to those in 
336 
other roast degrees of coffee in our study.  
337 
Although we monitored 10 important volatile compounds that contribute to coffee aroma, discrimination of the 
338 
roasted coffee samples according to roast degree was successfully performed using GC data. The results showed 
339 
that the composition of volatile compounds of Mexican Arabica coffee was highly dependent on roast degree, 
340 
resulting in significant differences between roasted coffee samples. This suggests that the 10 volatile compounds 
341 
are good indicators for evaluating the roast degree of Mexican Arabica coffee. 
342

3.3. Discrimination of coffee samples roasted to different roast degrees by CSA  
344 
Fig. 2 shows the difference images of roasted coffee samples, and original images are shown in Fig. S6. The 
345 
upper 6 sensing spots belong to the first class targeting aldehydes and ketones, while the lower 6 spots belong to 
346 
the second class targeting thiols and sulfides. The individual difference images show a unique pattern and can be 
347 
used to distinguish the samples according to roast degree with the naked eye. These distinctive patterns indicate 
348 
that the CSA correctly recognized the development of coffee aroma during roasting and, thus, can be used to 
349 
differentiate the samples.  
350 
Unlike GC-based methods, which enable component-by-component analysis, the CSA method is based on non-
351 

  
15 
 
specific sensitivity and cross-reactivity to a variety of volatile compounds in a sample. This is because a sensing 
352 
material of an array is simultaneously responsive to numerous analytes, and an analyte is simultaneously 
353 
responsive to all sensing materials of the array, making it difficult to relate a color change pattern with a specific 
354 
volatile compound (Li, Chen, Zhao, & Ouyang, 2014). However, our sensor array may be sensitive to specific 
355 
chemical species, such as aldehydes, ketones, thiols, and sulfides, because of the specific reactions induced by 
356 
the sensing materials used, such as DNPH and DTNB. Thus, color responses may be mainly induced by the 
357 
aforementioned specific chemical species, although the sensor array may still be simultaneously responsive to 
358 
numerous volatile compounds in the samples. The CSA responses were dynamic over 1 min and became nearly 
359 
stabilized within 5 min (Fig. S2). The sensing materials belonging to the second class showed more rapid and 
360 
dynamic color changes compared to those belonging to the first class, as observed in Fig. 2.  
361 
Fig. 3 shows a 2-dimensional score plot using the first two PCs obtained from PCA using the CSA results. PC1 
362 
and PC2 explain 66.75% and 12.32% of the total variance, respectively. The samples clearly clustered according 
363 
to roast degree without overlapping. They were separated by PC1 into three clusters, i.e., light roasted coffee, 
364 
medium roasted coffee, and medium-dark- and dark-roasted coffees. The light roasted samples were positioned 
365 
farthest away from the other samples. PC1 did not distinctly separate the medium-dark-roasted and dark-roasted 
366 
samples into each group, while PC2 showed good separation abilities for the two sample groups. In contrast, all 
367 
samples clearly clustered according to roast degree without any mis-clustering by HCA using the CSA results 
368 
(Fig. S7). In the HCA dendrogram, the last clustering included the light-roasted sample and a prior cluster. This 
369 
clustering pattern was similar to the pattern observed after separation by PC1 than by PC2 (Fig 3). These results 
370 
suggest that the CSA could identify the samples depending on roast degree.  
371

3.4. Correlations between the results obtained with HS-SPME-GC-FID and CSA 
373 
We demonstrated that both the HS-SPME-GC-FID and CSA methods could successfully discriminate coffee 
374 
samples roasted to different roast degrees. We also conducted PLSR to determine whether the CSA could predict 
375 
important aroma compounds in the roasted coffee samples and whether it can be used as a reliable alternative to 
376 
conventional methods. In general, in a PLSR model, the coefficient of determination and root mean square error 
377 
relate to accuracy and precision, respectively (Zaragozá, et al., 2013). Good accuracy indicates that the 
378 

  
16 
 
coefficient of determination is close to 1, while good precision indicates that root mean square error is close to 0 
379 
(Zaragozá, et al., 2013). As shown in Fig. 4 and Fig. S8, there was no significant difference between the 
380 
calibration and prediction sets. In addition, no significant difference between RMSEC and RMSEP was 
381 
observed in each model. This indicates that the samples assigned into the calibration set can be used to assess 
382 
the robustness of the final model. Most PLSR models show acceptable r2 values in both calibration and 
383 
prediction sets, and several models with higher r2 are shown in Fig. 4. Among the PLSR models for carbonyl 
384 
compounds, the models for 2-methylpropanal (rc

2 = 0.927), 2,3-butanedione (rc

385

2 = 0.954, rp

Among the PLSR models for sulfur compounds, the models for dimethyl sulfide (rc

than other sulfur compounds. Several prediction models using CSA data for food quality indices were also 
389 
successful in other studies (Huang, et al., 2014; Zaragozá, et al., 2013). Huang, et al. (2014) applied a CSA to

399

401 
4. Conclusions 
402 
We proposed a CSA method for the rapid and simple detection of key compounds contributing to coffee aroma. 
403 
Coffees roasted to different roast degrees were successfully discriminated by PCA and HCA using either CSA or 
404 
HS-SPME-GC-FID results. The PLSR results showed that the CSA responses were quantitatively correlated, 
405 

  
17 
 
with the concentrations of volatile compounds in an acceptable r2 range of 0.686–0.955. These results 
406 
demonstrate that the CSA method coupled with multivariate statistical analysis could clearly differentiate the 
407 
roasted coffee samples according to roast degree and could well predict the development of important coffee 
408 
aroma compounds. Therefore, the CSA is proposed as an alternative analytical tool to conventional methods, 
409 
such as GC and E-nose, for rapid discrimination of different roasted coffee samples. Moreover, this CSA method 
410 
may be useful for quality control of coffee products, development of optimal roasting processes, and evaluation 
411 
of coffee aroma.
