Role of hydroxycinnamates in coffee melanoidin formation
Fernando M. Nunes • Manuel A. Coimbra
Received: 21 February 2009 / Accepted: 24 September 2009 / Published online: 30 October 2009
 Springer Science+Business Media B.V. 2009
Abstract
Melanoidins
are
the
high
molecular
weight brown end products of the Maillard reaction.
They are formed during heat processing of foods like
coffee, bread, malt, and beef. A chemical deﬁnition
of these food polymers is still impossible, despite
several efforts to determine their structure. In the last
years, the interest in research on melanoidins has
increased due to their biological activities. Coffee
brew is one of the main sources of melanoidins in
human diet. Various melanoidin fractions
were
obtained by applying chromatographic separation
techniques or speciﬁc isolation procedures, allowing,
a partial view on structural features and diversity of
coffee brew melanoidins. Different melanoidin pop-
ulations can be found with respect to total carbohy-
drate contents and their structural features. In this
paper, the recent advances in research on coffee
melanoidin structures and formation mechanisms are
reviewed. The participation of hydroxycinnamates in
melanoidin formation, especially true for coffee
melanoidins, is a hypothesis older than three decades,
but only recently more consistent data have been
obtained for their presence. Although the role of
hydroxycinnamates in melanoidin formation is not
yet completely understood, it was demonstrated that
the interaction between phenolic compounds and
melanoidins can be of non-covalent or of covalent
nature. The most likely linkage point is through the
protein fragments incorporated in the coffee mela-
noidin during the roasting process, although carbo-
hydrates, such as arabinose, seem to be possible
binding sites for the chlorogenic acid derivatives on
these brown structures, too.

Introduction
Melanoidins are the ﬁnal brown products of the
Maillard reaction. They are responsible for the color
formed during the heat processing of foods like
coffee, bread, malt, and beef. They are high molec-
ular weight polymers with a polydisperse structure
containing nitrogen (Ledl and Schleider 1990; Ames
et al. 1999a, b; Hofmann 1999). There are several
reviews about the Maillard reaction mechanisms and
products formed (Ledl and Schleider 1990; Friedman
1996; Rizzi 1997; Henle et al. 1998; Hofmann 1999;

Martins et al. 2000; Erbersdobler and Somoza 2007).
Nevertheless, a chemical deﬁnition of these food
polymers is still today impossible, despite the several
efforts for determining their structure. There are at
present three main proposals for the melanoidin
structure: melanoidins can be seen as (1) polymers
built up of repeating units of furans and/or pyrroles,
formed during the advanced stages of the Maillard
reaction, linked by polycondensation reactions (Hey-
ns and Hauber 1970; Tressl et al. 1998a, b); (2)
modiﬁed proteins containing low-molecular-weight
colored substances able to crosslink proteins via
e-amino groups of lysine or through the guanidine
group of arginine (Hofmann 1998, b; Hofmann et al.
1999b, c); (3) a skeleton made up of polymerized
sugar degradation products through aldol-type con-
densation, formed in the early stages of a Maillard
reaction (Kato and Tsuchida 1981; Ca¨mmerer and
Kroh 1995; Ca¨mmerer et al. 2002). Evidence has
been obtained from model studies and electron spin
resonance spectroscopy experiments that the free
radical
1,4-bis-(5-amino-5-carboxy-1-pentyl)pyrazi-
nium (CROSSPY) might be an important intermedi-
ate in melanoidin formation during the thermal
treatment of foods, as solutions of this radical
undergo a rapid browning (Hofmann et al. 1999b,
c). The majority of the studies on melanoidin
formation are derived from model systems where a
reducing sugar (monomeric or oligomeric) was
reacted with one aminoacid or protein, or where
known intermediates of the Maillard reaction were
reacted, in the dry state or solution, using varying
reaction conditions like time, temperature, and pH.
These conditions are far from being representative for
heat treatment normally applied to foods.
In the last few years the interest in research on
melanoidins has increased as they were found to
show relevant biological activities. Table 1 summa-
rizes some examples, not intended to be exhaustive,

Recent research on the melanoidin structure iso-
lated from foods (Hashiba 1973; Steinhart et al. 1989;
Obretenov et al. 1993; Kuntcheva and Obretenov
1996; Lindenmeier et al. 2002; Bekedam et al. 2006,
2007, 2008a, b; Nunes and Coimbra 2007; Gniech-
witz et al. 2008b), has shown that the structure of the
melanoidins from these sources is somewhat different
from that obtained by the simpler model systems and
is dependent on the food composition and thermal
treatment applied. However, some physico-chemical
properties of melanoidins produced under model
systems seem to be identical to those isolated from
foods: they are partially hydrophobic (Onishi and
Proudlove 1994; Gniechwitz et al. 2008b) polydis-
perse high molecular weight substances with anionic
character (Homma et al. 1982; Morales 2002; Nunes

The main limitation of the structural studies on
melanoidins is the lack of speciﬁc analytical methods
for their isolation and separation in more homoge-
neous fractions. A common approach for the isolation
of melanoidins from foods is based on their high
molecular weight, employing dialysis or ultraﬁltra-
tion. Nevertheless, in foods, other high molecular
weight materials, such as proteins and polysaccha-
rides, are present and are co-isolated with these
procedures, demonstrating the need for suitable
procedures which are able to separate the different
polymers. Novel approaches have been recently
employed on the isolation and characterization of
coffee brew melanoidins with good results (Bekedam
et al. 2006, 2007, 2008a, b; Nunes and Coimbra 2007;
Gniechwitz et al. 2008b). The participation of
hydroxycinnamates in melanoidin formation, espe-
cially in coffee melanoidin formation, is a 38 years
old hypothesis (Klo¨cking et al. 1971), but only
recently more consistent data have been obtained for
their presence (Takenaka et al. 2005; Nunes and
Coimbra 2007; Bekedam et al. 2008a, b).
Thermal degradation of hydroxycinnamates
The role of hydroxycinnamates in coffee melanoidin
formation is thought to be closely linked to their
thermal and
oxidative
transformations occurring
during coffee roasting, and the structures expected
to be present can be inferred from the products
obtained by polymerization. Green coffee beans
contain up to 10% of chlorogenic acids by dry
weight (Herrmann 1989) and 5-caffeoylquinic acid
(Fig. 1) is the most abundant isomer. During coffee
roasting, the amount of chlorogenic acid decreases
signiﬁcantly, as 8–10% of its amount is lost for every
1% loss of dry matter (Clifford 1985, 2002). The
products derived from the thermal degradation of
chlorogenic acids during coffee roasting are not yet
fully characterized. However, it is already known that
they are diverse and range from simple phenols to
condensation products of high structural complexity.
The thermal treatment of cinnamic and chlorogenic
acids
results
in
rapid
decarboxylation
yielding
a range of simple phenols (Fig. 2; Fiddler et al.
1967; Rizzi and Boekley 1992; Sharma et al. 2002;
Muller et al. 2006). The caffeic acid moiety of
chlorogenic acids is the main precursor of catechol
(1), 4-methylcatechol (2), 4-ethylcatechol (3), and
4-vinylcatechol (4) (Fig. 2a) formed upon the roast-
ing of the beans. Roasting also converts the ferulic
acid moiety into guaiacol (5), 4-methylguaiacol (6),
4-ethylguaiacol (7), and 4-vinylguaiacol (8) (Fig. 2b),
and quinic acid moiety into catechol (1), pyrogallol
(9), and hydroxyhydroquinone (11) (Fig. 2c) (Muller
et al. 2006). Several model studies, either in solution
(Cilliers and Singleton 1989, 1991) or dry heating of
caffeic acid (Rizzi and Boekley 1992; Stadler et al.
1996; Frank et al. 2007) have identiﬁed a range of
condensation products. Autoxidation experiments of
caffeic acid carried out in solution, identiﬁed lignan-
like products (Fig. 3), called caffeicins, postulated to
be formed via the phenolate anion (Cilliers and
Singleton 1989, 1991), four 2,3-dihydro-1,4-benzo-
dioxan neolignans (caffeicins A-D, 1–4, respec-
tively), one 2,3-dihydrobenzofuran-type neolignan
(caffeicin F, 5), and one 1,2-dihydro-naphthalene-
type compound (caffeicin E, 6). Oxidation of caffeic
acid under acidic conditions or pyrolysis produces
two stereoisomers of 2,5-(30,40-dihydroxy-phenyl)
tetrahydrofuran-3,4-dicarboxylic acid (Stadler et al.

Coffee melanoidin structure
Despite all efforts, the chemical structure of coffee
brew melanoidins is still largely unknown. It has
always been discussed that carbohydrates, aminoac-
ids, and phenolic compounds are components of
coffee melanoidins (Maier et al. 1968; Klo¨cking et al.
1971; Maier and Buttle 1973; Henrich and Baltes
1987; Steinhart and Packert 1993; Terasawa et al.
1996; Adams et al. 2005) but deﬁnitive conclusions
have been hampered by the fact that melanoidins
have been always obtained as complex mixtures, not
ruling out the presence of co-isolated polysaccha-
rides, proteins, and phenolic compounds.
More recent work has shown that coffee brew
melanoidins have a structural diversity and contain
thermally transformed polysaccharides, proteins, and
phenolic compounds. Various melanoidin fractions
have been obtained by applying chromatographic
separation techniques or speciﬁc isolation procedures
that allowed a partial view on coffee brew melanoi-
dins’ structural features and diversity. Although the
methods employed for melanoidin isolation and the
analytical techniques used for their puriﬁcation are not
identical, the scientiﬁc basis for their application is the
same (Fig. 5). Melanoidins are isolated taking advan-
tage of their high molecular weight, and the puriﬁca-
tion of the different melanoidin populations is

With the purpose of obtaining melanoidin frac-
tions with different polysaccharide composition, the
HMWM can be subjected to an ethanol fractionation
procedure. The fractions enriched in galactomannans
mostly precipitate at 40–50% ethanol and the frac-
tions enriched in arabinogalactans mostly precipitate
at 75–80% ethanol aqueous solutions. The fractions
with low carbohydrate content are very soluble in
ethanol and remain soluble in 75–80% ethanol
solutions (Nunes and Coimbra 2001, 2002a, b,
2007; Bekedam et al. 2006; Gniechwitz et al. 2008a).
The coffee arabinogalactans have been shown to be
arabinogalactan-proteins (AGPs). These complexes
occur as coffee cell walls components (Redgwell et al.
2002a, b) and water soluble polysaccharides (Nunes
et al. 2008). For the isolation of melanoidins containing
intact AGP structures from the HMWM, a more
speciﬁc procedure can be performed, using a precip-
itation step with the Yariv phenyl glycoside reagent
(Bekedam et al. 2007).

From the results obtained by the above mentioned
studies, some conclusions can be drawn about the
physico-chemical properties of coffee brew melanoi-
dins. (1) There are various populations of melanoi-
dins. (2) The brownest melanoidins are anionic and
hydrophobic, and contain variable amounts of arabi-
nogalactans and galactomannans (Bekedam et al.
2006, 2007; Nunes and Coimbra 2007; Gniechwitz
et al. 2008b). (3) Some melanoidins do not present or
present only low anionic character (Nunes et al. 2006;
Nunes and Coimbra 2007). (4) Galactomannans and
arabinogalactans are integral parts of coffee brew
melanoidins. However, different melanoidin popula-
tions can be found with respect to total carbohydrate
contents and structural features of their carbohydrates
for example, different arabinose:galactose ratio, dif-
ferent degree of branching of the arabinogalactans,
and mantainance of the AGP structures (Nunes and
Coimbra 2007; Bekedam et al. 2007). (5) The
arabinogalactans seem to play a central role on the
melanoidin
formation,
as
melanoidin
fractions
obtained by selective precipitation with the Yariv
reagent had an intense brown color (Bekedam et al.
2007). However, approximately half of the melanoi-
dins were not removed from the HMWM by this
procedure. Also, the Yariv precipitation procedure
does not remove all the arabinogalactan polysaccha-
rides from the HMWM, probably representing highly
modiﬁed arabinogalactans that lost their speciﬁc
Yariv reagent binding characteristics, probably by
loss of their protein moiety (Bekedam et al. 2007). (6)
Almost half of the anionic melanoidins have copper
chelating properties (Nunes and Coimbra 2007).
Besides
galactomannans
and
arabinogalactans,
melanoidins also contain variable amounts of pro-
tein-like material that can be quantiﬁed by aminoacid
analysis after acid hydrolysis. Depending on the
experiments performed, during the roasting process
of coffee, the amount of protein in the HMWM was
shown to decrease (Nunes and Coimbra 2007) and to
increase (Bekedam et al. 2008a, b). Aminoacids
account for the majority of the HMWM nitrogen
(Bekedam et al. 2006; Nunes and Coimbra 2007) and
varies from 94% of the total nitrogen of roasted
coffee HMWM, for a light roasted coffee, to 70%, for
the dark roasted one. The aminoacid composition of
the different melanoidin populations are very similar
(Bekedam et al. 2006; Nunes and Coimbra 2007).
The most abundant amino acids are glutamic acid/
glutamine, glycine, alanine, aspartic acid/asparagine,
isoleucine, leucine, and proline, the least abundants
are methionine and the hydrophilic aminoacids, such
as lysine and tyrosine. Arginine residues were not
found, and histidine was reported to be present only
in trace amounts. Hydroxyproline was also found in
relatively high amounts in melanoidins. This hydrox-
ylated aminoacid residue has been found in green
coffee cell wall AGPs, as well as in the hot water
soluble material (Redgwell et al. 2002a, b; Nunes
et al. 2008). This ﬁnding is in line with the presence
of AGPs in coffee melanoidins, as discussed earlier.
The aminoacids composition of the melanoidin
fractions is similar to the aminoacids composition of
the whole roasted coffee beans and of the roasted
coffee infusions (Feldman et al. 1969; Casal et al.
2005; Bekedam et al. 2006). The amount of protein of
the melanoidins increases with the increase of their
solubility in ethanol solutions (Bekedam et al. 2006;
Nunes and Coimbra 2007). Also, the amount of non-
protein nitrogen increases with the increase of their
ethanol solubility (Bekedam et al. 2006). Various
modiﬁed lysine residues were identiﬁed in the
HMWM and melanoidin fractions, including fructo-
syl-lysine (detected as furosine after acid hydrolysis),
N-e-carboxymethyl lysine (CML) and N-e-carboxy-
ethyl-lysine (CEL) in the range of mg per kg of
proteic
material
(Nunes
and
Coimbra
2007).
Although
these
derivatives
occur
at
very
low
amounts, not considerably accounting for the amount
of non-nitrogen material present in the HMWM and
melanoidin fractions, they clearly underline the
signiﬁcance of Maillard reactions in the melanoidin
formation.

Role of hydroxycinnamates in melanoidin
formation
Apart from Maillard reaction products, the only other
class of compounds known to be present in the
melanoidins fractions is the class of phenolic com-
pounds. Their presence and quantiﬁcation has been
ascertained by a large range of techniques (Klo¨cking
et al. 1971; Henrich and Baltes 1987; Takenaka et al.
2005; Terasawa et al. 1996; Nunes and Coimbra
2007; Bekedam et al. 2006, 2007, 2008a, b), includ-
ing the use of the Prussian Blue method and Folin-
Ciocalteu reagent. However, the utilization of these
colorimetric methods for quantiﬁcation of phenolic
compounds in melanoidin fractions (Nunes and
Coimbra 2001, 2002a, b; Bekedam et al. 2006,
2007, 2008a, b) should be regarded with caution, as
these methods are not speciﬁc for phenolic com-
pounds (Waterhouse 2001) and melanoidins pro-
duced from model systems with single sugar and
amino acids also possess reducing capacity (Homma
et al. 1997).
The interaction between phenolic compounds and
melanoidins, as observed for phenolic compounds
and proteins, can be of non-covalent or of covalent
nature. Hydrogen bonding between the hydroxyl
groups of the phenolic compounds and the amide
carbonyls of the peptide bonds is a common non
covalent interaction, but interactions can be of
hydrophobic and ionic nature too (Hagerman 1992;
Kroll et al. 2003). The occurrence of non-covalently-
linked chlorogenic acids has been observed to occur
in coffee melanoidin fractions isolated by ultraﬁltra-
tion (D’Agostina et al. 2004; Delgado-Andrade and
Morales 2005), probably retained by ionic interac-
tions (Delgado-Andrade and Morales 2005).
However, coffee melanoidins also contain cova-
lently linked phenolic compounds. This statement is
based on experiments using fractions that have been
previously submitted to a reversed phase high
performance liquid chromatography to verify the
absence of non-covalently linked chlorogenic acids.
These fractions, when treated by alkaline fusion
(Nunes and Coimbra 2007), a method known to
release condensed phenolic structures (Newhall and
Ting 1967; Batistic and Mayaudon 1970), released a
range of simple phenols, listed in Table 2. Other
phenolic compounds such as phenol, 2-hydroxyben-
zoic
acid,
4-hydroxyphenylacetic

and

in trace amounts. These monomeric phenolic com-
pounds released by alkaline fusion accounted for 2.6–
3.2% of coffee melanoidins. Interestingly, these
compounds are similar to those reported for the
alkaline fusion of the melanoidin fraction obtained by
zinc precipitation (Takenaka et al. 2005). As alkaline
saponiﬁcation, even at 100C, did not release quan-

Covalently-linked phenolic compounds of coffee
melanoidins were, however, also shown by saponiﬁ-

alkaline saponiﬁcation of the HMWM of coffee
melanoidins, low amount of caffeic (0.5–0.9%) and
ferulic acids (0.1%), and relatively high amounts of
quinic acid (1.7–3.7%) have been observed. This
indicates that only low amounts of caffeic and ferulic
acids can be esteriﬁed to the melanoidins, but quinic
acid, derived from coffee chlorogenic acid residues,
is ester-linked to the melanoidins.

The most likely linkage between the phenolic
compounds and melanoidins is via the protein
fragments incorporated in the coffee melanoidins
during the roasting process (Bekedam et al. 2006;
Nunes and Coimbra 2007). The SDS–PAGE proﬁle
of extracted proteins changes drastically from green
to roasted coffee. It was shown that, during the
roasting process, the water soluble proteins are highly
transformed, especially the 11S storage proteins
(Nunes and Coimbra 2001; Nunes and Coimbra
2002a, b; Montavon et al. 2003). The change in the
water soluble protein SDS-PAGE proﬁle observed
during the roasting process is similar to what is
observed when oxidized chlorogenic acids are reacted
with the green coffee 11S storage protein in model
systems (Montavon et al. 2003; Rawel et al. 2005).
What is the type of linkage and structure of the
hydroxycinnamates present in coffee melanoidins—
this is a question that remains to be answered.
Hydroxycinnamates can occur as unmodiﬁed resi-
dues, chemically transformed, or esteriﬁed with
quinic acid. One of the possibilities of covalent
linkage of the chlorogenic acid to the melanoidins is
through the linkage of the oxidized caffeic acid, as
observed for the enzymatic browning reaction (Fried-
man 1996; Robards et al. 1999; Kroll et al. 2003).
The caffeoylquinone is a reactive electrophilic spe-
cies and nucleophilic addition of a lysine or cysteine
residue can occur (Cheynier et al. 1986; Friedman
1996; Kroll et al. 2003). Further oxidation of this
addition product, with the formation of its quinone,
and a second addition can occur (Salgues et al. 1986;
Friedman 1996; Kroll et al. 2003), causing protein
cross-linking and decreasing their solubility (Rawel
et al. 2005; Kroll et al. 2003). This reaction pathway
for chlorogenic acids would be consistent with the
results obtained by Bekedam et al. (2008a, b).
