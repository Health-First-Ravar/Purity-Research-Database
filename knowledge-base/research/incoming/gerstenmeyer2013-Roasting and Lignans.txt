a b s t r a c t
Consumption of lignan rich food is presumed to have positive effects on human health. As numerous
foods are consumed mainly in processed form it is important to investigate the changes of the lignan con-
tent during processing. To this end, unheated and heated sesame seeds, sesame products, rye grains, rye
ﬂour, rye bread and ﬂax seeds were extracted by sonication with ethanol/water (70:30, v:v) or sodium
methoxide. The extracts were additionally hydrolysed enzymatically (b-glucuronidase/arylsulphatase,
cellulase), the compounds separated on a reversed phase column by gradient elution and detected by
UV/ESI-MS in the negative ionisation multiple reaction monitoring mode (MRM). Secoisolariciresinol, lar-
iciresinol, pinoresinol, 7-hydroxymatairesinol, syringaresinol, isolariciresinol, secoisolariciresinol digly-
coside, lariciresinol monoglycoside, pinoresinol mono-, di- and triglycoside, sesaminol, sesaminol
triglycoside, sesamolinol and sesamolinol diglycoside were identiﬁed.
Moderate heating at 100 C did not degrade the lignan aglycones and glycosides in dry foods. In con-
trast, heating was responsible for the better extractability of the lignans. If samples with high moisture
content were heated, the degradation of the lignans in sesame seeds and rye was observed already at
100 C. Higher roasting temperatures caused degradation of aglycones and glycosides. Especially at
250 C, lignans were degraded rapidly in sesame seeds and rye but not in ﬂax seeds.

1. Introduction
Lignans are phytoestrogens present in seeds, vegetable oils,
cereals, legumes, fruits and vegetables as aglycones, glycosides,
esteriﬁed glycosides or as bio-oligomers (Bambagiotti-Alberti,
Coran, Ghiara, Moneti, & Raffaelli, 1994; Coran, Giannellini, &
Bambagiotti-Alberti, 1996; Johnsson et al., 2002; Kamal-Eldin
et al., 2001; Schwartz & Sontag, 2006; Smeds et al., 2007).
Frequently occurring lignans are lariciresinol, pinoresinol, secoisol-
ariciresinol, syringaresinol, matairesinol, 7-hydroxymatairesinol,
sesamin, sesamolin and sesamol. Plant foods or oils are used for
preparing various foods like bakery products, chips or blanched
and cooked vegetables. During their production, the raw materials
are heated to a lesser or greater extent and the stability of lignans
occurring in different conjugation patterns is inﬂuenced.
Roasting of sesame seeds at 200 C for 60 min cleaved and lib-
erated phenolic compounds (Jeong et al., 2004), and sesamolin
could be degraded into sesamol (Lee, Jeung, Park, Lee, & Lee,
2010). Infrared roasting of sesame seeds at 200 C for 30 min also
degraded sesamolin to sesamol (Kumar, Appu Pao, & Singh, 2009).
In sesame oil the content of sesamol increased under heating
conditions whilst that of sesamolin decreased slightly and the se-
samin content changed only little. Sesamolin also degraded when
the oil was heated at 200 C for 20 min (Wu, 2007). For pinoresinol
a high stability to thermal treatments below 180 C was observed
in olive oil (Brenes, Garcia, Dobarganes, Velasco, & Romero,
2002). However, a short microwave treatment already resulted in
a small decrease (Cerretani, Bendini, Rodriguez-Estrada, Vittadini,
& Chiavaro, 2009). Daskalaki, Keﬁ, Kotsiou, and Tasioula-Margari
(2009) reported that lignans like 1-acetoxypinoresinol remained
unchanged during boiling or frying in olive oil. Lee et al. (2003)
showed that simple heat treatment could not cleave covalently
bound phenolic compounds from rice hull, while far infrared treat-
ment could. In pumpkin seeds, secoisolariciresinol was degraded
by thermal heating with increasing roasting time (Murkovic
et al., 2004). Muir and Westcott (2000) and Hyvarinen et al.
(2006) reported that the complex ester of secoisolariciresinol dig-
lucoside and secoisolariciresinol diglucoside (SDG) itself are stable
in various bakery products.
Lignan consumption is believed to have positive effects on hu-
man health. Especially the beneﬁts of secoisolariciresinol digluco-
side have been revealed recently (Adolphe, Whiting, Juurlink,
Thorpe, & Alcorn, 2010; Touré & Xueming, 2010). In addition, the

The aim of this study was to investigate the thermal stability of
water soluble lignans in ﬂax seeds, sesame seeds and rye. Due to
the lack of appropriate standard compounds for glycosides an
analytical method was developed to estimate the SDG content
and to quantify the amount of aglycones. The content of lignans
of some raw, heat treated and commercial products as well as
home baked bread should be determined to obtain information
on the concentration of the remaining lignans.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Raw materials
Three kg of sesame from Bolivia and hulled sesame premium
from India were provided by the Department of Food Science and
Technology, University of Natural Resources and Life Sciences,
Vienna, Austria. Organic sesame, sesame black and ﬂax seeds
(1 kg) were purchased in health food shops (Vienna, Austria) in
2008. Organic rye ﬂour (2 kg) and rye grains (1 kg) were from Al-
natura GmbH (Bickenbach, Germany).
2.2. Commercial samples
2.2.1. Sesame
Sesam-Krokant (Alnatura GmbH, Bickenbach, Germany), Reis-
Snack Sesam (Schneekoppe GmbH, Nordheide, Germany), Sesam
Crunch orange and Sesam Cracker (black sesame, Ruschin GmbH,
Birkenheide, Germany) were purchased in a health food shop
(Erlangen, Germany) in 2008.
2.2.2. Rye
Kastenbrot was bought in a health food shop (Vienna, Austria)
in 2008.
2.2.3. Home baked bread
Three hundred and seventy grams of rye wholemeal ﬂour (Adler
Mühle, Balingen, Germany), 250 ml water, a teaspoon of salt, 20 g
of yeast and 7 g of sourdough were mixed. The dough was held
at room temperature for 3 h. Subsequently, it was ﬁrst baked at
190 C for 30 min and at 140 C for 10 min in a baking oven.

2.3.1. Stock solutions
Two milligrams of isolariciresinol, secoisolariciresinol, larici-
resinol, pinoresinol, matairesinol and 7-hydroxymatairesinol were
weighed and each of them transferred into a 10 ml ﬂask and dis-
solved in ethanol (99%).
2.3.2. Syringaresinol stock solution
Two milligrams of eleutheroside E (syringaresinol diglucoside)
were transferred in a ﬂask and dissolved in 10 ml methanol/water
(50:50, v:v). To 1 ml of this solution, 3.5 ml sodium acetate (0.1 M,
adapted to pH 5 with glacial acetic acid) and 100 ll b-glucuroni-
dase/arylsulphatase were added into a vial and incubated at
37 C for 19 h. The completeness of the enzymatic hydrolysis was
monitored by HPLC/UV–ESI-MS (Fig. 1), using the characteristic
m/z values of syringaresinol diglucoside (741.1), syringaresinol
monoglucoside (579.1) and of the aglycone (417.1). The solution
was transferred into a 5 ml ﬂask and ﬁlled up with acetonitrile.
2.3.3. Standard solution I
Two millilitres of pinoresinol stock solution and 1 ml of the
stock solutions of isolariciresinol, secoisolariciresinol, lariciresinol,
matairesinol and 7-hydroxymatairesinol were pipetted into a
round bottomed ﬂask. Ethanol was evaporated under vacuum
(Rotavapor RE 120, Büchi, Buchs, Switzerland) and the residue
was dissolved in 5 ml acetonitrile/water (20:80, v:v) and ﬁlled up
in a 10 ml ﬂask. From this solution ﬁve appropriate standard solu-
tions were prepared by dilution with acetonitrile/water (20.80,
v:v) and applied for determination of the lignans in sesame, ses-
ame products and ﬂax seed.
2.3.4. Standard solution II
Two millilitres of syringaresinol stock solution were mixed with
1 ml of the stock solutions of pinoresinol, isolariciresinol, secoiso-
lariciresinol, lariciresinol, matairesinol and 7-hydroxymatairesinol
in a ﬂask and evaporated under vacuum. The residue was dissolved
in 10 ml of acetonitrile/water (20:80, v:v). This solution was fur-
ther diluted and used for the determination of lignans in rye, rye
ﬂour and rye products.

2.4. Sample preparation
2.4.1. Heating
Thirty grams of ﬂax seeds, sesame seeds, rye ﬂour and grains
were heated in an industrial oven (Model 60/3 W, Manz Backtech-
nik, Münster, Germany) at 100 and 200 C for 10, 20, 30 and
40 min and at 250 C for 3.5 and 10 min. To simulate the baking
process, 30 g of the sesame seeds, rye ﬂour and grains were steam
treated for 2 min, then heated at 100 C, 200 C for 10, 20, 30,
40 min and at 250 C for 10 min. The moisture content of the sam-
ples was determined by weighing after steam treating and after
heating.
2.4.2. Sample handling
Thirty grams of Sesam Krokant, Sesam Cracker, Reis Snack Se-
sam, Sesam Crunch Orange, Sesam Cracker (black sesame), bread
crust and crumb were crushed with a food processor. Rye grains
were ground in a mill (MF 10 basic, IKA Werke, Staufen, Germany).
2.4.3. Defatting
Thirty grams aliquots of heated and unheated sesame seeds,
ﬂax seeds or sesame products were homogenised in a commercial
kitchen blender (Krupps Speedy Pro, Offenbach/Main, Germany)
and extracted with 200 ml n-hexane for 4 h under reﬂuxing in a
Soxhlet apparatus. The liquid was decanted and discarded and
the solid residue was dried under vacuum at 35 C for 30 min.
The samples were stored at 18 C under exclusion of light.
Due to the low fat content rye and rye products were not
defatted.
2.4.4. Nondestructive extraction
Fifty milligrams of sesame seeds, ﬂax seeds or sesame products
were weighed into a 20 ml screwable glass vial, 6 ml of ethanol/
0.1 M sodium acetate buffer (70:30, v:v, pH 5) were added and
the mixture was sonicated (Bandelin Sonorex super RK 103H, Iso-
bad GmbH, Heidelberg, Germany) at 60 C for 2 h. After cooling to
R.T., the extract was centrifuged at 4000 rpm (Hettich Universal,
Tuttlingen, Germany) for 15 min. The supernatant was transferred
into a 10 ml ﬂask. The residue was washed with 2 ml ethanol/
water (70:30, v:v) and centrifuged at 4000 rpm for 15 min. The
supernatant was again transferred into the 10 ml ﬂask. Ethanol
was removed under a constant stream of nitrogen and the ﬂask
was ﬁlled up for enzymatic hydrolysis with sodium acetate buffer
(0.1 M, pH 5) or with acetonitrile/water (20:80, v:v) for HPLC/MS
analysis. One to two ml of the obtained solution were ﬁltered
through a 13 mm PTFE 0.45 lm syringe ﬁlter (Alltech, Deerﬁeld,
IL, USA) before injection.
One to ﬁve grams of rye ﬂour, crushed rye crumb or crust were
weighed into a 30 ml screwable glass vial, 150 mg zinc sulphate
and 24 ml ethanol/water (70:30, v:v) were added, and the mixture
was sonicated at 60 C for 2 h. After cooling to R.T., the solution
was centrifuged at 4000 rpm for 15 min. The supernatant was
transferred into a ﬂask and the residue was sonicated with 10 ml
ethanol/water (70:30, v:v) for 10 min and then centrifuged at
4000 rpm for 15 min. The supernatant was added again into the
ﬂask and the solution was concentrated to 5 ml by evaporation
at 35 C under vacuum. This solution was transferred onto a solid
phase extraction cartridge (AccuBond II SPE ODS-C18 cartridge,
Agilent Technologies Inc., Palo Alto, CA, USA) which had previously
been conditioned with 5 ml of methanol and 5 ml of water. After-
wards, the column was washed with 5 ml water. Finally, the
lignans were eluted with 5 ml of methanol into a ﬂask. Methanol
was removed under a constant stream of nitrogen and the ﬂask
was ﬁlled up for enzymatic hydrolysis with sodium acetate buffer
(pH 5) or with acetonitrile/water (20.80, v:v) for HPLC/MS analysis.
One to two millilitres of the obtained solution were ﬁltered
through a 13 mm PTFE 0.45 lm syringe ﬁlter (Alltech, Deerﬁeld,
IL, USA) before injection.

2.4.5. Alkaline extraction
Two hundred and ﬁfty to ﬁve hundred milligrams of crushed
defatted ﬂax seeds, sesame seeds or sesame products were
weighed into a screwable glass vial, 6 ml sodium methoxide solu-
tion (200 mg solid sodium dissolved in 50 ml anhydrous methanol)
were added and the solution was sonicated in the closed vial at
40 C for 3 h. After cooling to R.T., the solution was neutralised
with glacial acetic acid, centrifuged at 4000 rpm for 15 min and
the supernatant was pipetted into a 10 ml ﬂask. Two ml sodium
acetate buffer (0.1 M, pH 5) were added to the residue, the solution
was sonicated for 15 min and centrifuged at 4000 rpm for 15 min.
Then the supernatant was pipetted into the 10 ml ﬂask and ﬁlled
up with sodium acetate buffer.
2.4.6. Enzymatic hydrolysis
One millilitre of the extract of the nondestructive or alkaline
extraction, 3.5 ml sodium acetate buffer (0.1 M, pH 5), 30 ll b-
glucuronidase/arylsulphatase mixture and 20 mg cellulase were
mixed in a 20 ml vial and incubated at 37 C for 19 h under gentle
stirring. The hydrolysate was transferred into a 5 ml ﬂask and ﬁlled
up with acetonitrile. The solution was centrifuged at 10000 rpm for
5 min and 10 ll were injected into the HPLC/ESI-MS apparatus.
2.5. Analysis
2.5.1. HPLC/UV/ESI-MS
The HPLC 1100 series system (Agilent Technology, Palo Alto,
USA) consisted of a G1312A binary pump, a G1322A mobile phase
vacuum degassing unit, a G1313A autosampler, an ACE 3 C18
column (100  2.1 mm, particle size: 3 lm from Advanced Chro-
matography Technologies, Aberdeen, Scotland) equipped with a
Phenomenex Gemini C18 precolumn (4  2 mm, particle size:
3 lm, Phenomenex, Torrance, CA, USA), a G1314 variable wave-
length detector and an ion trap mass spectrometric (MS) detector
(HCT plus, Bruker Daltonics, Bremen, Germany) equipped with an
electrospray ionisation (ESI) source. Data were acquired using
Hystar 3.1 and data evaluation was carried out using DataAnalysis
Version 3.3 from Bruker Daltonics.
2.5.2. Qualitative and quantitative analysis
Five to twenty microlitres of the standard solutions or 10 ll of
sample extracts were injected into the HPLC system and the com-
pounds were separated on the reversed phase column at 30 C by
gradient elution applying a ﬂow rate of 0.25 ml/min. Mobile phase
A consisted of 0.1% acetic acid in water, mobile phase B contained
0.1% acetic acid in acetonitrile. Chromatographic separation was
achieved using the following gradient: 0–0.5 min 10% B, 0.5–
12 min linear increase to 30% B, 12–20 min linear increase to 60%
B, 20–21 min decrease to 10% B, 21–30 min re-equilibration.
Electrospray ionisation was performed with nitrogen as nebulizing
gas (10 L/min), 40 psi nebulizing pressure, at 300 C drying gas
temperature. Optimum mass spectrometric parameters (capillary
voltage: +4.5 kV, skimmer potential: 40 V, capillary exit poten-
tial: 170 V and trap drive: 38) were determined by direct infusion
experiments of standard solutions and software controlled optimi-
sation. The total ion chromatograms (TIC) of ESI-MS in the negative
ion scan mode (100–1000 m/z) were used for qualitative analysis.
For quantitative analysis, the extracted ion chromatograms (EIC)
of the deprotonated molecular ions [MH] measured in the mul-
tiple reaction monitoring (MRM) mode (100–450 m/z) were used
for isolariciresinol (m/z 359.1), secoisolariciresinol (m/z 361.1),
syringaresinol (m/z 417.1), pinoresinol (m/z 357.1), matairesinol
(m/z 357.1) and 7-hydroxymatairesinol (m/z 373.1). In the case of
lariciresinol, fragmentation of the deprotonated molecular ion
(m/z 359.1) without isolation and isolation of the fragment ion
(m/z 329.1) was employed.
2.5.3. Estimation of the SDG content
Ten microlitres of extract of the alkaline extraction (see Sec-
tion 2.4.5) were injected into the HPLC/UV–ESI-MS system, the
compounds were separated by gradient reversed phase chroma-
tography and detected at 280 nm (Fig. 2). SDG was qualitatively
conﬁrmed by ESI/MS trace at m/z 685.7. For quantitative determi-
nation of SDG, the UV peak areas of SDG were put into a UV calibra-
tion function established for SECO and the obtained concentrations
were corrected for differences in molecular mass by multiplying by
the molecular mass of SDG and dividing by the molecular mass of
secoisolariciresinol.
2.5.4. Validation of the method
Standard calibration curves containing at least ﬁve concentra-
tions of each lignan were generated between 0.004 and 40 mg/l
by plotting the MS peak areas versus the concentration and the
correlation coefﬁcients (R2) were evaluated. The limits of detection
(LODs) and the limits of quantitation (LOQs) were determined at a
signal to noise ratio of 3 and 10, respectively. The intraday repro-
ducibility was assessed on the basis of ﬁve injections of a standard
solution containing the lignans in concentrations between 0.05 and
0.4 mg/l and on the basis of quintuplicate injection of extracts of
ﬂax seeds, sesame seeds and rye ﬂour. The interday reproducibility
was determined by injecting the above mentioned solutions
ﬁve times over a period of 5 days. To investigate the change of

2.5.5. Standard addition method
The concentration of each lignan in unheated and heated sam-
ples was estimated using the daily measured calibration curves.
Then, 50%, 100%, 150% and 200% of the estimated concentration
were added to the sample aliquots and the samples were worked
up and analysed. The concentrations of the lignans were deter-
mined by plotting the peak areas versus the added concentrations
of the corresponding lignan, linear regression, and division of the
y-intersection (d) by the slope of the regression line y = kx + d.
Recoveries (N = 3) of the individual lignans were determined in
heated and unheated ﬂax seeds, sesame seeds, sesame products,
rye ﬂour and grains, bread crust and crumb by dividing the slopes
of the standard addition curves by the slopes of calibration func-
tions recorded in neat standard solutions.

interday reproducibilities were found to be between 4.6% (isolaric-
iresinol) and 12% (syringaresinol). The slopes of the calibration
curves of the investigated lignans showed relative standard devia-
tions (N = 56) between 18% (pinoresinol) and 23% (secoisolaricires-
inol) over 13 months. To check the reliability of the analytical
method, an unroasted organic sesame sample was analysed
(N = 18) over a period of 8 months and the relative standard devi-
ations of the contents varied between 5.6% (lariciresinol) and 2.8%
(pinoresinol).
3.2.2. Analysis of lignans
To investigate to which extent aglycones, glycosides or complex
lignan compounds are present in ﬂax seeds, sesame seeds and rye,
three different sample preparation steps were applied: extraction
with ethanol/water, extraction with ethanol/water followed by
enzymatic hydrolysis, and alkaline extraction with sodium meth-
oxide combined with enzymatic hydrolysis. The efﬁciency of
enzymatic cleavage of the glycosides by means of a mixture of b-
glucuronidase/arylsulphatase/cellulase could be conﬁrmed on the
basis of the HPLC/MS chromatograms. In contrast, cellulase alone
did not achieve complete cleavage of the glycosides so that the
peaks of the glycosides could be observed in the chromatograms.

Flax
seeds. LC–MS

after

glycosides. However, after enzymatic hydrolysis of the extract,
small concentrations of secoisolariciresinol (0.1 mg/100 g), lari-

cleaved and SDG (Fig. 2) was formed in a relatively high concentra-

were found by Li, Yung, Xu, Wang, and Liu (2008). Though SDG is
the main glycoside, also glycosides of lariciresinol, pinoresinol
and isolariciresinol were detected in far lower concentrations.
Hydrolysis of the alkaline extract by the enzyme mixture increased
the concentration of lariciresinol to 3.3 mg/100 g, that of pinoresi-

that of secoisolariciresinol to 485 mg/100 g. The recoveries of the
lignans were in the range between 90.8% (isolariciresinol) and
97.7%

LOQs

When unheated and heated (100, 200 and 250 C) ﬂax seed
samples were extracted with sodium methoxide, the HPLC/ESI-
MS analysis showed a small increase of SDG from 1099 (unheated)
to 1200 mg/100 g (250 C, 3.5 min). The enzymatic hydrolysis of
the alkaline extracts resulted in an increase of secoisolariciresinol
from 485 to 610 mg/100 g, of lariciresinol from 3.3 to 3.75 mg/
100 g, of pinoresinol from 24.8 to 30.2 mg/100 g and of isolaricires-
inol from 10.4 to 13.8 mg/100 g when the samples were heated.
Hence it can be deduced that SDG and the aglycones are stable
even if the samples are heated to 250 C for 3.5 min. The increase
of their concentrations was most likely caused by a better extract-
ability of lignans due to increasing porosity of the heated seeds.
Muir and Westcott (2000) also observed that SDG is not formed
during the heating process but that its recovery depends on grind-
ing. Furthermore, the addition of pure SDG to bakery products
demonstrated that it is not thermally degraded (Hyvarinen et al.,
2006; Muir & Westcott, 2000).

Lariciresinol and pinoresinol were present in organic sesame seeds

3.2.2.3. Analysis of commercial sesame products. During the process-
ing of commercial sesame products like crunches, crackers or brit-
tle, various amounts of sesame are heated together with other raw
materials like honey, sugar or orange juice. In the ethanol/sodium
acetate extract of the defatted samples, small concentrations of the
aglycones isolariciresinol, lariciresinol, pinoresinol, 7-hydroxyma-
tairesinol were found. Additionally, lariciresinol monoglycoside
and the mono-, di- and triglycosides of pinoresinol were identiﬁed
beside the di-and triglycosides of sesaminol and the diglycoside of
sesamolinol. Enzymatic hydrolysis of the extract cleaved the glyco-
sidic compounds and the aglycones of lariciresinol, pinoresinol and
7-hydroxymatairesinol were quantiﬁed. Due to the known compo-
sition of the sesame products, the contents of lariciresinol and
pinoresinol could be estimated and compared with our data. For
this purpose, four sesame species were analysed (Table 1). Gener-
ally, the estimated values of the aglycones based on the analysed
sesame species were higher than in the products. This can be a hint
that thermal degradation of the glycosides and aglycones took
place during the preparations of the products. Yet, also the use of
hulled or non-hulled sesame seeds and natural variation between
different varieties of sesame can explain differences between esti-
mated and found concentrations.
3.2.2.4. Analysis of rye. In rye ﬂour and grains extracted with etha-
nol/water, lignan aglycones could not be identiﬁed until the extract
was hydrolysed by enzymes. At ﬁrst the sample preparation caused
problems due to the viscosity of the solution after extraction, but
the addition of zinc sulphate to the samples enhanced the recovery
of the lignans. Similar observations were made by the analysis of
isoﬂavones in different samples (Schwartz & Sontag, 2009). The
recoveries determined by the standard addition method for rye
ﬂour and grains were found to be 65.2% and 65.1% for lariciresinol,
67.3% and 64.8% for syringaresinol and 61.7% and 66.8% for
pinoresinol, respectively. The LOQs (S/N = 10) were 5.5 lg/100 g
grains for lariciresinol, 31.5 lg/100 g grains for syringaresinol
and 33.4 lg/100 g grains for pinoresinol. In rye ﬂour, the LOQs
were 6.5 lg/100 g (lariciresinol), 22.3 lg/100 g (syringaresinol)
and 17.2 lg/100 g (pinoresinol). Due to the presence of the hull,
the content of lignans in rye grains is higher than that in rye ﬂour
(Table 2). Still, the results are considerably lower than those re-
ported by (Smeds et al., 2007). Evidently, with the non-destructive
extraction with ethanol/water only aglycones and glycosides, but
not esteriﬁed glycosides could be extracted. However, when
destructive alkaline extraction with sodium methoxide was ap-
plied, the extracts were of high viscosity and particles were clog-
ging the cartridges. Therefore, it was abstained from alkaline
hydrolysis, knowing that esteriﬁed lignans would not be found in
the extract.
The heating of rye ﬂour and grains at 100 C caused an increase
of the concentrations of lariciresinol, pinoresinol and syringaresi-
nol with increasing time (Fig. 6). If the samples were treated with
steam before heating to 100 C, a decrease of the lignan concentra-
tion in comparison to the untreated samples was observed
(Gerstenmeyer, 2010). At 200 C, the degradation began after
10 min heating and became more pronounced with increased heat-
ing time. Heating rye ﬂour or grains at 250 C for more than 15 min
resulted in a complete degradation of lariciresinol, syringaresinol
and pinoresinol. The increase of the lignan concentrations with
heating time at 100 C can be explained by the better extractability
of the lignans due to the porosity of the matrix after heating. It was
also observed that heated samples can be easier worked up than
unheated ones.
3.2.2.5. Analysis of bread. The analysis of lignans in the ethanol/
water extract conﬁrmed the results of investigation of the raw
materials with respect to aglycones. The heating during baking
did not cleave the glycosidic bounds. Therefore, aglycones were
not found until enzymatic hydrolysis was applied. As expected,
the concentrations of the lignans were lower especially in the
bread crust, but the degradation of aglycones and glycosides dur-
ing the baking process was not extensive (Table 2).

tation of the kind of naturally occurring lignans in various food
matrices possible. Moderate heating at 100 C degraded lignan
aglycones, glycosides, and esters in some, but not in all analysed
