ABSTRACT
Biochar improves soil performance by altering soil physical characteristics. To
understand the effect of biochar particle size on soil hydrology, we measured
the hydraulic conductivity (Ks) of biochar-soil mixtures. To better understand
how physical properties influenced Ks, skeletal and (ρsÞ; bulk density (ρbÞ,
the water saturation (S), and the porosity (Ф) of mixtures were measured. A
sandy loam soil was mixed with two woody biochar particle sizes (1–2 and
<1 mm). All used biochars significantly decreased Ks compared to soil but this
reduction for finer biochars was more distinct. When finer particles of low and
high-tempered biochars were applied Ks decreased 14.3% and 18.9% while
coarser particles reduced Ks 13.9% and 17.5%, respectively. Also, all biochars
increased total, effective, inter and intraporosity of mixtures compared to
control after 180 days. Negative correlation between Ks and porosity demon-
strates the increase of porosity isn’t necessarily providing flow paths and thus
doesn’t have a positive influence on Ks. Decrease of Ks through the addition
of fine biochars was because they filled spaces between soil particles, which
increased tortuosity and reduced pore throat size. Also, the decrease of Ks
associated with coarser biochars was caused by the bimodal particle size
distribution, resulting in more compact packing and tortuosity.

Introduction
In the last decade biochar has been regarded as a valuable product that affords opportunities for
carbon sequestration and soil improvement (Peake, Reid, and Tang 2014). The incorporation of
biochar has an effect on soil water retention characteristics due to rearrangement of soil structure
and geometry. Biochar particle size affects water potential. Smaller sized particles have greater
surface area that may increase water holding capacity (WHC) (Mollinedo, Schumacher, and
Chintala 2015). Biochar soil amendment can increase crop productivity, potentially by improving
the hydrologic properties of the soils (Barnes et al. 2014) via changing pore arrangement between
particles (Mollinedo, Schumacher, and Chintala 2015) with corresponding effects on soil water
characteristics. Biochar can also increase soil WHC, plant available water content (PAWC), alter
soil hydrophobicity and change soil hydraulic conductivity (Ks), porosity (Ф), and bulk density (ρb)
(Barnes et al. 2014; Githinji 2014; Herath, Camps-Arbestain, and Hedley 2013).
The rate of water movement through soil is important for infiltration and delivery of water to plant
roots (Liu et al. 2016). Ks is a measurement of the ease of water movement through soil. Previous
researches showed that amending soil with biochar had contrasting results and can either increase or
decrease Ks (Barnes et al. 2014; Brockhoff et al. 2010; Githinji 2014; Herath, Camps-Arbestain, and
Hedley 2013). Jin et al. (2017) studied Ks of a storm water bio-retention media and a uniform sand
amended with 4% by weight wood biochar prepared in three different sizes. They concluded that

addition of the biochar universally decreased dry bulk density and increased the porosity compared to

15% when biochar particles were coarser than sand particles. When biochar and sand particle size were
comparable, no significant effect on Ks was observed. Using column experiments, Barnes et al. (2014)
showed that adding mesquite wood biochar can decrease the Ks of sand and organic soil by 92% and 67%,
respectively but increase the Ks of clay by 328%. Herath, Camps-Arbestain, and Hedley (2013) conducted
constant head experiment to determine Ks and documented increase in Ks of Alfisols by 32% and 139%
when adding unpyrolyzed 350°C corn stover biochar, and 550°C corn stover biochar, respectively.
Ibrahim et al. (2016) investigated the influence of conocarpus biochar particle size (<0.5 mm, 0.5–
1 mm, 1–2 mm) on hydraulic properties of a calcareous sandy soil. Their results suggested <0.5 mm
biochars caused the lowest values of Ks.
Porosity play an important role in how Ks changes in biochar amended soils as pores can allow
water movement and can alter the tortuosity of flow paths (Liu et al. 2016). Increased porosity in
soils after biochar amendment is resulted from pores inside biochar particles (Фintra, intraporosity)
and from pores between biochar and soil particles (Фinter, interporosity) (Masiello et al. 2015).
Depending on the pore size, the pore connectivity, and the hydrophobicity of the particle surface,
these two types of porosity may or may not be effective for water flow or water storage. When pores
are connected, water moves faster in larger pores than in smaller pores and thus larger pores
dominate water flow through porous media (Liu et al. 2016). Meanwhile, biochar particles can
have high initial hydrophobicity if they are produced at low temperatures (<400°C) (Kinney et al.
2012). Hydrophobic biochar has positive water entry pressure (Wang, Wu, and Wu 2000) which
means that an applied pressure is required for water to enter intrapores. If the hydrostatic pressure is
less than the water entry pressure, water will not enter these intrapores, therefore reducing their
effectiveness of water flow and water storage (Masiello et al. 2015). Amending soil with biochar will
likely change porosity, pore size, pore connectivity, and hydrophobicity of soil because of the dual
porosity of the system, changes in the particle size distribution, and hydrophobicity of biochar.
Combined these changes can affect the hydraulic conductivity.
We hypothesized that biochar amendment to soil can affect soil Ks by changing pore character-
istics and soil hydrophobicity. To better distinguish the influence of biochar on these soil properties,
Ks of biochar-soil mixtures was quantitatively investigated during falling head experiments in the lab.
Measuring changes in Ks associated with biochar particle size allowed us to better understand the
effects of biochar on water movement in a sandy loam soil. Also, we measured the changes of total,
effective, inter and intraporosity of biochars and mixtures to help understand the physical mechan-
isms that may cause changes in Ks.
Materials and methods
Biochar production and characterization
The biochars used in this research were obtained from apple-wood chips (Malus pumila). All materials
were first air-dried and then cut into small pieces (less than 4–5 cm). These residues were charred at a
heating rate of 10°C min−1 and held at 350 and 550°C for 1 h, respectively. The abbreviated names and
general properties of biochars are listed in Table 1. After cooling, each biochar was ground and passed
through 2 and 1-mm sieves respectively. Therefore we had two particle size diameters of biochars: <1 mm
and 1–2 mm. The elemental composition of carbon (C), hydrogen (H), and nitrogen (N) was determined
using the elemental analyzer (vario EL, Elementar, Germany). Particle size distribution of biochars was
determined using a Beckman coulter LS 13 Laser diffraction particle size analyzer. Biochar pH was

angle between water and external surface of biochars was measured to evaluate the hydrophilicity

Microscopy (SEM) to reveal surface morphology of biochars.
Experimental setup
Soil samples were collected from 0–20 cm of soil surface layering in the Qazvin plain, Qazvin
province, Iran. After air-drying, the soil samples were sieved using a 2-mm sieve and thoroughly
homogenized. It was a sandy loam soil with 53% sand, 30.8% silt and 16.2% clay. Soil ρb and SSA
were 1650 kg m−3 and 16.13 m2 g−1, respectively. Soil pH was 8.1 and its percentage of soil organic
matter (SOM) and N were 0.7 and 0.01, respectively. Amounts of CEC and electrical conductivity
(EC) were 10.51 cm olc kg−1 and 8.97 dS m−1, respectively.
The biochars were mixed into the soil at a ratio of 2% (w/w in dry weight basis). To measure the
changes of Ks after incubation period, samples of each treatment were filled into metal cylinders
(5 cm height and 5 cm diameter) based on the bulk densities. The bottom of cylinders was packed
using 250-mesh gauze. 6 replicate were set up. Ks, was measured based on falling head at the end of
incubation period. The incubation process was conducted at 25°C in a temperature-controlled room
for 180 days. During the incubation period, the moisture of the samples was kept at field capacity
(FC) by daily addition of deionized water based on weight loss.

Discussion
Soil properties such as porosity, pore size and connectivity, tortuosity and particle hydrophobicity
control Ks. According to MED indexs (Table 2) all biochars were slightly hydrophobic but all
biochar-soil mixtures were hydrophilic (MED index = 0). The difference between hydrophobicity
of biochars and hydrophilicity of mixtures indicated that these biochars didn’t cause fundamental
changes in hydrophobicity of biochar-soil mixtures at the biochar concentration we examined (2%
w/w). But this little amount of hydrophobicity of biocharˈs small intrapore size can cause a high
intrapore entry pressure and prevented water from penetrating intrapores (Liu et al. 2016). For
example, total porosity of all samples increased more than effective porosity. Also, treatments with
finer biochar particles were not fully saturated (SWL1 and SWH1, Table 2). In addition pure soil
sample was fully saturated. This suggested that the hydraulic pressure in our experiment was not
high enough to exceed the water entry pressure of some of the biochar intrapores due to their
hydrophobicity. This result leads us for biochar field implications. Because of existence of too small
intrapores in biochars, they can’t be invaded by water when biochar is applied near field surface.
However, it must be noticed that this partial saturation of biochar can’t significantly reduce its water
storage capacity because these small nanopores are a small fraction of total biochar porosity. This
result was in line with previous studies (Masiello et al. 2015; Novak et al. 2012). Total porosity of
SWH1 was 0.51 m3 m−3 while its effective porosity was 0.48 m3 m−3. This is likely because the
applied hydraulic pressure (0.3 m of water) at the beginning of falling head experiment was not
enough to overcome the entry pressure caused by the hydrophobicity of biochar particles or biochar
ˈs small intrapore size, or both (Liu et al. 2016). Therefore, the increase of total porosity by amending
biochar may not have been effective for water flow.

Biochar grinding may change its intrapore connectivity and pore size. As seen in Table 2, the
coarser biochars had lower ρsb that implied unconnected and isolated pores are preserved. These
unconnected intrapores act as gas pockets, creating an apparent skeletal density which is lower than
true skeletal density (Liu et al. 2016). Grinding biochar into finer particles opened the isolated
unconnected intrapores and allowed to helium (He) to enter so, the finer particles had a higher
skeletal density as determined by He pycnometry. Grinding can also change the SSA of particles.
After opening the unconnected intrapores, more pore surface can be exposed to water. According to
results (Table 1) SSA of low-tempered biochars increased from 10.01 to 10.21 m2 g−1 and of high-
tempered biochars increased from 48.69 to 79.43 m2 g−1 with decreasing particle size. SSA increased
with production temperature, too. The positive correlation between surface area and production
temperature has been widely reported and is explained by Kercher and Nagle (2003) as the result of
condensation of carbon into denser turbostratic crystallites leaving nanometer-sized voids (pyro-
genic nanopores) within the carbon structure (Gray et al. 2014). All mentioned reasons cause to
increase water retention of finer biochars contributing to the more apparent decrease in Ks than
coarser ones (Barnes et al. 2014).
In addition, the Pearson correlation coefficients (R) between Ks and [T, [eff, [inter, and [intra
were −0.82, −0.91, −0.23, and −0.61, respectively (Table 4). This negative correlation between Ks and
porosity demonstrates that the increase of porosity by introducing biochar intrapores is not
necessarily providing flow paths for water and thus doesn’t have a positive influence on Ks.
Therefore, it is concluded that the small intrapores were poorly connected and large interpores
dominated the flow path. Our results were in line with Liu et al. (2016).
Our results indicated that biochar amendment with different particle sizes to a sandy loam soil
can alter interpore size and tortuosity. Fine biochar particles can fill the pores between soil paticles,
resulting in more effective packing and higher bulk density so, available space for flow and pore
throat size reduced while tortuosity increased.
According our results, biocharˈs effect on soil Ks was mainly controlled by interpores. When fine
particles applied to soil, they filled interpores resulting in a creation of smaller pores and an increase
of tortuosity, thus decreasing Ks. Brockhoff et al. (2010) reported a decrease in Ks in a sandy soil
when a very fine biochar was applied. Our results were also similar to Liu et al. (2016). Finally, other
physical mechanisms, such as swelling and grain segregation, leading to clogging of pores and
decrease in pore radii (Barnes et al. 2014) cause to decrease Ks of sandy loam soil.
From our results, the most effective biochar application scenario could be concluded for the
desired field condition. We must consider changes in water storage and Ks of biochar-soil mixtures
when defining an optimal biochar particle size application to maximize water storage without
affecting water flow. To reduce water drainage in coarse-textured soils, finer biochar particles can
be more effective because either water storage or Ks may be modified.
Conclusion
The hydraulic conductivity (Ks) of sandy loam soil varied with biochar particle size. Ks decreased
18.9% (compared to control) when finer particles of a woody high-tempered biochar was applied
whilst it reduced 17.5% by application of coarser biochar particles. The decrease of Ks following the
addition of finer biochar particles was the result of increased tortuosity and reduced pore throat size
in treatments. Also, negative correlation between Ks and porosity demonstrates that the increase of
porosity by introducing biochar intrapores is not necessarily providing flow paths for water and thus
doesn’t have a positive influence on Ks.
