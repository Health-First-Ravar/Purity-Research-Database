A B S T R A C T
Scope: Coffee consumption is widely recognized to improve the antioxidant status. We hypothesized a dark-roast
coffee to reduce plasma oxidized LDL (oxLDL) and to improve alpha-tocopherol concentrations.
Methods and results: After a 4 week, coffee-free run-in period, 86 healthy, randomized volunteers completed
either a control (CTRL) or coffee (COFF) intervention in which either 750 mL water (CTRL) or coffee (COFF)
were consumed daily for 8 weeks. Blood samples were taken at the begin and after the intervention. Mean
changes in oxidized LDL concentrations after coffee consumption (−0.47 ± 15.4 U/L) differed from those of
the CTRL-G (5.69 ± 18.8 U/L, p < 0.05). Levels of alpha-tocopherol (+3.46 ± 16.48%, p < 0.05) as well as
non-esterified fatty acids increased in the COFF-G.
Conclusion: Improved plasma alpha-tocopherol levels and reduced levels of plasma oxLDL after 8 week con-
sumption of a dark-roast coffee rich in N-methylpyridinium are hypothesized to be caused by coffee-induced
lipolysis, resulting in increased alpha-tocopherol mobilisation.
1. Introduction
Coffee is one of the most commonly consumed beverages in Europe,
being highest in Denmark and Germany (Landais et al., 2018). Beside
its pleasant aroma and its stimulating effects on the central nervous
system, coffee beverages are commonly recognized as dietary sources of
antioxidants, mainly due to their contents of phenolic compounds, of
which chlorogenic acids (CGAs) are the most prevalent (Köseoğlu
Yılmaz & Kolak, 2017). A volume of 200 mL instant brew (2% w/v) has
been estimated to supply between 50 ± 150 mg CGAs (Clifford, 1999),
depending
on
plant
variety,
roasting
conditions
and
household
preparations. The dose dependent bioavailability of coffee-derived
CGAs is still under discussion (Renouf et al., 2014; Stalmach,
Williamson, & Crozier, 2014) due to pre-absorptive transformations in
the intestines (Erk et al., 2012; Monteiro, Farah, Perrone, Trugo, &
Donangelo, 2007; Renouf et al., 2010), extensive phase-I/-II metabolic
transformations upon absorption, and their high binding affinity to
plasma proteins (Rio, Stalmach, Calani, & Crozier, 2010). The resulting
CGA metabolites quantitated in the circulation are currently under in-
vestigation for their contribution to coffee’s widely recognized anti-
oxidant activity in vivo which may also depend on the method by which
the beverage is prepared (Martini et al., 2016). Extensive research has

also focused on the optimization of roasting conditions, hypothesizing a
high antioxidant potential of roasting-derived Maillard reaction pro-
ducts, namely high molecular weight melanoidins (Opitz et al., 2017),
and their ability to covalently bind low molecular weight phenolic
antioxidants (Delgado-Andrade & Morales, 2005). Here, the lack of
precise structure information and quantitative data for high molecular
weight melanoidins formed during coffee roasting and knowledge
about their metabolic pathways upon digestion, absorption and meta-
bolic transformations prevents the mechanistic evaluation of their an-
tioxidant potential in vivo.
Among low molecular weight products generated during coffee
roasting, N-methylpyridinium (NMP), formed by thermal degradation
of trigonelline, has not only been demonstrated to be positively corre-
lated with the roasting degree applied to arabica coffee (Stadler et al.,
2002). In an activity-guided screening approach by means of in vitro
antioxidant tests, cell culture experiments, and a proof-of-concept an-
imal feeding experiment, NMP has also been identified as a key anti-
oxidative and chemopreventively active principle in coffee (Somoza
et al., 2003). Mechanistically, the chemical structure of the alkylpyr-
idinium NMP does not bear any antioxidant motif and cannot act as an
antioxidant in vitro. Its antioxidant activity is limited to in vivo condi-
tions or isolated cells in culture, where it has been shown to increase
nuclear Nrf2 translocation, transcription of antioxidant response ele-
ment (ARE)-dependent genes, and expression of associated proteins
involved in the cellular antioxidant defence, e.g. glutathione-S-trans-
ferase (Boettler, Sommerfeld et al., 2011; Boettler, Volz et al., 2011). In
a human intervention trial, a dark roast market blend beverage high in
NMP (785 μmol/L) and low in CGAs (523 μmol/L) showed stronger
antioxidant effects on red blood cells than a coffee prepared from a light
roast with reverse proportions (CGAs: 4538 μmol/L; NMP: 56 μmol/L).
After a 4 week intervention period, consumption of 500 mL of the dark
roast blend increased vitamin E and glutathione concentrations in red
blood cells by 41% and 14%, respectively (Kotyczka et al., 2011),
whereas the light roast coffee had no effect. Whether the increased
vitamin E content in red blood cells after dark roast coffee consumption
contributed to the overall antioxidant status in the circulation by, e.g.,
reducing the levels of oxidized LDL, is not known yet. Data from dietary
intake records did not point to an increased intake of vitamin E during
the coffee intervention applied, suggesting metabolic changes as re-
sponsible factors for the elevated vitamin E concentrations in red blood
cells.
Among the different vitamin E homologs, alpha-tocopherol is the
predominant lipid soluble antioxidant in vivo. Chemically, its hydroxyl
group of the chromanol ring can donate a hydrogen atom to peroxyl
radicals, thereby protecting polyunsaturated fatty acids in membranes
and lipoproteins from oxidation (Niki & Traber, 2012), whereas, in
cellular systems, it activates the transcription factor Nrf2 by binding to
the antioxidant response element (Masoudi, Ploen, Kunz, & Hildt,
2014).
According to the 'oxidation theory' of atherosclerosis, oxidation of
low density lipoprotein (LDL) contributes to atherogenesis, since (i)
oxidized LDL (oxLDL) exhibits potentially pro-atherogenic activities
and (ii) lipoproteins isolated from atherosclerotic lesions are oxidized
(Linna, Ahotupa, Löppönen, Irjala, & Vasankari, 2013; Toshima et al.,
2000; Trpkovic et al., 2015). Vitamin E is generally considered as an-
tioxidant that effectively ameliorates lipid peroxidation of lipoproteins
(Cynshi & Stocker, 2005). Most of the dietary sources for vitamin E
contain a mixture of vitamin E homologs, of which alpha- and gamma-
tocopherol are most prevalent in foods. After dietary intake, alpha-to-
copherol is preferentially incorporated into cellular membranes and
LDL particles, transported via specific transport proteins, and shows
higher vitamin E and antioxidant activities in vivo (Niki & Traber,
2012), whereas in foods, the gamma-tocopherol homolog is the more
effective antioxidant (Huang, Frankel, & German, 1994). The European
Food Safety Agency (EFSA) considers protection of LDL particles from
oxidative damage a beneficial physiological effect (EFSA NDA Panel,
2011). However, although the effect of various dietary factors has been
studied widely (Lapointe, Couillard, & Lemieux, 2006) and, in parti-
cular, the effect of coffee consumption on oxLDL has been explored, no
conclusions regarding antioxidant effectiveness of coffee has been
reached based on human intervention trials (Agudelo-Ochoa et al.,
2016; Corrêa et al., 2012).
Here, we hypothesized consumption of a dark-roast coffee to im-
prove plasma oxLDL and alpha-tocopherol status. This hypothesis was
followed as secondary outcome measure in a study that was aimed at
elucidating whether a dark-roast coffee consumption has an effect on
levels of DNA strand breaks in blood cells as the primary endpoint
(Pahlke et al., 2019). Since (i) increased levels of DNA strand breaks
might, among other factors, result from a lack of antioxidants and (ii)
coffee consumption has been shown to increase vitamin E concentra-
tions in red blood cells, which very unlikely results from coffee-derived
vitamin E intake, in this work, we elucidated whether a previously
reported
coffee-induced
lipolysis
(Portugal-Alvarez,
Zamarron,
Yanguela, Perezagua, & Velasco, 1973) might be one of the underlying
mechanisms.
2. Experimental section
2.1. Study design and intervention
This 12-week randomized, prospective, controlled, parallel study
was
conducted
at
the
Department
of
Physiological
Chemistry,
University of Vienna, according to the guidelines laid down in the
Declaration of Helsinki and all procedures were approved by the ethical
committee of the University of Vienna (no 00202). In order to limit bias
as much as possible, the randomization protocol was disclosed to the
personnel involved in conducting the clinical trial and/or analyzing the
data after statistical analysis. Due to working with coded samples, all
laboratory experiments were blinded, rendering our intervention
single-blind. The randomization protocol was disclosed to the study
personnel collecting the samples. Due to working with coded samples
the laboratory personnel carrying out the measurements was blinded,
rendering our intervention single-blind. Study participants gave their
written informed consent for participation after detailed study in-
formation, and were respected for their privacy rights during the entire
study. For sample size calculation by means of G*Power 3.0.10 soft-
ware, the expected effect size was derived from the results of Bakuradze
et al. (2014), resulting in an estimated sample size of 39 per group as
calculated for the comparison between coffee and control group with
respect to levels of DNA strand breaks in blood cells as the primary
endpoint. Assuming a normal distribution of the data, the level of sig-
nificance was set at 5%, with a required power of 80%. Assuming a
drop-out rate of 10% and higher standard deviations induced by the
inclusion of women, a group size of 50 subjects was chosen, and a block
randomization procedure was used to assign subjects to treatment
groups.
In total, 136 metabolically healthy volunteers with a BMI between
19 and 32 kg/m2 were recruited in October 2016 and underwent a
medical screening prior to the intervention. Exclusion criteria were
smoking, alcohol or drug abuse and use of medication (except oral
contraceptives). Metabolic disorders as well as pregnancies were ex-
cluded by a medical screening as described earlier (Hochkogler et al.,
2014). Body height was obtained with a precision of 0.1% by means of a
stadiometer (Seca 213, Germany) and body weight was determined
with a digital scale (Seca Bella 840, Germany) to the nearest of 0.1 kg.
The screening led to 98 eligible subjects which were randomly al-
located to two groups (control group: n = 49, 25 females, 24 males and
coffee group: n = 49, 25 females, 24 males). Test and control group
were supposed to consist of men and women in equal numbers.
Furthermore, BMI was considered as possible confounding factor.
Therefore, subjects were divided in 4 blocks according gender and BMI
(higher or lower than overall mean BMI). A block randomization

reliable marker for coffee consumption, N-methylpyridinium (Lang,

Moreover, during the entire study period, volunteers had to refrain
from any other caffeine-containing food and drinks. They were also
instructed to avoid the consumption of polyphenol-rich food e.g. red
wine, cocoa, berry juices, as well as from any intensely coloured fruits
and vegetables or juices that were not listed in Supplemental material,

dietary habits.
2.2. Study population
Of the 98 volunteers allocated to either control (n = 49) or coffee
group (n = 49), one volunteer in each group dropped out during the
run-in period. In the course of the intervention another volunteer of the
coffee group did not continue participation due to personal reasons.
Exclusion of noncompliant subjects, as indicated by higher urine NMP/
creatinine concentrations than expected under abstention from coffee
consumption (i.e. > 0.2 nmol/µmol) as well as concentrations < 0.2
nmol/µmol during coffee consumption from the per protocol set re-
sulted in 44 and 42 subjects in the control and coffee group, respec-
tively, who were included in the statistical analysis. Baseline char-
acteristics are shown in Table 1. For all lipid parameters, additional 4
volunteers were excluded from the statistical analysis due to im-
plausible results for lipid contents of which the analytical measure-
ments could not be repeated due to limited sample volume (control
group n = 43 and coffee group n = 39).
2.3. Coffee characterization and coffee preparation
Volunteers in the coffee group were provided with a common
Senseo®-type pad-machine and they were asked to prepare the coffee
beverage freshly in the morning, midday and afternoon. In total, the
daily quantity was 3 large cups in equal portions, brewed from 6 pads
(3 × 2), each filled with 7.5 g coffee, and extracted with tap water to
achieve 250 mL of brewed beverage. The coffee had to be consumed
without the addition of milk, but the use of sugar was allowed.
For consumption, the Coffee C21 beverage was freshly prepared
with a common Senseo®-type pad-machine. The quantity of the coffee
was 3 large cups (250 mL) per day, brewed from 6 pads (3 × 2), each
filled with 7.5 g coffee, and extracted with tap water to achieve 250 mL
of brewed beverage.
The C21 coffee administered (45 g/d) was industrially manu-
factured according to one of our previous studies (Bakuradze et al.,
2015). Concentration ranges of this industrially produced C21 samples
for NMP and CGAs per gram coffee powder were 1.11–1.13 mg/g and

2.4. Dietary records
In the last week of the run-in and the intervention period, study
subjects had to document their food intake for a time period of 7 days.
Energy, macronutrient and vitamin E intake was then determined on
the basis of the food records, using the nutrition software GloboDiet
(previously EPIC-soft) for assessment and the German food composition
database Bundeslebensmittelschlüssel 3.02. for calculation of energy
and nutrient intake (Max et al.).
2.5. Test day
During the entire study period, the subjects refrained from all coffee
beverages (except study coffee for the intervention group in the inter-
vention phase), tea (black, green, white), and caffeine drinks. They also
refrained from red wine, cocoa, malt coffee, dark chocolate and berry
juices, nutritional supplements (such as vitamin supplements, herbal
extracts), multivitamin juice as well from any intensely coloured fruits
and vegetables or juices.
For the start of the intervention, volunteers were asked to come to
the institute at the end of the run-in period (day 28), after an overnight
fast at 8 a.m., and were administered either 250 mL of warm water of
coffee and a pretzel. Blood samples were taken two hours thereafter,
between 10 a.m. and 11 a.m. This procedure was repeated at the end of
the intervention period (day 84).
In between, body composition, fat and fat free mass, was assessed in
underwear by means of the BodPod® (COSMED USA, Concord, CA), a
system based on air displacement plethysmography (ADP) technology.
Moreover, body weight was determined by a digital precision scale.

2.6. Quantification of NMP in urine samples for compliance analyses
First morning urine samples were taken at day 28, after the run-in
period, as well as at day 56 and 84 of the intervention. Urine was ali-
quoted and immediately frozen at −80 °C. The quantification of NMP
was carried out by means of HILIC–HPLC–MS/MS according to Lang
et al. (2011). However, urine which was taken at day 56, was analyzed
only in a subset of 25 randomly selected samples per group for NMP
quantification.
2.7. Blood sample collection
Venous blood samples were collected in either EDTA-coated or in
serum tubes (without preparation) (Sarstedt, Germany). In order to
obtain plasma samples, EDTA-coated tubes were centrifuged at 1800g
at 4 °C for 15 min. For serum samples, blood was clotted within 30 min
and afterwards centrifuged at 1800g at 4 °C for 15 min. All blood
samples were stored in aliquots at −80 °C until further analyses.
2.8. Alpha-tocopherol concentrations in the coffee beverage and in plasma
samples
For quantification of alpha-tocopherol in the coffee beverage ad-
ministered, 250 mL of the beverage were concentrated under vacuum to
a final volume of 30 mL. This concentrate was subjected to a 50 mL
methyl-tert-butylether extraction, which was repeated trice, followed
by a washing step using 100 mL H2O, dried by means of sodium sulfate,
concentrated to dryness under vacuum and finally solved in hexane for
HPLC/UV analysis as previously described (Pignitter et al., 2014).
Plasma concentrations of alpha-tocopherol were quantified ac-
cordingly (Pignitter et al., 2014). For sample work-up, plasma was
treated with hexane for extraction of lipid soluble constituents and
separated on a reversed-phase column using methanol as the mobile
phase. Quantitation of alpha-tocopherol was performed by external
standard calibration curves. Adjusted alpha-tocopherol levels were
calculated as vitamin E per triglycerides (µmol/mmol) (Mehmetoglu,
Yerlikaya, & Kurban, 2011).
2.9. Non-esterified fatty acid and triglyceride levels, lipase activity in
plasma samples
Lipase activity in plasma samples and plasma concentration of non-
esterified fatty acids were analyzed by commercially available enzy-
matic assay kits (Cayman Europe) following the instructions given in
the manufacturer's protocols. Whereas serum triglycerides were as-
sessed photometrically by a medical laboratory (Labor Dr. Greiner,
Vienna, Austria).
2.10. LDL, oxidized LDL levels and oxidized triglyceride levels
Serum LDL-levels were analyzed photometrically by a medical la-
boratory (Labor Dr. Greiner, Vienna, Austria). Plasma oxLDL levels
were determined by means of a sandwich ELISA kit (Mercodia AB,
Uppsala, Sweden) according to the instructions from the manufacturer.
Oxidized triglycerides were detected by means of LC-MS/MS. Plasma
proteins were precipitated by adding methanol to plasma at a ratio of
3:1. Silica columns were used to isolate polar lipids from plasma. The
polar fraction was stored at −80 °C until analyses with LC-MS. LC-MS
analyses were performed in MRM(+) mode applying a total nitrogen
gas flow of 15 L/min and a collision-induced dissociation energy of
20 V. All analyses were conducted in duplicates on an HPLC system
(Shimadzu Prominence) equipped with a triple-quadrupole mass spec-
trometer (Shimadzu LCMS-8040) with ESI as interface. Samples (10 µL)
were injected on a C18 column (Kinetex EVO, 150 × 3.0 mm, 5 µm,
Phenomenex) using acetonitrile/H2O (60/40) with 0.1% formic acid
and 10 mM ammonium formate (A) and acetonitrile/isopropanol (20/
80) with 0.1% formic acid and 10 mM ammonium formate (B) as a
mobile phase with a flow rate at 0.5 mL/min at 25 °C. Within 8 min
solvent B was increased from 60 to 100%, keeping 100% B for 20 min.
Prior to injection the samples passed through a Sterican® filter
(0.8 × 40 mm). The mass spectrometer was operated in positive-ion
mode. Data integration was performed with Shimadzu LCMS software
LabSolutions Version 5”.
2.11. Statistical analyses
Computational statistics were performed using the software SAS
V9.0. First, normal distribution of variables was analyzed by the
Shapiro-Wilk test. In case of non-rejection of the normality hypothesis,
analysis of covariance (ANCOVA) was performed in order to compare
the test and the control group with baseline values as covariates. In case
the hypothesis of normally distributed data was rejected, Wilcoxońs
rank sum test (WRST) on base of intra-individual differences to baseline
was used.
Homogeneity of groups at baseline was checked by t-test. In case of
rejection of the hypothesis of normally distributed data, the WRST was
used alternatively. Changes over time per treatment group were eval-
uated by t-tests for paired samples or Wilcoxon signed rank test.
Analyses were based on the per protocol (PP) population, resulting
in the exclusion of data from (i) subjects with missing observations, or
(ii) from noncompliant subjects, as indicated higher urine NMP/crea-
tinine concentrations than it should be expected under abstention from
coffee consumption (i.e. > 0.2 nmol/µmol).
3. Results
The aim of the here presented work was to investigate the effects of
a daily 750 mL consumption of a dark-roast coffee on the status of
plasma alpha-tocopherol levels. Overall, the coffee beverage was well
accepted and no clinically relevant adverse effects appeared in the
coffee intervention group.
3.1. Quantification of NMP in urine samples
Quantitative analyses revealed that urine NMP/creatinine con-
centrations of compliant subjects were lower than 0.2 nmol/µmol after
completion of the “run-in” period in which participants had to abstain
from coffee for 4 weeks. Non-compliant subjects were identified by
respective concentrations ranging from 0.46 to 87 nmol NMP per µmol
creatinine. As a consequence, these four subjects were excluded from
further statistical analyses. Further two volunteers of the coffee group
failed compliance because the concentrations were below the threshold
of 0.2 nmol/µmol, indicating that participants did not follow the in-
structions of consuming 750 mL of the coffee beverage daily. Mean
values of NMP/creatinine concentrations in the coffee group during the
intervention period were 10.7 ± 7.57 nmol/µmol.
Moreover, three volunteers allocated to the control group were
excluded since they also failed compliance, as shown by NMP/creati-
nine concentrations between 1.13 and 4.38 nmol/µmol.
3.2. Food records
Volunteers were instructed to compile food records for 7 days at the
end of the run-in period and intervention period. Energy and macro-
nutrient intake as well as vitamin E intake remained unchanged during
the intervention period in both groups. Moreover, no statistically sig-
nificant group differences for the median changes of any of the assessed
parameters including fibre were calculated (Table 2).

3.4. Alpha-Tocopherol levels in coffee beverage and plasma samples
Quantification of vitamin E in the coffee beverage revealed a mean
0.24 ± 0.04 mg per 750 mL (triplicate analysis). Changes in total
plasma alpha-tocopherol concentrations are demonstrated in Fig. 2A. A
significant difference in means of alpha-tocopherol concentrations be-
tween groups could be shown after 8-weeks of intervention (p = 0.023,
WRST). This difference between groups is mainly due to a significant
decrease in mean alpha-tocopherol level in the control group, whereas
plasma alpha-tocopherol concentrations in volunteers of the coffee
group remained unchanged (mean change in the control group −
0.49 ± 1.19 µg/mL, mean change in the coffee group 0.05 ± 1.07 µg/
mL, p < 0.05, WRST).
Since plasma levels of fat-soluble vitamins like alpha-tocopherol are
closely linked to the amount of plasma lipids and might contribute to a
high variation, the quantified concentrations were further adjusted for
total plasma triglyceride concentrations. As shown in Fig. 2B, in both
groups adjusted alpha-tocopherol levels were significantly reduced
after 8 weeks of intervention (control group: −6.33 ± 29.8%; coffee
group: −12.2 ± 25.0%, p < 0.05). The comparison of mean changes
in the adjusted alpha-tocopherol levels between the two groups did not
reveal any statistically significant difference (p > 0.05, WRST).

4. Discussion
Recent epidemiologic evidence suggests moderate coffee consump-
tion of 3–5 cups per day to be associated with a lower prevalence of
subclinical coronary atherosclerosis (Choi et al., 2015), with the intake
of coffee antioxidants as one of the key mechanisms. Evidence from
clinical trials seems to be more diverse, as the reports on effects of
coffee on plasma antioxidant capacity and oxLDL concentrations vary
substantially, depending on the type of coffee, administered doses and
duration of exposure (Martini et al., 2016). Moreover, the lack of
comprehensive chemical characterization of the coffee brew adminis-
tered in the studies reviewed, not only makes it difficult to repeat a
clinical trial, but also prevents final conclusions on the most active
principle in coffee brews. However, health effects of individual coffee
constituents can be evaluated by testing coffee beverages w/o for-
tification of the target compound, although the impact of other coffee
constituents on the outcome measure chosen cannot be excluded.
A class of coffee constituents which has been most widely studied
for
its
anti-atherosclerotic
and
anti-oxidant
effects,
are
CGAs
(Amarowicz & Pegg, 2017). In a recent randomized, cross-over, con-
trolled clinical trial, consumption of a 35:65 (w/w) mixture of soluble
green and regular coffee in an amount of 6 g/day (4 cups) containing
high amounts of CGAs improved antioxidant capacity and reduced
further plasma markers of cardiovascular risk, like, e.g., total choles-
terol, in moderately hypercholesterolemic subjects, but not in healthy
individuals (Martinez-Lopez, Sarria, Mateos, & Bravo-Clemente, 2018).

These results might indicate a limited efficacy of CGAs in normocho-
lesterolemic subjects, which might be explained by their relatively low
bioavailability due to phase-I/-II metabolic transformations after ab-
sorption, and their high binding affinity to plasma proteins (Rio et al.,
2010) or by the fact that dietary interventions in general are more ef-
fective in compromised subjects than in healthy individuals. On the
other hand, CGAs might not be the only antioxidants in coffee. Besides
the chemically poorly characterized high molecular weight melanoidins
(Delgado-Andrade & Morales, 2005; Opitz et al., 2017), low molecular
weight compounds formed during the roasting process of coffee beans,
namely NMP, have been studied for their antioxidant effects in vivo
(Boettler, Sommerfeld et al., 2011; Boettler, Volz et al., 2011; Somoza
et al., 2003).
NMP has also been suggested as specific indicator of coffee con-
sumption (Lang et al., 2011), and to be chiefly responsible for a dark-
roast coffee blend being less effective in stimulating gastric acid se-
cretion in healthy volunteers compared to a medium roast market blend
(Rubach et al., 2012, 2014). At plasma relevant concentrations of about
0.9 µM (Lang et al., 2010), NMP has also been shown to decrease fatty
acid uptake in human liver cells in culture (Riedel et al., 2014).
In one of our recent intervention trials, we could demonstrate a dark
roast market blend beverage high in NMP (785 μmol/L) and low in
CGAs (523 μmol/L) to exhibit stronger antioxidant effects on red blood
cells and to improve plasma vitamin E and glutathione more effectively
by 41% and 14%, respectively, than a coffee prepared from a light roast
with opposite proportions (CGAs: 4538 μmol/L; NMP: 56 μmol/L)
(Kotyczka et al., 2011). However, whether the increased vitamin E
content in red blood cells after coffee consumption contributed to the
overall antioxidant status in the circulation was still not known. In the
here presented study, daily consumption of 3 large cups of a dark-roast
coffee (250 mL), brewed from 45 g coffee powder containing around
50 mg NMP, 423 mg CGAs and 301 mg caffeine, improved plasma
alpha-tocopherol as predominant vitamin E homolog in vivo, and re-
duced oxLDL in healthy subjects without affecting nutrient intake, body
weight or body composition. A contribution of coffee-derived vitamin E
intake can be neglected as the mean vitamin E ingested with 750 mL
coffee was 0.24 mg, accounting for about 2% of the daily vitamin E
intake in Austria (Rust et al., 2017).
The group difference for changes in total plasma alpha-tocopherol
concentrations assessed during the intervention period clearly demon-
strated that consumption of a dark-roast coffee improved plasma alpha-
tocopherol levels compared to equal consumption of hot water. The
mean increase in plasma alpha-tocopherol was 3.46 ± 16.5%. For
oxLDL, a mean decrease of 4.27 ± 38.3% was demonstrated. Here, we
hypothesize the increased alpha-tocopherol concentrations in the
plasma being caused by lipolysis since concentrations of non-esterified
fatty acids increased after coffee intervention as well. Moreover, there
was no inter-group difference when alpha-tocopherol concentrations
were adjusted for those of non-esterified fatty acids.
Considering the reduced plasma oxLDL concentrations, and alpha-
tocopherol‘s antioxidant activity on isolated LDLs (Cynshi & Stocker,
2005), we also hypothesize a significant contribution of the increased
plasma concentrations of alpha-tocopherol to this finding. Future stu-
dies will need to elucidate which coffee constituent(s) are chiefly re-
sponsible for this effect: whether and to what extend CGAs, NMP, tri-
gonelline, or other, chemically yet unknown constituents, individually
or synergistically promote lipolysis has not been clarified yet. Although
for a bolus dose of 250 mg caffeine, a lipolytic effect in healthy subjects
has been described before (Patwardhan et al., 1980), the lipolytic effect
seen in this study cannot confidently be ascribed to the amount of
300 mg of caffeine administered through the coffee intervention solely,
especially since NMP has been demonstrated to modulate cellular up-
take of free fatty acids in vitro and a lipolytic effect of CGAs has recently
been shown in human adipocyte explants (Flanagan, Bily, Rolland, &
Roller, 2014).
4.1. Limitations of the study
This study has some strengths but also some limitations. The
strengths include the controlled setting and the large population size as
well as the relatively long intervention period. The lack of measuring
body fat distribution might be one possible limitation of our study since
in the here presented study only total body fat was analysed and po-
tential changes in body fat distribution or reduction in visceral or ab-
dominal body fat were not studied. Moreover, seasonal effects and ef-
fects associated with changes in habitual physical activities cannot
completely be excluded in intra-group analyses. Lacking a complete
chemical characterization of the coffee beverage, with quantitative data
for all constituents possibly acting as antioxidants in vivo leaves the key
antioxidatively active compound still unknown.

4.2. Conclusions
The 8 week administration of 750 mL of a dark-roast coffee im-
proved plasma oxidized LDL and alpha-tocopherol levels in healthy
subjects when compared to a water control group. We, first, hypothe-
size the improved plasma alpha-tocopherol levels to contribute to the
reduced levels of oxidized LDL due to the antioxidant activity of alpha-
tocopherol. Second, we hypothesize augmented plasma alpha-toco-
pherol levels by coffee-induced lipolysis, as indicated by an increase in
plasma non-esterified fatty acids and plasma LDL. Future studies need
to quantify differential effects on body fat stores, e.g. abdominal and
visceral fat tissue, and have to identify the key active antioxidant and
lipolytic principles in coffee beverages.
