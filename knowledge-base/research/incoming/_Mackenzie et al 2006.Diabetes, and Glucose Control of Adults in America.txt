Beverage Intake, Diabetes, and Glucose Control of Adults in America
TODD MACKENZIE, PHD, BLAIR BROOKS, MD, AND GERRY O’CONNOR, PHD, DSC
PURPOSE: Beverages are important components of diet and a route for the intake of caffeine, ethanol,
and other bioactive substances. The aim of the study is to examine the association between type of bever-
ages consumed and glucose control in American adults with and without diabetes.
METHODS: Diabetes status, glycosylated hemoglobin (hemoglobin A1c [HbA1c]) level, and 1-month re-
call food frequency questionnaires were all collected in the Third National Health and Nutrition Exami-
nation Survey (1988 to 1994), based on a nationally representative sample of the noninstitutionalized
civilian US population. We used regression and other methods for clustered data to examine the association
of HbA1c levels with self-reported intake of carbonated drinks, alcohol, coffee, tea, juices, and milk in par-
ticipants aged 18 to 75 years with and without diabetes.
RESULTS: Adults with diabetes reported drinking half the amount of alcohol as adults without diabetes.
Compared with nondrinkers, subjects who had 30 or more drinks per month of alcohol had mean HbA1c
levels 1.2 units less (p ! 0.001) in persons with diabetes and 0.2% less (p ! 0.001) in persons without di-
abetes. Adults with diabetes reported drinking three times as much diet soda as adults without diabetes.
However, in adults with diabetes who had one or more drinks of diet soda per day, HbA1c level was 0.7 units
greater (p ! 0.001) compared with those who drank none.
CONCLUSIONS: Alcohol consumption, at least in moderate amounts, correlates with better glucose
control. There is a correlation between drinking diet soda and glucose control in adults with diabetes.

INTRODUCTION
Several lines of evidence suggest that risk for diabetes and
glucose control in persons with diabetes are both associated
with choice in beverages. Moderate alcohol consumption
was associated with decreased risk for diabetes, whereas in-
creased risk was linked to having four or more alcoholic
drinks per day (1). Moderate intake of alcohol was associat-
ed with improved glucose control in men with type 2 diabe-
tes (2). High intake of sugar-sweetened beverages, such as
sodas, was associated with an increase in incidence of type
2 diabetes in middle-aged women (3). Caffeine, a stimulant
in coffee, tea, and colas, acutely impairs glucose metabolism
and decreases insulin sensitivity in adults with and without
diabetes (4–9). However, heavy coffee drinking was associ-
ated with a decreased risk for diabetes in multiple longitudi-
nal observational studies (10–13). Oolong, a tea from the
same plant as green and black tea, substantially decreased
fasting blood glucose levels in adults with diabetes (14)
when consumed in large amounts (1.5 L/d) for a month.
The association of glucose control with intake of alcohol,
sodas, coffee, tea, juices, and milk in adults with diabetes has
not been examined. Similarly, the association of glycosy-
lated hemoglobin (hemoglobin A1c [HbA1c]) levels with
beverage choices in adults without diabetes has not been in-
vestigated. To explore these issues, we assessed the associa-
tion of HbA1c levels with consumption of various beverages
in persons with and without diabetes, using the Third Na-

regression was used to assess the association of HbA1c lev-

RESULTS
NHANES III obtained information regarding diabetes and
beverage intake for approximately 14,900 adults between
the ages of 18 and 75 years. Of these, 1024 adults reported
a physician diagnosis of diabetes (excluding gestational dia-
betes), as described by Saaddine et al. (17). In brief, 581 par-
ticipants with diabetes were women, 443 were men, 236
were aged 18 to 49 years, 495 were aged 50 to 64 years,
393 were aged 65 to 75 years, 303 were non-Hispanic whites,
326 were African Americans, 364 were Mexican American ,
and 31 were of ‘‘other’’ race. Insulin was used by 310 adults
with diabetes; 413 of the remainder were using oral hypogly-
cemics, and 240 reported using neither.

DISCUSSION
In this nationally representative cross-sectional study, we
identiﬁed an inverse correlation of HbA1c level and alcohol
intake in adults with and without diabetes. In adults with di-
abetes, those who reported drinking 30 or more drinks of al-
cohol per month had a mean HbA1c level 1.2% less than
those who drank no alcohol. This association was restricted
to persons with diabetes who reported no insulin or oral hy-
poglycemic use. In adults without diabetes, the difference
was only 4%. There also was a correlation between HbA1c
level and intake of diet soda in adults with diabetes. No as-
sociations were found between HbA1c level and drinking
tea, coffee, juice, and milk.
Our ﬁnding that adults with diabetes who drank alcohol
had better glucose control is supported by a small random-
ized controlled trial (18) that found that acute alcohol
consumption improved insulin action in persons with
type 2 diabetes. A systematic review (1) concluded that
moderate intake of alcohol does not impair glucose control
and suggested that it might improve control. Our ﬁnding of
an association of HbA1c level and alcohol in adults with-
out diabetes is consistent with a randomized crossover trial
that showed that alcohol improved postprandial glucose
levels in postmenopausal women without diabetes (19).
Our ﬁnding that diet soda consumption is associated with
poor glucose control has little support in other literature.
One explanation is that in adults with diabetes, the choice
of a sugar-free beverage may be more likely in patients
who have a tendency to poor glucose control. However, a re-
cent study in mice found that intake of artiﬁcial sweeteners
increased food intake and weight (20), which can, in turn,
worsen glucose control.
We did not identify a clinically relevant association of
HbA1c levels with caffeinated coffee drinking in adults
with or without diabetes. This ﬁnding is somewhat at odds
with recent observational studies (10–13) that showed
that high daily coffee consumption is related to a lower in-
cidence of diabetes.
This study identiﬁed no association between caffeinated
tea consumption and glucose control in adults with diabetes.
A randomized controlled trial of Taiwanese adults with dia-
betes found that drinking 1.5 L/d of oolong tea (derived from
the same plant as green and black teas) for a month decreased
mean fasting blood glucose levels from 220 to 160 mg/dL
(14). Our negative ﬁnding could be explained by insufﬁcient
power because only 30 NHANES III participants with diabe-
tes reported drinking two or more cups of tea per day.

This study has several limitations. First, the data we
used are cross-sectional and therefore we can make no
ﬁrm conclusions about the direction of causality between
glucose control and beverage consumption. For example,
the interpretation that diet soda consumption worsens
glucose control is no more tenable than the reverse-cau-
sality interpretation that adults with worse diabetes
choose a glucose-free soda in favor of regular soda. An-
other limitation of the NHANES III survey is that assess-
ment of beverage consumption was through self-report,
which is open to measurement error and bias. Measure-
ment error is in both the actual number of beverages con-
sumed in the past month and use of this ﬁgure as an
estimate of how many drinks are consumed in the average
month. Furthermore, the food-frequency questionnaire
made no speciﬁcations about portion size, which may
vary between participants and within participants be-
tween different times. This would have the effect of at-
tenuating associations. The bias could arise if persons
with diabetes underreport alcohol consumption. For ex-
ample, it is possible that adults with diabetes and poor
glucose control reported drinking more diet soda and
less alcohol simply because these habits are encouraged
in persons with diabetes, not because that is what they ac-
tually drank. Finally, we are limited by the age of the sur-
vey, which was conducted 10 years ago. This is relevant
to our ﬁnding of an association of diet soda and glucose
control because there have been changes in the types of
artiﬁcial sweeteners (saccharin, aspartame, acesulfame,
and sucralose) used in diet sodas since NHANES III. New-
er NHANES surveys (1999 to 2000 and 2001 to 2002)
do not ask questions about beverages other than milk.
In conclusion, this study shows an inverse cross-sectional
association of intake of alcoholic beverages with glucose
control in American adults with diabetes and a detrimental
association of diet soda consumption with glucose control.
These ﬁndings indicate the need to investigate further the
effect of beverage choice on glucose control by using pro-
spective studies.
