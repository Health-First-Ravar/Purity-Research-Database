Original Scientific Paper
Coffee and myocardial infarction: heterogeneity of an
association in Portuguese men
Ana Azevedo and Henrique Barros
Department of Hygiene and Epidemiology, University of Porto Medical School, Porto, Portugal.
Received 10 October 2004 Accepted 21January 2006
Background Despite the amount of published studies on the subject, controversy still exists about the effect of coffee
drinking on the occurrence of coronary heart disease. In general, epidemiological studies do not support the belief that it is
a major risk factor.
Design A community-based case–control study was conducted.
Methods A random sample of men aged 40 years and over with no previous myocardial infarction was compared with
patients with a first acute myocardial infarction (AMI). A structured questionnaire was used to collect information on
sociodemographic characteristics, personal and family medical history and lifestyles. Dietary data were obtained by a food
frequency questionnaire. Odds ratios were calculated by unconditional logistic regression. After exclusions, 290 cases and
364 controls were considered in the final analysis.
Results Coffee drinking is a widespread habit in this population, and most individuals did not change their habit throughout
life. Most participants reported the consumption of moderate amounts of coffee ( < 25% drank more than four cups a day)
and espresso was by far the most commonly used brewing. The crude odds ratio for having ever drunk coffee regularly was
0.5 (95% confidence interval 0.3–1.1). However, there was a significant effect–measure modification by family history of
AMI (P = 0.02).
Conclusions The results suggest different susceptibility to coffee drinking, such that coffee drinking was positively
although not significantly associated with an increased risk of AMI in men with a family history of AMI, whereas there was a
significant inverse association with the occurrence of myocardial infarction among men with no family history of AMI. Eur J

Introduction
Several decades of investigation on the relationship
between coffee consumption and coronary heart disease
(CHD) illustrate how unclear the subject remains. In
western countries, the widespread exposure to coffee and
the high frequency of CHD would result in an enormous
impact at the population level even for a weak associa-
tion. There is a rationale for thinking that coffee might be
associated with an increased risk of CHD, namely
through its putative effects on blood pressure [1–4],
arrhythmia [5], serum lipids [1,6–9], calcium balance
[10]
or
levels
of
homocysteine
[11–13].
However,
epidemiological evidence suggests that coffee is not a
major risk factor for CHD [14–18]. Some studies reported
a modest positive association, with relative risks consis-
tently below 1.5 for intakes higher than four cups per day
[14,19,20], which could, however, arise from combined
residual confounding by such factors as smoking, diet and
exercise [14,21,22]. Drinking coffee correlates well with
these and other known risk factors for CHD, and it might
just be a lifestyle marker [21].
However, the hypothesis of different effects of coffee
across the strata of other exposure variables was not ruled

modifier of carcinogenic exposures, based on its effects
on a large variety of physiological, cellular and molecular

Methods
A community-based case–control study was designed and
conducted in an urban sample of Portuguese adults. Cases
and controls were men and women aged 40 years or more,
with no previous acute myocardial infarction (AMI),
which scored more than 23 points in the mini-mental
state examination [24] if older than 64 years. Cases were
patients consecutively admitted to Hospital de S. Joa˜o, in
Porto, with a diagnosis of first AMI who survived beyond
the fourth day after the acute episode. Controls were
living in the hospital catchment area and were recruited
by random digit dialing: from each household one person
was randomly selected among all inhabitants older than
39 years, as previously reported [25]. In this report only
data on men are presented.
Among 413 community participants older than 39 years
who agreed to participate, 28 were excluded because of
previous myocardial infarction, seven were excluded
because of a low score on the mini-mental state
examination, and 14 were excluded because of an
unknown family history of AMI, leaving 364 controls for
analysis. Among 384 cases of first AMI, 71 were excluded
because of death during the first 4 days after infarction, or
disability that prevented them from answering, eight had
missing information on key variables and 15 were
excluded because of an unknown family history of AMI,
290 cases remained for analysis.
Trained interviewers used a structured questionnaire to
obtain information on social and demographic character-
istics, personal and family medical history, smoking and
physical activity; anthropometric data were also recorded
(weight, height, waist and hip circumference). The
presence of hypertension, dyslipidaemia and diabetes
mellitus was considered as self-reported. A positive family
history of AMI was considered when one or more first-
degree relatives (parents or siblings) had had an AMI or
sudden death. Physical activity was categorized in three
levels, taking into account the type of exercise and time
spent in all professional, domestic and leisure-time
activities. As a measure of upper-body obesity we used
the waist-to-hip ratio, which is the simple ratio between
waist and hip circumferences, and categorized it in three
levels according to tertiles of the distribution in controls.
A validated semi-quantitative food frequency question-
naire referring to the previous year was used to assess
dietary factors in detail. In this report we use data from
that questionnaire on total energy intake and caffeine.
Both these continuous variables were categorized in three
levels according to tertiles of the distribution in controls.
Each interviewer assessed both cases and controls, and
the participants were unaware of the hypothesis evalu-
ated in this particular study.
A detailed questionnaire was designed to assess lifetime
coffee consumption. For individuals who ever drank
coffee, information was collected on age at start and end
of
consumption,
type
of
coffee
(espresso,
filtered,
instantaneous, decaffeinated) and daily quantity sepa-
rately for each type of coffee (for individuals who at some
point in their lives changed their pattern of consumption,
the quantity that they usually drank for the longest period
was registered).
Proportions were compared by the chi-squared test or
Fisher’s exact test. Relative risks were estimated by
unconditional logistic regression as odds ratios (OR) and
their 95% confidence intervals (CI). The role of age,
education, smoking, upper-body adiposity, physical activ-
ity, total energy intake, diabetes and family history of
AMI as effect-modifiers or confounders was evaluated by
stratification and was finally considered in the logistic
models.
The study was approved by the local ethics committee
and each participant gave informed consent.
Results
The distribution of cases and controls by categories of the
assessed covariates is shown in Table 1. Cases were less
educated, were more frequently smokers and smoked a
greater daily number of cigarettes, more frequently had
diabetes, and had a higher waist-to-hip ratio. A family
history of AMI or sudden death in first-degree relatives
was significantly more common among cases (crude OR
1.9,
95%
CI
1.3–2.7).
There
were
no
significant
differences in levels of physical activity and energy intake
between cases and controls.
It can be seen in Table 1 that coffee drinking is very
common in this population (96.2% of community controls
reported having ever drunk coffee regularly), and it is
usually a lifelong maintained habit, because only a small
proportion of coffee drinkers stopped drinking coffee
(11.9% of controls and 12.8% of cases, P = 0.73). More-
over, the majority of participants reported the consump-
tion of moderate amounts of coffee (approximately a
quarter drank more than four cups a day). The type of
coffee most commonly drunk was by far espresso and very
few individuals reported drinking only paper-filtered

The crude OR for having ever drunk coffee regularly was

history of AMI, the OR for ever drinking coffee was 0.3
(95% CI 0.2–0.8), and when there was such history the

because of the much smaller sample size (118 non-
smokers without and 40 with a family history of AMI).
Coffee drinking was significantly associated with smoking
(P = 0.007), higher education (P < 0.001), younger age
(P = 0.008), and higher energy intake (P < 0.001); coffee
drinking was more common in non-diabetic individuals,
although not significantly (P = 0.43).

measure

we

interaction term representing effect–measure modifica-
tion by a family history of AMI was significant only when
assessing the life prevalence of coffee consumption and
the consumption of unfiltered coffee. Because of the
small number of participants who reported drinking
filtered coffee (see Table 1), we did not calculate OR
for classes of exposure to other types of coffee. There was
a trend towards a positive interaction when we assessed
dose expressed as cups per day as well as caffeine
consumption during the previous year, but it did not
reach statistical significance.
Discussion
This study shows heterogeneity of the OR as a measure
of the effect of drinking coffee on the risk of AMI in men,
according to the strata of a family history of AMI. In men
with no family history of myocardial infarction the effect
of coffee drinking was essentially protective, whereas in
men with a family history of myocardial infarction it was
non-significantly associated with an increased risk of
AMI. To the best of our knowledge no such heterogeneity
was previously reported. In several studies this variable
has been controlled for as a confounder, but there is no
reference to it as an effect–measure modifier. The meta-
analysis by Kawachi et al. [26] stratified the analyses by
disease endpoint, smoking and sex, and did not find any
effect modification, but it does not mention a family
history of CHD.
The results of this study can be misinterpreted if one
does not keep two facts in mind. First, the study deals
with a population in which coffee drinking is a wide-
spread and usually lifelong sustained habit (the group of
never drinkers is a small proportion of the population and

consumes low to moderate amounts of coffee. It is thus
impossible to evaluate the effect of larger doses of coffee,
and it cannot be ruled out that an increase in risk would be
associated with heavier consumption. A recently published
study has shown a J-shaped effect of coffee consumption
on the risk of developing acute coronary syndromes [27].
The amount of coffee labeled as ‘moderate’ in that study
includes all the categories of cups per day defined in our
own study and was inversely associated with the outcome
in a statistically significant way.
The more commonly reported brewing method is by far
espresso, which can be considered unfiltered because the
metal filter does not retain the diterpenes that are held
by a paper filter [6]. These substances are supposedly
responsible for the effects of coffee on serum lipids.
Nevertheless, an experimental study involving Italian
young adult men [28] demonstrated that after a 6-week
break from coffee consumption that followed a period of
espresso consumption, there was no significant change in
total cholesterol, low-density lipoprotein and high-den-
sity lipoprotein-cholesterol. An evaluation and compar-
ison of other brewing methods could be better assessed
by comparing data from different geographical regions,
because in each place people tend to drink the same type
of coffee. Decaffeinated coffee was rarely used so analysis
was restricted to caffeine-containing coffee.
Methodological problems with sample selection and
information gathering might be a source of error and
introduce bias in our estimations. The age distribution of
our control sample is similar to the general population
of Porto, and more than 95% of the households had a
telephone at the time of recruitment. The relatively high
participation rates and the fact that participants were
similar to refusals for demographic, social and behavioural
variables do not eliminate but reduce the probability of
bias [29].
Recall bias cannot be positively ruled out in a case–
control study, particularly when assessing exposures that
are so imprecisely measured. However, the fact that
participants were not aware of the study hypothesis and
that coffee is not generally considered an unhealthy habit
in our society should have reduced any differential
misclassification. We only assessed cases that survived
to the fourth day after the acute event. This could be a
problem if coffee drinking was associated with lower
survival, namely caused by an increased risk of arrhyth-
mia. However, there is no consistent evidence favouring
such a hypothesis [1,14].
Because
hypertension
and
dyslipidaemia
might
be
considered intermediate steps in the final effect of coffee
or other dietary variables [30], these variables were not
included in the final logistic regression model.

event. The OR for family history regardless of age at the
time of the event was 1.9 (95% CI 1.3–2.7). However,

impossible with such small numbers.
The relative risk estimates might be unstable because of
the small size of the unexposed group. To deal with this
problem we have considered creating a low consumption
group that would include non-drinkers and drinkers of small
quantities of coffee (less than one cup per day, for example).
However, in a society in which coffee drinking is so usual,
the group of non-drinkers is probably a very particular one,
and so the difference between non-drinkers and occasional
drinkers is more important than that between regular and
occasional drinkers. Moreover, most coffee drinkers con-
sume low to moderate amounts of coffee, for which no
association was found with CHD, even in studies that found
increased risks for higher doses [32]. This means that a
category including non-drinkers and drinkers of up to one
cup of coffee per day would be mainly composed of the
latter. In that case, the above-described strategy would
introduce a significant amount of misclassification.
One could expect that families with a history of AMI
could adopt different lifestyles because of an awareness of
an increased risk. Controls with a positive family history
had a lower proportion of heavy drinkers (more than four
cups a day: 23.7 versus 26.8% among controls with no
family history of AMI) and a lower frequency of smokers,
particularly light smokers. In contrast, cases reported
more similar coffee drinking and smoking habits across
the strata of a family history of AMI. If antecedents of
AMI in the family led control subjects to underreport
coffee drinking and smoking, that could be an explanation
for the observed increased risk in this stratum. The
hypothesis of such a differential misclassification needs to
be further evaluated and possibly assessed using biologi-
cal markers. Anyway it points to the importance of
assessing separately the effect of exposures according to
the past medical history.
We cannot exclude the hypothesis that there is some
residual confounding by variables not included in the
multivariate models, such as physical activity and dietary
factors. Nevertheless, partial adjustment for these factors
was most probably achieved as a result of their association
with the waist-to-hip ratio and educational level. We did
not include energy intake in the multivariate models
because it was not significantly associated with the
occurrence of AMI and its inclusion did not change the
effect–measure for coffee variables.
Our results
suggest that
there could
be
different
susceptibilities to the effects of coffee, possibly depend-
ing on genetic factors as expected to explain some of the
family aggregation of CHD [33]. It is likely that a variety
of genes interact to alter the susceptibility of the arterial
wall to damage from risk factors, namely damage to the
endothelium. Clarkson et al. [34] described early en-
dothelium dysfunction in young first-degree relatives of
coronary patients in whom family history was the only
major cardiovascular risk factor. Endothelium dysfunc-
tion, if associated with loss of integrity, might expose the
muscular arterial wall to aggressive substances, or it might
prevent the action of substances that would otherwise
lead to endothelium-dependent dilatation. We do not
know, however, which of the hundreds of substances
already identified as components of coffee, or possibly
other still unidentified substances, are responsible for its
effect on susceptible individuals.
Long-term caffeine use reportedly results in an improve-
ment in mood, and it has been shown that there is a
strong inverse association between coffee intake and the
risk of suicide in a large cohort of US female nurses [35].
We have no reason to believe that the same is not true in
men in the absence of particular susceptibility, such as
having a family history of AMI. It can thus be speculated
that coffee drinkers are protected to some extent as a
result of the psychological and emotional effects of coffee
intake.
The 6-year Multiple Risk Factor Intervention Trial data
showed a significant, independent, inverse relationship
between caffeine intake and both systolic and diastolic
blood pressure, including in multivariate models with
several other dietary factors considered [36]. The authors
considered attributing this finding
to the putative
diuretic–natriuretic effects of caffeine. Given the associa-
tion between blood pressure and the occurrence of AMI,
this finding supports our own results on the inverse
association between coffee drinking and AMI in the
absence of a family history of AMI.
