Abstract 
36 
Obesity is the main risk factor for type 2 diabetes. Studies performed over the last 20 years 
37 
have identified inflammation as the most important link between these two diseases. During 
38 
the development of obesity, there is activation of subclinical inflammatory activity in tissues 
39 
involved in metabolism and energy homeostasis. Intracellular serine/threonine kinases 
40 
activated in response to inflammatory factors can catalyse the inhibitory phosphorylation of 
41 
key proteins of the insulin-signalling pathway, leading to insulin resistance. Moreover, 
42 
during the progression of obesity and insulin resistance, the pancreatic islets are also 
43 
affected by inflammation, contributing to β-cell failure and leading to the onset of type 2 
44 
diabetes. In this review, we will present the main mechanisms involved in the activation of 
45 
obesity-associated metabolic inflammation and discuss potential therapeutic opportunities 
46 
that can be developed to treat obesity-associated metabolic diseases.

Introduction 
49 
Type 2 diabetes (T2D) results from the combination of insulin resistance and a relative 
50 
deficiency of insulin production (1). Despite the fact that both insulin resistance and insulin 
51 
insufficiency may be induced by a number of factors that comprise genetic defects, 
52 
sedentary lifestyle, dietary factors and endocrine disruptors, among others (1-5), 
53 
inflammation has emerged as a unifying mechanism capable of affecting both the action and 
54 
production of insulin (3; 4; 6; 7). Here, we will review the main aspects linking inflammation 
55 
with T2D and point some of the therapeutic opportunities that may emerge from the 
56 
detailed characterisation of this phenomenon. For didactic reasons, we will organise the 
57 
mechanisms involved in metabolic inflammation into three levels: i) triggers, ii) mediators, 
58 
and iii) amplifiers. Initially, we will introduce these concepts in the context of the general 
59 
metabolic inflammation that accompanies obesity. Thereafter, we will present data showing 
60 
that inflammation is also an important mechanism contributing to β-cell failure in T2D. 
61 
Finally, we will present and discuss studies that have evaluated methods to dampen 
62 
metabolic inflammation as an approach to treat T2D. 
63 
 
64 
Historical aspects 
65 
The association between infections and the worsening of metabolic control in patients with 
66 
diabetes provided the earliest evidence to support a role for inflammation in glucose 
67 
intolerance. The first detailed clinical description of diabetes complications in a patient with 
68 
an infection was published in 1924 (8) and was followed by several other reports showing 
69 
that different types of infection could also affect the control of blood glucose levels and 
70 
whole body metabolism (9-12). However, it was only in 1940 that a hypothesis was 
71 
formulated placing the consumption of dietary fats in parallel with infections as causes for 
72 
the defective action of insulin and glucose intolerance in diabetes (13; 14). 
73 
Page 3 of 31

 
4
The search for the mechanisms linking inflammation and the consumption of dietary fats 
74 
with insulin resistance was proven challenging, and in 1993, tumor necrosis factor-α (TNFα) 
75 
was first shown to be produced in the adipose tissue of obese rodents, mediating at least 
76 
part of the effects of dietary fats to induce insulin resistance (15). Hotamisligil, Shargill and 
77 
Spiegelman’s (15) work had a revolutionary impact on the field, which resulted in the 
78 
identification of a number of inflammatory factors that were either produced in the adipose 
79 
tissue or were directly stimulated by dietary fats or LPS, playing important roles in the 
80 
induction of insulin resistance (3; 16). In addition, more recently, studies have shown that 
81 
inflammation plays an important role in the progressive deterioration of β-cell function in 
82 
T2D (17-19).  
83 
 
84 
The triggers of metabolic inflammation 
85 
The consumption of dietary fats is amongst the most important environmental factors 
86 
leading to obesity and insulin resistance (20; 21). As obesity evolves, most of the long-chain 
87 
fatty acids in the body are stored within the cells of the adipose tissue as esterified lipids 
88 
(22). However, it is not only intracellular lipids that are increased in obesity. In fact, both the 
89 
consumption of dietary fats and obesity result in increased blood levels of free fatty acids 
90 
(FFAs) (22), which is a well known risk factor involved in the development of metabolic 
91 
diseases (23-25).  
92 
For years, researchers have investigated the mechanisms connecting FFAs and insulin 
93 
resistance, and it was only during the late 1990s that the first mechanisms began to be 
94 
defined (26). It is currently known that FFAs can trigger metabolic inflammation and insulin 
95 
resistance through at least four distinct mechanisms: i) activation of endoplasmic reticulum 
96 
stress (27), ii) activation of TLR4 signalling (3), iii) activation of PKCε/PKCΘ (28), and 
97 
Page 4 of 31

 
5
activation of PKR (29). Most of the studies focused in the adipose tissue and liver, but there 
98 
is evidence of similar phenomena occurring in muscle and hypothalamus, as well. 
99 
Endoplasmic reticulum stress. The endoplasmic reticulum (ER) is the organelle responsible 
100 
for translating and folding up to 30% of the proteins in a cell (30). Most membrane-bound 
101 
and secretory proteins are handled in the ER (30) in a process with a considerable degree of 
102 
functional complexity. Depending on the cell type, a number of proteins can be synthesised 
103 
in huge amounts; some proteins require covalent disulfide bonding and some are inserted 
104 
into the membranes. Under certain stressful conditions, the pace of protein synthesis and 
105 
folding can slow down, potentially threatening cellular viability (31). To minimise the chance 
106 
of protein jamming in the ER, a sensor system has evolved – the unfolded protein response 
107 
(UPR), which is activated in response to the accumulation of misfolded proteins in the ER 
108 
lumen (32). In the ER membrane, there are three UPR sensor proteins, PERK, IRE1 and ATF6, 
109 
which can be engaged by two distinct mechanisms: i) the consumption of chaperones, which 
110 
leads to the removal of the inhibitory chaperone BiP from the ER luminal portion of the 
111 
sensor proteins, promoting their activation, and ii) the binding of misfolded proteins to the 
112 
luminal part of sensor proteins, promoting their dimerization and activation (30). The main 
113 
consequence of the UPR is the reduction of general protein translation accompanied by the 
114 
increase of ER chaperone expression. However, the prolonged incapacity to re-establish ER 
115 
homeostasis results in the UPR-dependent activation of inflammation and, eventually, 
116 
apoptosis (27; 33).  
117 
In 2004, Ozcan and colleagues (34) showed that obesity was accompanied by ER stress (ERS) 
118 
in the adipose tissue, leading to the activation of JNK. In cultured adipocytes, a similar 
119 
phenomenon was obtained through the exposure to a high concentration of lipids. The 
120 
activation of JNK was capable of inducing insulin resistance through the inhibitory serine 
121 
phosphorylation of IRS1. In another study, it was shown that chemical chaperones could 
122 
stabilise the ER and rescue the metabolic phenotype of obese mice (35). Moreover, in 
123 
Page 5 of 31

 
6
addition to the activation of inflammatory signalling through JNK, ERS was shown to activate 
124 
IKK (36), which demonstrates the wide range pro-inflammatory impact of this pathway. 
125 
These findings not only confirmed the important role played by ERS as a trigger of metabolic 
126 
inflammation, but they also provided proof of the concept that targeting ERS could offer an 
127 
attractive approach to treat metabolic diseases. In general, the involvement of ERS in 
128 
metabolic inflammation and insulin resistance emerges as a general mechanism occurring in 
129 
most, if not all, tissues of the body, such as the adipose tissue, liver, muscle and 
130 
hypothalamus (35-38). 
131 
TLR4. Toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) belongs to the TLR family of innate immune system 
132 
receptors that respond to pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) (39; 40). Lipid-A, 
133 
a component of the Gram-negative bacterial endotoxin, lipopolysaccharide (LPS), is the main 
134 
ligand of TLR4, inducing the activation of signal transduction through MyD88/IL-1R-
135 
associated kinase (IRAK)-4 (41). However, studies have shown that saturated fatty acids, 
136 
some of which are commonly found in the human diet, can also activate TLR4 inflammatory 
137 
signal transduction (42; 43), resulting in the inhibition of the insulin signalling system (44). 
138 
Both, genetic and diet-induced rodent models of obesity exhibit increased expression of 
139 
TLR4 in the adipose tissue (45). In lean mice, the intravenous infusion of lipids leads to the 
140 
activation of NFB and increased expression of IL6 in the adipose tissue of wild type mice 
141 
but not in mice knockout for TLR4 (45). In addition, studies have shown that genetic 
142 
targeting of TLR4 results in protection against diet-induced insulin resistance (45; 46), and 
143 
depending on the genetic background of the mice, it can also protect against diet-induced 
144 
obesity (38; 47). Most of the metabolic inflammatory phenotypes triggered by the activation 
145 
of TLR4 depend on the expression of this receptor in bone marrow derived cells (48; 49). 
146 
Studies have shown that the presence and the inflammatory phenotype of macrophages in 
147 
tissues affected by metabolic inflammation is an important requirement for the induction of 
148 
insulin resistance. These facts reinforce the pivotal role of TLR4-expressing macrophages 
149 
Page 6 of 31

 
7
infiltrating metabolically relevant tissues as the central cellular players in metabolic 
150 
inflammation (50; 51).  
151 
Several attempts have been made to elucidate the mechanisms involved in fatty acid-
152 
induced activation of TLR4 (42; 52; 53). The greatest advance was made in 2012 when Pal 
153 
and colleagues (54) reported that fetuin-A, an inflammatory glycoprotein produced by the 
154 
liver and adipose tissue (55), could act as a bridge linking FFA with TLR4, leading to its 
155 
activation. Clinical studies have provided further evidence for the role of fetuin-A as a 
156 
mediator of FFA-induced metabolic inflammation (56-58). 
157 
In addition to the direct effect of FFAs as triggers of metabolic inflammation through the 
158 
activation of TLR4, studies have shown that blood levels of LPS are increased in experimental 
159 
models of diet-induced obesity and also in lean rodents or humans fed a high-fat diet (59; 
160 
60). Although the blood level of LPS detected in obesity or after the consumption of dietary 
161 
fat is not as high as in sepsis, for example, it is sufficient to activate TLR4 in macrophages 
162 
and in cells of metabolically relevant tissues, such as adipocytes, hepatocytes and muscle 
163 
cells (59; 61; 62). Therefore, it is currently believed that obesity and dietary fats can 
164 
promote the activation of TLR4 through the direct action of FFAs in addition to the classical 
165 
action of LPS (3; 59; 63). 
166 
An important piece of information regarding the activation of TLR4 in obesity and metabolic 
167 
diseases was added by studies showing changes in gut microbiota of obese rodents and 
168 
humans (64). According to these studies, the composition of the diet has a profound 
169 
influence on the landscape of bacterial species present in the gut (65). A diet composed of 
170 
large amounts of fibre-rich foods results in an increased proportion of Prevotella, whereas 
171 
Bacterioides correlates positively with the consumption of a protein-rich diet (66). However, 
172 
the greatest advance was provided by the demonstration that obese humans and 
173 
experimental models of obesity present a shift in the relative amounts of the two most 
174 
Page 7 of 31

 
8
common phyla of gut bacteria, Bacteroidetes and Firmicutes (67; 68). In lean subjects, there 
175 
is proportionally more Bacteroidetes, whereas in obese subjects there is proportionally more 
176 
Firmicutes (68). Changes in the gut microbiota landscape result in modifications to gut 
177 
physiology, which can be affected by a number of distinct mechanisms. At least two 
178 
mechanisms have a direct impact on obesity: i) certain bacteria present in larger amounts in 
179 
the gut of obese subjects express enzymes that improve energy harvesting from nutrients 
180 
(69) and ii) obese gut microbiota can change the permeability of the intestine to nutrients 
181 
and toxins (70). As a consequence of these modifications, there is increased whole body 
182 
energy availability, increased absorption of fatty acids and increased gut transposition of 
183 
LPS, which act in concert to accelerate body mass gain and trigger insulin resistance through 
184 
TLR4. 
185 
PKC isoforms. At least three isoforms of protein kinase C (PKC), PKCε, PKCδ and PKCΘ, have 
186 
been implicated as links between lipid overload insulin resistance in liver and muscle, 
187 
respectively (28; 71). The activation of the novel PKC isoforms depends on the increase of 
188 
diacylglycerol (DAG) in the intracellular compartment, which is induced by increased lipid 
189 
uptake (72). Upon activation, PKCε/PKCδ/PKCΘ can catalyse the serine phosphorylation of 
190 
IRS1 in muscle (PKCδ and PKCΘ) and liver (PKCε), leading to the insulin resistance phenotype 
191 
(26; 28; 73). Different genetic approaches have been employed with success to reduce the 
192 
action of PKCs, resulting in increased insulin sensitivity and improved glucose tolerance (28; 
193 
74).  
194 
PKR. Protein kinase R (PKR) was first identified as a cytosolic sensor of viral double-stranded 
195 
RNA, leading to the activation of an inflammatory response aimed at eliminating the invader 
196 
(75; 76). Later on, it was shown that dietary lipids could activate PKR, triggering an 
197 
intracellular inflammatory response that integrates nutrient overload with metabolic 
198 
pathways (29). In animal models of obesity and in lean mice submitted to a lipid overload, 
199 
Page 8 of 31

 
9
PKR is activated, leading to the inhibition of insulin signalling by two distinct mechanisms: i) 
200 
direct phosphorylation of IRS-1 in serine residues and ii) integration with ERS, leading to the 
201 
activation of JNK and thus, indirectly targeting proteins of the insulin signalling cascade (29). 
202 
In addition, it was shown that PKR is also activated in metabolically relevant tissues in obese 
203 
subjects, and body mass reduction resulting from bariatric surgery attenuates PKR activity 
204 
(77). A study has reported that the inhibition of PKR using chemical inhibitors resulted in 
205 
considerable improvement of the metabolic phenotype, which places PKR in an attractive 
206 
position as a potential target for the treatment of T2D (78; 79).  
207 
 
208 
The intracellular mediators of metabolic inflammation 
209 
In cells of the immune system and metabolically relevant tissues, the cellular signals 
210 
generated in response to the triggers of metabolic inflammation can activate signal 
211 
transducers that act as mediators of metabolic inflammation. At least three mediators have 
212 
been characterised in depth: i) JNK, ii) IKK/NFkB, and iii) mTOR/S6K. It is important to 
213 
mention that PKCε/PKCΘ and PKR can act both as triggers and as mediators of metabolic 
214 
inflammation.  
215 
JNK. Jun N-terminal kinase (JNK), also known as the stress-activated protein kinase (SAPK), 
216 
belongs to the mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) superfamily and exists as 10 
217 
different isoforms (80). Stress signals, such as hyperlipidemia, cytokines (IL6, TNFα), hypoxia, 
218 
toxins, heat shock, and drugs, can activate JNK, which binds and phosphorylates c-Jun in two 
219 
different serine residues, Ser63 and Ser73 (81; 82). Phosphorylated c-Jun forms dimers with 
220 
members of Fos (c-Fos), Jun (JunB, JunD) or ATF families, resulting in the assemblage of the 
221 
transcription factor activator protein 1 (AP-1), which is an important regulator of 
222 
inflammation, cytokine production, apoptosis, neurodegeneration, cellular differentiation, 
223 
migration and proliferation (83). Studies have shown that the isoform JNK1 is implicated in 
224 
Page 9 of 31

 
10
the pathogenesis of insulin resistance, T2D and obesity (80; 84). In experimental obesity, 
225 
JNK1 is activated in the adipose tissue, leading to insulin resistance, because it catalyses the 
226 
phosphorylation of insulin substrate receptor 1 (IRS1) in serine residues (84).  
227 
In obesity, excessive dietary fats can be sensed by TLR4 in the hypothalamus, triggering 
228 
activation of JNK and IKK/NFkB in a MyD88-dependent manner or indirectly via the 
229 
activation of ERS (34; 47; 85). Indeed, the UPR sensor protein IRE1 activates JNK, leading to 
230 
phosphorylation of IRS1 in serine residues and contributing to the development of insulin 
231 
resistance (34; 86). Moreover, JNK1 ablation, specifically in the CNS, normalises adiposity, 
232 
glucose intolerance, insulin resistance and obesity in DIO mice (87). Tsaousidou and 
233 
colleagues developed an interesting model with constitutive activation of JNK1 in AgRP 
234 
neurons, which resulted in leptin resistance and obesity when fed a HFD (88).  
235 
Several approaches have been used to inhibit JNK in T2D and obesity, and in all cases, there 
236 
was at least some beneficial impact on metabolic abnormalities [revised in (3; 16)]. 
237 
Therefore, JNK is regarded as one of the most important intracellular mediators of metabolic 
238 
inflammation.  
239 
IKK/NFkB. At least three triggers of metabolic inflammation can lead to the activation of 
240 
IKK/NFB pathway – i) ERS, ii) TLR4 and iii) PKR (3; 16; 29). The nuclear factor kappa B (NFkB) 
241 
family is composed of highly evolutionary conserved proteins, ubiquitously expressed in all 
242 
mammalian cells. It acts as a transcription factor that controls a number of cellular 
243 
processes, such as, cell growth, proliferation, cellular adhesion, apoptosis, inflammation and 
244 
immune response (89; 90). Primarily, NFkB activity is negatively regulated by protein 
245 
interaction with the inhibitor of kappaB (IKK), which maintains NFkB in the cytosol, 
246 
preventing its nuclear activation and transcriptional function. In response to inflammatory 
247 
cytokines, bacterial products or others stress signals, cytokines or TLR receptors activate a 
248 
signalling cascade that converges on the phosphorylation of the inhibitor of the kappaB 
249 
Page 10 of 31

 
11
kinase (IKK) complex (91). The IKK complex is composed of the kinases IKKα and IKKβ, and of 
250 
the regulatory subunit NEMO/IKKγ. The activation of IKK complex induces serine 
251 
phosphorylation of IB, which allows its polyubiquitination and degradation by the 
252 
proteasome machinery. The degradation of IB exposes the nuclear localisation sequence 
253 
and the DNA binding domain of the NFkB, allowing its translocation to the nucleus and 
254 
regulation of target genes (89; 90; 92). Under normal circumstances, a signal decay and 
255 
termination of NFB activity plays a role in limiting the appearance of deleterious effects. 
256 
The protein deubiquitinase A20 and de novo synthesis of IB molecules act as important 
257 
negative regulators. In addition, NFB can regulate the transcriptional activity of A20 and 
258 
IBs proteins, suggesting that NFB controls its own signal decay and termination (91; 93). 
259 
Several studies have implicated IKK/NFB in the pathogenesis of metabolic disorders, such 
260 
as T2D, obesity and atherosclerosis (36; 94; 95). In experimental models of T2D and in 
261 
obese, insulin resistant humans, IKK is activated, leading to the inhibitory serine 
262 
phosphorylation of the insulin receptor substrate 1 (95-97). Both rodents and humans with 
263 
T2D treated with inhibitors of IKK present at least a partial improvement of glucose 
264 
intolerance (95; 98). Moreover, under nutrient overload, fatty acids can be sensed by TLR4 
265 
receptors, triggering activation of IKK/NFB signalling pathway, which leads to a burst in 
266 
inflammatory responses (38; 43). It has been shown that consumption of dietary fats can 
267 
lead to the activation of IKK/NFB in the hypothalamus, which results in the anomalous 
268 
regulation of food intake and energy expenditure, therefore contributing to continuous body 
269 
mass gain and further enhancing whole body insulin resistance (36). These findings place 
270 
IKK/NFB in a strategic position as potential targets for treating glucose intolerance and 
271 
insulin resistance. 
272 
mTOR/S6K. The serine/threonine kinase mTOR (mammalian target of rapamycin) is a highly 
273 
evolutionally conserved protein, which exists as the catalytic subunit of two different 
274 
complexes: mTORC1 and mTORC2 (99). The role of mTORC2 is still under investigation, but 
275 
Page 11 of 31

 
12
there are studies showing that it can be regulated by growth factors leading to the activation 
276 
of PI3-kinase/Akt signalling and the control of cellular cycle, proliferation and cellular 
277 
survival (100-102). Conversely, more is known about mTORC1, which can induce cellular 
278 
growth by integrating and coordinating signals arising from: i) nutrients (glucose and amino 
279 
acids), ii) growth factors (insulin and IGF1), iii) energy sensors (AMPK), and iv) stress 
280 
(hypoxia) (102). When activated by growth factors and nutrients, mTORC1 induces 
281 
anabolism with consequent lipid, protein and nucleotide synthesis, lipid accumulation and 
282 
inhibition of the autophagic catabolic process (102). mTORC1 activity induces the 
283 
phosphorylation of three main target substrates: the ribosomal S6 kinase 1 and 2 (S6K1 and 
284 
S6K2) and the eukaryotic initiation factor 4E (eIF-4E), which regulate the initiation and 
285 
progression of mRNA translation, leading to the enhancement of protein synthesis (101-
286 
103). 
287 
Studies have provided evidence for a role of mTOR in metabolic inflammation. In an 
288 
experimental study, IL6 was shown to activate mTOR action through STAT3, leading to 
289 
insulin resistance due to the increased expression of SOCS3 (104). The inhibition of mTOR 
290 
using rapamycin was sufficient to revert IL6-induced insulin resistance (104). In another 
291 
study using diet-induced obese mice, the disruption of the mTORC1 complex using a genetic 
292 
approach resulted in improved insulin action due to the inhibition of the obesity-associated 
293 
activation of the JNK and NFkB pathways (105). Overnutrition is also involved in the chronic 
294 
activation of mTOR-signalling, leading to lipogenesis in important metabolic tissues, such as 
295 
muscle, liver and WAT. mTORC1 overstimulation triggers the S6K-dependent negative 
296 
feedback loop, in which S6K phosphorylates and down-regulates the main substrates of the 
297 
insulin receptor, IRS1 and IRS2, in serine residues, leading to insulin resistance (100; 103; 
298 
106; 107). In addition, it was shown that S6K1 deficiency results in enhanced insulin 
299 
sensitivity, which is sustained even under prolonged exposure to a high-fat diet, supporting 
300 
the important role of mTORC1/S6K in the pathogenesis of T2D.

The amplifiers of metabolic inflammation 
303 
A number of cytokines and pro-inflammatory factors can be induced and secreted by 
304 
immune cells infiltrating metabolically relevant tissues, leading to the amplification of 
305 
metabolic inflammation. Studies have shown that some cells of metabolically relevant 
306 
tissues, such as adipocytes and hypothalamic neurons, can also produce and secrete such 
307 
inflammatory amplifiers. Here, we present data regarding the roles of three important 
308 
cytokines that are involved in the pathogenesis of type 2 diabetes: i) TNFα, ii) IL1β, and, iii) 
309 
IL6. 
310 
TNFα. TNFα was the first pro-inflammatory cytokine implicated in the pathogenesis of 
311 
insulin resistance, T2D and obesity (15). TNFα is mainly produced by macrophages and 
312 
regulates pleiotropic functions, such as innate immunity, Th1 response, inflammation, cell 
313 
differentiation, proliferation, apoptosis, and energy metabolism (108; 109). TNFα is 
314 
synthesised as a 24KDa-transmembrane precursor (mTNFα), which undergoes cleavage by 
315 
the metalloproteinase TNFα converting enzyme (TACE) to a bioactive 17KDa soluble 
316 
molecule (sTNFα). In obesity and T2D, both isoforms are increased and exert their biological 
317 
functions by binding to TNFα receptors TNFR1 (p55) and TNFR2 (p75) in cells of the adipose 
318 
tissue, liver and other metabolically relevant tissues (108; 110). Receptor activation leads to 
319 
the induction of pro-inflammatory IKK/NFB and JNK/AP-1 signalling, which busts 
320 
inflammation by inducing gene transcription of cytokines, cytokines receptors, growth 
321 
factors, adhesion molecules, and nitric oxide, among others. Additionally, TNFα can trigger 
322 
apoptosis through the recruitment of caspases to the death domain of the p55/TNFR1 
323 
receptor.  
324 
The pro-inflammatory cytokines TNFα, IL1β and IL6 are increased in murine models of 
325 
obesity and T2D, and pharmacological or genetic neutralisation of TNFα increased insulin 
326 
Page 13 of 31

 
14
sensitivity reduces hepatic glucose production, while increased thermogenesis reduces 
327 
inflammation and body mass gain (47; 111; 112). TNFα contributes to the pathogenesis of 
328 
insulin resistance through the induction of serine kinases that attenuate IR and IRS1 
329 
signalling pathways in important metabolic tissues (113; 114). Taken together, these data 
330 
suggest that TNFα or its signalling pathway could be a therapeutic target for metabolic 
331 
diseases. In fact, long-term treatment with TNFα antagonists (infliximab or etanercept) 
332 
decreases inflammation, as well as fasting glucose, while increasing adiponectin levels; 
333 
however it did not affect insulin sensitivity in obese diabetic subjects (115; 116).  
334 
IL1β. The polypeptide interleukin 1 (IL1) belongs to a family of cytokines mainly secreted by 
335 
macrophages, monocytes and dendritic cells responsible for mediating immunological 
336 
reactions, inflammation and tissue injury (117). The family member IL1β (17KDa), which is 
337 
secreted early in immune responses, is initially synthesised as a 31KDa precursor, pro- IL1β, 
338 
which undergoes capase-1-mediated cleavage to become active (118). IL1β is one of the 
339 
most important pro-inflammatory cytokines, which activates JNK/AP1 and IKK/NFkB, leading 
340 
to regulation of its own gene transcription, in addition to other cytokines, such as TNFα, 
341 
therefore boosting the inflammatory response (117). 
342 
Over the last 30 years, several studies have shown the important role of IL1β on the 
343 
pathogenesis of T2D, insulin resistance and obesity (119-121). IL1β mediates diet-induced 
344 
inflammation (121; 122). Studies have shown that murine models lacking components of the 
345 
inflammasomes IL1β or IL1R1 are protected from insulin resistance and diet-induced 
346 
inflammation, supporting an important role for IL1β activity in the development of 
347 
metabolic disorders (123; 124). Several studies have used different approaches to inhibit 
348 
IL1β as an attempt to modify the course of metabolic disorders. The natural endogenous 
349 
inhibitor IL1Ra (IL1β receptor antagonist), produced by healthy resting cells, binds to IL1R1 
350 
and blocks the interaction and signal transduction of IL1β (125). The use of a recombinant 
351 
Page 14 of 31

 
15
IL1Ra (anakinra) or IL1β antagonists (gevokizumab, canakizumab and LY2189102) improved 
352 
insulin sensitivity, reduced inflammation markers, corrected glycated haemoglobin levels 
353 
and improved β-cell function in patients with T2D (126; 127). Taken together, all of these 
354 
studies show that targeting IL1β activity has a high impact on the treatment of obesity-
355 
induced inflammation, hyperglycaemia, and insulin resistance. 
356 
IL6. Almost 30 years ago, interleukin-6 (IL6) was identified as a T-cell derived factor, 
357 
responsible for differentiating the activated B-lymphocytes into plasma antibody-producing 
358 
cells (128). IL6 (21–26KDa) is a pleiotropic cytokine produced by a wide spectrum of cells, 
359 
including adipocytes, myocytes, islet, endothelial and immune cells, involved in the acute 
360 
phase response, inflammation, immune regulation, haematopoiesis, and tissue regeneration 
361 
(129). IL6 binds to its cognate receptor, IL6R, present in leucocytes, hepatocytes, and many 
362 
other cells (130; 131). In addition, it can also bind to the soluble receptor (sIL6R-55KDa) 
363 
present in the serum and tissue fluids forming the complex IL6/sIL6R (130; 131). Upon 
364 
binding to the transmembrane IL6R, IL6 promotes the phosphorylation of the gp130 protein 
365 
in tyrosine residues, triggering the activation of JAK2/STAT3 and MAPK/PI3-kinase signalling 
366 
pathway and inducing the expression of acute-phase proteins, adhesion molecules, 
367 
chemokines, antiapoptotic proteins and cytokines (129; 132). The gp130/JAK2/STAT3 
368 
signalling upregulates the expression of SOCS3, which acts as a negative feedback inhibitor 
369 
of JAK2 activation (129; 132). 
370 
In health, the expression of IL6 is transient, contributing to host defence and tissue injury, 
371 
and decreasing when tissue homeostasis is restored. However, the chronic and anomalous 
372 
production of IL6 plays an important role in the pathogenesis of several inflammatory 
373 
diseases, including obesity, insulin resistance and T2D (131; 133; 134). Interestingly, IL6 can 
374 
act as both a pro- and an anti-inflammatory cytokine, depending on the target tissue and 
375 
metabolic state. Elevated plasma levels of IL6 are correlated with T2D development, body 
376 
Page 15 of 31

 
16
mass gain and circulating FFA, as seen in diabetic patients (134; 135). Additionally, in 
377 
adipose tissue and liver, IL6 has pro-inflammatory properties, inducing insulin resistance by 
378 
enhancing SOCS3 expression, which impairs IR/IRS1 phosphorylation (113; 136; 137). Thus, 
379 
IL6 positively modulates insulin resistance and inflammation when secreted acutely, during 
380 
exercise, or negatively when secreted chronically, as seen in T2D and obesity.  
381 
 
382 
Metabolic inflammation and pancreatic islet dysfunction in type 2 diabetes 
383 
Systemic metabolic inflammation can affect pancreatic islets through distinct mechanisms, 
384 
contributing to β-cell failure in T2D (4). Early studies focused on the adipose tissue as the 
385 
source for inflammatory factors, leading to pancreatic islet dysfunction in obesity (138). In 
386 
humans, the removal of part of the visceral adipose tissue has a profound impact on whole 
387 
body glucose homeostasis, mostly because of improved first phase insulin secretion (139). 
388 
However, inflammation associated with obesity can affect insulin secretion by other 
389 
mechanisms. Obesity-associated hypothalamic inflammation is accompanied by the loss of 
390 
the first phase of insulin secretion (140). Furthermore, intracerebroventricular 
391 
administration of saturated fatty acids or TNFα can induce a dysfunctional increase in insulin 
392 
secretion, which is accompanied by the increased expression of apoptotic markers, such as 
393 
BAX, and also proteins involved in mitochondria function, such as PGC1α and UCP2 in 
394 
pancreatic islets (140). The increased expression of UCP2 is responsible for affecting glucose-
395 
induced insulin secretion due to the reduction of ATP production (140). The connection 
396 
between the dysfunctional hypothalamus and pancreatic islets is dependent on sympathetic 
397 
innervation, and sympathectomy is sufficient to restore insulin secretion (140).  
398 
It has been suggested that pancreatic islet inflammation is associated with very early insulin 
399 
resistance. Using a nonhuman primate model exposed during foetal life to a fat-rich diet, 
400 
there was an early increase of IL1β and IL6 expression in pancreatic islets, which was 
401 
Page 16 of 31

 
17
associated with a decreased first-phase insulin secretion, increased fasting glucose levels 
402 
and infiltration of the pancreatic islets with macrophages before the onset of glucose 
403 
deregulation and obese phenotypes (141). 
404 
The multiprotein activation complexes – inflammasomes – regulate the processing and 
405 
release of proinflammatory cytokines during insulin resistance and T2D. The recruitment of 
406 
pro-caspase-1 to inflammasomes leads to caspase-1 activation, which induces the 
407 
proteolytic maturation and secretion of active IL1β (142). Inflammasomes can be activated 
408 
by PAMPs (pathogen-associated molecular patterns) and DAMPs (damage-associated 
409 
molecular patterns), such as aggregates of insoluble islet amyloid deposits, derived from the 
410 
amylin peptide (IAPP) secreted by β-cells. In humans, IAPP increases the transcription of IL1β 
411 
in macrophages and contributes to islet inflammation (143; 144). IL1β has been described as 
412 
playing a central role in inflammation in the absence of infection (sterile inflammation) and 
413 
its secretion has a negative impact on β-cell function and survival (122). There is strong 
414 
evidence showing that the recruitment and activation of IL1β-producing macrophages 
415 
mediate islet inflammation (122). A study demonstrated increased numbers of immune cells 
416 
in the pancreatic islet of T2D patients and in animal models of obesity (138). Additionally, β-
417 
cells producing and secreting IL1β have been observed in pancreatic sections obtained from 
418 
patients with type 2 diabetes, suggesting an autocrine effect of IL1β on β-cell survival and 
419 
function (120; 145). Interestingly, leptin, which is increased in obesity, can act as a 
420 
proinflammatory cytokine, inducing β-cell apoptosis and generating an imbalance between 
421 
IL1β and the endogenous antagonist of the IL1 receptor (IL-1Ra) in human islets (146). 
422 
ERS has been described as an important mechanism leading to pancreatic islet inflammation 
423 
and β-cell dysfunction (4; 147). The first piece of evidence of the role of ERS in defective 
424 
insulin secretion came from studies with monogenic forms of diabetes (148; 149). However, 
425 
it was subsequently shown that the increased demand for insulin production in common 
426 
forms of T2D could also impose perturbations in the ER, leading to the activation of the UPR 
427 
Page 17 of 31

 
18
(150; 151). Furthermore, as in other tissues (147), FFAs can act as a trigger of ERS in 
428 
pancreatic islets, acting in concert with high glucose (19; 152).  
429 
Oxidative stress is also an important mechanism leading to β-cells dysfunction and death in 
430 
T2D (153). In β-cells, mitochondrial activity is more than two times greater than in any other 
431 
cell, since insulin secretion is coupled with exogenous glucose sensing and endogenous 
432 
glucose oxidation in mitochondria (120). Moreover, β-cells are particularly sensitive to 
433 
oxidative stress since the expression of antioxidant enzymes is lower compared to other 
434 
metabolically active tissues (154). Reactive oxygen species (ROS) generation in β-cells 
435 
induces the activation of stress kinases, such as JNK1. ROS also initiates the formation of 
436 
inflammasomes through its association with thioredoxin-interacting protein (TXNIP) released 
437 
from the complex with thioredoxin, which activates IL1β processing (155; 156).  
438 
Recent evidence suggests that products from arachidonic acid metabolism are also involved 
439 
in the generation of ROS in β-cells. 12-HETE, a 12-lipoxygenase (12-LO) product, is involved 
440 
in NADPH oxidase-1 (NOX-1) activation in mouse and human islets (157). The use of 12-LO 
441 
inhibitors reduces ROS and restores glucose-stimulated insulin secretion in response to 
442 
proinflammatory cytokines through the attenuation of NOX-1 expression (157). In addition, 
443 
in pancreatic 12-LO-knockout mice fed a HFD, the major transcriptional activator of 
444 
antioxidant enzymes superoxide dismutase and glutathione peroxidase, the nuclear factor 
445 
erythroid 2-related factor 2 (Nrf2), was present in high levels, demonstrating the role of 12-
446 
LO and its products in oxidative stress in pancreatic islets (158).  
447 
As a whole, pancreatic islet inflammation can be triggered by distinct mechanisms, which 
448 
occur frequently in obesity. It is expected that therapeutic approaches targeting islet 
449 
inflammation may be useful for delaying or interrupting the progression towards relative 
450 
insulin insufficiency, which is a hallmark of T2D. 
451 
 
452 
Page 18 of 31

 
19
The potential role of anti-inflammatory approaches to treat T2D 
453 
A number of experimental studies have employed distinct pharmacological and genetic 
454 
methods to inhibit metabolic inflammation. In many circumstances, there were beneficial 
455 
outcomes of such inhibitory approaches and many of them have been cited in the preceding 
456 
sections. However, only a few clinical studies were designed and performed to evaluate the 
457 
impact of anti-inflammatory approaches in human T2D. 
458 
An observational study reported improved insulin sensitivity in patients under prolonged use 
459 
of immunoneutralising antibodies against TNFα to treat psoriatic or rheumatoid arthritis 
460 
(115). With this concept in mind, a randomised, placebo-controlled, six-month intervention 
461 
was undertaken and etanercept was shown to improve fasting glucose and increase 
462 
adiponectin levels in obese subjects (116). The inhibition of IL1β was also evaluated in 
463 
humans with metabolic disease. In a double-blind, phase II, randomised study, the 
464 
immunoneutralisation of IL1β for 12 weeks reduced fasting glucose and HbA1c levels in 
465 
patients with T2D (126). In addition, inhibition of the IL1 receptor using a recombinant 
466 
receptor antagonist resulted in reduced HbA1c levels and increased C-peptides, suggesting a 
467 
beneficial effect for the pancreatic β-cell (159). 
468 
Attempts have been made to inhibit NFkB for the treatment of T2D. Early observational data 
469 
reported improvement of glucose tolerance during the use of salicylates (160). However, 
470 
side effects were always a concern and to date, no inhibitor of NFkB is approved for the 
471 
treatment of T2D. Recently, salsalate, a non-acetylated pro-drug form of salicylic acid with 
472 
fewer side effects was employed in several clinical trials with generally positive results, 
473 
reducing fasting glucose and HbA1c (161; 162).  
474 
An additional issue regarding the future perspectives for employing anti-inflammatory 
475 
approaches to treat patients with T2D is the pleotropic nature of the subclinical 
476 
inflammatory process in metabolic diseases. It is possible that optimal effects of anti-
477 
Page 19 of 31

 
20
inflammatory approaches are to be achieved on individualized basis only (163). Therefore, 
478 
further studies are required to provide advance in the pathophysiology of metabolic 
479 
inflammation.  
480 
 
481 
Conclusions  
482 
Currently, obesity- and diet-associated inflammation is considered an important inducer of 
483 
insulin resistance and defective β-cell function. As most patients with T2D are obese, 
484 
prevention must be focused on reducing the prevalence of obesity. However, 
485 
pharmacological approaches aimed at restoring glucose tolerance may benefit from the 
486 
development of drugs that can reduce metabolic inflammation without important side 
487 
effects. It is expected that in the near future, new drugs with anti-inflammatory activity will 
488 
be evaluated in clinical trials for the treatment of T2D.
