32.1  INTRODUCTION
32.1.1  Mosquitoes as a Public Health Problem
Mosquito-borne diseases continue to be a grave global 
public health problem. Mosquito control is an essential 
component of disease control and relies on the use of 
chemical insecticides, though they are expensive and 
toxic to nontarget organisms. The discovery of biopes­
ticides has revolutionized the use of insecticides, espe­
cially compared with conventional insecticides, used in 
mosquito eradication programs. The high cost of con­
ventional media components to produce these biopesti­
cides on a large scale makes it imperative to use cheap 
and commonly available biological waste materials cre­
ated through simple fermentation technology.
In the past, many formulations produced from con­
ventional media (Luria Bertani and nutrient yeast extracts 
mineral salt medium (NYSM), etc.) have been tested for 
mosquito control. Cost-effective formulations were sub­
sequently developed for biopesticide production.1 Bacillus 
thuringiensis subspecies israelensis (Bti) cultured on five 
media formulated from seeds of legumes (groundnut 
cake, white and black varieties of cow pea, soya bean, 
bambara beans), dried cow blood, and mineral salts. 
Other researchers also have made use of potatoes, coco­
nuts, fishmeal, cornsteep liquor, and soybean for the pro­
duction of biopesticides.2,3 A recent fermentation method 
using mosquitocidal bacteria produced from slaughter­
house waste indicates that the growth and production 
of endotoxins were substantial and analogous with that 
of conventional medium. Bioassays of Bti against mos­
quito larvae showed considerable toxicity. This toxicity is 
caused by the binding of active toxins (crystals) to spe­
cific receptors present in the midgut brush border mem­
brane.4 The crystal toxins from spore/crystal complex are 
ingested, along with the food material, by the mosquito 
larvae, and after solubilization and proteolytic cleavage 
the activated toxin interacts with the midgut epithelium, 
leading to death of the larvae.5 Hence, the present study 
suggested that the toxin produced from coffee husk waste 
(CHW) are equally effective as that of Luria Bertani, and a 
judicious combination of CHW with manganese(II) chlo­
ride for mosquito control produced Bti at an enhanced 
rate. A reduction in the larval density also was noticed in 
the field trials. Our results corroborate earlier findings.6,7
The cost of Bti production depends on many factors; 
however, the raw material cost is one of the most impor­
tant criteria, comprising >70% of the overall production 
cost.8 Therefore the selection of growth medium or raw 
material is critical for commercial production of these 
biopesticides. To encourage the commercial produc­
tion of biopesticides, use of less expensive raw material 
is advisable.9 In the present study, the bioremediation

32.1.2  Microbial Fermentation Technology
Microorganisms are capable of growing on a wide 
range of substrates and can produce a remarkable spec­
trum of products. The relatively recent advent of fermen­
tation technology has extended the range of products 
that may be produced by microorganisms and has pro­
vided new methods for increasing the yield of existing 
ones. The exorbitant rate of commercial preparation of 
culture media using synthetic chemicals makes it nec­
essary to develop a new fermentation process using 
indigenous raw materials to lower production expendi­
tures. There are several synthetic culture media, such as 
Luria Bertani, mannitol broth, M17, M9 minerals, marine 
broth, nutrient broth, NYSM, and super broth, that are 
currently in use for bacterial production but involve 
heavy production expenses. Therein, searching for new 
cost-effective culture media is indispensable. This study 
addresses the possibility of using solid waste disposal 
from coffee industries (CHW) to develop a new micro­
bial fermentation technology for use in public health pro­
grams, especially in mosquito vector control operations.
32.1.3  Bio-organic Waste Materials
It is known that vast quantities of bio-organic wastes 
are discharged every day from factories, fisheries, poul­
try and food processing industries globally. Within the 
food processing sectors, various liquids, sludge, and solid 
biological and organic wastes require remediation, and 
alternative disposal methods are increasingly being inves­
tigated.10 Degrading or handling these wastes as unused 
disposals without acquiring additional benefits has led to 
an idea of developing a suitable technology to use bio-
organic wastes in mosquito vector control programs by 
means of simple fermentation technology.11An organic 
waste treatment system using waste materials from food 
processing industries has recently been developed.12 These 
recycling strategies enable the production of biologically 
active substances such as soil improvement agents and 
compost.13 It would be useful to manufacture and market 
a biomass fermentation and treatment method capable of 
reusing wastes produced by food bioprocessing industries 
into biologically active substances, animal feed, fertilizers, 
and fermentation agents. Such methods would include a 
system for treating wastes and a new fermentation agent 
that would be useful in waste decomposition. Use of such 
methods would reduce waste treatment costs, prevent 
environmental pollution, improve soil conditions, and 
yield biologically active reusable substances.
32.1.4  Biopesticides
Considering the above-mentioned applications, in 
this study we identified new organic biological mate­
rial, that is, the coffee husk (coffee seed coat), discarded 
by coffee processing industries as waste material after 
removing the pulp. The raw material is rich in nutrient 
sources (carbohydrates and proteins).13 In the present 
work, these materials were bioprocessed, leading to the 
production of bacterial biopesticides (Bacillus sphaeri­
cus (Bs) and Bacillus thuringiensis subspecies ­israelensis 
(Bti)) that are well-known biocontrol agents used in 
mosquito vector control programs.11,14,15 The fermenta­
tion process using agro-industrial residues is, neverthe­
less, an easy-to-follow, inexpensive technique, efficient 
enough to produce effective bacterial toxins lethal to 
disease-­transmitting mosquito vectors (Culex quinquefas­
ciatus, Anopheles stephensi, and Aedes aegypti). This new 
approach ultimately would help to manage solid waste 
from the environment in a judicious manner.
It is understood that Bs and Bti are aerobic, rod-
shaped, endospore-forming, Gram-positive soil bacte­
ria.3,4,16 More than 50,000 isolates have been screened and 
tested in insect control programs.17 These bacteria syn­
thesize proteins during sporulation that assemble into 
crystals which are toxic to mosquito larvae.14 The devel­
opment of crystals during sporulation of Bti strains has 
been studied extensively.15 The crystals are composed of 
two polypeptides for Bs (51 and 42 kDa) and four poly­
peptides for Bti (135-, 127-, 68-, and 27-kDa proteins).15,4 
These crystal toxins kill the mosquito larvae after inges­
tion along with food materials.18 This study explored the 
possibility of using extract from coffee husks from agro-
industries to produce Bs and Bti for use in mosquito vec­
tor control programs. Thus, it is a novel approach to the 
management of solid waste from the environment.

32.2.2  Conventional Culture Medium (NYSM)
A standard conventional medium (NYSM) was used 
as reference: glucose 5 gL, peptone 5 gL, NaCl 5 gL, beef 
extract 3 gL, yeast extract 0.5 gL, MgCl2 0.203 gL, CaCl2 
0.102 gL, MnCl2 0.01 gL (pH 7.5).
32.2.3  Coffee Husk Waste
The agro-industrial residues (coffee husk) produced 
as a byproduct during the processing of pulp from cof­
fee seeds were collected, air-dried, powdered, and finally 
stored at 30 °C until further use (Figure 32.1(A) and (B)). 
A small quantity of CHW (50 g/l) was boiled in ordinary 
tap water for 30 min. After cooling, the CHW was filtered 
and the pH of the filtrate was adjusted (pH 7.8) (Figure 
32.1(C)). The extract was dispensed separately into three 
2-L Erlenmeyer flasks for culturing Bs, Bti, and control 
(plain medium without inoculation of Bs and Bti). All bac­
terial culture media (conventional as well as experimen­
tal) were autoclaved at 120 °C at 20 lb/in2 for 20 min.
32.2.4  Bacterial Inoculation
Precultures of Bti and Bs were inoculated into experi­
mental (CHW) and conventional culture media (NYSM) 
and allowed to grow under constant agitation (200 rpm 
at 30 °C). Samples (2.5 ml) from each culture medium 
were drawn every 6 h, up to a 72-h growth period. The 
pH and culture density (turbidity at 650 nm) were mea­
sured using a digital pH meter (Genei, India) and SP-75 
UV–VIS spectrophotometer (Sanyo, UK), respectively. 
The bacterial stages (vegetative to sporulation stage) 
also were examined using a LEICA DM 1000 LED micro­
scope (Germany).
32.2.5  Bacterial Toxin Separation
Bacterial spores/crystals were purified according to 
the method described by Poopathi et  al.4 The Bti and 
Bs spores/crystals from culture media were harvested by 
centrifugation (10,000 × g for 30 min at 4 °C) using a Sor­
vall Evolution RC superspeed centrifuge (Kendro, USA), 
and the culture supernatants were discarded. The pellets 
(cell mass) containing the spore/crystal toxin were thor­
oughly washed (three times each with 0.1 M sodium chlo­
ride and sterile distilled water). The toxins were finally 
treated with a protease inhibitor (1 mM phenyl methyl 
sulphonyl fluoride; Sigma) and resuspended in sterile 
distilled water. The spores/crystals of Bti and Bs were 
sonicated (30% scale output, 25% duty cycle for 4 min) 
and crystals were separated by discontinuous sucrose 
gradients (72% and 79% mass/vol). The pure crystals 
then were solubilized in 50 mM sodium hydroxide for 
90 min at 30 °C. After neutralization, the solubilized

32.2.6  Toxicity Assays
C. quinquefasciatus, A. stephensi, and A. aegypti 
mosquito larvae were procured from the Division of 
Mosquito Rearing and Colonization, Vector Control 
Research Centre, Pondicherry, India, for use in the pres­
ent study. Toxicity assays (bioassays) for Bti and Bs were 
carried out as described in earlier research.21–23 A stock 
solution was prepared (100 mg/l) using Bti/Bs toxins 
and homogenized, using glass beads, in a mechani­
cal shaker. From this stock, serial dilutions were made 
to obtain the required dosages within the limit (Bti: 
0.001 to 0.05 mg/l; Bs: 0.05 to 0.20 mg/l). Toxicity tests 
were conducted in disposable wax-coated paper cups 
(350 ml). Test media were prepared by adding appro­
priate volumes of Bti/Bs in 300 ml of water to which 20 
early third instar larvae from the respective mosquito 
species were introduced separately. Food supplement 
(dog biscuit and yeast mixture, 2:1) was provided for 
larvae under Bs treatment, whereas no food was given 
to Bti-treated larvae, as per World Health Organization 
recommendations.22,23 Toxicity assays were conducted 
at room temperature (33 °C) and mortality of larvae 
receiving Bti and Bs treatment was monitored after 24 
and 48 h, respectively. Moribund larvae (if any) in the 
replicates were counted as dead. Control mortality was 
corrected.24
32.2.7  Statistical Analysis
The growth and sporulation data of Bti/Bs cultured 
from test (CHW) and control medium (NYSM) were sub­
jected to the Student t test to analyze the significance of 
the difference (P ≤ 0.05). The lethal concentration values 
(LC50 and LC90) were calculated by regression analysis 
using the ASSAY feature of SPSS software (courtesy of 
Dr. C. F. Curtis, London School of Tropical Medicine and 
Hygiene, London, UK).

32.4  CONCLUSION
Coffee husks have been discarded in bulk as residues 
from agro-industries, especially from coffee manufac­
turing industries globally. They normally accumulate 
carbohydrates (carbon and nitrogen) and are used for 
manure.12 This study mainly focused on the use of 
extract from CHW by Bs and Bti for the production of 
mosquitocidal toxins. Biochemical studies indicated 
that the mosquitocidal spore/crystal toxins produced 
from an experimental culture medium (CHW) are 
similar to those produced by a conventional medium 
(NYSM). These toxins were bioassayed against the 
major mosquito vectors (C. quinquefasciatus, A. stephensi, 
and A. aegypti) and it was found that the larvicidal 
effect of both the media was equally good. Literature 
surveys revealed that the formulated media from seeds 
of legumes (groundnut cake, white and black varieties 
of cow pea, soya bean, and bambara beans), dried cow 
blood, and mineral salts were ideal for the production 
of Bti.1 Potatoes, coconuts, fishmeal, starch, and corn­
steep liquor also have been reported to be efficient for 
the production of biopesticides.7,8,25,26 A few studies 
recently evaluated the improvement of biopesticides 
and alkaline proteases by Bti from waste water and 
sewage sludge.27–29 Gruel and fish meal media also 
have been used for delta-endotoxin production from B. 
thuringiensis.30 Our fermentation studies using agrores­
idues from mosquitocidal bacteria corroborated earlier 
reports of other cost-effective culture media.
Bioassays of Bs and Bti toxins against mosquito larvae 
in this work showed considerable toxicity. It is evident 
from the mode of action of toxins that the toxicity is due 
to the binding of active crystal toxins to specific recep­
tors present in the larval midgut brush border mem­
brane.4 The crystal toxins from spore/crystal complex 
are initially ingested, along with food materials, by the 
mosquito larvae, and after solubilization and proteolytic 
cleavage, the activated toxin interacts with midgut epi­
thelium, leading to the death of the larvae.3 Hence, our 
bioassay results suggest that the toxins produced from 
CHW had larvicidal toxicity similar to that of NYSM, 
following the usual mode of action.
The production of this new culture medium (CHW) 
is very economical compared to that of the conventional 
medium because CHW is plentiful as industrial bio-
organic waste. Further studies of the optimal fermenta­
tion conditions for maximizing Bs and Bti production 
need to be carried out.
From the aforesaid results, it could be inferred that 
the use of coffee husk from agro-industries is highly eco­
nomical for the industrial production of these mosquito 
pathogenic bacilli, in terms of easy availability, cost-
effectiveness, efficacy, environmental safety and bio-
waste remediation. The Bs and Bti spore/crystal toxins 
produced from the experimental culture medium was 
equal to that produced by the conventional medium, 
and their protein profiles were also similar. The ento­
motoxicity studies of different mosquito species showed 
that the efficacy of Bs and Bti toxins produced from 
both media were similar. The raw material cost analysis 
indicated that the use of CHW is highly economical for 
the industrial production of these mosquito pathogenic 
bacilli. This study is, therefore, very important from the 
perspective of recycling environmental waste because 
it shows that CHW possesses the dual benefits of effec­
tive use of environmental bio-organic waste and efficient 
production of mosquitocidal biopesticides.
32.5  SUMMARY POINTS
	•	 It is well known that CHW discarded as bio-
organic waste from coffee industries is rich in 
carbohydrates.
	•	 This work emphasizes the management of solid 
waste from agro-industrial residues for the 
production of biopesticides (Bs and Bti) to control 
disease transmitting mosquito vectors.
	•	 An experimental culture medium (CHW) was 
prepared by extracting the filtrates from coffee husks. 
A conventional culture medium (NYSM) also was 
prepared. The studies revealed that the quantity of 
mosquitocidal toxins produced from CHW is on par 
with NYSM.
	•	 The bacteria produced in these media were 
bioassayed against mosquito vectors (C. 
quinquefasciatus, A. stephensi, and A. aegypti) and 
it was found that the toxic effect was statistically 
comparable.
	•	 Cost-effective analysis has revealed that production 
of biopesticides from CHW is highly economical. 
Therefore, the study is very important because it 
shows that CHW possesses the dual benefits of 
effective use of environmental waste and efficient 
production of mosquitocidal toxins.
