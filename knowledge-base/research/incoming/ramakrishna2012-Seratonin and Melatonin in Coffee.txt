Melatonin and serotonin proﬁles in beans of Coffea species
Introduction
Coﬀee is one of the most important and widely consumed
beverages. From the commercial point of view, only two
coﬀee species are cultivated extensively: Coﬀea arabica
(arabica) and Coﬀea canephora (robusta) [1]. Many bene-
ﬁcial health properties are attributed to coﬀee drink [2, 3].
In recent years, the number of studies on the composition
of phenolic acids and nitrogenous compounds such as
caﬀeine and trigonellin has increased signiﬁcantly [4].
Amorim et al. [5] were the ﬁrst to study the levels of free
amines in coﬀee. However, a few studies have been reported
on bioactive amines, which play an important role in
human health and plant development [6–9]. Melatonin
(N-acetyl-5-methoxytryptamine) and serotonin (5-hydroxy-
tryptamine, 5-HT) are neurotransmitters in animals, and
they have also been reported in plants [10, 11]. Melatonin
was ﬁrst identiﬁed in edible plants [12]. Since then, it has
been reported in tomato, olive oil, grape, walnuts, sun-
ﬂower, mustard oil, etc. [13–15], and the concentration of
melatonin in these products varies from ng to pg per gram
levels. Melatonin was identiﬁed in diﬀerent parts of the
plant including roots, stem, leaves, fruits and seeds [16–19],
and also in in vitro plant tissues, wherein it inﬂuences
growth responses [20–22]. Melatonin has been reported as a
phytochemical in olive oil, which may account for the
healthy eﬀects of the Mediterranean diet [23]. The presence
of melatonin and 5-HT in plants has been demonstrated
qualitatively by its identiﬁcation using GC-MS or LC-MS
analyses [24–27]. Recently, Sturtz et al. [28] reported the
melatonin content of diﬀerent varieties of tomatoes (Lyc-
opersicon esculentum) and strawberries (Fragaria ananassa).
5-HT was ﬁrst identiﬁed in the legume Mucuna prureins
[29] and subsequently reported in many plants including
Musa sapientum (banana) [30], pineapple [31], strawberry
[32], cherries [33] and rice [34]. 5-HT exerts several
physiological roles such as ﬂowering, morphogenesis and
adaptation to environmental changes [34, 35]. As both
melatonin and 5-HT are structurally related to the plant
hormone, indole-3-acetic acid (IAA), possibly they inﬂu-
ence physiology and development in plants [36]. The fact
that consumption of edible plant tissues that contain the
melatonin increases the circulating melatonin levels [37],
would have made wide interest to screen various plant
species for the presence of melatonin. In view of the
presence of melatonin and 5-HT in several edible plants,
medicinal herbs and seeds, their antioxidant activity may be
linked to their medicinal value for the treatment of many
human diseases such as cardiovascular damage [38], Alz-
heimers disease [39] and cancer [40]. Intake of tryptophan-
rich foods can result in elevated melatonin levels both in
tissue and in blood [41, 42].
The presence of biogenic amines in coﬀee is interesting
because they have physiological actions in humans as
neurological mediators [6] and they are also used as
chemical markers to diﬀerentiate between arabica and
robusta coﬀee samples [7]. Bioactive amine proﬁles in
instant coﬀee have been reported [43]. To our knowledge,
Abstract: A high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) and an
electrospray ionization mass spectrometry (LC/ESI-MS) methods were
applied to quantify the proﬁles of melatonin and serotonin (5-HT) in green
and roasted beans of Coﬀea canephora (robusta) and Coﬀea arabica
(arabica). Both melatonin and 5-HT were detected in green coﬀee beans
(5.8 ± 0.8 lg/g dry weight (DW), 10.5 ± 0.6 lg/g DW) and also in roasted
beans of C. canephora (8.0 ± 0.9 lg/g DW, 7.3 ± 0.5 lg/g DW). Melatonin
(3.0 ± 0.6 lg/50 mL) and 5-HT (4.0 ± 0.7 lg/50 mL) were detected in
coﬀee brew. In C. arabica, 5-HT was high in green beans (12.5 ± 0.8 lg/g
DW) compared with roasted beans (8.7 ± 0.4). The levels of melatonin were
higher (9.6 ± 0.8 lg/g DW) in roasted beans compared with green beans
(6.8 ± 0.4 lg/g DW). Both melatonin (3.9 ± 0.2 lg/50 mL) and 5-HT
(7.3 ± 0.6 lg/50 mL) were detected in coﬀee brew. Because of the relevance
of indoleamines as bioactive molecules with implications for food,
nutritional sciences and human health, it was of interest to explore their
levels in coﬀee, an important universal beverage.

Materials and methods
Plant material
Green beans (robusta cherry, grade A) and roasted beans
were obtained from M/S. Mudremane coﬀee curing works,
Mudigere, Chikamangalur district, Karnataka, India.
To prepare coﬀee brew, 5 g of ground coﬀee was placed
in a ﬁlter paper (No.4, Filter a cafe blanc, Grand Jury,
France), to which 25 mL of boiled water was added and the
brew was collected. The same procedure has been repeated
by adding 25 mL of boiled water to the powder and the
brew was collected. Both the portions of the brew were
pooled (50 mL) and were used for the extraction and
analysis of indoleamines.
Melatonin and Serotonin extraction
Extraction of melatonin and 5-HT was performed accord-
ing to the method described in the study by da Silveira et al.
[43]. Ground coﬀee sample (5-g green coﬀee or 5-g roasted
coﬀee) were mixed with 5% trichloroacetic acid (7 mL) and
submitted to agitation for 5 min in a vortex mixer. The
slurry and coﬀee brew samples were centrifuged at 10,000 g
(4C) and supernatant was collected. The sample was
passed through a Sep-pak C18 (Waters, Waters Corpora-
tion, Milford, Ireland) cartridge. The cartridge was washed
with 7.5 mL of 80% methanol. The passing solution and
washing solution were combined. The extraction procedure
was repeated twice. The mixture was then evaporated to
dryness and dissolved in 0.5 mL of 50% methanol. This
solution was injected into the chromatograph.

Chromatography and mass spectrometry
Chromatography was performed using a LC-10AD system
connected with a RF-551 spectroﬂuorometric detector at 340
and 445 nm of excitation and emission, respectively. A
reversed-phase C18 column, 300 · 3.9, 10 mm, was em-
ployed. The mobile phases were: A, a solution of 0.2 m
sodium acetate and 15 mm 1-octanesulphonic acid sodium
salt, adjusted to pH 4.9 with acetic acid, and B, acetonitrile.
The ﬂow rate was set at 0.6 mL/min. The gradient was:
13 min at 11%, B, 19 min at 29% B, 24 min at 11% B [8]. The
methanolic solution of the sample of 20 lL is injected and the
separated peaks were collected and ﬂash evaporated and
diluted in methanol and used for MS analysis.
MS unit model – Alliance equipped with an electrospray
ionization source (ESI) and coupled to Waters 2696
operation module and Waters 2696 photo diode array
detector (DAD) – was achieved with isocratic mode with
methanol/water (3:2) with a ﬂow rate of 1 mL/min with the
column temperature maintained at 25C. The detector was
set to a wavelength of 255 nm, and the eluent peak was
separately subjected to mass spectrometer unit model
QTOF Ultima Waters Corporation, Micro Mass, Man-
chester, UK. The mass spectral data were accompanied by
a Mass Lynux 4.0 SP4 data acquisition system. The
adopted ionization mode was of positive type. The interface
and the MSD parameters were as follows. The source
capillary was set to 3.5 kV, with a temperature of 80C. The
cone temperature was operated at 100C, and dry gas (N2)
was used as desolvation gas, 500 L/H; cone voltage, 35 V;
desolvation temperature, 150C; collision energy, 10 eV;
TOF kV, 9.10; connected with scan range m/z 50–1000;
injection volume, 20 lL; and data type accurate mass. The
HPLC-separated sample infusion of each peak is injected
directly on the mass head of MS instrument to conﬁrm the
identiﬁcation of the compounds. The conﬁrmation of the
occurrence of compounds is based on the molecular ions in
the MS spectra as well as with authentic standards.

Discussion
In this study, signiﬁcant levels of melatonin and 5-HT were
detected in beans of both Coﬀea spp. as analysed by HPLC
and LC-MS. Similar reports on melatonin and 5-HT
identiﬁcation in plants by various analytical methods were
reported [24, 27, 28, 33], wherein HPLC with ﬂuorimetric
detection, solid-phase ELISA tests [14] and radioimmuno-
assay (RIA) [45] were used. But RIA has limitation as
plant tissues contain a variety of molecules that may cross-
react
with
the
antimelatonin
antibody
leading
to
a
potential over estimation of the actual melatonin values
[10]. HPLC with ﬂuorescence detector provided sensitive
methodology to identify melatonin and 5-HT samples [25].
The presence of 5-HT in coﬀee beans might have beneﬁcial
inﬂuence on consumer health [7]. Phytomelatonin in
walnuts is reported to contribute to enhancement of
indoles in oﬀering protection against cardiovascular dam-
age [37]. Earlier studies suggest that antioxidant potential
of melatonin [46] and 5-HT protects diﬀerent plant tissues,
particularly reproductive tissues, fruits and germ tissues of
the seed, from oxidative stress caused by abiotic and
environmental stresses such as drought, cold, heat, ultra-
violet light and ozone [34, 35]. Previous ﬁndings revealed
that melatonin is not only an endogenously produced
antioxidant, but also consumed in the diet. Dietary
melatonin could be signiﬁcant in protecting against oxida-
tive
damage
[47].
Hardman
et al.
[48]
reported
the
suppression of cancer growth by dietary walnut. Moreover,
the presence of melatonin in plant products and its
consumption are reported to elevate melatonin proﬁles of
blood, leading to improved antioxidant activity [37, 45].
Robusta green coﬀee is reported to contain high trypt-
amine levels; therefore, the use of this coﬀee variety at a
higher proportion in the blend could provide a coﬀee with
higher tryptamine content [6]. Even Coﬀee seeds wax
(4.1 mg/g 5-HT) [44] and also polished coﬀee [49] called
Astra contain 20–40 mg 5-HT/100 g. In the present study,
we were able to identify 5-HT and melatonin both in green
and roasted beans and also in decoction. Da Silveira et al.
[43] reported that during roasting of coﬀee, there is a
signiﬁcant reduction in the levels of amines. Also, there is
a large variation in biogenic amines in Coﬀea [50].
Similarly, roasting temperature inﬂuences indoleamine
proﬁle. However, coﬀee beans submitted to a light roast
contain low levels of 5-HT [7, 8]. In regular coﬀee, 5-HT
contributed most to total levels, followed by cadaverine,
spermidine and tryptamine [43]. According to Lima [51],
intake of four to ﬁve 50-mL cups of coﬀee beverage
provide 24.5 lg tryptamine and 39 lg 5-HT daily. During
the roasting process, most of the free amines present in
green coﬀee are degraded because of pyrolysis, which has
an adverse eﬀect on the 5-HT because of oxidation and
breakage of the carbon chain. However, roasted coﬀee
contained mainly 5-HT, which seems to be the most
resistant amine to the roasting process [43]. It is inferred
that the diﬀerence in melatonin and 5-HT in green to
roasted beans is signiﬁcant. The presence of indoleamines
in roasted beans that pass into coﬀee brew may be useful
for consumers. For the ﬁrst time, we are able to identify
the presence of signiﬁcant levels of melatonin in coﬀee
beans. Moreover, 5-HT serves as key substrate for the
synthesis of 5-HT derivatives such as feruloylserotonin and
4–coumaroylserotonin, which have strong antioxidative
activity, chemotherapeutic eﬀects and antimicrobial activ-
ity against plant pathogens [52].
To summarize, the occurrence of both melatonin and
5-HT in green and roasted beans as well as in brew of
Coﬀea spp. was evident from our study. Coﬀea contain
substantial amounts of melatonin. Considering melatonins
antioxidant activity coﬀee may be beneﬁcial in counteract-
ing some conditions in which free radicals damage [46, 53].
In view of the antioxidant power of these molecules, they
would be of nutraceutical value apart from their role as
neurotransmitters. The levels of 5-HT is high in wax layer
of coﬀee beans when compared to roasted beans. However,
it would be of interest to investigate the high levels of 5-HT
(up to 4.1 mg/g) in coﬀee wax and its signiﬁcance, if any, in
protecting the green beans from damage upon long
preservation before subjecting them for roasting. Further
studies are needed to determine the importance of these
indoleamines in human health.
