Cafestol, a coffee-speciﬁc diterpene, induces peripheral antinociception
mediated by endogenous opioid peptides
Luciana S Guzzo,* Andrea C Perez,* Thiago RL Romero,* Adolfo O Azevedo† and Igor DG Duarte*
*Department of Pharmacology, Institute of Biological Sciences, Federal University of Minas Gerais (UFMG), Belo
Horizonte, and †Department of Pharmacology, University Center of Lavras (UNILAVRAS), Lavras, Minas Gerais, Brazil
SUMMARY
1. The opioid peptides have been implicated in peripheral
antinociception
induced
by
non-opioidergic
compounds,
including non-steroidal anti-inﬂammatory drugs and a2-adre-
noceptor agonists. The aims of the present study were to
investigate the possible peripheral antinociceptive effect of
cafestol, a diterpene present in the oil derived from coffee
beans, and to evaluate the involvement of opioid peptides in
its effect.
2. The rat paw pressure test was used to assess antinoci-
peptive effects. Hyperalgesia was induced by intraplantar
injection of prostaglandin E2 (2 lg/paw). All drugs were
locally administered into the hind-paws of male Wistar rats.
3. Intraplantar injection of cafestol (20, 40 and 80 lg)
induced peripheral antinociception. The antinociceptive effect
of cafestol was due to a local action because the higher dose
(80 lg/paw) did not produce any effect in the contralateral
paw. The opioid receptor antagonist naloxone (25, 50 and
100 lg/paw) prevented the action of cafestol (80 lg/paw),
whereas the aminopeptidase inhibitor bestatin (400 lg/paw)
potentiated the antinociceptive effect of cafestol (40 lg/paw).
4. The results of the present study provide evidence that
cafestol treatment has a peripheral antinociceptive effect and
suggest that this effect is mediated by the release of endoge-
nous opioids.
Key words: cafestol, opioid, peripheral antinociception.
INTRODUCTION
It is being increasingly acknowledged that foods and beverages
contain non-nutritional constituents that may have biological
activity with beneﬁcial health effects, such as antinociceptive and
anticarcinogenic actions.1,2 The full assessment of such food
components requires a thorough investigation of both efﬁcacy
and safety.
Cafestol and kahweol can be considered as examples of such
biologically active food components. Kahweol and its dehydro
derivative cafestol are naturally occurring diterpenes found only
in the unsaponiﬁable lipid fraction of coffee. The cafestol and
kahweol content in a coffee drink is signiﬁcantly inﬂuenced by
the brewing method. These diterpenes are extracted from ground
coffee during brewing, but are mostly removed from coffee by
paper ﬁlters. Turkish-, Scandinavian- and French press (cafetière)-
style coffees contain high levels of cafestol and kahweol, whereas
ﬁltered, percolated and instant coffees contain low levels of these
diterpenes.3
Cafestol and kahweol have been shown to exhibit both
adverse and chemoprotective effects. It is well documented that
a mixture of these diterpenes increases blood cholesterol in both
human and animal models.4 However, animal studies have
shown that cafestol and kahweol afford protection against well-
known carcinogens.5 The chemoprotective effects of cafestol
and kahweol have thus far been primarily related to beneﬁcial
modiﬁcations of xenobiotic metabolism. Such effects include
the reduced activation of mutagens and carcinogens (e.g. via the
inhibition
of
cytochrome
P450
enzymes),6
as
well
as
the
enhanced detoxiﬁcation of carcinogens and mutagens
(e.g. via
the induction of carcinogen-detoxifying enzyme systems such as
glutathione S-transferase and uridine 50 diphospho-glucuronosyl
transferase).7
Prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) is a principal pro-inﬂammatory pro-
stanoid that is the primary contributor to one of the key features
of inﬂammation, namely pain hypersensitivity.8 Kim et al.9 found
that cafestol and kahweol have strong inhibitory activity against
PGE2 production in vitro, suggesting an anti-inﬂammatory and
analgesic activity of these diterpenes. Although one study demon-
strated the anti-inﬂammatory activity of kahweol in vivo,10 there
have been no studies that have investigated the antinociceptive
actions of these diterpenes. Thus, the aim of the present study
was to determine whether cafestol has a peripheral antinocicep-
tive effect and, if so, to evaluate the involvement of endogenous
opioid peptides in this effect.

0600–1800 hours). All tests were conducted during the light

Experimentation of UFMG.
Measurement of hyperalgesia
Hyperalgesia was induced by subcutaneous injection of PGE2
(2 lg) into the plantar surface of the hind paw. Hyperalgesia was
measured according to the paw pressure test described by Randall
and Sellito11 and Green and Young.12 An analgesimeter was used
(Ugo Basile, Varese, Italy) ﬁtted with a cone-shaped paw presser
with a rounded tip, which applies a linearly increasing force to
the hind paw. The weight (in g) required to elicit a nociceptive
response of paw ﬂexion was determined as the nociceptive
threshold. A cut-off value of 300 g was used to reduce the possi-
bility of paw damage. The nociceptive threshold was measured in
the right paw and determined as the average of three consecutive
trials recorded prior to and 3 h after PGE2 injection. The thresh-
old was calculated as the difference between these two averages
(0 h – 3 h values, D nociceptive threshold) and is expressed in
grams. The positive value for the D nociceptive threshold means
hyperalgesia.
Chemicals
The following chemicals were used: PGE2 (Sigma, St Louis, MO,
USA), cafestol acetate (Sigma), naloxone (Sigma) and bestatin
(Tocris, Ellisville, MO, USA). Drugs were dissolved prior to use
as follows: PGE2 was dissolved in 2% ethanol in saline; cafestol
acetate was dissolved in 10% dimethylsulphoxide (DMSO) in sal-
ine; naloxone and bestatin were dissolved in saline. All drugs were
administered in a volume of 50 lL/paw, with the exception of
PGE2, which was injected in a volume of 100 lL/paw.
Experimental protocol
Cafestol was administered subcutaneously in the right hind paw
2 h and 55 min after local injection of PGE2. In studies examin-
ing whether cafestol had any effect outside the injected paw,
PGE2 was injected into both hind paws, cafestol was adminis-
tered 2 h and 55 min later into the left paw and the nociceptive
threshold was measured in the right paw. Naloxone, bestatin and
the vehicle controls were administered 30 min prior to injection
of cafestol.
It should be noted that the doses and timing of administration
of naloxone and bestatin were decided on the basis of data avail-
able in the literature,13 as well as preliminary experiments. How-
ever, there are no published studies regarding the antinociceptive
effects of cafestol. Thus, various doses of cafestol were adminis-
tered at various times, with the nociceptive threshold always
measured in the third hour after the administration of PGE2 to
coincide the peak of PGE2 hyperalgesia and cafestol antinocicep-
tion. On the basis of results from preliminary experiments, we
concluded that the highest antinociceptive effect of cafestol was
seen 5 min after the injection of 80 lg/paw. Two researchers per-
formed each of the experiments: the researcher making the mea-
surements was unaware of the treatments given.
Statistical analysis
The data obtained from the nitrite determination were evaluated
by Student’s t-test, whereas other data were analysed by one-way
analysis of variance (ANOVA) with the post hoc Bonferroni test for
multiple comparisons. All P-values were two-sided and P < 0.05
was considered signiﬁcant. Statistical analyses were performed
using GraphPad Prism version 5.0 for Windows (GraphPad Soft-
ware, San Diego, CA, USA).
RESULTS
Peripheral antinociceptive effects of cafestol
Cafestol injection (20, 40 and 80 lg) into the right hind paw
dose-dependently produced an antinociceptive response against
PGE2-induced hyperalgesia (2 lg/paw; Fig. 1), although the
effect did not reach statistical signiﬁcance for the lowest dose
tested (20 lg/paw). The antinociceptive effect of cafestol at the
highest dose (80 lg/paw) was signiﬁcant 5 and 20 min (85.1%
and 36.6%, respectively) after intraplantar injection, whereas
the effect of 40 lg/paw cafestol was signiﬁcant only 5 min
after intraplantar injection (42.0%). Intraplantar injections of
2% ethanol and 10% DMSO solutions did not have any antin-
ociceptive or hyperalgesic effects. Despite 80 lg/paw cafestol
almost completely reverse the hyperalgesia induced by PGE2,
this dose of cafestol alone did not alter the nociceptive thresh-
old (Fig. 1). When administered into the left paw, 80 lg cafe-
stol did not produce an antinociceptive effect in the right paw,

Involvement of opioid receptors and endogenous opioids
in cafestol-induced antinociception
The opioid antagonist naloxone (25, 50 and 100 lg/paw) dose-
dependently antagonized cafestol-induced peripheral antinocicep-
tion (80 lg/paw), producing 3.9%, 49.5% and 95.3% inhibition,
respectively (Fig. 3). When injected alone, naloxone did not
induce antinociception or inhibit PGE2-induced hyperalgesia
(Fig. 3). After having determined the involvement of opioid
receptors in the mechanism of cafestol-induced antinociception,
we went on to show that endogenous opioids were also involved
in this effect. Intraplantar administration of bestatin (400 lg),
which inhibits the aminopeptidase involved in the degradation
of endogenous opioid peptides, increased the peripheral antinoci-
ceptive effect of 40 lg/paw cafestol (41.4% increase; Fig. 4).
Alone, bestatin had no effect on peripheral antinociception
(Fig. 4).
DISCUSSION
In the present study, we demonstrated that cafestol had a periph-
eral antinociceptive effect against PGE2-induced rat paw hyperal-
gesia. It should be noted that the control solutions (2% ethanol
and 10% DMSO) did not cause any antinociceptive or hyperalge-
sic effects, as demonstrated in previous studies.14,15
To exclude the possibility that 80 lg/paw cafestol produced its
antinociceptive effect by acting at sites outside the paw, we eval-
uated the efﬁcacy of ipsilateral versus contralateral paw adminis-
tration. In these experiments, PGE2 was administered into both
hind paws, thus creating the same tissue conditions and equal
possibility that the agents would reach sites outside the injected
paw. Cafestol was then administered into the left paw and the
nociceptive threshold was measured in the right hind paw. Cafe-
stol was ineffective at producing antinociception in the right paw,
indicating that, at this dose, it was only effective locally.

Previous studies have described the involvement of opioid pep-
tides in peripheral antinociception induced by some non-opioider-
gic
compounds,
including
non-steroidal
anti-inﬂammatory
drugs,16 the a2-adrenergic drugs clonidine17,18 and xylazine13 and
a selective endothelin ETB receptor agonist.19 To test this
hypothesis, the opioid receptor antagonist naloxone was used in
the present study as a pretreatment to cafestol. In the present
experiments, naloxone blocked the peripheral antinociceptive
effect of cafestol in a dose-dependent manner.
In addition to naloxone, bestatin was used to inhibit the action
of aminopeptidase, enzyme involved in the degradation of endog-
enous opioid peptides.20,21 Bestatin improved the peripheral an-
tinociceptive effect of cafestol, strongly suggesting mobilization
of endogenous opioid peptides.
Opioid peptides binding to their receptors may attenuate the
excitability of nociceptive afferent neurons, impair the propaga-
tion of action potentials and inhibit the release of excitatory
neurotransmitters and pro-inﬂammatory neuropeptides (e.g. sub-
stance P, calcitonin gene-related peptide) from nociceptor end-
ings, producing analgesia.22 In studies involving hybridization,
the presence of mRNA encoding the opioid peptides was
observed in T and B lymphocytes, monocytes and macrophages.23
The occurrence of opioid peptides, such as endorphin, enkephalin
and
dynorphin,
in
cells
of
the
immune
system
has
been
reported.24,25 The mechanism by which CB2 cannabinoid agon-
ists26 and a selective endothelin ETB receptor agonist27 induce
peripheral antinociception is dependent on keratinocyte action,
which releases the opioid peptide b-endorphin. In addition, stud-
ies using reverse transcription–polymerase chain reaction and
immunohistochemistry revealed that keratinocytes express the
j-opioid receptor and its ligand dynorphin A.28
Knowing that opioid peptides can activate opioid receptors in
peripheral sensory nerves and inhibit nociception29 and that the
peripheral opioid receptors are largely expressed by primary sen-
sory neurons,30 it is probable that cafestol produces peripheral
antinociception indirectly via stimulation of resident immune cells
and/or keratinocytes containing opioid peptides.
The results of the present study demonstrate, for the ﬁrst time,
the antinociceptive effect of cafestol. Moreover, after analysing
the effects of naloxone and bestatin on the actions of cafestol, we
propose that cafestol may induce peripheral antinociception indi-
rectly via the release of endogenous opioid peptides, leading to
the activation of opioid receptors in nociceptive afferent neurons.
These results suggest that cafestol may be a substance with
potential applicability in pain therapy, which broadens prospects
for future research.
