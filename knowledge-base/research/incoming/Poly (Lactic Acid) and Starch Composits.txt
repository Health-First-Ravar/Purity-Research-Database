Physical Properties of Poly(Lactic Acid) and Starch Composites
with Various Blending Ratios1
Tianyi Ke2 and Xiuzhi Sun2,3
ABSTRACT
Cereal Chem. 77(6):761–768
Two-phase polymer blends of poly(lactic acid) (PLA) and corn or wheat
starches at various ratios were prepared by using a laboratory-scale twin-
screw extruder and compression molding. The blends were characterized
for thermal transitions, mechanical properties, and water absorption. Starch
and PLA were immiscible polymers, and the thermal behavior of PLA
was not affected by starch. Crystallinity of the blends decreased in some
degree as starch content increased 20–40%. Tensile strength and elongation
of the blends decreased as starch content increased, but modulus increased
as starch content increased up to 70%. As starch content increased to
>60%, the PLA phase became discontinuous, and water absorption of the
blends increased sharply. Blends made from wheat starch gave slightly
better mechanical properties than those made from corn starch, and no
differences in other properties were observed.
Advanced technology in petrochemical polymers has brought
many benefits to mankind. However, it becomes more evident that
the ecosystem is considerably disturbed and damaged as a result
of the nondegradable materials used for disposable items. The
environmental impact of persistent plastic wastes is causing more
global concern, and alternative disposal methods are limited. Incin-
eration may generate toxic air pollution, and satisfactory landfill
sites are limited. Also, petroleum resources are finite and are becom-
ing limited. Finding durable plastic substitutes, especially for short-
term packaging and disposable applications has become important.
Recently, the continuously growing concern of the public for the
problem has stimulated research interest in biodegradable polymers
as alternatives to conventional nondegradable polymers such as
polyethylene and polystyrene.
Starch may offer a substitute for petroleum-based plastics. It is
a renewable, degradable, carbohydrate biopolymer derived from
various sources. However, starch by itself has severe limitations
because of its sensitivity to water. Articles made from starch will
swell and deform upon exposure to moisture. Starch often has been
blended with petroleum polymers to improve its properties, to
increase its biodegradability, and to reduce the usage of petroleum
polymer. For example, starch was blended with polyethylene (PE)
(Griffin 1994a,b; St.-Pierre et al 1997), low-density polyethylene
(LDPE) (Arvanitoyannis et al 1997, 1998; Willett 1994), poly
(ethylene-co-acrylic acid) (EAA) (Otey et al 1974, Fanta et al 1990,
Shogren et al 1991), polystyrene (PS) (Vaidya and Bhattacharya
1994, Yang et al 1996, Cha et al 1999), poly(ethylene-vinyl
alcohol) (EVA) (Simmons anf Thomas 1995), etc. However, the
fraction of petroleum polymers in the blends still remains nonde-
gradable. Blending starch with biodegradable synthetic polymers
became an interesting and promising target.
The most common biodegradable synthetic polymers include
aliphatic polyesters, such as poly(glycolic acids) (PGA), poly(lactic
acids) (PLA), polycaprolactone (PCL), polyhydroxybutyrate (PHB)
and poly(hydroxybutyrate-co-hydroxyvalerate) (PHBV). PGA was
first developed as a fully synthetic absorbable suture that has been
commercially available under the trade name of Dexon since 1970
(Frazza and Schmitt 1971), but the suture tended to lose mechanical
strength rapidly due to the hydrophilic nature of PGA (Reed and
Gilding 1981). PCL is a semicystalline polymer with a melting
temperature of 59–64°C and a glass transition of –60°C. PCL has
a significantly slower degradation rate compared with PGA or
PLA and is suitable for relevant long-term application (Pitt 1990),
but its low melting temperature greatly limits the application. PHB
was identified as a promising biomaterial in the 1980s, and PHB
and PHBV were commercially available under the trade name of
Biopol in the late 1980s (Miller and Williams 1987). Among these
biopolymers, PLA has been studied extensively for tissue engineering
and drug delivery systems since the 1980s, and is the most widely
used synthetic degradable polymers in human medicine (Huang 1989).
PLA has good biodegradability (Reed and Gilding 1981, Leenslag
and Pennings 1987, Nakamura et al 1989) and mechanical prop-
erties (Engelberg and Kohn 1991), and the semicrystalline PLA is
preferred in cases where high mechanical strength and toughness
are required (Leenslag and Pennings 1987, Vainiopaa et al 1987,
Hay et al 1988). These properties have made PLA attractive for dis-
posable and biodegradable plastic substitutes. However, in some
instances, PLA is still more expensive than conventional plastics,
and the degradation rate is still slow compared with the waste
accumulation rate (Ramsay et al 1993).
Native starch for industrial use is ≈$0.09/lb, so blending starches
with PLA could dramatically reduce the cost and increase the degra-
dation rate. In addition, the raw material of PLA, lactic acid, is derived
from carbohydrates of renewable resources such as corn by fermen-
tation. Blending PLA with starch will develop a kind of biodegra-
dable plastics completely derived from agriculture products. Starch has
been blended with synthetic biodegradable polymers such as PCL
(Tokiwa et al 1990, Schroeter and Hobelsberger 1992, Doi and
Fukuda 1994, Koening and Huang 1995, Krishnan and Narayan
1996), PHB, and PHBV (Ramsay et al 1993; Shogren 1995; Iman
et al 1995, 1998; Kontis et al 1995; Zhang et al 1997; Willet and
O’Brien 1997). Recently some works about PLA and starch blends have
also been published. Ajioka et al (1995) prepared starch and PLA
blends by using a sealed mixer and hot-pressed the blends into trans-
parent film using ethyl oleate as the plasticizer. Jacobsen and Fritz
(1996) studied the thermal behavior of PLA filled with native corn
starch in the presence of polyethylene-glycol as a plasticizer. PEG
lowered the glass transition temperature and enhanced crystalli-
zation of PLA at room temperature. The addition of native corn starch
to either pure PLA or to the blend of PLA-PEG did not influence
the thermal behavior of PLA. Kim et al (1998) prepared blends
from poly(L-lactic acid) (PLLA) and corn starch or high-amylose
corn starch to ≤40%. They found that the tensile strength and elon-
gation at the break of the blends decreased as starch content increased.
At the same starch content, the blend with high-amylose corn
starch gave higher tensile strength than with native corn starch.
Park et al (1999) compared the blends from PLLA and S-PLA (star-
shaped PLLA) in the presence of PCL (≤30%) and high-amylose
corn starch (≤30%). They found that the blends from PLLA had
better mechanical properties than that from S-PLA containing the

MATERIALS AND METHODS
Industrial corn starch (Silver Medal Pearl-1100) was purchased
from Cargill (Minneapolis, MN) and wheat starch (Midsol 50) was
obtained from Midwest Grain Products (Atchison, KS). Granules
of both starches contained ≈75% amylopectin and 25% amylose.
PLA obtained from Shimadzu (Japan), had a molecular weight of
≈120,000 Da and was polymerized mainly from L-lactic acid.
Preparation of Blends
Both corn and wheat starches were dried in an air oven at 130°C
for ≥2 hr to assure that no weight loss was observed. The PLA
was ground into small particles of ≈2 mm. The dried corn and wheat
starches and ground PLA were premixed using a stand mixer
(Ultra Power Kitchen Aid, St. Joseph, MI) at ratios of 20:80
(starch-to-PLA), 40:60, 50:50, 60:40, 70:30, and 80:20 at room
conditions. The mixture was stored in a polyethylene zip plastic
bag for ≈1 hr and then blended in a laboratory-scale co-rotating
conical twin-screw extruder (TW-100, Haake, Paramus, NJ) with
a screw diameter of 19.1 mm, a L/D ratio of 25:1, and with constant
pitch and constant flight depth.
The mixture was loaded into a feeding funnel by hand. It was
extruded through a rod die 8 mm in diameter at a temperature
profile of 120°C (zone 1, near to the feed inlet), 185°C (zone 2),
185°C (zone 3), and 185°C (at the die). The screw speed was fixed
at 100 rpm. The output rate was ≈15 g/min. The extruded rods were
cut into short cylinders ≈10 mm in length and then were ground
into small particles of ≈2 mm using a laboratory mill (model 4,
Thomas-Wiley Co., Philadelphia, PA). The ground blends were
dried again in the oven at 110°C for 24 hr before molding.

Dried ground blend sample (≈4.5 g) was placed in a dog bone
shaped tensile bar mold and compression-molded using a hot press
(model, 3890 Auto “M”, Carver. Inc., Wabash, IN) according to
standard method D638-92 (ASTM 1995). The tensile bar was pressed
at 180°C and 4.3 MPa for ≈4.5 min. The mold was removed from
the hot press and cooled to room temperature, and then the tensile
bar was removed with an aluminum foil and zinc stearate mold
release. Tensile bars were stored in plastic bags for a week at room
temperature and then were conditioned for 48 hr in the chamber
(50% rh, 23°C) before mechanical analysis. Five tensile bars were
prepared for each experiment.
Thermal Analysis
Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) analysis was performed
to measure thermal transition (DSC7, Perkin-Elmer, Norwalk, CT).
The DSC instrument was calibrated using the melting temperature
and enthalpy of the standard material indium. The samples of dried
and ground blends were weighed accurately into an aluminum pan
and sealed hermetically. An empty pan was used as a reference.
The DSC temperature increased at 25–200°C at a heating rate of
10°C/min. Peak temperatures and enthalpies at glass transition,
crystallization, and melting were calculated. The sum of the enthalpy
of the blends at different thermal stages was used as a rough esti-
mation for crystallinity (Xc) according Eq. 1 (Fischer et al 1973):
Xc (%) = (∆Hm + ∆Hci) × 100/(93 × XPLA)
(1)
where ∆Hm and ∆Hci are the enthalpies (J/g) of fusion and cry-
stallization of the blend, respectively; 93 J/g is the enthalpy of fusion
of a PLA crystal of infinite size; and XPLA is the PLA content.
Mechanical Analysis
Tensile tests were performed using an Instron testing machine
(model 4466, Canton, MA) according to standard method D638-92

(ASTM 1995) with a crosshead speed of 5 mm/min and a gauge
length of 30 mm. All samples were preconditioned at an ambient
temperature (23°C) and 50% rh for 48 hr, and tests were also per-
formed at these conditions. Dynamic mechanical properties were
determined with a dynamic mechanical analyzer (DMA) (DMA-
7e, Perkin-Elmer, Norwalk, CT) using a three-point bending rec-

loss (E″) modulus and tanδ were analyzed.
Scanning Electron Microscope
Microstructures of the fractured samples obtained after tensile
testing were observed by using a Hitachi S-3500N scanning electron
microscope (SEM) (Hitachi Science Systems, Ltd., Ibaraki, Japan)
at an accelerated voltage of 20 kV.
Water Absorption
Water absorption was determined using standard method D570-81
(ASTM 1995). Tensile bars were weighed and then soaked in distilled
water at ambient temperature for 1, 3, 6, 9, and 15 days. The
samples were removed from the soaking tank and blotted gently
with tissue paper, weighed, and then put back into the soaking tank
for the next measurement. To observe water absorption and starch
leaching of these blends, tensile bars were immersed in boiling
water for 1 hr, dried at 110°C for 24 hr, cooled to room temper-
ature in a desiccator, and then weighed. Weight loss was estimated
according to the weight recorded before immersing into the water
and after drying. In this experiment, it is assumed that the hydrolyza-
tion of PLA at boiling temperature for 1 hr was minor (Gruber et
al 1996) and was not determined. Each measurement was replicated
three times.
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
Extrudate and Tensile Bar Appearance
The color of extrudates with 20% starch in the blends was light
gray and became basically off-white as the starch contents increased
to ≥40%. The extrudates were a continuous strand from the die even
when the starch content was 70% but became partly discontinuous
when the starch content was >70%. The surface of extrudate was
smooth at <60% starch and became rough after that. The surfaces of
all tensile bars were smooth, and no starch lump was observed at
<60% starch content. At 70% starch content, starch lump slightly
appeared on the surface of the tensile bar, and at 80% starch con-
tent, more starch lump was observed.
Scanning Electron Microscope
The fracture structure of tensile bars after the tensile test was
recorded by SEM (Fig. 1). PLA was clearly the continuous phase
with starch as a filler in this two-phase system. The PLA phase
became discontinuous when starch content increased to ≥60% and,
at 80% starch, the starch particles were not completely covered by
PLA phase and more starch particles were aggregated. Some gaps
were also observed between the starch granules and PLA phase in
the blends for all weight ratios, which indicated likely poor adhe-
sion between the two phases.
The corn starch particles in the blend were the mixture of round
and angular granules of ≈10 µm, and the wheat starch particles in
the blend were mostly oval granules of 10–20 µm, in agreement with
the shape and size of raw corn and wheat starch. This indicated
that the starch maintained its granular state mostly due to the lack
of water even though heat and mechanical shear were applied during
blending in the extruder.

was an aging peak, which was in agreement with the results reported
by Cai et al (1996). Besides the Tg and Tm, two crystallization
peaks, a major one at 102.6°C and a minor one at 158.3°C, were
observed for the extruded pure PLA (Fig. 2). The major crystal-

cooled to room temperature at ambient conditions. Although the

recrystallization, the PLA still gained some degree of crystallinity.
The thermal transition behavior of the two blends (corn starch
and PLA 40:60, wheat starch and PLA 40:60) was similar to that
of extruded pure PLA (Fig. 2). These two blends had a glass
transition accompanied by an aging peak at 58–63°C, a major cold
crystallinization peak at 100–110°C, a minor cold crystallinization
peak at ≈155°C, and a melting peak at 170°C. No significant change
in Tg among these blends at varied weight ratios for both corn and
wheat starches was observed (Table I). As expected, the starch
and PLA blend was a filled system, which was supported by the
SEM observations (Fig. 1). Similarly, the crystallinization tempera-
tures of the blends were not influenced by starch content, which
was not in agreement with the results reported by Park et al (1999),
who stated that the crystallization temperature of the blend was
reduced to 105°C from 121.8°C (for raw PLA) because of the
addition of starch, but the crystallization temperature of the blend
was not affected by starch content in this study. As mentioned
above, such a large decrease of extruded sample from that of the
raw PLA in crystallization temperature might be caused by heating
(extrusion) history instead of the addition of starch. In this study,
at starch contents of <20%, the crystallinity of the blends was
almost the same as that of the extruded pure PLA, but decreased
slightly as starch content increased to 40%, and then leveled off as
starch content continued to increase (Fig. 3). The lowest crystal-
linity occurred at 60% corn starch and 50% wheat starch (Fig. 3).
The difference between corn and wheat starch might be caused by
the difference in starch granule shape and size as well as size dis-
tribution.
Mechanical Properties
As expected, the tensile strength and elongation of the blends
decreased as starch content increased (Table II) for both corn and
wheat starches. Starch acted as a filler in the PLA continuous matrix.
As the blends were subjected to an external tensile load, the PLA
matrix was the main load-bearing phase. As the starch content
increased, the PLA matrix became discontinuous and the effective
cross-section area of the continuous phase reduced, resulting in
decreases in strength and elongation. The modulus of the blends
increased slightly as the starch content increased. As starch content
increased to 80%, the modulus of the blends decreased due to the
degree of the discontinuous PLA phase increasing.

The blends with wheat starch had slightly higher tensile strength
and elongation than the blends with corn starch (Table II). This
might have been caused by the variations in starch granular size
and shape. The average granular size of wheat starch was larger than
that of corn starch, so the contact surface area at the interface
between PLA and wheat starch would be smaller than that between
PLA and corn starch. Because PLA and starch are immiscible, the
blend with larger contact interface area would possibly have lower
tensile strength.

then leveled off at 65.6–72.9°C, completing the glass transition.

Water Absorption
As mentioned before, starch is a hydrophilic polymer and sensi-
tive to water, and PLA is a hydrophobic polymer with high water
resistance. As expected, the water absorption of the blends increased
when PLA decreased. Water absorption behaviors of the blends
were the same for both corn and wheat starches (Figs. 7 and 8).
Water absorption of the blends increased greatly in the first day
and then leveled off after three days of water soaking (Figs. 7A and
8A). It increased slowly at a lower starch content to ≤60%, and then
increased fast as starch content increased from 70 to 80% (Figs.
7B and 8B). The water absorbed by the blends was contributed
mainly by the starch. In this two-phase blending, starch acted as a
filler in the continuous PLA matrix. At low starch content, such as
20%, PLA formed a very good continuous phase that covered the
starch (Fig. 1), resulting in a lower water absorption. For example,
it was <5% at 20% starch content during 15 days of water soaking.
However, as starch content increased to >60%, the degree of PLA
discontinuous phase increased greatly. For example, at 70–80%
starch (Fig. 1) the void in the blends was evident. Water could pene-
trate the blends through voids and be absorbed by the starch, result-
ing in a higher water absorption.
The blends may be used as fast food utensil and may withstand
contact with hot water for a short time. The water absorption of the
blends in boiling water was preliminarily observed and measured.
The water absorption of the blends in boiling water for 1 hr was
obviously higher than that in the water at room temperature (Fig. 9).
The water absorption increased slowly as starch content increased
from 20 to 50%, and then increased greatly as starch content increased
continuously. The inflection points in the water absorption curves
decreased from 60 to 50%. In addition, free starch leached from the
blends into the boiling water and increased as starch content increased
(Fig. 9). This may have been due to the solubility of starch in the
hot water. The observed bar became soft in hot water but still more
stiff than the pure PLA bar. The softening of the blends at higher
temperature need to be further improved.
CONCLUSIONS
No change in PLA thermal behaviors was observed in the presence
of starch in the blends. Starch acted as a filler and PLA as a contin-
uous matrix phase. Tensile strength and elongation of the blends
decreased as starch content increased. However, the rate of decrease
was slower when compared with that predicted by the pure particle
filling equations. This indicates that possible adhesion between starch
and PLA existed. Both Young’s modulus and the dynamic storage
modulus of the blends increased as starch content increased to
≤70%. Water absorption increased slowly as starch content increased
to ≤60% and then increased rapidly at higher starch contents. Cry-
stallinity was not affected significantly by starch content. Generally,
wheat and corn starches had similar blending properties, but blends
from wheat starch had slightly higher mechanical properties.
