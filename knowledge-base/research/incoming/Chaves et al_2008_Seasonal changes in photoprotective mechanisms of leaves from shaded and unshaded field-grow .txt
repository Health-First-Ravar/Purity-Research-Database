Abstract
Coffee is native to shady environments, but
often grows and yields better without shade. Thus, it may
be reasoned that coffee leaves should display enough
plasticity to acclimate themselves to contrasting light
environments. However, little is known about mechanisms
associated with such plasticity in coffee. This work aimed,
therefore, to explore differences in leaf photoprotective
mechanisms. Plants were grown in the ﬁeld and received
either 48 or 100% natural light. Evaluations were made
using outer leaves from the sun-facing sides of the coffee
hedgerow in Vic¸osa (Brazil) in August and October, when
growth and photosynthetic rates are expected to be minimal
and maximal, respectively, and in December, when tem-
porary
depressions
in
those
variables
are
common.
Regardless of light treatments, coffee leaves showed: (1)
very low photosynthetic rates (generally below 2.5 lmol
m-2 s-1), (2) chronic photoinhibition in August (dry, cool
season) that was accompanied by strong loss of pigment
concentration, and (3) discrete, dynamic photoinhibition in
October and December (rainy, warm season). Compared
with shaded leaves, sunlit leaves generally exhibited lower
pigment concentration, lower quantum yield of electron
transport, steeper inclinations and similar electron transport
rate. Total ascorbate pool tended to be larger in sunlit than
in shaded leaves (but with similar redox state), whereas
activities of key antioxidant enzymes, as well as mal-
ondialdehyde accumulation and electrolyte leakage, were
similar between those leaf types. As a whole, the photo-
synthetic apparatus of the coffee tree showed a low
phenotypic plasticity to varying irradiance.

Introduction
Exposure of plants to light energy that is in excess of that
required for photosynthesis may result in an energy
imbalance that often leads to photoinhibition. This process
has been associated either with rapidly reversible down-
regulation of photosystem (PS) II photochemical efﬁciency
(i.e., dynamic photoinhibition) or with the maintenance of
slow reversible energy dissipating mechanisms, repair
processes or with permanent damage to the photosynthetic
apparatus (i.e., chronic photoinhibition; Allen and Ort
2001). Chronic photoinhibition can signiﬁcantly decrease
plant productivity (Huner et al. 1998). In this context,
avoidance of light absorption, e.g., through low chlorophyll
(Chl) contents and steep inclinations to, and large reﬂec-
tance of, incident radiation plays an important role in
protecting the photosynthetic apparatus of leaves (Adams
et al. 2004). Nonetheless, whenever the absorbed light
energy exceeds the capacity of the plant to use the trapped

such as superoxide and H2O2. To cope with ROS, plants
are endowed with a complex antioxidant system including
enzymes such as superoxide dismutases (SOD), which
catalyze the reaction from superoxide to H2O2, and catalase
(CAT) and ascorbate peroxidase (APX), which function to
detoxify the H2O2 produced (Asada 1999; Logan et al.
2006). In addition, detoxiﬁcation of ROS can be conducted
by means of antioxidant molecules, both lipophilic (a-
tocopherol, b-carotene) and hydrophilic (ascorbate, gluta-
thione; Logan et al. 2006). Failure in the antioxidant
defense system may result in damage when metabolites and
components of the cellular machinery react with ROS,
resulting in lipid peroxidation and oxidation of proteins and
nucleic acids.
Coffee, an evergreen tropical crop, is the most important
commodity in the international agricultural trade, generat-
ing over 90 billion dollars each year and involving about
500 million people from cultivation to ﬁnal consumption.
Currently, C. arabica accounts for about 62% of the coffee
consumed worldwide. The species has small rates of net
CO2 assimilation (A), typically below 10 lmol m-2 s-1
with current atmospheric CO2 concentration and saturating
light (Silva et al. 2004; Franck et al. 2006), which is in the
lower range recorded for trees (Ceulemans and Saugier
1993). Coffee evolved in the forest understorey is thus
considered to be shade-obligatory. For this reason, in early
plantations, coffee bushes were planted under taller shade
trees to simulate their natural habitat. In many situations,
however, coffee grows well without shade and even out-
yields shaded coffee (Beer et al. 1998). This is particularly
found under optimal or near-optimal edaphoclimatic con-
ditions for coffee cultivation, where shading provides little,
if any, beneﬁt to the crop (DaMatta 2004). Shading was
therefore abandoned as a regular cultural practice in several
regions, such as in southeastern Brazil where it was almost
completely abandoned since the 1950s (DaMatta 2004).
However, shaded plantations have beneﬁcial features,
including conservation of natural resources, increased
biodiversity, stability of coffee production, and also have
ﬁnancial beneﬁts, e.g., shade trees increase cash income
from fruits or timber. These characteristics of shaded cof-
fee have stimulated renewed interest in the use of shade
trees, especially in areas where they had previously been
eliminated (Beer et al. 1998).
From the above, it may be reasoned that coffee leaves,
regardless of exhibiting low A, should have enough plas-
ticity
to
acclimate
themselves
to
contrasting
light
environments. So far, however, there has been no consistent
examination about physiological mechanisms associated
with such plasticity in coffee. The major goal of this study
was therefore to investigate seasonal differences in leaf
photoprotective mechanisms in coffee grown with full
exposure to solar radiation or under shade. Because many
leaves are partly to deeply shaded within the coffee canopy,
photoinhibition, if occurring, would be mainly manifested in
the outer leaves. Experiments were thus conducted using
such leaves from the sun-facing sides of a coffee hedgerow.
Materials and methods
Growth conditions and experimental design
This work was carried out with trees of Coffea arabica L.
‘‘Red Catuaı´’’ growing in the ﬁeld as a hedgerow (east–
west orientation) on red-yellowish Oxisol, in Vic¸osa
(20450S, 42150W, 690 m asl), southeastern Brazil. The
site is characterized by a subtropical climate with an
average annual temperature of 19.0C. It receives an
average annual rainfall of 1,200 mm with a marked dry
season coinciding with the cooler months (from April to
September). During winter months, relatively cool nights
(minimum temperatures typically ranging from 5 to 12C)
followed by warm, sunny days (maximum temperature
exceeding 25C) are frequently observed. From September
to December, air temperature rises progressively; during
this period, cloudy/rainy days are common. Maximum
solar radiation on clear days, even during winter, reaches
more
than
2,000 lmol m-2 s-1
of
photosynthetically
active radiation (PAR).
Twelve-year-old trees were 3.0 9 1.0 m spaced and
trained with two heads (orthotropic branches). They
received routine agricultural practices for commercial
coffee bean production including hoeing, fertilization and
chemical control of insects and pathogens attack. No supple-
mental irrigation was provided. The trees received either
natural solar radiation or were shaded to give, on average,
48% of the incident PAR. This shade level was chosen
based on the fact that shading extent above 50% often
results in depressed growth and productivity of the coffee
tree (DaMatta 2004). Reduction of PAR, implemented in
October 2001 and extended until the end of the experiment,
was achieved by suspending black nylon netting horizon-
tally 90 cm above the top of the plants in the hedgerow,
with the length in row direction.
The experimental design was a 2 9 3 factorial [two
PAR and three sampling dates (see below)] in completely

randomized blocks with three replicates. Each experimen-
tal plot consisted of a row of ﬁve plants. Sampling and
measurements were made using two of the three central
plants of each plot. Six trees per PAR treatment were thus
analyzed. Unless otherwise indicated, data for each vari-

Environment and sampling periods
Environmental data, such as precipitation and insolation,
were monitored daily from a meteorological station located
about 2,000 m from the coffee plantation. Leaf-to-air vapor
pressure deﬁcit (de) was calculated as described in
Landsberg (1986). The incident PAR was measured with a
photometer/radiometer (Li-185, Li-Cor, Nebraska, USA).
The PAR intercepted by an individual leaf was estimated
using azimuth and elevation angle of the sun, the solar
declination at the sampling dates, in addition to leaf
inclination and leaf azimuth angles (Pearcy 1989). Three
peripheral leaves per plant were randomly chosen for
measuring azimuth and inclination angles (Norman and
Campbell 1989). Leaf inclination angle was measured
relative to the horizon (= 0).
From previous results (Silva et al. 2004), three con-
trasting periods were selected for measurements: August
(winter) and October (mid-spring), when vegetative growth
rates and A are expected to be minimal and maximal,
respectively, and December (late spring), when temporary
depressions in growth rates and A are common, particularly
in warm, sunny spells. In August, coffee trees in Vic¸osa are
in their pre-blossoming stage, while from October through
mid-December trees bear pinhead fruits (stage of negligible
fruit growth).
The data presented were obtained on selected cloudless
days (insolation duration above 9 h): 20 August 2003, 12
December 2003, and 30 October 2004. On these dates,
minimum and maximum temperatures were, respectively,
8.6 and 26.5C, 18.2 and 34.0C, and 15.5 and 29.4C.
Mean daily insolation duration was 7.6 h (August), 4.6 h
(October), and 3.3 h (December) during the week prece-
ding
measurements.

October and 12 December. Leaf water potential before
dawn, as measured with a Scholander pressure chamber,
was about -0.25 MPa on 20 August 2003 and above
-0.10 MPa on the other two dates, irrespective of PAR
treatments. Measurements were repeated on 16 July 2003, 22
August 2003 and 22 July 2004 (leaf water potential before
dawn about -1.5, -0.3 and -0.9 MPa, respectively); in the
winter measurements, results for gas exchange, Chl ﬂuo-
rescence parameters, photosynthetic pigments and cellular
damages were very similar, regardless of water potential.
Therefore, it may be proposed that water supply caused no
signiﬁcant effect on the coffee’s photosynthetic performance
during winter, as earlier demonstrated (Silva et al. 2004).
Only results obtained on 20 August 2003 were reported.
Due to frequently cloudy, rainy weather, it was not pos-
sible to repeat measurements at least during 1 month after 12
December 2003 and 30 October 2004. Even if a single
measurement day could not represent a given period of the
season, we believe that those selected days represented just
the harshest conditions (high insolation and temperature)
found by the coffee trees during the growing season. As such,
coffee’s photoprotective responses are expected to be maxi-
mized and should therefore reﬂect the treatment effects.
In the selected dates, north-face side of the coffee
hedgerow on August, and south-face side on October and
December, received direct solar radiation from dawn to
dusk. The youngest, fully expanded, upper-canopy outer
leaves from these sun-facing sides were used.
Photosynthetic measurements
The net carbon assimilation rate (A) and stomatal conduc-
tance to water vapour (gs) were measured under natural PAR
with a portable open-ﬂow gas exchange system (LCA4,
ADC, Hoddesdon, UK), as described in DaMatta et al.
(1997). Chlorophyll a ﬂuorescence was measured with a
portable pulse amplitude modulation ﬂuorometer (FMS2,
Hansatech, King’s Lynn, Norfolk, UK). Minimum (F0) and
maximum (Fm) dark-adapted ﬂuorescence were measured,
from which Fv/Fm ratio, in which Fv = Fm - F0, was cal-
culated. This ratio has been used as a measure of the
potential photochemical efﬁciency of PSII. The quantum
yield of PSII electron transport (UPSII) under ambient light
conditions was calculated according to Genty et al. (1989)
using a 1 s pulse of saturating light of 6,000 lmol m-2 s-1.
A leaf clip equipped with a PAR sensor and a ﬁne thermo-
couple was used for this purpose. To calculate the apparent
electron transport rate (ETR), it was assumed that both PSII
and PSI absorbed equal amounts of light; for the estimation
of absorbed PAR, leaf absorptance was computed from leaf
chlorophyll concentration per unit area (Evans 1993). ETR
was then estimated as:

Enzyme activities, ascorbate, pigments and MDA were
expressed on an area basis, since slight differences in
speciﬁc leaf area and protein content had occurred between
sampling dates, in both shaded and sunlit leaves. However,
similar patterns for enzyme activities and concentrations of
those compounds were found, regardless of whether
expressed on an area, protein or mass basis (data not
shown).
Data analysis and plasticity index
Data were statistically examined using analysis of vari-
ance and tested for signiﬁcant treatment differences using
Newman-Keuls and F-tests, at P B 0.05. Differences for
plasticity index associated with biochemical, physiolo-
gical and morphological traits were analyzed using the
post hoc Bonferroni’s test at P B 0.05. This index,
ranging from 0 to 1, was calculated for each variable as
the difference between the minimum and the maximum
mean value divided by the maximum mean value (per
trait per sampling date), according to Valladares et al.
(2000).
Results
On all sampling dates, maximum PAR was slightly above
2,000 lmol m-2 s-1 and was reduced, on average, by 55%
in the shade (Fig. 1a). Diurnal changes in leaf temperature
(TL; Fig. 1b) and de (Fig. 1c) approximately paralleled
each other. In general, sunlit leaves showed both higher TL
(1–4C, although the differences were not signiﬁcant in
October) and de (up to 1.1 kPa) than shaded leaves.
Regardless of PAR treatments, TL and de were greater in
December than in the other two dates, reaching values as
high as 35C and 3.5 kPa, respectively, for sunlit leaves at
08:00 hours.
Regardless of the treatments, gs was very low, often
below 20 mmol m-2 s-1; its maximum values, as observed
at 08:00 hours, did not exceed 55 mmol m-2 s-1 (Fig. 2a)
Similarly, A was also low and did not respond consistently
to PAR treatments. The maximum rate of A did not exceed
6.0 lmol m-2 s-1, found for sunlit leaves in October at
08:00 hours. Otherwise, A was always below 2.5 lmol
m-2 s-1 during morning, and below 1.0 lmol m-2 s-1
during afternoon, irrespective of PAR treatments (Fig. 2b).
Although
measurements
were
made
until
15:30,
no
recovery of gas exchange (as well as Fv/Fm ratio) was
found at later periods, even in December when the length
of the day length was greater (not shown).
In August, Fv/Fm ratio before dawn was 0.66 in sunlit
leaves, and 0.73 in shaded leaves (Table 1). These values
were accompanied by increased F0 as compared with data
from December and October (Table 1). In August, as the
day progressed, photoinhibition was not exacerbated, as
judged from the fairly constant values of Fv/Fm, averaging
on 0.64 ± 0.02 in sunlit leaves, against 0.72 ± 0.01 in
shaded ones (Fig. 3). For the other sampling dates, Fv/Fm
before dawn was above 0.79, regardless of the treatments
(Table 1). Throughout the measurement days, Fv/Fm ratio
was slightly lower in sunlit leaves than in shaded leaves,
but the differences between them were small, with the
lowest Fv/Fm, as observed for sunlit leaves, always above
0.76 (Fig. 3).
Azimuth orientation of coffee leaves did not change in
response to treatments (not shown). By contrast, sunlit
leaves showed steeper inclinations than shaded leaves,
although this was not signiﬁcant in October (Fig. 4a). In
August and October, mean leaf angles were similar, aver-
aging on 60 and 45 for sunlit and shaded leaves,
respectively. In those months, there was no change in mean
leaf angle during the day. By contrast, leaf inclination was
signiﬁcantly lower in December, with small, although
signiﬁcant, changes throughout the sampling day, in both
shaded and sunlit leaves (Fig. 4a). In these leaves, the
intercepted PAR (Fig. 4b) was almost always larger, and
UPSII (Fig. 4c) was generally lower than in shaded leaves

Regardless of the treatments, P decreased (Fig. 5a) and
D increased (Fig. 5b) with rising PAR, particularly beyond
600 lmol m-2 s-1, a value often quoted as high enough to
saturate the photosynthetic apparatus of sunlit coffee
leaves. In August, but not in October or in December, P
was lower and D was higher for sunlit than for shaded
leaves in the range of 150–600 lmol (photons) m-2 s-1. At
higher PAR, however, P and D were similar in both kinds
of leaves. It should be noted that D did not apparently reach
saturation until a PAR of 1500 lmol m-2 s-1. At this
PAR, for sunlit leaves, D was signiﬁcantly higher in
December and lower in October, with intermediate values
in August; for shaded leaves, D was slightly lower in
October than in the other two sampling dates.
Total Chl (a + b) concentration was greater in shaded
leaves than in sunlit ones, although the difference was not
statistically signiﬁcant in December (Table 2). However,
total Chl concentration was much lower in August relative
to the other sampling dates. For both shaded and sunlit
leaves, a strong loss of Chl a (not shown) occurred that
decreased the Chl a/b ratio to 1.3 against values around 3.0
in December and October (Table 2). Because carotenoid
concentration declined to a greater extent than total Chl,
the Chl/ carotenoid ratio increased in August relative to the
other sampling dates, regardless of the treatments. Only in
October that ratio was signiﬁcantly larger (20%) in shaded
over sunlit leaves (Table 2). Changes in pigment concen-
trations were independent of leaf nitrogen concentration,
which was maintained (*3.0 g N kg-1 DW) within an
optimal range for coffee throughout the experiment (not
shown).
Irradiance treatments did not alter the activities of key
antioxidant enzymes, namely SOD, APX, CAT, and GR
(Table 3). In contrast, ascorbate pool tended to be larger in
sunlit than in shaded leaves (with statistical signiﬁcance
only in August), whereas ascorbate redox state was similar
between those kinds of leaves (Table 3). Ascorbate and
enzyme activities, with the exception of GR, changed
through the sampling dates. For ascorbate, its concentration
was remarkably lower in December than in both August
and October, regardless of PAR treatments. For SOD, a
marked increase was observed in October as compared
with August and December. For CAT, activity in Decem-
ber and October was, on average, three fold higher than in
August. For APX, activity increased remarkably from
August to December, attaining the highest values in
October. It should be stressed that little, if any, diurnal
variation, in ascorbate as well as total enzyme activities, as
measured at 08:00, 12:00 and 16:00 hours, was observed
(unpublished results).
Irradiance treatments did not also affect MDA accu-
mulation or the extent of electrolyte leakage. In any case,
these variables tended to be slightly lower in December
than in the other two sampling dates (Table 3).
Overall, coffee leaves showed poor plastic responses to
contrasting light treatments irrespective of sampling dates.

Discussion
The photosynthetic rates of the coffee tree have been
shown to be very low (DaMatta et al. 1997, 2001; Ramalho
et al. 2003; Silva et al. 2004; Vaast et al. 2005; Franck et al.
2006; Ronquim et al. 2006) as compared with several tree
species (Ceulemans and Saugier 1993), and the present
results conﬁrm that. This was independent of PAR treat-
ments, but was to a great extent apparently associated with
low gs, and high TL and de. Under these circumstances,
light can become excessive, thus favoring the occurrence of
photoinhibitory processes.
In December and October, values of Fv/Fm before dawn
were near or within the optimal range (0.80–0.83 found in
unstressed leaves of higher plants) reported by Bjo¨rkman
and Demmig (1987). Even during the day, Fv/Fm ratio did
not fall below 0.75, suggesting therefore that potential
decreases in that ratio should be attributed to photopro-
tection, i.e., dynamic photoinhibition (Demmig-Adams and
Adams 1992). By contrast, in August, the sustained
decreases in Fv/Fm associated with increased F0 indicated
the occurrence of chronic photoinhibition, as no recovery
of photochemical efﬁciency following the night period was
found. DaMatta et al. (1997) had already reported similar
results for potted coffee plants grown under shade (ca. one-
third of incident PAR) in a greenhouse. Persistent decrea-
ses in Fv/Fm have been mainly attributed to the retention of

Overall, compared to shaded leaves, sunlit leaves
decreased the ability to absorb incident radiation via
decreased light-harvesting capacity (lower Chl concentra-
tion; Adams and Baker 1998) and steeper inclinations, an
adaptation that reduces radiation load particularly at high
solar angles (James and Bell 2000; Falster and Westoby
2003). Also, sunlit leaves should have had an increased
capacity to dissipate excess excitation energy, as indirectly
inferred from their lower UPSII (Demmig-Adams et al.
1996) under the prevailing PAR conditions. Nonetheless,
as deduced from PAR response curves, both sunlit and
shaded leaves generally showed similar plastic abilities to
dissipate energy as heat at a given PAR. Taken together,
changes in Chl concentration, leaf angles and UPSII led to
strong reductions in ETR in sunlit leaves; thus, only slight
differences in the capacity for electron transport when
comparing sunlit and shaded leaves were noted on most
occasions. Inasmuch as A, the main sink for absorbed PAR
in chloroplasts, was similar between these kinds of leaves,
it may be proposed that the pressure of excitation energy
through photosystems should have also been similar
regardless of PAR treatments. This fact might partially
explain why the activity of key antioxidant enzymes did
not change with varying PAR.
The activities of SOD and APX, which are the ﬁrst
enzymes of the water–water cycle (Asada 1999), decreased
in August relative to October despite the high insolation
and chronic photoinhibition occurring in that month. Also,
according to Dias (2006), activities of SOD and APX, as
measured either in winter or in summer, were similar when
comparing upper, east-facing leaves with lower, west-fac-
ing coffee leaves (with no major differences in A between
them), despite the former having intercepted threefold
more PAR integrated over the course of the day than the
latter. Taken together, these data suggest that the activity of
ROS scavenging system, via water–water cycle, might not
play a central role: (1) in photoprotection during winter
conditions in either sunlit or shaded coffee leaves nor (2) in
acclimation to light availability per se. It should be noted,
however, that extracted enzyme activities might not reﬂect
the in situ activities; in addition, only total activity of

In contrast with SOD and APX, GR activity did not
change seasonally. It has been suggested that GR may play
a central role in regenerating reduced glutathione and
reduced ascorbate (Logan et al. 2006). The fairly constant
ascorbate redox state is consistent with this suggestion.
Reduced ascorbate is a linchpin in the chloroplast antiox-
idant system: it is required for zeaxanthin formation and a-
tocopherol regeneration and, like reduced glutathione, can
directly scavenge ROS (Asada 1999). Therefore, the
maintenance (or increase) in the ascorbate pool, and the
ability to regenerate it, could be important in keeping ROS
at low levels, especially during winter conditions in which
the activity of key antioxidant enzymes was relatively low.
It is noteworthy that regardless of PAR treatments,
ascorbate pools as well as activities of SOD and APX were
remarkably lower in December than in October, although
the extent of cellular damages (evaluated via MDA accu-
mulation and electrolyte leakage) tended to be lower in
December than in October. Perhaps, this response might be
related to the lower insolation duration in that month and, if
so, it might have been the result of less cumulative effects of
stress. Lower insolation might also be related to the lower
leaf inclinations as observed in December, since leaf angle
may be determined by the light availability during leaf
expansion, as found in eucalyptus by James and Bell (2000).
In conclusion, the strategies developed by the coffee tree
were characterized by a low phenotypic plasticity and a
relatively high tolerance in relation to excessive radiation
budget, and despite its origin as an understorey species.
Even during the harshest conditions (August), the coffee
tree showed relatively high values of Fv/Fm and ETR
regardless of the very low photosynthetic rates. Under such
circumstances, an increase in ROS production is to be
expected. The overall data suggest that enzyme-driven
water–water cycle may not be as important as other anti-
oxidants to cope with any production of ROS, particularly
during winter conditions. Notwithstanding, the small sea-
sonal changes and the similar MDA concentration and
electrolyte leakage in both sunlit and shaded leaves indi-
cated the effectiveness of the photoprotective processes in
preventing photoinhibitory damages. Also, since chronic
photoinhibition occurs in a period of restrained growth,
associated with a build-up in carbohydrate content (Silva
et al. 2004), it is unlikely that photoinhibition per se would
largely result in decreased coffee productivity. Further-
more, photoinhibited outer leaves may play a role to
attenuate light intensity and so protect inner leaves from
photoinhibition (Kitao et al. 2003). Finally, the present
results suggest that shading would result in little, if any,
additional photoprotection to the peripheral leaves. Taken
together, our results can, at least partially, explain the
successful cultivation of the coffee tree in the open, par-
ticularly in regions with optimal or near-optimal climatic
conditions for coffee cultivation, as in southeastern Brazil.
