98.1 INTRODUCTION 
 
Furan  is  a colorless  liquid  with  an  ether-like  
odor, which is highly lipophilic and also volatile, with a 
boil- ing  point  at  31 °C.1–3  Furan  is  carcinogenic  to  
rats  and mice,4   and  the  International  Agency  for  
Research  on Cancer (IRAC) of the World Health 
Organization (WHO) classified the compound into 
group 2B as “possibly car- cinogenic  to  humans.”5,6   In  
their  risk  assessment,  the Joint FAO/WHO Expert 
Committee on Food Additives (JECFA)  considered  that  
furan  is  a  carcinogenic  com- pound with a potentially 
genotoxic metabolite, and pos- tulated a human health 
concern in its risk assessment.7 
The existence of furan in coffee has long been 
known.3 
Furan as a component of the flavor of coffee was chemi- 
cally  detected  by  Johnston  and  Frey  in  1938,8  
whereas 
Merritt et  al.  and  Radtke  et  al.  accomplished  the  
first 
chromatographic  analysis  in  1963.9–11   Since  2004,  the 
European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) collected moni- 
toring data on furan in Europe, with the aim to create 
a  database  for  the  health  risk  assessment.  The  latest 
update  in  2011  covers  a  total  of  5050  analytical  
results 
for  furan  content  from  20  countries  and  confirms  
that higher concentrations only occur in products 
subjected to thermal treatment, such as roasted coffee 
beans and coffee, which had the highest furan levels 
from all ana- lyzed food groups.12 
According to several studies, green coffee beans con- 
tain only traces of furan, whereas roasted coffee beans 
show  the  highest  concentrations  among  all  tested  
cof- fee products.2,3,13–15  This finding confirms that furan 
is formed during the roasting process of coffee beans.3 
The studies also showed that the content of the coffee 
prod- ucts  is  dependent  on  several  factors,  such  as  
roasting conditions, processing steps, or consumer 
handling.3 
Considering that coffee is at the top of the beverage 
list  in  Germany,  with  an  average  annual  
consumption of  144liters  per  capita  (in  2005),2,16   the  
determination of the exposure for consumers is of 
importance for the health  risk  assessment.3   A  detailed  
exposure  assess- ment   was   previously   conducted   
for   coffee-drinking adolescents.3  The aim of this 
chapter was to provide an exposure assessment for the 
whole population and for coffee-drinking adults. In 
contrast to previous research,3 which   was   based   on   
point   estimates,   this   chapter will apply a 
probabilistic 
exposure 
estimation 
similar 
to 
the 
approach previously developed for the estimation of 
caffeine intake due to coffee consumption.17

and brewed coffee with a standard home coffee 
machine.2,11 
Other factors which are responsible for the reduction of 
furan in brewed coffee beverages are hot plates to keep 
the coffee beverages warm or the pouring into a cup.15 
All these influences lead to the wide variation in furan 
contents in the coffee brews prepared for drinking.

98.3 EXPOSURE ESTIMATION 
 
Similar  to  a  previous  study  on  caffeine  intake,17  
the data source for dietary intake assessment was 
published dietary  intake  data  from  the  second  
national  nutrition survey (Nationale Verzehrsstudie II, 
NVS II) in Germany, 
which provides representative food intake data of 14- to 
80-year-olds.  A total  of  20,000  participants  in  500  ran- 
domly chosen towns in Germany were selected. The sur- 
vey method was based on a combination of instruments, 
such as computer-supported diet history questionnaires 
in  the  study  center,  telephone  interviews  using  com- 
puterized  24-h  recall  methods,  and  2 ×4-day  weighing 
protocols in a random selection of participants. The data 
on  beverage  consumption  are  based  on  15,371  
records 
(54%  females,  46%  males)  from  the  diet  history  
assess- 
ment conducted between November 2005 and November 
2006.   Several   studies   provide   details   about   NVS   
II 
method and results.20–23 
The calculation method was similar to a method 
devel- 
oped for assessment of caffeine intake.17  The calculation 
of coffee-related daily furan intake per kg body weight 
(bw) requires the following information: amount of furan

98.4 RISK ASSESSMENT OF FURAN IN 
COFFEE PRODUCTS 
 
The risk assessment for furan in coffee was conducted 
according  to  the  harmonized  approach  of  the  
EFSA,30 which  recommends  the  use  of  the  margin  of  
exposure (MOE)  based  on  the  benchmark  dose  
(BMD)  as  stan- dardized  reference  point.  The  BMD  
lower  confidence limit for a benchmark response of 10% 
(BMDL10) used for the calculations was identified by 
Carthew et al.31 via detailed  dose–response  models  of  
furan.  The  BMDL10 found in that study was 1.28mg/kg 
bw/day. 
The MOE was also calculated probabilistically as 
above with the formula: 
 
MOE = BMDL/Furan _ intake 
To  make  a  judgment  about  the  risk  associated  
with consuming  coffee  products,  the  MOE  was  
evaluated. According  to  the  EFSA,  an  MOE  of  10,000  
or  higher would be considered as a low public health 
concern and

as a low priority for risk management actions as well.30 
The calculated MOEs are shown in Table 98.1. 
Regarding   the   population-based   assessment,   it   is 
obvious that the mean MOE is higher than the threshold 
of 10,000. However, the median MOE is slightly lower 
than 10,000, as are the P90 and P95 MOE. 
For  the  individual  drinkers,  the  average  MOE  for 
more  than  5 cups/day  and  the  median  MOE  for  more 
than  3cups/day  are  lower  than  10,000.  P90  and  P95 
MOEs are in all cases lower than 10,000, even when only 
drinking one single cup of coffee. 
The probabilities of results falling into the potentially 
health-relevant  category  lower  than  an  MOE  of  10,000 
are  presented  in  more  detail  in  Figure  98.4.  For  
drink- 
ing one cup of coffee per day, approximately 27% of the 
10,000   probabilistically   calculated   intake   estimations 
would fall into this category, whereas at 8 cups/day this 
number is 87%. 
The   limitations   of   the   application   of   the   MOE 
approach  to  the  risk  assessment  of  furan  have  to  be 
mentioned because the genotoxic mechanism in the low- 
dose  range  is  a  matter  of  debate.3,32  Nevertheless,  this 
assessment is rather conservative as some tumor types 
may occur at even lower concentrations than what was 
chosen as BMDL10 (see detailed discussion in Carthew 
et al.31  and Lachenmeier et al.33). In a recent risk assess- 
ment  of  furan,  the  JECFA7  also  suggested  the  use  of  
a 
BMDL10  value  in  a  similar  order  of  magnitude  as  
the 
one published by Carthew et al.31 
Recent  studies  have  analyzed  the  risk  of  furan,  
and 
coffee appears to be the major contributor to its dietary 
exposure.7 The evaluation in this chapter confirms these 
results  because  coffee  consumption  alone  may  lead  to

98.5 SUMMARY POINTS 
 
•  Furan is a possible human carcinogen, which 
naturally occurs in coffee. 
•  The furan exposure of consumers is probabilistically 
estimated in this chapter by combining food 
monitoring data and data from a representative 
nutrition survey. 
•  The average furan intake per 0.2-l cup of coffee was 
estimated as 0.12μg/kg bw/day. 
•  The average population-based furan exposure from 
coffee was estimated as 0.26μg/kg bw/day, and the 
corresponding MOE was 19,112. 
•  Only for drinkers of more than five cups of coffee 
per day, the average MOE would be lower than a 
threshold of 10,000.
