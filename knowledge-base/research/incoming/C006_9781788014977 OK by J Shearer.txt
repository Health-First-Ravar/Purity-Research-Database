The global incidence of diabetes has reached epidemic proportions with an 
estimated 9% of adults over 18 years having some form of the disease. Approx-
imately 90% of these cases will be due to type 2 diabetes (T2D) character-
ized by hyperglycemia and impaired insulin action. Given this, cost-effective 
lifestyle strategies to prevent disease onset and progression are imperative. 
One such intervention is coffee consumption. Chronic coffee consumption 
dose-dependently reduces the risk of T2D and its associated comorbidities 
including obesity and the metabolic syndrome. Compared to non-consum-
ers, 1 cup per day is associated with a risk reduction of ∼0.92, while high 
levels (6 cups per day) result in a risk reduction of ∼0.67 for T2D, a level that 
far exceeds many other known interventions. This relation is independent of 
ethnicity, age, sex, socioeconomic status and genetic factors predisposing an 
individual to consume coffee. Coffee may also reduce the severity of diabetes 
symptoms early in the course of the disease. This review considers these rela-
tionships and the potential mechanisms by which chronic coffee consump-
tion is protective. As coffee consumption and T2D reduction has been shown 
for both caffeinated and decaffeinated coffee, non-caffeine components of 
the coffee bean are likely responsible.

6.1  Introduction
According to the World Health Organization, the global incidence of diabe-
tes has reached epidemic proportions with an estimated 9% of adults over 
18 years of age having some form of the disease.1 This number translates 
into 1.5 million deaths directly attributable to the disease, the majority in 
low- and middle-income countries. Approximately 90% of these cases will 
be due to type 2 diabetes characterized by hyperglycemia and impaired insu-
lin action. The health risks and complications of diabetes are well known 
and include cardiovascular disease, blindness, amputation and kidney fail-
ure. Given these statistics and the significant strain type 2 diabetes places on 
health care budgets, finding effective prevention and treatment strategies is 
imperative.

Over the past decade, a strong association between coffee consumption 
and type 2 diabetes has emerged. Coffee is one of the most widely consumed 
beverages in the world. Derived from the Coffea genus, coffee contains thou-
sands of biologically active compounds including lipids, caffeine, minerals 
and antioxidants. Roasting further increases this complexity, producing 
many compounds unique to coffee and not found in other foods and bev-
erages. The beverage is widely available and relatively inexpensive, and the 
majority of adults enjoy consuming the beverage. From a clinical perspec-
tive, it is perhaps the most effective, modifiable lifestyle factor proven to 
reduce the incidence of type 2 diabetes found to date. A 2014 meta-analysis by 
Ding et al.2 of 1 109 272 study participants and 45 335 cases with a follow-up 
between 10 months and 20 years showed that, compared to no or very little 
coffee consumption, coffee dose dependently reduces the risk of the disease. 
Relative risk reductions are 0.92, 0.85, 0.79, 0.75, 0.71 and 0.67 for 1–6 cups 
of coffee per day respectively.2 This relation exists across the globe, in multi-
ple ethnicities, and is independent of age (young and old adults),3 sex,4 and 
socioeconomic status.3 A similar meta-analysis of prospective cohort stud-
ies on caffeine, coffee and type 2 diabetes confirmed these findings but also 
showed the strongest associations in women, non-smokers and those with a 
body mass index under 25 kg m2.5
As the method of coffee preparation can affect its composition, investiga-
tions have also focused on whether specific methods of preparation or coffee 
additives alter its protective effects against type 2 diabetes. There appears 
to be no impact of the method of preparation (filtered, instant, press, pot, 
espresso), type (decaffeinated, caffeine containing),2 or the use of additives 
(milk, cream, sugar or sweetener).3 The only variables that appear to mat-
ter in considering the positive effects of the beverage on type 2 diabetes is 
the volume and frequency of consumption.2 Other provocative questions 
warranting consideration are as follows: Is coffee effective in just preventing 
type 2 diabetes risk or is it effective at all stages of disease including disease 
progression and management once it has become established? Likewise, is cof-
fee protective against other related metabolic disease states involving insulin 
resistance including obesity and the metabolic syndrome? What physiological or 
biochemical mechanism(s) are involved in mediating coffee's protective effects? 
These questions are the basis of this chapter exploring the inverse relation 
between coffee consumption and type 2 diabetes. Lastly, we will briefly dis-
cuss the caffeine-coffee paradox. While coffee is clearly protective against the 
disease, acute caffeine consumption (in coffee, energy drinks and other for-
mats) appears to cause transient glucose intolerance, an effect that would be 
expected to promote rather than prevent the disease.

genetically high coffee intake alone is simply associated with lower risk

metabolism of coffee and caffeine are determined in part by their genetics.6–8 
According to Griffiths et al.9 some individuals can accurately discriminate as 
little as 10 mg of caffeine while others require ten times this dose or 100 mg 
to detect the drug. This is also reflected by numerous reports of responders 
and non-responders to caffeine in the literature.10–13 The heritability for caf-
feine-induced traits has been found to be relatively stable over the lifetime of 
individuals.14 Variation in adenosine receptors (caffeine is a non-specific ade-
nosine receptor antagonist), dopamine receptors and the liver cytochrome 
P450, CYP1A2 (accounting for 95% of caffeine metabolism by the liver), all 
impact an individual's response to caffeine.7,15,16 For example, there is evi-
dence that caffeine can be detrimental to cardiovascular risk, but only in 
individuals who are slow metabolizers (CYP1A2/1F variant, present in ∼58% 
of the population).17 As such, understanding the relationship between cof-
fee consumption and genetic variance is an important consideration in the 
assessment of diet–disease associations.
Whether individuals who are slow to metabolize caffeine are more prone 
to type 2 diabetes is an interesting question. Slow metabolizing individ-
uals would have higher levels of circulating caffeine for a given dose of 
coffee/caffeine compared to a fast metabolizer. This difference also likely 
explains why slower metabolizing individuals drink less.18 To answer the 
question of whether having a genotypic preference for caffeine and/or cof-
fee consumption is protective towards diabetes development itself, Nord-
estgaard and colleagues19 conducted a Mendelian randomization analysis. 
This analysis assumes that a genetic predisposition for high coffee intake 
would be associated with a lower risk of obesity, type 2 diabetes and the 
metabolic syndrome. In other words, this study examined associations 
in relation to the naturally randomized genes of interest to determine if 
genetics alone could explain the effects of coffee consumption on lower-
ing type 2 diabetes risk. Two large population cohorts consisting of 93 179 
individuals were examined for five genetic variants or single nucleotide 
polymorphisms (SNPs, rs4410790, rs6968865, rs2470893, rs2472297 and 
rs2472299) for coffee/caffeine related variants in relation to both their coffee 
intake as well as their body mass index, type 2 diabetes and metabolic syn-
drome prevalence. These genetic variants have been previously associated 
with coffee or caffeine intake6,20–22 and are shown in Table 6.1 along with 
their global minor allele frequency with other SNPs of interest. Predicted 
associations were then examined on a separate validation cohort consist-
ing of 78 021 individuals. Results demonstrated that genetically derived 
high coffee intake was not associated with obesity, type 2 diabetes, the 
metabolic syndrome or any other risk variable including blood pressure, 
cholesterol, fasting glucose levels, triglycerides and waist circumference 
among others.

6.1.2  Coffee and Diabetes Progression
While data on coffee and type 2 diabetes risk reduction is abundant within 
the literature, relatively few studies examine the relationship between 
coffee consumption and newly diagnosed diabetes. Yarmolinsky and col-
leagues3 assessed coffee intake in relation to a number of diabetes-related 
parameters in newly diagnosed individuals including fasting glucose, the 
2-hour 75 g oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT), fasting and post-OGTT insu-
lin (standard measures of both whole body glucose disposal and insulin 
sensitivity). After adjustment for covariates, results of this study demon-
strate that those consuming coffee 2–3 times per day and >3 times per day 
were at 23% and 26% lower odds of newly diagnosed diabetes compared to 
those reporting no consumption. Coffee consumption was also related to 
lower levels of impaired glucose tolerance, 2 h post-OGTT insulin and glu-
cose (>11.1 mmol L−1) levels and a lower composite insulin sensitivity index 
as defined by DeFronzo and Matsuda.23 However, no differences were noted 
in other parameters including fasting glucose (>7.0 mmol L−1), percent 
glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c), fasting insulin and the homeostatic model 
of assessment for both insulin resistance (HOMA-IR) and β-cell function 
(HOMA-β). Of interest, the lack of response for HbA1c, the most commonly 
used measure of glucose control did not change with coffee consumption 
in this group.
This is consistent with another report by Smith and colleagues24 exam-
ining the relationship between coffee consumption and oral glucose tol-
erance in healthy individuals and those with impaired glucose tolerance. 
Impaired glucose tolerance (fasting plasma glucose <6.1 mmol L−1 and 7.8 
mmol L−1 ≤ post challenge glucose ≤ 11.1 mmol L−1) is an early stage of 
type 2 diabetes or pre-diabetes. A total of 910 adults (aged >50 years) were 
followed for ∼8 years. Follow-up identified 317 participants with impaired 
glucose tolerance – of these, those who were past or current coffee drinkers 
were at reduced risk for incident diabetes with odds reductions of 0.31 and 
0.36, respectively. These results were adjusted for sex, age, physical activ-
ity, body mass index, smoking, alcohol, hypertension and baseline fasting 
plasma glucose. Together, these results suggest that coffee consumption 
can reduce the severity of newly diagnosed diabetes as well as the progres-
sion from glucose intolerance to more severe disease. Despite this, coffee 
consumption is not on the radar of most health professionals (physicians, 
dietitians, endocrinologists, nurses) as a modifiable lifestyle intervention 
for type 2 diabetes.

Comorbidities associated with type 2 diabetes include neuropathy, ret-
inopathy as well as a host of psychosocial problems such as anxiety and 
depression.25,26 A review of the influence of type 2 diabetes and depression 
on quality of life in adults found that comorbid depression and type 2 dia-
betes further diminishes the health-related quality of life by more than two 
factors of magnitude.26 Depression in patients with type 2 diabetes is asso-
ciated with hyperglycemia.27 Likely resulting from persistent hyperglycemia, 
diabetic patients with depression are also more likely to suffer from neuropa-
thy and retinopathy.28 A cross-sectional study on Japanese patients with type 
2 diabetes involving diabetic medical variables as well as self-administered 
questionnaires to assess food intake frequency, physical activity levels and 
depressive state found that coffee consumption was inversely associated with 
symptoms of depression. The study did not distinguish between caffeinated 
and decaffeinated coffee. Furthermore, a similar association was not noted 
between tea or green tea consumption and depressive state.29 Given this, 
consumption of coffee may reduce psychosocial problems in individuals 
with type 2 diabetes, and potentially diminish the occurrence of detrimental 
comorbidities.
It is estimated that 60% of individuals with type 2 diabetes have some 
form of cardiac dysfunction or diabetic cardiomyopathy.30,31 As a result, indi-
viduals with the disease are 4–6 times more likely to have heart disease or 
a stroke compared to healthy controls making it the number one complica-
tion of the disease.32–39 Following a cardiovascular event, individuals with 
diabetes are also at greater risk of an adverse outcome due to an imbalance 
in substrate utilization.40,41 Declines in cardiac function with type 2 diabe-
tes are largely attributable to systemic insulin resistance. The relationship 
between coffee or caffeine consumption and cardiovascular risk is complex 
and appears to be partly dependent on genetics. For example, there is evi-
dence that caffeine can be detrimental to cardiovascular risk, but only in 
individuals who are slow metabolizers (CYP1A2/1F variant) of the drug.17 As 
such, understanding the relationship between caffeine or coffee and genetic 
variance is an important consideration in the assessment of diet–disease 
associations.
Conversely, coffee consumption may exert some protective effects on the 
heart through its actions on glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1).42 GLP-1 recep-
tor agonists and dipeptidyl peptidase-4 (DPP-4) inhibitors have emerged as 
a potential therapy for type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular comorbidities, to 
prolong and amplify effects of GLP-1, which are naturally limited due to a 
very short half-life, via metabolism by DPP-4. In addition to the beneficial 
impacts on glucoregulation, there is growing evidence suggesting that GLP-1 
and GLP-1 receptor agonists may reduce the risk of coronary heart disease 
through improved atherosclerosis markers, lipid metabolism, blood pres-
sure and coronary blood flow.43 Coffee consumption has been found to cause 
an increase in GLP-1 production; this could contribute to cardioprotective 
properties of coffee as well as glucoregulatory improvements in individuals 
with type 2 diabetes mellitus.42

Nephropathy is another common comorbidity of type 2 diabetes. Per-

6.2.2  Clinical, Biochemical and Molecular Data
6.2.2.1  Gut Peptides and the Microbiota
Coffee consumption appears to modulate the incretin hormones gluca-
gon-like peptide 1 (GLP-1) and glucose-dependent insulinotropic peptide 
(GIP).49,50 These gut hormones are secreted into the blood by enteroendo-
crine cells and one of their most important roles is to regulate the insu-
lin secretion in the postprandial state. Greenberg and colleagues51 report 
an increase in GLP-1 concentrations after ingestion of decaffeinated cof-
fee. Since GLP-1 secretion is reduced in type 2 diabetes, it is possible that 
increasing GLP-1 secretion induced by decaffeinated coffee intake is one 
important mechanism related to its protective effects. Peptide YY (PYY), 
secreted from the same intestinal L-cells as GLP-1, is another gastrointesti-
nal peptide secreted in response to lipids and carbohydrates and involved in 
satiation.52 Greenberg and Geliebter53 conducted a placebo-controlled ran-
domized crossover trial to examine the acute effects of caffeine, caffeinated 
coffee and decaffeinated coffee on hunger and satiety hormone response in 
healthy adults. The trial revealed that decaffeinated coffee resulted in higher 
plasma PYY concentrations than placebo and caffeine. This was accom-
panied by lower perceived hunger levels, as assessed through participant 
ratings of visual analog scales. While higher PYY lasted for only 1.5 hours, 
and was diminished for the final 1.5 hours of the trial, these changes were 
related to significantly lower hunger 3 hours following consumption of the 
decaffeinated coffee. Similar results were not found following consumption 
of regular, caffeinated coffee. These results indicate that decaffeinated coffee 
consumption acutely increases PYY secretion and improves satiety, poten-
tially contributing to the protective nature of coffee consumption against 
the onset of type 2 diabetes.

also known to influence the composition of the gut microbiota. Collectively, 
the human gut consists of up to 100 trillion microorganisms, a number 
far exceeding the total number of our somatic cells.54,55 The gut microbi-

dietary sources. Alterations in the microbiome have been linked to numer-
ous metabolic disease states including obesity,54,56 insulin resistance57 
and cardiovascular disease58 in observational and cross-sectional studies, 
while direct causation has been proven in animal models employing fecal 
transplant experiments.59 In fact, the gut microbiota are now widely con-
sidered to be an environmental factor promoting both obesity and insulin 
resistance.
Chronic coffee administration to rodents in combination with a chow 
or high-fat diet for 10 weeks demonstrated coffee to decrease body weight, 
adiposity, liver triglycerides and energy intake.60 However, coffee-consum-
ing animals also displayed profound systemic insulin resistance, likely due 
to caffeine (addressed in the below section on the caffeine-coffee paradox). 
Coffee consumption attenuated the increase in Firmicutes to Bacteroidetes 
bacterial ratio normally associated with obesity and high fat feeding. Given 
this, and the fact that coffee metabolites can appear >12 h after ingestion,61 it 
is very likely that colonic gut microbiota are exposed to numerous coffee-de-
rived compounds.62 Whether the microbiota are responsible for coffee's ben-
eficial effects in humans requires further study.
6.2.2.2  Modulation of Glucose and Fatty Acid Metabolism
Chronic coffee consumption impacts the response to a glucose chal-
lenge.63 This is evident in oral glucose tests wherein the glucose peak, area 
under the curve and various indices of glucose tolerance are improved with 
chronic coffee consumption.3 This reduction may involve a slower absorp-
tion in the intestine by inhibition of sodium glucose co-transporters64 or 
a reduction in glucose-6-phosphatase activity in the liver thereby inhib-
iting glucose release.65 A number of studies have also shown coffee con-
sumption to alter liver enzymes (aspartate aminotransferase and alanine 
aminotransferase)66–69 and glucose kinetics.70,71 Glucose mediation may 
also stem from alterations to fatty acid metabolism. Glucose metabolism 
is intertwined and dependent in many ways on the storage and metabo-
lism of fatty acids. Numerous recent investigations have focused on under-
standing the impacts of coffee consumption on fat deposition, lipolysis 
and regulation.
Adiponectin is a hormone secreted from adipocytes involved in glucoregu-
lation and is considered a mediator of metabolic disease risk.72 Low plasma 
adiponectin concentrations are a predicting factor of future diabetes devel-
opment while elevated levels increase insulin sensitivity.73 Administration 
of chlorogenic acid, a key coffee derived antioxidant, for 12 weeks to the

6.2.2.3  Micronutrients and Other Coffee Derived Compounds
Coffee makes a large contribution to daily intake of magnesium for individ-
uals of various nationalities.77 Prospective studies have found an inverse 
association with magnesium intake and incidence of type 2 diabetes, inde-
pendent of other typical risk factors. This inverse relationship also appears 
to exist between magnesium and typical predictors of type 2 diabetes, 
such as body mass index, activity levels and family history.78 A prospec-
tive study of magnesium intake and type 2 diabetes incidence in a Japa-
nese population reaffirmed this inverse relationship between magnesium 
intake and disease incidence, but found magnesium to be an especially 
effective protective factor for individuals with insulin resistance and low-
grade inflammation.79 Another study examined the relationship between 
magnesium intake and 7-year changes to insulin resistance, insulin sensi-
tivity, fasting glucose and insulin in individuals with pre-diabetes. Results 
indicated that magnesium intake was associated with lower fasting glucose 
and insulin resistance, but not insulin sensitivity or fasting insulin levels. 
Overall, higher magnesium intake was associated with a lower risk of dia-
betes.80 These data suggest that magnesium content in coffee may be one 
of the components involved in its protective effect against the onset of type 
2 diabetes, and that it may be especially beneficial in preventing disease 
progression.

which is used in traditional Chinese medicine to treat diabetes.82 A study

6.2.2.4  Alteration of Secretory, Inflammatory and Signalling 
Cascades
Type 2 diabetes is a complex disease, affecting multiple organ systems. 
The disease is well documented to alter gene expression, cell signalling 
and nutrient handling. It is also a state of low-grade inflammation with 
the induction of pro-inflammatory cytokines. Tumor necrosis factor-α 
(TNF-α) is a cytokine that has been associated with the development of 
diabetes complications including diabetic nephropathy. Lampropoulou 
and colleagues85 reported that TNF-α urinary levels of patients with type 
2 diabetes are associated with microalbuminuria, indicating its role in 
the pathogenesis and progression of diabetic nephropathy.85 A study in 
diabetic mice showed that high consumption of coffee or pure caffeine 
exerted positive effects in reducing the expression of TNF-α, hyperglyce-
mia, fat mass and also the expression of hepatic genes involved in fatty 
acid synthesis.86 Coffee consumption may also exert anti-inflammatory 
effects and increase insulin sensitivity via AMPK activation. This enzyme 
is involved in the regulation of several physiological processes such as 
glucose transport, mitochondrial function, synthesis of proteins and lip-
ids, cellular growth and factors that are associated with inflammation and 
insulin resistance.87 Ong and colleagues88 studied the beneficial effects of 
coffee consumption and found chlorogenic acid to increase AMPK activity 
in male diabetic rats.88
6.3  Coffee-Caffeine Paradox
As there is a strong relation between coffee and caffeine consumption, 
it is difficult to separate the individual effects of each on glucose toler-
ance and the development of type 2 diabetes. While coffee consumption 
appears to promote glucose tolerance and whole body insulin sensitivity, 
the consumption of caffeine has the opposite effect.89,90 It is accepted that 
co-administration of a carbohydrate load in the presence of caffeine alone

6.4  Conclusion
In this chapter we have reviewed the evidence concerning the relation-
ship between long-term coffee consumption and the risk, development, 
progression and management of type 2 diabetes. While the literature on 
the relationship of other disease states (e.g. cardiovascular disease, can-
cer) and coffee consumption remains controversial, it is widely accepted 
that long-term coffee consumption is of significant benefit to the dis-
ease. With escalating rates of type 2 diabetes in the population, its high 
rate of misdiagnosis and its expensive management coffee consumption 
represents an easily implemented and effective lifestyle strategy. Going 
forward, there are gaps in our knowledge of the mechanisms by which cof-
fee exerts its beneficial effects, and given the range of evidence, it likely 
impacts multiple sites.
