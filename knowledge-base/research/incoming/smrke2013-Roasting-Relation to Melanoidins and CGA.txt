How does roasting aﬀect the antioxidants of a coﬀee
brew? Exploring the antioxidant capacity of coﬀee via
on-line antioxidant assays coupled with size exclusion
chromatography
Samo Smrke,ab Sebastian E. W. Opitz,*a Irena Vovkbc and Chahan Yeretziana
During coﬀee roasting major changes occur in coﬀee bean composition. Among others dark coloured
melanoidins are formed, which are high molecular weight Maillard reaction products. A new approach
is presented here to monitor the inﬂuence of roasting conditions on the antioxidant capacity of
melanoidins and chlorogenic acids (CGAs) in a coﬀee brew. Validated Folin–Ciocalteu (FC) and ABTS
assays were used as on-line antioxidant assays coupled (post-column) with high performance size-
exclusion chromatography (HPSEC). HPSEC enabled the separation of melanoidins from CGAs and the
determination of the antioxidant capacity of each fraction, within a total elution time of 25 min. Besides
the on-line assay measurements, both assays were also applied oﬀ-line with ﬂow injection analysis (FIA).
The maximum antioxidant capacity was determined to be at a light-to-medium roast degree, measured
with both ABTS-FIA and FC-FIA assays as well as on-line ABTS assay. With FC on-line assay the maximum
was found to be at a very light roast degree. Based on the peak areas obtained with the new coupled
technique the roasting eﬀects on the variability of melanoidin and CGA contents in coﬀee brews were
studied. The majority of melanoidins are already formed in the early stage of the roasting process and
the relative contribution of melanoidins to the total antioxidant capacity increases towards darker
roasts, mainly because CGAs degrade during roasting. A new parameter, the ratio of melanoidin to CGA
peak area, was introduced as a possible predictor of the roast degree.
Introduction
We are witnessing today a growing awareness of the multiple
links between the intake of dietary antioxidants (AOs) and our
health and well-being.1 In particular, a vast literature is avail-
able, where a variety of analytical methods and assays have been
developed and applied to separate food with high total AO
contents into fractions and individual compounds, and probed
for their AO capacity.2–4
In green coﬀee the main AO compounds are the chlorogenic
acids (CGAs), which are conjugates between quinic acid and
various trans-phenyl-3-propenoic acids. The dominant CGA in
coﬀee is 5-caﬀeoylquinic acid (5-CQA), followed by 3-CQA and 4-
CQA,1 and the total CGAs account for up to 10% of the dry
matter of green (un-roasted) coﬀee beans. It represents one of
the highest CGA contents known in foods, although many other
plant taxa, foods and beverages are also rich in CGAs.1 Among
the two commercially relevant coﬀee varieties, Robusta (Coﬀea
canephora L.) has signicantly higher amounts of CGA than
Arabica (Coﬀea arabica L.).5 Although CGAs are altered and
destroyed during roasting, dark roasted coﬀee still contains
CGAs.6 Recently, a comprehensive study of the CGA degradation
pathways and mechanisms leading to the formation of low
molecular weight (LMW) products was published.7
Major changes in coﬀee bean composition occur during
roasting as a result of the Maillard reaction, caramelisation and
pyrolysis. The thereby produced melanoidins, high molecular
weight (HMW) Maillard reaction end-products, are a heteroge-
neous mixture of brown coloured, nitrogen containing poly-
mers, formed through the reaction of reducing sugars with
proteins/amino acids.8,9 A part of CGAs is incorporated into the
melanoidins during the roasting process.10,11 While intact
sugars were reported in the melanoidin skeleton,12 no intact
caﬀeic or chlorogenic acid moieties could be detected.13 Mela-
noidins have attracted huge interest recently as a result of
claims for potential health benets,8 metal chelation proper-
ties,14 AO activity11,15 and as sources of AO dietary bres.16 It was
shown that model melanoidins exhibit AO activity17 as well as
iron chelation properties.18 It is believed that the AO properties
of melanoidins are gained either by incorporation of CGAs10,11

or by macromolecular, roasting-derived Maillard reaction
products.19 CGAs incorporated into the melanoidins can be
either covalently or ionically bound. Incorporation of ionically
bound CGAs was rst proposed by Delgado et al.20 and subse-
quent more detailed analyses by Bekedam et al.21 conrmed the
existence of ionically bound CGAs; they were released aer
incubation of a coﬀee brew in 2 M NaCl. In addition, covalently
bound CGAs have also been reported.21 However, there are
reports where no ionically bound CGAs could be detected in a
coﬀee brew.22
With regard to AO assays, a large number of studies have
focused on measuring the AO capacity of both melanoidin
and CGA fractions as well as the total AO capacity (TAC) of the
unfractionated coﬀee. Results of these investigations strongly
diverge among each other, and it is believed that this is due to
a lack of standardised denition or control of the roast degree
as well as chemical and mechanistic diﬀerences between the
AO assays used in such studies.4,22 More specically, a range
of studies have investigated the impact of the roast degree on
TAC of coﬀee. Depending on the study, highest AO capacity is
reported for a light,15,23 medium15,24 or dark roast25,26 and in
some publications no trend was found at all.19,27 To evaluate
the contribution of melanoidins and incorporated CGAs to
the TAC of roasted coﬀee, a separation/fractionation step is
necessary. Most commonly used methods for fractionation
and isolation of melanoidins are dialysis, dialtration and
ultraltration.28 Size-exclusion separation is another tech-
nique that is based e.g. on gel ltration chromatography with
a Sephadex G-25 matrix,29–31 or alternatively with Sepadex
G-75,18 Toyopearl HW-40F,32 Sephadex LH-20,13 and Sephacryl
S-400 HR.10 There have also been attempts of separation by
capillary electrophoresis.33 The AO assays for melanoidins
and CGAs are usually done as batch AO assays (oﬀ-line), either
aer isolation of melanoidins or by directly measuring
obtained fractions.
To improve the resolution, high performance size-exclusion
chromatography (HPSEC) was introduced to characterise HMW
fractions of melanoidins in coﬀee brews.34 In the case of
complex matrices such as coﬀee extracts, high resolution
screening techniques are advantageous and in order to further
expand the analytical information, such techniques can be
coupled with on-line post-column AO assays, creating a
powerful approach for screening of AO components in a coﬀee
brew. On-line AO assays are already well established. First
applications were published about 10 years ago35,36 and a review
has summarized the state of the art in the eld.37 While new on-
line assays are still emerging38,39 and extending the range of
possible applications, more traditional and established batch
assays are being implemented as on-line assays as well.40 On-
line assays used aer reverse phase liquid chromatography were
applied to analyse coﬀee AOs.41,42 To the best of our knowledge,
only one attempt to couple an on-line assay with SEC was
published.43 Predominantly stable oxidising reagents are used
in post-column derivatisation as they are easy to use and
control, compared to assays involving biologically more relevant
reactive oxygen species (ROS).37 Until now no such on-line assay
involving biologically relevant ROS has been reported, due to
the rather low reaction rate of generated ROS with AOs eluting
from the column.
In this work, a coﬀee brew was separated using HPSEC and
coupled with two diﬀerent post-column AO assays with the
following reagents: (i) 2,2-azinobis-(3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-
sulphonate) stable radical (ABTS_+) and (ii) Folin–Ciocalteu (FC)
reagent. An ABTS assay is sometimes also known as Trolox
equivalent AO assay (TEAC). The free radical is a coloured
compound and the AO capacity is quantied by measuring the
decolouration of the test solution as a result of reduction by
AOs.44 An FC assay is oen described as a total phenol assay,
although it is an assay to determine TAC.45 One benet of an on-
line experimental approach is high throughput. But most
importantly, measurements can be performed without prior
sample work-up and pre-treatment to prevent loss of organic
matter. Here, we report on the AO capacity proles of mela-
noidins and CGAs for coﬀees roasted to diﬀerent roast degrees,
using the above-mentioned post-column AO assays. Thus the
impact of the roast degree on the formation of melanoidins and
the degradation of CGAs in a coﬀee brew (“in the cup”) could be
assessed. Furthermore, based on the ratio of melanoidin to CGA
content, we propose a new parameter to determine the roast
degree.
Materials and methods
Chemicals and reagents
5-Caﬀeoylquinic acid was supplied by Acros Organic (Geel,
Belgium), 3- and 4-caﬀeoylquinic acids, Trolox (6-hydroxy-
2,5,7,8-tetramethylchroman-2-carboxylic acid), gallic acid (GA),
caﬀeine,
2,2-azinobis-(3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulphonate)
(ABTS), FC reagent, salts and solvents were supplied by Sigma-
Aldrich (Buchs SG, Switzerland).
The FC reagent for the assay was prepared by 10 dilution of
the commercial 2 M (with respect to acid) FC reagent. ABTS_+
solutions were synthesized by oxidation with potassium per-
sulphate;44 equal volumes of 7 mM ABTS and 2.45 mM potas-
sium persulphate solutions were mixed and stored before use

Standard solutions were prepared by dissolving the stan-
dards in water followed by appropriate dilution of the stock
solution with water.

process was stopped at a xed roast degree and the colour of the
roasted and ground coﬀee was measured with a colour
measuring instrument (Colorette 3, Probat-Werke, Emmerich,
Germany). Green coﬀee and coﬀee roasted for 5 min were
ground using a mixer (B¨uchi Mixer B-400, Flawil, Switzerland)
aer freezing the beans in liquid nitrogen. All other roasts were

Coﬀee brew preparation
Brews were prepared using a 200 mL French press (Bodum,
Triengen, Switzerland). 12 g of ground coﬀee were weighed,
infused with approximately 200 mL (mean value 190.5 g  0.5
SD) of hot water at 92 C and shortly stirred. Aer two minutes
the plunger was pressed down to lter the suspension and the
brew was lestanding for two additional minutes. The coﬀee
brew was then poured into 250 mL asks (Schott, Germany) and
cooled to room temperature by placing the asks in a water bath
at room temperature. The extract was also diluted to adjust the
concentration to the specic dynamic range of the AO assays.
For HPSEC, coﬀee samples were diluted 20 : 50, except for green
and 5 min roasts that were diluted 10 : 50. Samples for FC FIA
analysis were diluted 1 : 50 and for ABTS FIA analysis 0.5 : 100.
All samples were ltered before analysis using 0.45 mm PET
lters (Machery-Nagel, D¨uren, Germany).
Flow injection analysis
A FIAlab 3200 (FiALab Instruments Inc., Bellevue, WA, USA)
instrument was used for all FIA measurements. For a FC-FIA
assay an automated multi syringe FIA (MSFIA) method was
developed.46 In short, two 5 mL syringe pumps were used to
pump the 0.2 M FC and 0.25 M NaOH solutions at a ow rate of
30 mL s1. A 6-port injection valve with a 100 mL injection loop
was used to introduce the sample into the stream of FC reagent
solution and a reaction coil (1 m tubing length) to produce the
necessary dispersion of the sample for the reaction. An ABTS-
FIA assay was used as a sequential injection analysis (SIA) setup
to reduce the consumption of the ABTS_+ reagent. A 2.5 mL
syringe pump was used for the SIA system. ABTS_+ reagent
(20 mL), sample (40 mL) and carrier solution (200 mL; 0.1 M
disodium hydrogen phosphate, pH 7.4) were mixed in a heated
holding coil (tubing length: 1.5 m), thermostated at 35 C, and a
30 s reaction delay was applied. A Cetac ASX-260 autosampler
(Omaha, NE, USA) together with a 1 mL syringe pump were used
for automatic introduction of the samples for both assays.
Detection was achieved by measuring the absorbance at 734 nm
for the ABTS assay and at 765 nm for the FC assay.
Separation of melanoidins and chlorogenic acids
Chromatographic separation was performed with an HPLC
system (Agilent Series 1200, Santa Clara, CA, USA) using three
diﬀerent HPSEC TSKgel columns (Tosoh Bioscience, Stuttgart,
Germany):
G4000PWxl,

ammonium phosphate buﬀer adjusted to pH ¼ 7 as the eluent,
pumped at a ow rate of 0.4 mL min1, a sample injection
volume of 5 mL and a column oven temperature of 30 C. The
eluent was monitored at 210, 280, 325 and 405 nm using a diode
array detector (DAD).
On-line antioxidant assays
An Ismatec ISM8273 peristaltic pump (IDEX Health and Science
SA, Glattbrugg, Switzerland) was used to pump reagents for
both assays. The reagents were introduced into the stream via T-
elements aer the rst detector (DAD) and the formation of
products was monitored by a MWD (Multiple Wavelength
Detector, Agilent Series 1100) at the same wavelengths as for FIA
methods. For ABTS on-line assay a reaction coil in an additional
HPLC column compartment at 60 C, producing a 30 s delay,
was used to adjust and control the reaction conditions (Fig. 1).
Reagent ow rates for ABTS and FC assays were 0.12 and
0.15 mL min1. For the ABTS on-line assay the ABTS_+ concen-
tration was experimentally set so that the initial baseline
absorbance was 0.80 AU (absorbance unit) in order to ensure
that the starting conditions are equal throughout all experi-
ments. The assays were calibrated by standard solutions of
5-CQA. In order to present data in GA equivalents, which is a
commonly used reference, the transformation from 5-CQA
equivalents to GA equivalents was determined by a separate set
of validation experiments.

Results and discussion
Size-based separation of coﬀee brews
A high performance size exclusion chromatography (HPSEC)
method for size-based separation of coﬀee coupled with anti-
oxidant assays was developed to separate HMW melanoidins
from CGAs and to measure their AO capacity as a function of
roast degree. Comparing HPSEC to other methods for size
based separation of a coﬀee brew such as dialysis and ultral-
tration, a separation based on HPSEC has some key advantages.
These are: (i) short analysis time, (ii) the ability to resolve the
high
molecular
weight
melanoidins
or
even
individual
compounds among the CGAs and (iii) the possibility to use on-
line post-column derivatisation. Ultraltration and dialysis
divide a given sample only into two fractions with a broadly
dened cut-oﬀmass. However, with HPSEC the use of buﬀered
eluents is crucial to suppress unwanted ionic interactions of
analytes with the gel matrix, and we cannot rule out the possi-
bility of interactions with the matrix; therefore total elution of
the injected coﬀee sample is questionable, but no carry-over has
been observed in our setup. With dialysis no quantitative
separation of HMW from LMW compounds is possible. Losses
occur during separation as small molecules react with mela-
noidins during the separation process and presumably increase
the mass of the HMW fraction.34 The Sephadex G-25 gel with
water as the eluent was oen used for SEC to fractionate a coﬀee
brew,29 although it provides only low resolution. Our prelimi-
nary studies indicated that SEC separation based on Sephadex
G-25 is not suﬃciently robust and only allows a rough separa-
tion of one melanoidin peak from a peak with all the CGAs. A
lack of reproducible results obtained with a Sephadex G-25 gel
was previously reported.34 Instead, Bekedam et al. used a stack
of pre-packed TSKgel G4000PWxl and G2500PWxl HPSEC
columns to achieve both superior resolution and better repro-
ducibility in the separation of coﬀee components.34 We tested
the newest HPSEC technology from Tosoh, the TSKgel Super-
multiporePW technology that uses a packed material with a
wide distribution of pore sizes. This matrix enabled eﬃcient
separation in only 25 min, compared to 60 min for the
G4000PWxl and 250 min for the G-25. Aer thorough optimi-
sation of eluent composition for the separation of a coﬀee brew
it was concluded that use of a 0.1 M ammonium phosphate
buﬀer at pH ¼ 7 as the eluent was most eﬀective for measure-
ments and validation of the on-line AO methods. The optimi-
sation was carried out by testing the eﬀects of diﬀerent salts in
the eluent: (i) sodium salts without buﬀer capacity, (ii) sodium
salts with buﬀer capacity and no pH adjustment, (iii) nitrate
salts (based on the method of Bekedam et al.34) and (iv)
ammonium salts. The concentration of the salt in the eluent
and eﬀects of organic modier, acetonitrile or methanol, added
to the eluent were studied as well. Finally, adjusting the pH-
value of the buﬀered eluent was one major parameter that
severely aﬀected the retention times (tR) of CGAs with higher
retention and better separation towards lower pH value
(Fig. 2a). It aﬀected sensitivity as well and the melanoidin
sensitivity decreased with decreasing pH, while the CGA sensi-
tivity decreased towards higher pH values, e.g. pH 8 (Fig. 2b).
The sensitivity might have changed due to the change of mel-
anoidin absorption and alkaline autoxidation of CGAs, while
the cause of the tR change of CGAs is not explainable. It was
estimated that no profound irreversible interaction of the coﬀee
matrix with the SEC gel occurred. Two experiments provide
evidence for this: (i) injecting numerous coﬀee samples at a low
pH, where melanoidins had a low peak area, did not aﬀect the

On-line assay validation
For validation of both FC and ABTS on-line assays, three
diﬀerent standards were tested (GA, Trolox and 5-CQA) as
antioxidants and 5-CQA was chosen as it is the most abundant
single antioxidant compound in a coﬀee brew. The other two
standards were not used due to the limited water solubility of
Trolox and the insuﬃcient stability of GA in HPLC vials during
the measurements. Using the response values of standards,
5-CQA AO equivalents can be converted to GA and Trolox
equivalents (Table 1). Both assays were reproducible, robust and
had low LOQ (FC: 12 ppm and ABTS: 0.12 ppm 5-CQA equiva-
lents) and LOD values (FC: 10 ppm and ABTS: 0.04 ppm 5-CQA
equivalents). Recovery rates were between 102% and 108% for
both assays. A possible drawback was seen in the limited
dynamic range of the ABTS assay. Still, as the LOQ is very low
compared to the total coﬀee brew AO capacity (for FC > 1400
ppm and for ABTS > 1600 ppm 5-CQA equivalents), coﬀee
samples can be diluted to t the dynamic range. Caﬀeine was
tested for its AO eﬀect and it did not produce a signal, although
it has been reported to show radical scavenging activity.47,48
Both assays were compared for their relative selectivity and
sensitivity to compounds and compound classes eluting from
the SEC column by conducting the same experiments with one
coﬀee sample in both assays (Fig. 3). Aer normalizing the
chromatograms to the 5-CQA peak (strong signal at a tR of 16
min), diﬀerences in sensitivity in relation to the 5-CQA peak are
visible (Fig. 3). The ABTS assay shows higher relative sensitivity
towards the melanoidins compared to the FC assay. This is in
line with the view that the FC assay has been considered and
frequently described as a total phenol assay in coﬀee.11 The FC
assay
shows
higher
sensitivity
to
polyphenols,
but
also
measures some AO capacity of melanoidins that originates from
the Maillard reaction. It likely measures all components that
have a lower redox potential than the reagent.4 Considering that
CGAs are incorporated into melanoidins, the response of the FC
assay to melanoidins might as well stem from incorporated
CGAs into the melanoidin skeleton. Hence, the complete story
is a superposition of both eﬀects, incorporation of CGAs and
melanoidin AO activity. The higher sensitivity of the ABTS assay
towards melanoidins can be tentatively assigned to a fraction of
non-polyphenolic AOs formed during roasting via the Maillard
reaction and its sensitivity towards CGAs is not so pronounced.
Total antioxidant capacity of coﬀee aﬀected by roast degree
Arabica green coﬀee from Guatemala was roasted on a 100 g
batch roaster to 5 diﬀerent roast degrees and Arabica green
coﬀee from Costa Rica on a 15 kg drum roaster to three diﬀerent
roast degrees, each with three replicates (Table 2). The coﬀee
brew was rst analysed with ABTS and FC assays using FIA,
measuring the TAC as GA equivalents. The TAC was also esti-
mated from the on-line assays coupled with HPSEC by inte-
grating over the total area of the chromatogram.15 Diﬀerences in
obtained AO capacity values and roast degree eﬀects on the
maximum AO capacity were found between the two assays
measured with FIA and the two assays measured with on-line
assays (Fig. 4a and b). From all coﬀee brews, only the four brews
roasted to or beyond the rst crack are shown and the focus was
on the development of the TAC with roast degree. The FC-FIA
assay (Fig. 4a) shows similar maximum values at light and light-
medium roasts (164 and 118 Pt) with a signicant drop towards
the two dark roast degrees (81 and 64 Pt). The ABTS-FIA assay
(Fig. 4b) shows a signicant increase of the AO capacity from
light roast to reach a maximum at a light–medium to medium–
dark roast degree (118 to 81 Pt). Then the AO capacity drops
signicantly towards the dark roast degree at 64 Pt. The FC on-
line assay showed the highest AO capacity at light roast degrees
(164 Pt) and it dropped signicantly from light–medium
towards the dark roast degrees (Fig. 4a). The ABTS on-line assay

towards the medium–dark and dark roasts (81 and 64 Pt) (the
roast degrees within one assay were compared by using one-way
ANOVA followed by a multiple t-test). The highest values for the
ABTS on-line and the FC on-line assays were at the light–
medium and light roast degrees with values of 596  7 and
950  42 ppm GA equivalents. For both FC-FIA and ABTS-FIA
assays the maximum TAC was measured at the 118 Pt colour
roast degree (8 min roast time), the values being 2548  21 ppm
GA equivalents for the FC assay and 647  8 ppm GA equiva-
lents for the ABTS assay.
The observed diﬀerences between both FIA assays are
consistent with the already observed sensitivity diﬀerence
between the two assays towards melanoidins or CGAs (Fig. 3). As
the FC assay is relatively more sensitive to CGAs, the signicant
drop in AO capacity for the medium–dark and dark roasts is
probably caused by the degradation of CGAs. The ABTS-FIA
assay shows rst a signicant rise in AO capacity from the light
to the light–medium and medium–dark roast degrees when
melanoidins are formed and gradually altered, while beyond
the medium–dark roast degree, the degradation of CGAs likely
causes the observed signicant drop in AO capacity (Fig. 4b).
With the on-line assays, the CGA degradation eﬀect seems to be
more prominent, leading to a larger decrease in AO capacity
with darker roast degrees for both assays. Compared to the
ABTS-FIA assay the signicant drop in AO capacity towards the
two darker roasts is very pronounced with the ABTS on-line
assay, similar to the drop in the two FC assays. These results,
proving that the total phenol content and FC AO capacity drop
with increasing roast degree, are consistent with the published
data.11,22,49
For the coﬀee with the overall highest AO capacity (118 Pt, 8
min roast, the highest for three out of four assays) the GA
equivalent values obtained by the FC on-line assays were 65%
lower than those by the FIA-FC assay and those obtained by the
ABTS on-line assay were 8% lower than those by the FIA-ABTS
assay. One major diﬀerence between FIA and on-line assays lies
in the diﬀerent reaction conditions applied. Using a highly
buﬀered eluent for the FC on-line assay, the high alkaline
conditions in FIA could not be met, which probably caused the
diﬀerence in measured antioxidant capacity. Therefore the AO
values obtained with the FC on-line assay were considerably
lower than the ones measured with the FC-FIA assay. Similarly,
small diﬀerences in pH were present between the ABTS on-line
and the ABTS-FIA assays. Other diﬀerences caused by the setup
architecture are reaction time, reaction temperature and
diﬀerent dispersion of the reagent, all of which will aﬀect the
nal value to some degree. Furthermore, the coﬀee matrix could
aﬀect the AO activity as it is much more complex in the non-
separated brew for FIA analysis compared to HPSEC. Conse-
quently, two potential phenomena may as well contribute to the
diﬀerences between FIA and on-line assays. (i) In FIA analysis,
potential AOs could be masked in the complex and unfractio-
nated coﬀee brew. This masking actually would cause lower AO
values for the FIA assays, which is not the case here. Addition-
ally, (ii) not all compounds might have eluted from the column
in on-line assays due to possible irreversible interactions of the
coﬀee components with the column. Hence, while these two
eﬀects are likely contributing to the diﬀerences between FIA
and on-line assays, their contribution is only marginal.
Green coﬀee and coﬀee roasted to 5 min roast time (no
colour roast degree determined) produced clearly lower FIA AO
values (FC – green coﬀee: 2053 ppm GA equivalents, 5 min roast:
1283  94 ppm GA equivalents; ABTS – green coﬀee: 368 ppm
GA equivalents, 5 min roast: 247  17 ppm GA equivalents). The
5 min roast degree resulted in the overall lowest AO values
measured using both FIA and both on-line HPSEC assays. The
explanation for the lower values is probably lower extraction
eﬃciency, which was conrmed by lower CGA content when
measured with DAD at 325 nm. Green coﬀee and coﬀee roasted
for 5 min were ground using a mixer aer freezing the beans in
liquid nitrogen as these beans were too hard to be ground in a
regular coﬀee grinder. This diﬀerence in grinding could result
in uneven, coarser, less penetrable and less porous coﬀee
ground. The very high AO capacity for green coﬀee reported in
the literature probably stems from diﬀerent and longer extrac-
tion processes.32,50 These extraction methods are not compa-
rable with the hot water extraction process applied here that is
analogous to the extraction of a cup of coﬀee for consumption.

light to light–medium roast degree (Fig. 5). For further eluci-

taining and the CGA containing fractions, contribute to the AO
capacity, the peak areas of both fractions were quantied by
dividing the chromatogram at a tR of 12.8 min (Fig. 6) into two
sections. Both fractions are the main contributors to the TAC in
coﬀee, but other LMW Maillard reaction products could
contribute to a small extent as well. In order to explain the
observed change in the composition of coﬀee during roasting, a
new parameter, the melanoidin to CGA ratio (melanoidin/CGA),
is introduced. The ratio can be plotted by either using the peak
areas from one of the AO assays or by measuring the absorbance
at the two characteristic wavelengths 325 or 405 nm. At those
wavelengths green coﬀee HMW compounds do not absorb
(HMW contribution to total absorbance at 325 nm was 1.0%
and absorbance at 405 nm was 2.8%). Green coﬀee HMW
compounds also do not produce strong AO signals (HMW
contribution to TAC – ABTS assay: 7.1%, FC assay: 5.8%). In
green coﬀee mainly the AO capacity of CGAs is measured and
this results in a very low melanoidin/CGA ratio. As the extrac-
tion eﬃciency is not important in calculating this ratio, also
green and the 5 min roasted coﬀees are plotted together with
the other four roast degrees (Fig. 7). A low melanoidin/CGA ratio
with little variation was calculated for the 5 min roast, although
some Maillard reaction products are already formed and iso-
merisation of CGAs commenced. At the rst crack the majority
of melanoidins are already formed, measured either by AO
capacity or UV absorbance (ABTS AO capacity of the HMW
fractions is given at diﬀerent roast degrees expressed as GA
equivalents: 26 ppm for green coﬀee; 39 ppm for 5 min;
230 ppm for 7 min; 266 ppm for 8 min; 285 ppm for 10 min and
281 ppm for 12 min roast times). This melanoidin increase
results in an increase of the melanoidin/CGA ratio between the
5 min roast and the 7 min roast due to melanoidin formation,
which is in accordance with the literature.15,49 Melanoidins can
be further transformed at darker roasts, but the fate of mela-
noidins remains unknown and a molecular mass increase21 or
decrease11 is reported. In our HPSEC measurements (Fig. 5) no
retention time shis of the major melanoidin peaks were
observed, which indicates that the changes in average molec-
ular mass of melanoidins during roasting are negligible. The
melanoidin/CGA ratio continuously increases further with
increasing roast degree. At lower roast degrees the melanoidin
formation is the main contributing factor to the rise of the ratio,
while at higher roast degrees the higher degradation rate of CGA
becomes the major contributor. The linear behaviour of the
observed melanoidin/CGA ratio with roast degree for the ABTS
assay (Fig. 7) is most probably a coincidence. This ratio prole
could be aﬀected by roaster performance and bean quality, but
the trend of a continuous increase of the ratio with roast degree
will likely not change. Regardless of the quantication method
used (either one of the AO assays or DAD), coﬀee origin (Costa
Rica, Guatemala) or roaster capacity (100 g, 15 kg) a continuous
increase in the melanoidin/CGA ratio with increasing roast
degree was detected.

Melanoidin and chlorogenic acid spectra
Apart from sizing melanoidin fractions obtained from dialysis,
dialtration or ultraltration,28 a common way to quantify
melanoidins is to measure the absorbance of a sample at
wavelengths over 400 nm, 405 nm being used for coﬀee mela-
noidins.52 For this reason our chromatograms were also moni-
tored at 405 nm and absorbance spectra of both fractions were
measured using the DAD detector (Fig. 9). Except for the darkest
roasted coﬀee, the melanoidin peak was not the sole contrib-

anoidins and CGAs contributed equally to the 405 nm
absorbance, although the absorbance of the CGA peak area (tR:
12.8 min to 18 min) was actually only tailing out from its main
absorbance at 325 nm. This indicates that CGAs or their

bance at wavelengths generally considered specic to mela-
noidins. The contribution of CGAs to the absorbance of
melanoidins would be much lower if measured at wavelengths
$450 nm, while one must put up with less sensitivity.

Conclusion
On-line assays coupled with HPSEC are very powerful tools for
analysing the AO capacities of coﬀee brews. The individual
contributions of melanoidins and CGAs to the total AO capacity
can be rapidly assessed. However, care is needed in evaluation
of results as the reaction conditions for an on-line assay may be
substantially diﬀerent from those used in batch methods. In
addition, standardised assays are necessary if results are to be
comparable.4 Here we present two validated assays that diﬀer in
their responses towards diﬀerent classes of compounds, mela-
noidins and CGAs. The AO assays measure diﬀerent AO capac-
ities of coﬀee brews that are dependent on the roast degree. The
assay conditions as well as extraction conditions play an
important role in determining the AO capacity of coﬀee55,56 and
they aﬀect the roast degree at which the maximum AO capacity
is measured.
Apart from measuring AO capacity on-line, separation with
HPSEC enables a more detailed understanding of the roasting
process. We observed that at the start of the rst crack the
majority of melanoidins had already formed and isomerisation
of the CGAs commenced. In progress from medium to dark
roast degrees, the CGAs degraded rapidly. Finally, by calculating
the melanoidin/CGA ratio from AO signals, we have introduced
a new parameter for roast degree prediction. This takes into
account both melanoidin formation and CGA degradation at
darker roast degrees, and the variability in CGA contents can be
assessed for similar colours of roast.
