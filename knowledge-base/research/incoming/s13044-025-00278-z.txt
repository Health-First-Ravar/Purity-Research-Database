Abstract
Background and objectives  The risk of thyroid cancer (TC) has been steadily increasing globally, prompting interest 
in identifying modifiable risk factors. Coffee, rich in bioactive compounds with potential metabolic effects, may 
influence TC risk, yet the relationship remains unclear. Therefore, this study aims to systematically review and conduct 
a meta-analysis of observational studies to assess the association between coffee consumption and the risk of TC.
Methods  Comprehensive searches were conducted in major databases through September 2025 to identify 
appropriate studies. Following the study selection process, relevant data were extracted. Pooled hazard ratio (HR) 
and odds ratio (OR) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs) were calculated using random-effects models to account for 
heterogeneity.
Results  This meta-analysis of 10 observational (cohort and case-control) studies found no statistically significant 
association between coffee consumption and TC risk across both study designs. The pooled analysis of four cohort 
studies (6 effect sizes) yielded a pooled HR of 0.94 (95% CI: [0.74, 1.20], p = 0.645; I² = 31%), indicating no meaningful 
risk reduction. The pooled analysis of six case-control studies (8 effect sizes) showed a non-significant trend toward a 
protective effect, with a pooled OR of 0.78 (95% CI: [0.59, 1.03], p = 0.091; I² = 12%), indicating low heterogeneity across 
the designs.
Conclusions  Overall, the findings suggest that coffee consumption is not significantly associated with an increased 
risk of TC. Case-control studies indicate a possible protective trend that warrants further investigation, and the low 
heterogeneity supports the consistency of these results. Nonetheless, additional large-scale prospective studies are 
needed to confirm these findings and clarify the underlying biological mechanisms.

Introduction
Thyroid cancer (TC), the most prevalent malignancy of 
the endocrine system, represents a considerable por­
tion of cancer diagnoses globally [1]. Over the past few 
decades, its incidence has shown a notable rise, which 
is partly explained by improved diagnostic capabilities 
and increased awareness among healthcare providers 
and the general population [2]. The development of TC 
is recognized as multifactorial, with contributions from 
genetic predispositions, environmental exposures, and 
lifestyle behaviors [3, 4]. Among these factors, the influ­
ence of diet and nutrition has gained significant attention 
in recent years, as dietary habits are modifiable and thus 
present opportunities for preventive interventions [5–8]. 
Within this context, coffee—a beverage enjoyed by mil­
lions daily for its stimulating and social qualities—has 
emerged as a subject of interest due to its potential role 
in modulating metabolic and cancer risk [6, 7, 9, 10]. 
Coffee, a globally consumed beverage, is rich in bio­
active compounds like polyphenols, caffeine, and diter­
penes, which exhibit antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and 
antimutagenic properties [11, 12]. These attributes have 
been linked to potential anticancer effects, particularly 
in reducing the risk of liver, colorectal, and endometrial 
cancers [13]. By modulating cellular damage, enhancing 
DNA repair, and decreasing chronic inflammation, mod­
erate coffee consumption is associated with lower cancer 
incidence [14, 15]. However, genetic, environmental, and 
lifestyle factors influence these outcomes, highlighting 
the need for further research to validate these findings 
conclusively.
Despite promising associations, studies on coffee and 
cancer risk have yielded mixed results. Some research 
highlights its protective role, possibly through antioxi­
dant mechanisms and hormonal regulation, whereas 
others find no link or even a slight risk increase [16]. Dis­
crepancies arise due to variations in study design, sample 
populations, and confounding factors such as smoking 
and alcohol use. A study showed that Coffee consump­
tion is linked to reduced risk of colorectal cancer recur­
rence and all-cause mortality [17]. Another research 
demonstrated that higher coffee consumption is inversely 
associated with TC occurrence, with a 5% risk reduction 
per additional cup consumed daily [18]. A meta-anal­
ysis conducted in 2017 found no significant association 
between coffee consumption and risk of TC [19].
Although numerous observational studies have exam­
ined the association between coffee consumption and the 
TC risk, a definitive synthesis is lacking. This study fills 
that gap by conducting a systematic review and meta-
analysis to evaluate the association between coffee intake 
and TC, aiming to determine whether coffee intake is 
protective, neutral, or harmful.
Methods
Study design and protocol
This systematic review and meta-analysis of observa­
tional studies evaluated the association between coffee 
consumption and the risk of TC. The study adhered to 
the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews 
and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) statement [20], and was 
designed following the PICOS (Population, Intervention, 
Comparison, Outcomes, and Study design) framework 
[21], defined as follows:
 	•
P (Population): Adults.
 	•
I (Intervention/Exposure): Coffee consumption 
(highest).
 	•
C (Control/Comparator): No coffee consumption 
(lowest vs. highest coffee consumption).
 	•
O (Outcomes): Risk of thyroid cancer (TC).
 	•
S (Study Design): Observational (case-control and 
cohort) designs.
Search strategy
A comprehensive literature search was conducted across 
multiple 
databases, 
including 
PubMed/MEDLINE, 
Embase, Web of Science, and Scopus, covering publica­
tions from their inception through September 2025. To 
ensure thoroughness, a manual search was conducted on 
Google Scholar. The search strategy incorporated MeSH 
terms and relevant synonyms for coffee and TC applied 
to both the Title and Abstract fields. The keywords 
included: (“coffee” OR “Caffeine” OR “caffeinated”) AND 
(“Thyroid cancer” OR “Thyroid malignancy” OR “Thyroid 
carcinoma” OR “Thyroid adenocarcinoma” OR “Thyroid 
tumor” OR “Thyroid papillary carcinoma” OR “Follicular 
thyroid carcinoma”). No restrictions were placed on lan­
guage or publication date, ensuring the inclusion of stud­
ies from diverse time periods and linguistic backgrounds. 
Additionally, the reference lists of included articles were 
manually reviewed for any relevant studies that may have 
been missed during the initial search. The detailed search 
keywords, search line, and query box for each database 
are provided in Supplementary Tables 1 and 2.
Eligibility criteria
Inclusion criteria for this meta-analysis were as follows: 
(1) Studies that investigated the association between cof­
fee consumption (including caffeinated coffee or caffeine) 
and risk of TC (2), Original research articles, including 
cohort, case-control, and cross-sectional (3) Studies that 
provided sufficient data to calculate or extract effect sizes 
(ES), such as hazard ratios (HR), odds ratios (OR), or rela­
tive risks (RR) with corresponding 95% confidence inter­
vals (CI) (4), Studies published in peer-reviewed journals 
or gray literature (5), Studies published in any language 
(6), Studies conducted in human populations.

Exclusion criteria were as follows: (1) experimental 
studies, reviews, meta-analyses, and conference abstracts 
(2), Animal studies (3), Studies without sufficient data 
for ES calculation or extraction (4), Studies that did not 
focus specifically on TC or its subtypes (5), Studies where 
the primary focus was not on coffee or caffeine consump­
tion as the main exposure variable (6), Studies with major 
methodological flaws, such as lack of control for con­
founding variables.
Study selection and data extraction
Two independent reviewers (M.B. and A.M.) systemati­
cally screened all identified records in a two-stage pro­
cess. First, titles and abstracts were assessed for potential 
eligibility. Studies meeting the preliminary criteria 
underwent full-text review to confirm inclusion. Any dis­
agreements at either stage were resolved through discus­
sion, and unresolved cases were referred to the project 
supervisor (M.K.) for final decision. This rigorous screen­
ing ensured that only studies that met the research objec­
tives for the association between coffee consumption and 
TC risk were included in the meta-analysis.
Following study selection, two reviewers (M.B. and 
A.M.) independently extracted data from selected stud­
ies. Extracted information included: study characteris­
tics (first author, publication year, country, study design, 
sample size, participant age, and follow-up duration), 
exposure details (type of coffee and consumption dose 
in cups/day), outcome measures (type of TC diagnosed), 
and effect estimates (HR, OR, or RR with 95% CI, along 
with adjustment variables). For each study, the ES for the 
highest versus the lowest coffee consumption, along with 
the most fully adjusted and non-overlapping effect esti­
mate, was selected. Discrepancies in data extraction were 
resolved through discussion or, if necessary, consulta­
tion with the third reviewer (M.K.). The finalized dataset 
was organized in an Excel sheet and was then prepared 
for statistical synthesis to ensure comparability across 
studies.
Quality assessment
The quality assessment of non-randomized studies was 
conducted using the ROBINS-I (Risk Of Bias In Non-ran­
domized Studies of Interventions) tool, as recommended 
by Cochrane [22]. Each study was evaluated across seven 
domains: (1) bias due to confounding (2), bias in selec­
tion of participants (3), bias in classification of exposures 
(4), bias due to deviations from intended exposures (5), 
bias due to missing data (6), bias in measurement of out­
comes, and (7) bias in selection of the reported result. 
For each domain, the risk was rated as low, moderate, 
serious, or critical, in accordance with ROBINS-I guid­
ance. Judgements were based on reported study design, 
adjustment for confounding variables, completeness of 
follow-up, accuracy and objectivity of exposure and out­
come measurements, and selective reporting practices. 
The overall risk of bias for each study was determined by 
the highest level of bias in any single domain.
Statistical analysis
To evaluate the prognostic association between caffeine 
consumption and TC, HRs for cohort studies and ORs for 
case-control studies, along with corresponding 95% CIs, 
were synthesized. Given the methodological heterogene­
ity among the included studies, a Random Effects Model 
(REM) was employed for pooling the HRs and ORs [23]. 
Heterogeneity was evaluated using the Chi-square test 
and quantified with the I² statistic. I² values below 25% 
indicate low heterogeneity, 25–50% indicate moderate 
heterogeneity, 50–75% indicate substantial heterogeneity, 
and values above 75% indicate high heterogeneity [24]. 
Sensitivity analyses were conducted to determine the 
influence of individual studies on the overall meta-ana­
lytic results. Egger’s and Begg’s statistical tests, alongside 
the funnel plot and the trim-and-fill approach, were uti­
lized to evaluate publication bias [25, 26]. Statistical anal­
yses were performed using Stata software, and p-values < 
0.05 were considered statistically significant.
Results
Study selection
An initial search across three major databases—PubMed 
(n = 169), Web of Science (n = 202), and Scopus (n = 
549)—yielded 920 records. After removing 238 dupli­
cates, 682 unique records remained for screening based 
on titles and abstracts. During this phase, 665 studies 
were excluded for being review articles, experimental 
trials, animal or laboratory studies (in vitro, in vivo, in 
cito), letters to the editor, or for other irrelevant study 
designs. This process narrowed the selection to 17 stud­
ies for a detailed full-text review to assess their eligibility 
for inclusion in the meta-analysis. Of these, seven studies 
were excluded due to insufficient data or failure to meet 
the inclusion criteria of a cohort or case-control design. 
Ultimately, 10 observational studies [9, 27–35], compris­
ing four cohort studies [9, 14, 29, 34] and six case-control 
studies [27, 28, 31–33, 35], met the eligibility criteria and 
were included in the final meta-analysis. The study selec­
tion process is detailed in the PRISMA flow diagram (Fig. 
1).
Effect sizes
The 10 included studies in the meta-analysis provided 
a total of 14 effect sizes. Six studies contributed a single 
effect size [14–34], while four studies reported two dis­
tinct effect sizes each [9, 28, 29, 35]. The detailed char­
acteristics of each study are summarized in Table 1.

Characteristics of cohort studies
The four cohort studies included in this meta-analysis [9, 
14, 29, 34] were conducted in Japan, the USA, and Euro­
pean countries, with follow-up periods ranging from 10 
to 15.3 years and sample sizes ranging from 68,698 to 
467,108 participants. The participants’ ages varied widely, 
from 35 to 75 years, and most studies included both 
males and females, though one Japanese study reported 
results separately by sex. Types of TC studied included 
papillary carcinoma, follicular carcinoma, and overall 
TC. Coffee consumption varied across studies, ranging 
from more than one cup per day to the highest quartile of 
intake, and ESs (HR or RR) ranged from 0.44 (0.23–0.87) 
to 1.48 (0.78–2.80). Most studies adjusted for multiple 
confounders, including age, sex, body mass index (BMI), 
smoking, alcohol consumption, education, and physical 
activity. Some also accounted for dietary factors, meno­
pausal status, and family history. Overall, the results were 
largely null, with only one study showing a statistically 
significant protective association between high coffee 
intake and TC risk (Table 1).

Quality assessments of included studies
Across the 10 included observational studies, the risk 
of bias varied from low to serious. Four cohort studies 
(Michikawa et al. 2011 [29]; Hashibe et al. 2015 [34]; Park 
et al. 2018 [14]; Zamora-Ros et al. 2019 [9]) were rated as 
low risk in all domains, reflecting robust cohort designs, 
adequate control for confounding factors, and com­
plete outcome data. Three studies (Galanti et al., 1997 
[28]; Frentzel-Beyme & Helmert, 2000 [35]; Myoyoun & 
Young, 2021 [31]) had a serious overall risk, primarily due 
to inadequate adjustment for key confounders and poten­
tial selection bias. The remaining three studies (Takezaki 
et al., 1996 [27]; Mack et al., 2002 [33]; Fiore et al., 2020 
[32]) showed moderate overall risk, most often due to 
incomplete control for lifestyle-related confounders or 
moderate selection bias. Common limitations across 
studies included limited adjustment for dietary and 
lifestyle variables, potential recall bias in case–control 
designs, and less precise exposure measurements in ear­
lier studies (Table 2; Fig. 2).
Meta-analysis of cohort studies
A meta-analysis of 4 cohort studies with 6 ES found no 
significant association between coffee consumption and 
TC risk. The pooled HR was 0.94 (95% CI: 0.74–1.20; 
p = 0.645), indicating a slight, statistically nonsignificant 
reduction in risk. Heterogeneity across studies was low 
(I² = 31.2%), and the test for heterogeneity was not sta­
tistically significant (p = 0.201), suggesting reasonably 
consistent findings among the included cohort studies 
(Table 3; Fig. 3).

Similarly, the pooled results from six case-control stud­
ies comprising eight ES, suggested a potential inverse 
association between coffee consumption and risk of TC, 
with a pooled OR of 0.78 (95% CI: 0.59–1.03; p = 0.091).

Sensitivity analysis
A sensitivity analysis was conducted to assess the robust­
ness of the overall results by sequentially excluding 
individual studies. For case-control studies, removing 
Frentzel-Beyme and Helmert [2000] (A) [35] resulted 
in an OR of 0.79 (95% CI: 0.58–0.09), while excluding 
Frentzel-Beyme and Helmert [2000] (B) [35] produced 
an OR of 0.73 (95% CI: 0.56–0.95), and omitting Mack 
et al. [2002] [33] yielded an OR of 0.74 (95% CI: 0.56–
0.99). These findings indicate that the overall associa­
tion remained generally consistent even after removing 
influential studies, supporting the stability of the meta-
analysis results despite potential publication bias in the 
case-control data (Table 4).
Publication bias
Publication bias was assessed using Begg’s and Egger’s 
tests for both cohort (Fig.  5) and case-control (Fig.  6) 
studies. As shown in Table 4, the results for cohort stud­
ies indicated no evidence of publication bias (Begg’s test 
p = 0.707, Egger’s test p = 0.930). In contrast, case-control 
studies showed a potential for publication bias, with Egg­
er’s test reaching statistical significance (p = 0.012), while 
Begg’s test was marginally non-significant (p = 0.063). 
This suggests that the findings from case-control stud­
ies may be influenced by small-study effects or selective 
reporting, warranting cautious interpretation of their 
pooled estimates.
Discussion
In contemporary society, coffee has become one of the 
most widely consumed beverages worldwide, appreciated 
for its stimulating effects, rich flavor, and cultural signifi­
cance. Simultaneously, the prevalence of various cancers, 
including TC, has been on the rise, becoming a critical 
concern for public health [1, 2]. This parallel trend raises 
questions about the potential role of lifestyle factors, 
including coffee consumption, in influencing cancer risk. 
Motivated by these considerations, we conducted a meta-
analysis of observational studies to examine the potential 
association between coffee consumption and TC risk.
The meta-analysis of four cohort studies found no sig­
nificant association between coffee consumption and 
the risk of TC. Similarly, the meta-analysis of six case-
control studies suggested a potential, albeit non-signif­
icant, inverse association between coffee intake and the

Coffee contains two notable diterpenes, cafestol and 
kahweol, that exhibit biological effects consistent with 
anticarcinogenic properties. These effects include the 
induction of phase II enzymes that play a role in the 
detoxification of carcinogens [36], the specific inhi­
bition of phase I enzymes responsible for activating 
carcinogens, and the enhancement of intracellular anti­
oxidant defense mechanisms [37]. Additionally, coffee 
is rich in polyphenols, including lignan phytoestrogens 
and flavonoids, which have been shown to possess anti­
carcinogenic properties in various studies [38]. Caffeic 
acid, a component of coffee, has been shown to inhibit 
DNA methylation in cultured human cancer cells and is 
linked to the inactivation of several pathways involved 
in tumorigenesis, including those related to cell cycle 
regulation, inflammatory responses, and apoptosis [39]. 
Furthermore, coffee serves as a significant source of chlo­
rogenic acid, which contributes to its antioxidant effects. 
Research indicates that chlorogenic acid intake can lower 
glucose levels in rats, whereas quinides, the degradation 
products of chlorogenic acid, have been found to enhance 
insulin sensitivity [40]. Chronic hyperinsulinemia and 
insulin resistance are recognized as significant risk fac­
tors for certain types of cancer [41].
The current meta-analysis findings align with some 
previous findings. For example, Mack et al. [42] con­
ducted a pooled analysis that found no association 
between coffee or tea consumption and the risk of TC, 
and Han et al. [16] similarly reported no significant asso­
ciation between coffee intake and the risk of TC in their 
meta-analysis. Zamora-Ros et al. [9] also concluded that 
coffee consumption was not associated with overall dif­
ferentiated risk of TC or its subtypes. However, other 
studies have presented contrasting findings. For example,

Strengths and limitations
This study’s strengths include its comprehensive, meth­
odologically rigorous approach, a large pooled sample 
from observational studies across diverse populations, 
and low heterogeneity. The use of the ROBINS-I tool 
ensured systematic quality assessment, and sensitivity 
and publication bias analyses confirmed the stability of 
results. However, several limitations must be considered. 
As all included studies were observational, causality can­
not be inferred, and residual confounding from factors 
such as diet, physical activity, or socioeconomic status is 
possible. Coffee consumption was self-reported, intro­
ducing potential recall bias and measurement errors. 
Some studies had small sample sizes and varied in their 
adjustment for confounders, which may have limited sta­
tistical power. Inconsistent reporting of intake categories 
across studies reduced precision and hindered robust 
dose–response analysis. Variations in cultural and dietary 
practices, as well as racial backgrounds across countries, 
may affect both the risk of TC and the generalizability 
of findings. Future research should employ larger, well-
designed studies to clarify optimal amounts and types of 
coffee for potential protective effects.
Conclusions
In conclusion, the current evidence from both cohort 
and case-control studies suggests that coffee consump­
tion is not significantly associated with an increased risk 
of TC. While case-control studies suggested a modest, 
non-significant protective trend, overall findings were 
consistent and showed low heterogeneity across studies. 
These results suggest that coffee intake alone is unlikely 
to substantially influence the development of TC. How­
ever, given the potential indication of a slight protective 
effect in some populations, further well-designed, large-
scale prospective studies are warranted to clarify the rela­
tionship and explore potential mechanisms.
