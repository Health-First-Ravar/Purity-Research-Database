A dark brown roast coffee blend is less effective at
stimulating gastric acid secretion in healthy volunteers
compared to a medium roast market blend
Malte Rubach1,2∗, Roman Lang3∗, Gerhard Bytof 4, Herbert Stiebitz4, Ingo Lantz4,
Thomas Hofmann3 and Veronika Somoza1,2,5
1 German Research Center for Food Chemistry, Freising, Germany

Coffee consumption sometimes is associated with symptoms of stomach discomfort. This work
aimed to elucidate whether two coffee beverages, containing similar amounts of caffeine, but
differing in their concentrations of N-alkanoyl-5-hydroxytryptamides (C5HTs), chlorogenic
acids (CGAs), trigonelline, and N-methylpyridinium (N-MP) have different effects on gastric
acid secretion in healthy volunteers. The intragastric pH after administration of bicarbonate
with/without 200 mL of a coffee beverage prepared from a market blend or dark roast blend
was analyzed in nine healthy volunteers. Coffee beverages were analyzed for their contents of
C5HT, N-MP, trigonelline, CGAs, and caffeine using HPLC-DAD and HPLC-MS/MS. Chem-
ical analysis revealed higher concentrations of N-MP for the dark brown blend (87 mg/L)
compared to the market blend coffee (29 mg/L), whereas concentrations of C5HT (0.012 versus
0.343 mg/L), CGAs (323 versus 1126 mg/L), and trigonelline (119 versus 343 mg/L) were lower,
and caffeine concentrations were similar (607 versus 674 mg/mL). Gastric acid secretion was
less effectively stimulated after administration of the dark roast blend coffee compared to the
market blend. Future studies are warranted to verify whether a high ratio of N-MP to C5HT
and CGAs is beneﬁcial for reducing coffee-associated gastric acid secretion.

administration of a coffee beverage containing high amounts
of C5HTs induced ulcer formation, whereas no ulcers were
detected in rats that received a steam-treated, C5HTs-depleted
coffee beverage [3]. The pivotal role of C5HTs in stimulating
gastric acid secretion was conﬁrmed in previous studies of
our own group, using, ﬁrst, an activity-guided approach [8,9]
and, second, a multiparametric approach [10] in human gas-
tric tumor cells (HGT-1 cells). Treatment of HGT-1 cells
with C5HTs in coffee representative concentrations not only
induced proton secretion, but also induced the expression
of prosecretory genes, for example, those encoding for the
H+,K+-ATPase alpha-subunit or the acetylcholine receptor
CHRM3, and prosecretory EGFr signaling pathways. Beyond
C5HTs, caffeine also effectively stimulated mechanisms of
gastric acid secretion, whereas N-methylpyridinium (N-MP)
inhibited not only proton secretion but also prosecretory path-
ways in HGT-1 cells [10]. N-MP is formed from trigonelline
upon roasting of coffee beans [11, 12], and has been shown
to have chemopreventive and antioxidative activities in vitro
and in vivo [13–15], and to downregulate the expression of the
gastrin-receptor, which is involved in the molecular mecha-
nism inducing gastric acid secretion in parietal cells [16].
However, previous studies demonstrated that N-MP most
effectively decreased proton secretion in HGT-1 cells when
applied in biomimetic mixtures of a coffee beverage, sug-
gesting that not a single constituent rather than the relative
proportion of coffee constituents determines the beverage’s
potential to stimulate gastric acid secretion [10]. This hypothe-
sis is supported by ﬁndings from Weiß et al. [17] who reported
that bean processing, affecting the concentrations of a large
variety of coffee constituents, had a more pronounced im-
pact than individual compounds discriminated by applying a
principal component analysis. However, mechanistic studies
from our group indicate that the quantitative ratio of N-MP
to C5HTs and CGAs in a coffee beverage might play a crucial
role for its acid stimulatory potential [10,18]. To prove this hy-
pothesis, we here performed a randomized, double-blinded
intervention in nine healthy volunteers (two males, seven fe-
males, mean age 25 ± 2 years, habitual coffee consumption
of 2–3 cups of regular coffee per day, no Helicobacter pylori
infection) who were administered a bolus of 200 mL of a cof-
fee beverage prepared either from a market blend of equal
amounts of ﬁve of the brand leading roasts in Germany (all
Arabica blends), resulting in a medium brown roast color,
or 200 mL of a dark brown blend prepared from dewaxed
Arabica coffee beans, yielding a dark roast coffee product.
Whereas caffeine concentrations analyzed (cf. Materials and
methods in Supporting Information) in the market blend
(674 mg/L) and the dark brown blend (607 mg/L) were compa-
rable, the dark brown blend contained higher concentrations
of N-MP (87 versus 29 mg/L), but lower amounts of C5HTs
(0.012 versus 0.343 mg/L), CGAs (323 versus 1126 mg/L),
and trigonelline (119 versus 343 mg/L). With these compo-
sitional differences, we hypothesized the dark brown blend
beverage to be less effective in stimulating acid secretion in
nine healthy volunteers than the market blend beverage.
Gastric acid secretion in the study subjects was analyzed
by means of the telemetric Heidelberg Detection System, as
described previously [19]. Brieﬂy, the system consists of a
pH-sensitive capsule (called Heidelberg capsule) that, after
being swallowed, sends a signal to a recorder connected to a
computer. In order to analyze a decrease in the gastric pH
induced by acid secretion, 2 mL of a saturated sodium bi-
carbonate solution (NaHCO3) is administered. This alkaline
challenge triggers a rise in stomach pH and subsequently
leads to the secretion of gastric acid.
Each volunteer completed four interventions at four in-
dividual visits. At the ﬁrst visit, subjects were administered
2 mL NaHCO3 solely (visit 1). For testing the effect of the
samples, the subjects received, at consecutive visits, the alka-
line solution, and, 5 min later, either 200 mL of tap water as
volume control (visit 2), 200 mL of the market blend coffee
brew (visit 3), or 200 mL of the dark brown blend coffee brew
(visit 4). Initial and maximum intra-gastric pH values as well
as the change in pH per minute (pH/min) were analyzed.
Further experimental details are given in the Materials and
methods section of the Supporting Information.
No differences were detected for the intragastric pH af-
ter administration of the bicarbonate solution solely (2 mL),
and the combination of bicarbonate (2 mL) and 200 mL of
water (pHinitial 0.53 ± 0.09 versus 0.54 ± 0.13; pHmax 5.52 ±
0.40 versus 6.14 ± 0.66; pH/min 0.52 ± 0.22 versus 0.38
± 0.20). Administration of the coffee beverages in addition
to the bicarbonate solution resulted in marked differences
in gastric acid response compared to ingestion of the bicar-
bonate solution alone: Administration of the market blend
coffee increased gastric acid secretion, whereas intake of the
dark brown blend resulted in a retarded gastric acid secretion
compared to bicarbonate administration. Thus, the gastric
acid response differed between both coffee beverages, with
the market blend more effectively stimulating gastric acid se-
cretion compared to the dark brown blend. A typical gastro-
gram from one volunteer is shown in Fig. 1. Mean pH/min
values calculated from gastrograms of all study subjects after
administration of bicarbonate, market blend and dark brown
blend revealed 0.52 ± 0.22, 0.97 ± 0.36, and 0.26 ± 0.17,
respectively.
Not only the pH/min, which indicates the acid secretion
over time, but also the time needed to reach the initial pH
prior to administration of the bicarbonate solution was sig-
niﬁcantly different after administration of both coffees. For
the market blend, a shorter (p < 0.05) restoring time index of
0.58 ± 0.17 compared to the dark brown blend (0.81 ± 0.21)
was calculated (Fig. 2A). In agreement with these ﬁndings,
area under curve (AUC) values calculated from the individual
response curves over time also indicated a less stimulating
effect (p < 0.05) of the dark brown blend (AUC of 0.49 ± 0.23)
compared to the market blend (AUC 0.25 ± 0.26; Fig. 2B).
These results clearly support our hypothesis that a dark
brown blend coffee less effectively stimulates gastric acid
secretion in healthy volunteers compared to a lighter mar-
ket blend beverage. Although these results do not provide

acid secretion. From a mechanistic point of view, this hy-
pothesis is supported by previous studies in which N-MP
was identiﬁed as an antisecretory constituent [20]. More-
over, N-methylnicotinamide, another pyridine derivative, was
shown to exhibit an anti-ulcerogenic potential [21]. In con-
trast, high concentrations of C5HTs may increase gastric
acid secretion, as revealed by in vitro data that indicate the
chain length of the corresponding fatty acid linked to the 5-
hydroxytryptamide being more important for the structure
speciﬁc effect than the degree of saturation [9]. Although
structure-speciﬁc effect for C5HTs in vivo are lacking, Fehlau
and Netter [3] clearly demonstrated that a coffee beverage de-
pleted in C5HTs was signiﬁcantly less potent in inducing
gastric lesions in rats compared to a corresponding, non-
C5HT depleted coffee. Since the stomach-irritating potential
of C5HTs has been widely recognized, several technological
procedures have been developed to reduce C5HTs. For exam-
ple, Kurzhals et al. [22] developed a solvent assisted extraction
of raw coffee beans, yielding a reduction of C5HTs of about
65%. Alternatively, mechanical dewaxing of raw coffee beans
is used to eliminate C5HTs [23]. Furthermore, roasting con-
ditions applied to the coffee beans affect the degradation of
C5HTs, for example, convection roasting leads to a higher
reduction of C5HTs compared to microwave treatment [24].
Intense roasting is the main determinant of N-MP formation
in the coffee bean [11]. Optimization of the roasting condi-
tions, taking into account the sensory proﬁle of the resulting
