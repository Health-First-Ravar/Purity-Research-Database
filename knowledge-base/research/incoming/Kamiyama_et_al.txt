Role of Degradation Products of Chlorogenic Acid in the Antioxidant
Activity of Roasted Coﬀee
Masumi Kamiyama,† Joon-Kwan Moon,‡ Hae Won Jang,§ and Takayuki Shibamoto*
Department of Environmental Toxicology, University of California, Davis, California 95616, United States
ABSTRACT: Antioxidant activities of brewed coﬀees prepared from six commercial brands ranged from 63.13 ± 1.01 to
96.80 ± 1.68% at the highest levels tested. Generally, the degree of antioxidant activity of the brewed coﬀee was inversely
proportional to the total chlorogenic acid concentration. A sample obtained from the major chlorogenic acid, 5-caﬀeoylquinic
acid (5-CQA), heated at 250 °C exhibited potent antioxidant activity (79.12 ± 2.49%) at the level of 10 μg/mL, whereas
unheated 5-CQA showed only moderate antioxidant activity (44.41 ± 0.27%) at the level of 100 μg/mL. Heat produced
relatively high levels of pyrocatechol (2,809.3 μg/g) and 2-methoxy-4-vinylphenol (46.4 μg/g) from 5-CQA, and their
antioxidant activity levels were 76.57 ± 3.00 and 98.63 ± 0.01%, respectively. The results of the present study suggest that
roasting degrades chlorogenic acids to form potent antioxidants and thus plays an important role in the preparation of
high-antioxidant low-acid coﬀee.
KEYWORDS: antioxidant activity, chlorogenic acid, coﬀee roasting, decomposition products
■INTRODUCTION
There have been numerous reports on the health beneﬁts and
risks of coﬀee drinking.1 Until the late 1990s, the risks of coﬀee
drinking had generally received more attention. However, after
the antioxidant activity of brewed coﬀee was reported in 1997,2
attention turned to the beneﬁcial eﬀects of coﬀee drinking, not
only among coﬀee drinkers but also among food scientists.
In fact, a recent review on coﬀee and health indicates that today
there is a greater emphasis on coﬀee and its health beneﬁts
than on its risks.3 One epidemiological study also indicates
that coﬀee drinking provides a chemopreventive eﬀect.4 Various
other studies conducted in recent years have also reported on
the health beneﬁts of coﬀee drinking, including antidiabetic5
and anticarcinogenic eﬀects.6 It has been proposed that these
beneﬁcial eﬀects attenuated by coﬀee are strongly associated
with the presence of polyphenols, in particular chlorogenic
acids (CGAs), which possess strong antioxidant activity.7
Foodborne antioxidants, such as chlorogenic acids, are extremely
important because they are well-known to prevent diseases
caused by oxidative damage.8,9
Although there have been many reports on the impact of
coﬀee drinking on human health,3,10 details of the functional
compounds are not yet completely understood. Among the
functional components present in foods and plants, antiox-
idants, in particular polyphenols, have been recognized to have
beneﬁcial eﬀects on human health.11 Moreover, many studies
have demonstrated that coﬀee is one of the most important
sources of polyphenols, in particular CQAs, among foods and
beverages.12
There are about 71 species of CQAs found in coﬀee beans,
fruits, and vegetables.13 Among the 8 CQAs present in green
coﬀee beans, the concentration of 5-caﬀeoylquinic acid (5-CQA)
is the highest (40−50 mg/g).14 One study demonstrated that
5-CQA exhibited only slight pro-oxidant activity at relatively high
levels (100 and 200 μg/mL), whereas it exhibited moderate
antioxidant activity at lower levels (5 μg/mL). Roasted coﬀee
beans reportedly exhibited higher antioxidant activities than non-
roasted green beans,15 even though the CQA content decreased
with roasting.14 These results suggest that, in the roasting
process, CQA produces some potent antioxidants.
In the present study, identiﬁcation of the chemicals formed
from 5-CQA in coﬀee beans under simulated roasting
conditions was performed by gas chromatography and gas
chromatography−mass spectrometry. The levels of antioxidant
activity of the chemicals identiﬁed were also measured to
investigate the relationship between antioxidant activity and
roasting conditions in coﬀee.
■MATERIALS AND METHODS
Chemicals and Materials. CQAs, including 5-CQA, were brought
from Cayman Chemical Co. (Ann Arbor, MI, USA). Pyrocatechol,
4-ethylcatechol, 4-vinylcatechol, phenylethyl alcohol, benzyl alcohol,
4-methoxybenzyl alcohol, 2-phenyl-2-propanol, 2,4-di-tert-butylphenol,
phenol, 4-vinylphenol, 2-methyoxy-4-vinylphenol, and γ-butyrolactone
were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO, USA). All solvents
were obtained from reliable commercial sources. Many volatile
chemicals were provided by Takata Koryo Co., Ltd. (Hyogo, Japan)
as a gift.
Preparation of Coﬀee Samples for Antioxidant Activity. Six
typical brands of ground, roasted coﬀee beans were bought from
a local market (brands I, II, III, IV, V, and VI). Our previous study
indicated that brand VI possessed the lowest acidity and CQA content
of the six commercial brands. Additionally, a strong correlation
between the level of chlorogenic acids and antioxidant activity in
roasted coﬀees has been reported.7 Therefore, four additional varieties
of brand VI (VIa, VIb, VIc, and VId) coﬀee were used to investigate
the role of CQAs in the antioxidant activity of brewed coﬀees.
Ground, roasted coﬀee beans (50 g) were brewed with deionized
water (600 mL) using a Mr. Coﬀee model NCX-20 coﬀee maker

Sample Preparation of Dichloromethane Extracts from
Brewed Coﬀee for Antioxidant Assays. Brewed coﬀee VIa
prepared above (500 mL) was extracted for 6 h with 150 mL of
dichloromethane using a liquid−liquid continuous extractor at room
temperature. After the extract was dried over anhydrous sodium
sulfate, it was condensed to exactly 4 mL in volume by rotary evaporator,
followed by purging with a puriﬁed nitrogen stream. The extracted
sample was stored at 5 °C until analysis for volatile chemicals.
Preparation of 5-CQA Degradation Samples for Antioxidant
Activities and Analysis. Two degradation methods were used
to prepare 5-CQA degradation samples. The entire experimental
processes are shown in Figure 1.
Degradation of 5-CQA in Aqueous Solution. An aqueous solution
containing 5-CQA (mg/mL) was reﬂuxed for 2 h in a 250 mL round-
bottomed ﬂask. The pH of the solution was adjusted to 4, 6, or 12 with
1 N sodium hydroxide solution. The pH of the original solution
was 2.39 (blank sample). After the solution was cooled to room
temperature, it was extracted for 3 h with 50 mL of dichloromethane
using a liquid−liquid continuous extractor. The extract was dried over
anhydrous sodium sulfate and then condensed to 1 mL in volume. The
extract obtained from the sample reﬂuxed at pH 12 was analyzed
by GC, and the total volatiles were calculated to be 8.5 mg.
The concentration of each chemical identiﬁed was calculated using
a previously reported equation.16 Residual aqueous solutions and
dichloromethane extracts were stored at 5 °C for analysis and
antioxidant assays.
Thermal Degradation of 5-CQA. Thermal degradation of 5-CQA
and collection of products were conducted using a previously reported
apparatus and method.17 Brieﬂy, the surfaces of 4 mm diameter glass
beads were coated with 5-CQA (1 g) in a glass tube (250 mm ×
40 mm i.d.). The sample tube was heated at 100, 200, 230, or 250 °C
for 30 min while being purged with an air stream at 500 mL/min.
The volatile degradation compounds were trapped in 200 mL of
dichloromethane in an impinger (200 mm × 40 mm i.d.) cooled with
ice-cooled brine water (−20 °C). After the dichloromethane solution
was dried over anhydrous sodium sulfate, it was condensed to 1 mL in
volume using a rotary ﬂash evaporator followed by purging with a
puriﬁed nitrogen stream. The samples were stored at 5 °C for analysis
and antioxidant assays.
5-CQA (1 g) was heated at 250 °C for 30 min using the apparatus
described above without purging with an air stream, then the heated
sample was removed with 20 mL of methanol. The methanol solution
was reduced to dryness by purging with a puriﬁed nitrogen stream.
Approximately 800 mg of viscous brown liquid was obtained and
stored at 5 °C until used for antioxidant assays and residual 5-CQA
analysis by HPLC.
Determination of Antioxidant Activities of Samples. The
antioxidant activities of the coﬀee samples were examined by two
diﬀerent assays, the DPPH assay and the MA-GC assay. Antioxidant
activity was calculated using a previously reported method.18

method.19 Brieﬂy, a mixture containing a test sample (500 μL/mL)
and 300 mM DPPH (500 μL) was incubated at room temperature
for 30 min, and then the absorbance at 517 nm was measured
using a Hewlett-Packard 8452A diode array spectrophotometer
(Hewlett-Packard Co., Palo Alto, CA, USA). Butylated hydroxytoluene

■RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
Antioxidant Activity of Commercial Coﬀees Tested by
DPPH and MA-GC Assays. The DPPH assay may be the
most commonly and widely used in food and plant components
because it is relatively simple. One of the drawbacks of this
method is that the spectrophotometric absorption is interfered
with by other components if a sample is dark in color, as in the
case of coﬀee. Therefore, it is not possible to use this assay to
examine the antioxidant activity of brewed coﬀee samples
without diluting the samples. The MA-GC assay, on the other
hand, is applicable to a dark-colored samples such as brewed
coﬀee because it monitors the amount of MA by GC.
We previously reported that the total content of CQAs
in commercial brands of coﬀee ranged from 5.26 ± 0.09 to
17.10 ± 0.34 mg/g.7 The amount of 5-CQA was the greatest of
the nine CQA isomers found, ranging from 2.13 to 7.06 mg/g
in ground roasted coﬀee beans. 5-CQA comprised nearly 40%
of the total CQAs in roasted coﬀee beans. It has also been
reported that the lower the level of CQAs, the less acidic
(higher pH) the resultant brewed coﬀees was.21 In the present
study, the pH of brewed coﬀee samples was 5.15 in brand I,
4.78 in brand II, 5.01 in brand III, 5.11 in brand IV, 5.06 in
brand V, 5.49 in brand VIa, 5.57 in brand VIb, 5.64 in brand
VIc, and 5.63 in brand VId. Therefore, 5-CQA was chosen to
investigate the possible formation of antioxidants from
degradation of chlorogenic acids in coﬀee during processing.
Figure 2 shows the results of the antioxidant assays on six
commercial coﬀee brands. Values are means ± SD (n = 3). The
antioxidant activity of BHT (standard antioxidant) was 88.05 ±
1.18% by the DPPH assay and 86.87 ± 4.38% by the MA-GC
assay at the level of 100 μg/mL, indicating that these two
antioxidant assays are valid in the present study. All brewed
coﬀee samples exhibited dose-dependent antioxidant activity
except for brand V in the MA-GC assay. The antioxidant
activity of the brewed coﬀee samples ranged from 52.19 ±
1.18% (brand III) to 90.24 ± 0.67% (brand VIc) by DPPH
assay and from 63.13 ± 1.01% (brand III) to 96.80 ± 1.68%
(brand VIa) by MA-GC assay at the highest levels tested
(1/500 dilution for the DPPH assay and 1/10 assay for
the MA-GC assay). Generally, brand VI showed the most

Degradation Studies on 5-CQA. In this experiment,
samples were prepared to simulate brewing processes and
roasting processes. Figure 3 shows the results of the antioxidant
assays on the dichloromethane extracts of the samples from
5-CQA reﬂuxed in pH 4, 6, or 12 aqueous solution. A standard
antioxidant, BHT, exhibited activities of 85.67 ± 6.58 and
93.35 ± 2.27% in DPPH and MA-GC assays, respectively,
indicating that these assays are valid in the present study. Similar
results were obtained from the two assays. The control samples
did not show any appreciable antioxidant activity in either assay,
suggesting that 5-CQA does not degrade or produce suﬃcient
volatile antioxidants at the relatively acidic condition of pH 3.4.
In fact, LC-MS analysis revealed only 8.46% of 5-CQA was
degraded by reﬂux at this pH. On the other hand, 49.92%
degradation was observed at pH 4, 63.59% degradation at pH 6,
and 99.99% degradation at pH 12. The degradation of 5-CQA is
obviously correlated with the antioxidant activity of the samples
obtained from reﬂux. A dichloromethane extract from the
5-CQA solution reﬂuxed at pH 12 exhibited potent antioxidant
activities at the level of 1000 μL/mL (90.44 ± 2.48% in
the DPPH assay and 84. 89 ± 1.35% in the MA-GC assay).
These results suggest that some CQAs degrade into potent
volatile antioxidants during the brewing process.
The extracts from 5-CQA reﬂuxed at pH 6 exhibited
moderate antioxidant activity at the level of 1000 μL/mL
(37.99 ± 1.61 and 41.84 ± 9.21% in DPPH and MA-GC assays,
respectively). Therefore, the commercial brewed coﬀees with a
pH near 6 (brand VI) may produce volatile antioxidants during
brewing and, consequently, have higher antioxidant activity than
those with a pH near 5 because neither the samples from the
control nor those with a pH of 4 showed appreciable antioxidant
activity. These results suggest that chlorogenic acids degrade
into some volatile antioxidants during brewing.
Figure 4 shows the results of the antioxidant assays on the
residual aqueous solutions from the reﬂux experiments. The
antioxidant activities of BHT at a level of 100 μg/mL were 84.01 ±
3/31 and 90.31 ± 2.61% in the DPPH and MA-GC assays,
respectively, indicating that these assays are valid in the present
study. All samples exhibited moderate (40−50%) or potent (>70%)
dose-dependent antioxidant activities. The antioxidant activities at
the highest levels (500 μL/mL) tested ranged from 54.78 ± 5.33%
(pH 6 sample) to 92.10 ± 1.10% (blank) in the DPPH assay and
from 55.70 ± 3.54% (pH 12 sample) to 78.03 ± 2.98% (blank) in
the MA-GC assay. The low antioxidant activity of the samples with
a high pH may be because the volatile antioxidants formed from
5-CQA were removed with the extraction.

Figure 5 shows the results of the antioxidant assays on the
headspace samples trapped in dichloromethane from the experi-
ments on the thermal degradation of 5-CQA. The antioxidant
activities of BHT were 85.74 ± 5.42 and 96.32 ± 3.68% in the
DPPH and MA-GC assays, respectively, indicating that these
assays were valid in the present study. The samples from 5-CQA
heated at 100, 200, and 230 °C for 30 min did not show any
appreciable antioxidant activity in either the DPPH or MA-GC
assay. On the other hand, when 5-CQA was heated at 250 °C,
obvious formation of antioxidants was observed: 7.708 ± 0.72
and 43.46 ± 2.58% at the level of 20 μL/mL in the DPPH and
MA-GC assays, respectively. The samples heated at 250 °C
exhibited clear dose-dependent activity. Our previous study
demonstrated that the amount of total CQAs decreases with
increased roasting temperature.14 In this study, we reported that
roasting green coﬀee beans at 230 °C for 12 min (light roasting)
decreased the total CQAs from 69.09 ± 2.36 to 32.09 ±
0.75 mg/g and decreased 5-CQA from 50.70 ± 1.61 to 15.11 ±
0.35 mg/g, whereas when they were heated at 250 °C for
21 min (French roasting), the total for CQAs was 0.34 ±
0.003 mg/g and that for 5-CQA was 0.16 ± 0.01 mg/g. These
results indicate that CQAs, including 5-CQA, degrade under
generally used roasting conditions, particularly in French roasts,
and consequently increase antioxidant activity.
Figure 6 shows the results of the antioxidant tests on samples
from unheated 5-CQA and from 5-CQA heated at 250 °C.
BHT exhibited 93.25 ± 1.26 and 89.33 ± 3.77% at the level of
100 μg/mL in the DPPH and MA-GC assays, respectively,
indicating that these assays are valid in this experiment. 5-CQA
showed moderate antioxidant activities at the level of 100 μg/mL
in both assays (44.41 ± 0.27%). The antioxidant activities of
5-CQA exhibited clear dose dependence and ranged from
5.18 ± 0.82% (1 μg/mL) to 50.69 ± 0.27% (20 μg/mL) in the
DPPH assay and from 1.36 ± 0.54 to 44.41 ± 0.27% in the
MA-GC assay. On the other hand, the sample from the heated
5-CQA exhibited potent antioxidant activities at 10 μg/mL
(79.12 ± 2.49% in the DPPH assay and 70.88 ± 7.97% in the
MA-GC assay). The same sample showed moderate antioxidant
activities at 1 μg/mL (31.32 ± 1.10% in the DPPH assay and
43.96 ± 5.79% in the MA-GC assay), whereas 5-CQA did
not show any appreciable antioxidant activity at the same level
(5.18 ± 0.82% in the DPPH assay and 1.36 ± 0.54% in the
MA-GC assay). These results clearly indicate that degradation
of 5-CQA produced additional antioxidants. HPLC analysis of a

Table 1 shows the compounds identiﬁed in the dichloro-
methane samples (refer to Figure 1). The amount of each
compound identiﬁed was calculated according to a previously
reported method.20 Among the 29 compounds positively
identiﬁed in sample A, acetone was found in the greatest amount
(3060.0 μg/g), followed by 4-vinylphenol (1783.3 μg/g), benzoic
acid (1269.1 μg/g), hexadecanoic acid (748 μg/g), octadecanoic
acid (183.6 μg/g), 2-methoxy-4-vinylphenol (159.0 μg/g), and
pyrocatechol (147.9 μg/g). All of these chemicals have been
found in coﬀee except 4-vinylphenol.22 It is interesting to note
that 4-vinylphenol may have antioxidant activity, but it has not
been reported in coﬀee. Only eight volatile compounds were
isolated and identiﬁed in sample B. Pyrocatechol was found in
the greatest concentration (2809.3 μg/g), followed by phenol
(1275.0 μg/g), 2,5-dimethylfuran (259.3 μg/g), 2-methylcyclo-
hexanone (107.1 μg/g), and 2-methoxy-4-vinylphenol (46.4 μg/g).
All of these chemicals were reported in coﬀee except 2-methyl-
cyclohexanone.22 The compositional diﬀerences between the
samples are due to the diﬀerent preparation methods; sample A
was prepared by direct extraction, whereas sample B was
prepared from the headspace. Therefore, direct comparison of
these samples is not appropriate. Three possible phenolic
antioxidants (2-methoxy-4-vinylphenol, 4-vinylphenol, and
pyrocatechol) were formed in relatively high concentrations in
both samples A and B. Therefore, the antioxidant activity of
these three phenolic chemicals identiﬁed in the present study
along with caﬀeic acid and 4-ethylcatechol was examined. Caﬀeic
acid and 4-ethylcatechol were examined because they were
reported as chlorogenic degradation products in our previous
study.20 Figure 7 shows the proposed formation pathways of
possible antioxidants formed from the caﬀeic acid that was
formed from 5-CQA upon hydrolysis.20
Figure 8 shows the antioxidant activities of the major
chemicals found in the samples obtained from the 5-CQA
degradation experiments. BHT showed antioxidant activities
(82.38 ± 0.49% in the DPPH assay and 85.23 ± 1.42% in the
MA-GC assay) at a level of 100 μg/mL, indicating that the
assays used in these experiments were valid. All chemicals
tested exhibited dose-dependent antioxidant activity. Among
them, 2-methoxy-4-vinylphenol showed the strongest antiox-
idant activities (98.63 ± 0.01% in the DPPH assay and
98.63 ± 2.18 in the MA-GC assay) at a level of 20 μg/mL. On
the other hand, pyrocatechol had the most potent antioxidant
activity (71.66 ± 0.01% in the DPPH assay and 76.57 ± 3.00%
in the MA-GC assay) at a level of 10 μg/mL. All 5-CQA
degradation products exhibited higher antioxidant activities
than those of 5-CQA at all levels tested.
4-Vinylphenol, which has not been reported in coﬀee but
was found in a sample from 5-CQA reﬂuxed at pH 12 in
relatively high concentration (1783.3 μg/g), exhibited
antioxidant activity comparable to that of a standard anti-
oxidant, BHT, with dose dependence. The antioxidant activity
in 4-vinylphenol was 65.9 ± 0.81% in the DPPH assay and
85.23 ± 1.42% in the MA-GC assay at the level of 20 μg/mL.
These results suggest that 4-vinylphenol forms from
chlorogenic acids during brewing under low acidic conditions.
The other chemicals formed in large quantities in this sample
acetone (3060.0 μg/mg) and benzoic acid (1269.1 μg/g)did
not show any appreciable antioxidant activity at the levels tested
(1, 10, and 20 μg/mL).

Also, 2,5-dimethylfuran (259.3 mg/g) and phenol (1275.0 mg/g)
formed from 5-CQA heated at 250 °C in relatively high levels

The present study demonstrates that chlorogenic acids present
