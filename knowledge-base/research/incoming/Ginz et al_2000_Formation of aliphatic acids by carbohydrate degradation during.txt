Formation of aliphatic acids by carbohydrate degradation during
roasting of coffee

Abstract A major fraction of the acidity generated on
coffee roasting can be attributed to formation of the
four aliphatic acids formic, acetic, glycolic and lactic.
Addition of sucrose, glucose or fructose to green coffee
beans gave, compared to untreated beans, significant
increases in the yields of the four acids on subsequent
roasting. In addition, model studies using these three
carbohydrates confirmed sucrose as the principal green
bean precursor of the acids. Arabinose, erythrose and
1,6-anhydroglucose were identified as reaction products
of sucrose thermal degradation and also subsequently
served as precursors for acid formation. Isotopic labell-
ing experiments indicated that known degradation
pathways could be used to explain the formation of the
four aliphatic acids from sucrose.

Introduction
Acids are responsible for sourness, which together with
aroma and bitterness, is a key contributor to the total
sensory impact of a coffee beverage. The degree of
acidity, and hence sourness, of the two most important
coffee varities, Coffea arabica (Arabica) and Coffea ca-
nephora var. robusta (Robusta), differ significantly.
Arabica coffee brews are more acidic (pH-range
4.85–5.15), than Robusta brews (pH range 5.25–5.40)
[1]. In general, the acids present in coffee are responsi-
ble for about 11% of the green beans weight and for
about 6% of the roasted coffee beans weight [2]. The
principal acids in green coffee beans are citric acid,
malic acid, chlorogenic acids and quinic acid [3]. During
the roasting process the former three acids decrease
while quinic acid increases due to the degradation of
chlorogenic acid. As chlorogenic and quinic acids form
lactones during roasting [4], and the other hydrolysis
products phenolic acids are degraded, chlorogenic acid
is apparently not a contributor to increased acidity on
roasting. Formic and acetic acids yields increase up to
the medium roasting degree and then begin to fall as
roasting is continued. Glycolic and lactic acids yields
continue to increase even at dark roasts [5]. Formation
of these four acids, particularly of formic and acetic
acids, is considered to be a key factor in the pH mini-
mum obtained at medium roasting levels.
The acid compositions of various coffee products
have been determined by several authors [6–9]. Howev-
er, little is known about the corresponding precursors
and formation mechanisms of aliphatic acids in coffee.
A likely precursor of acids in coffee is the carbohydrate
fraction, of which the disaccharide sucrose, present at
levels between 3% and 8% in green beans, is the most
reactive component. Several publications have investi-
gated the formation of acids from various carbohy-
drates in model systems. Olsson et al. [10] and Beck
[11] reported on the acid formation from carbohydrate
precursors under non-enzymatic browning. Nakabaya-
shi [12] carried out model experiments with sucrose un-
der roasting conditions. Formic, acetic and lactic acids
were identified in the roasting mixture. Barlianto and
Maier [13] examined the thermal degradation of fruc-
tose, maltose and inulin in model reactions. Formic,
acetic, lactic and glycolic acids were identified amongst
several other acids produced.
The aim of this study was to define a correlation be-
tween the major aliphatic acids in roast coffee and their
corresponding precursors. Isotopic labelling experi-
ments were used to provide insight into the mechanism
of acid formation.

Materials and methods
Coffee. The coffee used in this study was an Indonesian Robus-
ta.
Preparation of carbohydrate-treated coffee beans. Carbohydrate
(30 g) was dissolved in water (112 ml, HPLC grade). Green coffee
(250 g) was added and stirred at 80 7C for 60 min, by which time
the solution was absorbed by the beans. Finally, the beans were
dried to their initial water content (ca. 10%) in a forced air oven
at 35 7C for 18 h.
Roasting. The roaster was a Henzler-Quick sample roasting ap-
paratus. Roasting time was 3 min. Five different roasting temper-
atures were applied: 150 7C, 200 7C, 220 7C, 240 7C and 280 7C.
pH-value. pH-value was determined with a Mettler Titrator
DL70. Of each filtered extract (2.0 g coffee suspended in 100 ml
of water), 50 ml was used for analysis.
Sample preparation for capillary electrophoresis and ion exclusion
chromatography.
Roasted coffee beans (2 g) were extracted
(100 ml of water) in an ultrasonic bath (40 7C, 10 min). All sam-
ples were passed through an RP-18 cartridge and a 0.45 mm mem-
brane filter.
Capillary electrophoresis of aliphatic acids. The method of Weers
et al. [5] was used. The instrument was a Hewlett Packard 3DCE
with a HP fused-silica capillary and an extended light path (72 cm,
25 mm id) at 30 7C.
Ion exclusion chromatography of carbohydrates. Samples were
analysed with a Dionex ion chromatograph [14] equipped with an
electrochemical detector. The column was a Dionex Carbopak
PA1 (4!250 mm) combined with a pre-column.
Gaschromatographic detection of 1,6-anhydroglucose. A Hewlett
Packard-5890 series II gaschromatograph with a J&W Scientific
DB5 (60 m, 0.32 mm id, 0.25 mm df) was used. The carrier gas was
helium, and detection was by FID.
Model roasting. The roasting experiments were carried out in a
converted GC oven. The model substance (0.5 g) was mixed in a
double neck flask with 5 g of sea sand. The flask was then heated
in the oven (240 7C, 15 min). The system was constructed so as to
trap all volatile acids quantitatively.
Spectroscopic analysis.
13C-NMR-analyses were done with a Joel
EX400 at 100 and 41 MHz using TMS (tetramethyl silane) as in-
ternal standard. All samples were measured in D2O.
Results and discussion
Coffee roasting experiments
In order to study the contribution of low molecular
weight carbohydrate to acid generation on roasting in
coffee, green beans were treated with sucrose, glucose
and fructose using the procedure described in Materials
and methods. The sugar content after addition was
about 3.5 times higher than the original content in the
beans. One sample was treated only with water to mon-
itor the influence of the addition procedure. The sam-
ples were subsequently roasted at 150 7C, 200 7C,
220 7C, 240 7C and 280 7C for 3 min. Subsequent inspec-
tion of the beans revealed a uniform brown colour

throughout which indicated that penetration of the ad-
ded sugar was uniform. pH-values for extracts of the
five roast series are given in Fig. 1. Both the control
and the water treated Robusta coffees showed a pH
drop from 6.1 at 150 7C to 5.7 at 240 7C, followed by an
increase again to 6.3 at 280 7C. The pH decrease was
significantly stronger in the carbohydrate treated cof-
fees with a minimum at 220 7C. The results indicated
that sugar contributes to acid formation in coffee.
The aliphatic acids in the series of roasted coffees
were analysed by capillary electrophoresis. The acid
profiles of a roasted control sample and a sucrose
treated coffee are given in Fig. 2a,b. The contents of the
principal acids (formic, acetic, lactic and glycolic) were
significantly higher in the treated beans than in those of
the control coffee. Yields of formic and acetic acids
were more than 2 times higher than in the control cof-
fee. Glycolic acid showed an even bigger yield increase
while lactic acid did not show a significant yield change.
Acid generation from glucose- and fructose treated cof-
fees was higher than those of the corresponding sucrose
samples at the lower temperatures, but differences
were not significant at 280 7C (data not shown). The in-
itial lower reactivity of sucrose can be attributed to its
glycoside linkage which must be cleaved in order to
yield the more reactive reducing sugars.
The sugar profiles were analysed for all of the roast
series by means of ion exclusion chromatography. In
each series, sucrose contents fell rapidly, and it was not
detectable in coffee roasted at 280 7C (data not shown).
In contrast, fructose and glucose showed different kine-
tics (Fig. 3). In fructose treated coffee, glucose contents
increased up to 200 7C (Fig. 3a) whilst in glucose-
treated coffee, fructose exhibited the same behaviour
(Fig. 3b). The interconversion of the two monosacchar-
ides thus seems to be a factor in their degradation
chemistry. In the sucrose added coffee both fructose
and glucose were formed indicating that these sugars
are primary reaction products in the sucrose break-

Roasting of model compounds
Sugars
The described coffee roasting experiments showed a
correlation between the green coffee carbohydrate con-
tent and the amount of aliphatic acids which are
formed during roasting. In order to investigate the for-
mation mechanisms of formic, acetic, glycolic and lactic
acids more closely, model experiments with the carbo-
hydrates fructose, glucose and sucrose were performed.
For this purpose, the sugars were mixed with sea sand
(1:10) and heated in the dry form at 240 7C for 15 min.
The closed model roasting apparatus used allowed
quantitative trapping of even the volatile formic and

Sugar/amino acid mixtures
Although the lower formic acid yields in coffee can be
attributed to the volatility of this acid, other factors
may also be involved. For example, there may be in-
volvement of reaction partners such as amino acids in
coffee compared to the model system. In order to in-
vestigate this, sucrose and glycine in different ratios
were roasted together in model reactions. In all su-
crose/glycine model roastings the usual four aliphatic
acids were determined (Fig. 6). Glycine levels showed a
major influence by reducing the yields of formic acid

indicating that reactants such as amino acids and pro-
teins can affect aliphatic acid yields.
Arabinose
Since arabinose has been identified as an intermediate
breakdown product of fructose, glucose and sucrose,
model roastings were also conducted with this sugar in
order to ascertain whether arabinose itself forms ali-
phatic acids. Again formic, acetic, glycolic and lactic
acids were found in the reaction mixture. Analysis of
the carbohydrate composition of this mixture indicated
the presence of erythrose in significant amounts. Model
roasts of erythrose also gave, in turn, the four aliphatic
acids (data not shown).
Arabinogalactan
Arabinogalactan, one of the major polysaccharides in
green coffee [15], also undergoes some degradation
during roasting. Arabinose is the terminal unit on most
of the side chains and due to its furanoside structure,
labile to hydrolysis. A model roast using larch wood
arabinogalactan (structurally similar to coffee arabino-
galactan) gave the four aliphatic acids but in contrast to
the model reactions of fructose, glucose and sucrose,
the ratio of the acetic acid yield to that of the other
aliphatic acids was significantly higher (data not
shown).

1,6-Anhydroglucose is a known thermal dehydration
product from glucosidically-bound glucose compounds
[16]. By means of gas chromatography 1,6-anhydroglu-
cose was indeed identified not only in the model reac-

Inert atmosphere
Model reactions with sucrose under an argon atmo-
sphere gave similar aliphatic acid profiles to those ob-
tained under atmospheric conditions (data not shown).
It was concluded that oxygen does not play a measura-
ble role in the mechanism of aliphatic acids formation
from low molecular weight carbohydrate.
13C-labelled glucoses
In order to gain insight into the sugar breakdown path-
ways leading to the formation of the aliphatic acids,
model reactions were performed using 13C1–, 13C2– and
13C3–labelled glucose. As in the previous model reac-
tions, the labelled carbohydrates were mixed with sea
sand (1:10) and heated in the dry form at 240 7C for
15 min. 13C-NMR spectra of the resulting reaction mix-
tures were recorded. 13C-labelling increased the natural
abundance of 13C-atoms, and thus signal intensity by a
factor of 90, giving the opportunity to identify at which
position the labelled 13C-atom had been incorporated
into the acid molecule. For signal assignment, 13C-
NMR-data of the standard aliphatic acids were taken.
Table 1 shows which acids labelled at specific positions
were identified for the model reactions with the three
13C-labelled glucoses.
13C1–labelled glucose yielded formic acid from the
C1–atom of the carbohydrate (dp167.2 ppm NMR
chemical shift). A signal at dp59.8 ppm was assigned to
the CH2OH-group of glycolic acid (C2), and the signal
at dp20.5 ppm to the methyl group of acetic acid (C2).
Signals for the carboxyl groups of acetic and glycolic
acids were not detected, thus indicating that they were
not formed from C1 of glucose. A peak at 19.3 ppm cor-
responded to the CH3–group of lactic acid (C3).
Model roasting of 13C2–labelled glucose gave the
same four signals at dp167.2, 59.8, 20.5 and 19.3 ppm
NMR chemical shift as above, but some additional sig-
nals were also found (Table 1). Signals at dp176.9 and
176.6 ppm were assigned to the carboxyl groups of ace-
tic (C1) and glycolic (C1) acids, respectively. The signal
at 66.7 ppm corresponded to the CHOH-group of lactic
acid (C2).
In the model roasting of 13C3–labelled glucose, sig-
nals for all carbon atoms of the four aliphatic acids
were observed (Table 1).
Mechanistic Aspects of Acid Formation
The above model reactions confirmed that sucrose is
the major source of the four principal aliphatic acids
(formic, acetic, glycolic and lactic) produced on coffee
roasting. The higher reactivity of glucose and fructose
and their presence in the model reactions with sucrose
suggests that scission of sucrose, either hydrolytic to
give glucose and fructose or without the addition of wa-
ter to give a reducing sugar plus an anhydrous sugar,
occurs. Both the reducing sugar and the anhydrous su-
gar can degrade further to give the four aliphatic
acids.
Based on the results of the model reactions of 13C-
labelled glucose, a scheme for the fragmentation pat-
tern of the sugar to give the four aliphatic acids can be
derived (Fig. 7). Glucose and fructose can interconvert
via the 1,2-endiol as intermediate (Lobry-de-Bruyn-
van-Eckenstein-rearrangement). Fructose can also
rearrange to give the 2,3-endiol. In addition, thermal
dehydration of these sugars to give 1-deoxyglucosone
and 3-deoxyglucosone is well known [17, 18]. These
four intermediate compounds can be regarded as acid
precursors. The endiol compounds preferentially cleave
at the C-C double bond, while scission of the deoxyglu-
cosones is principally at the a-dicarbonyl group. For ex-
ample, 3-deoxyglucosone can yield formic acid and 2-
deoxyarabinose by cleavage of the C1–C2 bond. By this
mechanism only one isotopomer of each of the four
acids can be formed: C1–labelled formic acid, C2–la-
belled acetic and glycolic acids and C3-labelled lactic
acid. This correlates to the 13C-NMR results in Ta-
ble 1.
If the same mechanistic principles are applied to
13C2–labelled glucose, the following isotopomers would
be expected: C1–labelled acetic and glycolic acids and
C2–labelled lactic acid, but not C1–labelled formic acid.

Indeed, the expected peaks were identified, but several
non-expected peaks as well. All of the signals of the
isotopomers found in the 13C1–glucose degradation (in-
cluding C1–labelled formic acid) were found, which
suggests the involvement of arabinose. It was shown
that arabinose yields from roasting of coffee were en-
hanced by pretreatment with the sugars. The sugars
also yielded arabinose directly in the model reactions.
The pentose could be formed by cleavage of the C1–C2
bond of the 1,2-endiol to yield C1–labelled arabinose
with the additional formation of formaldehyde. An ad-
ditional source of arabinose is coffee arabinogalactan
(see above). Arabinose can subsequently degrade to
yield the acids via the same pathways illustrated for the
hexoses (Fig. 7).
Finally, the tetrose erythrose can be formed in an
analogous manner from the the 1,2-endiol of arabinose
or by degradation of 1-deoxyglucosone. Erythrose can
degrade via the pathways depicted in Fig. 7 and this is
consistent with the labelling pattern of the acids ob-
tained from the thermal treatment of 13C3–glucose.
To summarise, using model roasting reactions with
sugar treated coffee beans together with thermal treat-
ments of model compounds, it was shown that the prin-
cipal acids (formic, acetic, glycolic and lactic) generated
on roasting coffee are formed from carbohydrates. The

410
principal precursor of these acids in green coffee beans
is sucrose although the polysaccharide fraction, particu-
larly arabinogalactan, also makes a contribution. The
acids are readily formed by thermal treatment of the
pure carbohydrates which suggests that secondary reac-
tions with other components of the green bean do not
play a key role in acid formation on roasting.
