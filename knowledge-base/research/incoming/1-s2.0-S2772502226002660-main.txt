A B S T R A C T
This study evaluated how the type of milk matrix (whole milk) and plant-based matrices (soy, almond, and 
coconut), together with their proportions with coffee (70:30, 50:50, and 30:70), influence the physicochemical, 
colorimetric, rheological, bioactive, and sensory properties of the beverages. As the amount of matrix increased, 
pH rose, titratable acidity decreased, and soluble solids (◦Brix) increased, especially in whole milk, which 
reached 10.13 ◦Brix due to its higher lactose and total solids content. The values of L, C, h◦, and WI also increased 
with greater matrix content; whole milk produced beverages with higher brightness and whiteness, whereas 
almond and coconut matrices produced darker beverages with more stable color. The plant-based matrices, 
especially almond and soy, showed antioxidant capacity values ranging from 13.77 to 14.22 mg TE/g and total 
phenolic content values reaching a maximum of 23.17 mg GAE/g in the 50:50 mixture with almond milk, 
reflecting matrix- and ratio-dependent responses. UHPLC analysis showed that chlorogenic acid (5-CQA) and 
caffeine (CAF) decreased as the proportion of milk matrix increased, while caffeic acid (CFA) was more 
frequently detected in formulations with higher matrix content. Regarding rheology, soy beverages exhibited the 
highest viscosities (up to 9.37 mPa⋅s) and a more pronounced pseudoplastic behavior as matrix concentration 
increased. Sensory acceptance was evaluated using a 9-point hedonic test with 60 untrained consumers. Sig­
nificant differences in sensory scores were observed among formulations (p < 0.05). Whole milk and almond 
formulations showed higher numerical scores when coffee was predominant (70:30) or balanced (50:50). In 
contrast, soy and coconut showed higher numerical scores in mixtures with higher matrix content (30:70), with 
coconut 30:70 reaching the highest overall acceptance 7.42. Pearson correlation analysis revealed that overall 
acceptance was primarily associated with flavor, while ◦Brix, titratable acidity, viscosity, and key bioactive 
compounds showed matrix-dependent associations with sensory perception. Overall, plant-based matrices eli­
cited bioactive and sensory responses comparable to those of whole milk in specific formulations under the 
experimental conditions evaluated.

1. Introduction
Coffee is universally recognized as one of the most appreciated and 
widely consumed beverages worldwide, attributed to both its functional 
health benefits and its unique sensory profile (Barrea et al., 2023; 
Chiang et al., 2018). Its intense flavor and aroma give it a special 
identity, acknowledged by many cultures since ancient times (Nú˜nez 
et al., 2024). However, the final acceptance of coffee depends on a 
complex interaction of factors, including its intrinsic chemical compo­
sition, plant variety, geographical origin, and roasting level. The 
convergence of these factors is decisive in shaping its sensory profile 
and, consequently, in consumer perception (Aswathi et al., 2022; 
Cordoba et al., 2020).
From the perspective of food technology and formulation, coffee 
consumption is also influenced by practices aimed at modifying its 
sensory profile by adding ingredients that optimize flavor (Lee et al., 
2025). The most widespread strategy is the incorporation of milk, which 
reduces the inherent bitterness and acidity of coffee while providing 
superior texture and creaminess (Rashidinejad et al., 2022). However, 
the frequent use of animal-derived milk has a significant environmental 
impact, evidenced by increasing livestock demand, high water and land 
consumption, and its contribution to emissions that drive global 
warming. Additionally, there are health concerns associated with its 
consumption, including cholesterol, lactose intolerance, and milk pro­
tein allergies (Craig et al., 2023; Haas et al., 2019).
From a cultural and consumption perspective, coffee is rarely 
consumed as a standalone beverage in many regions. However, it is 
instead commonly prepared as milk-based formulations such as lattes, 
cappuccinos, and other espresso-based drinks (Fitri et al., 2024). These 
preparations have historically shaped consumer expectations regarding 
bitterness balance, creaminess, mouthfeel, and overall sensory har­
mony, positioning milk addition as an integral component of coffee

The growing consumption of plant-based milk alternatives (PMA) 
represents a significant trend in food innovation (Sethi et al., 2016). This 
is driven by consumer demand for options perceived as healthier and 
more sustainable, characterized by the absence of lactose, hormones, 
and antibiotics (Craig et al., 2023). These alternatives have gained 
notable popularity within specialty coffee culture, where beverages such 
as lattes or cappuccinos prepared with PMA made from almond, soy, oat, 
or coconut offer a sensory experience similar to that of traditional dairy 
products (Fadardi et al., 2024). This trend underscores the need to un­
derstand better how plant-based matrices interact with coffee systems 
beyond their perceived sustainability benefits.
Although quantitative studies on the use of PMA in coffee are still 
limited, recent evidence highlights their growing market acceptance. In 
this regard, Gorman et al. (2021) documented that 50 % of the evaluated 
consumers (n = 116) regularly used PMA. Similarly, Zakidou et al. 
(2022) reported that 92 % of their panelists were willing to consume 
cappuccinos prepared with these alternatives. In this context, Chung 
et al. (2022) identified that flavor and texture are key factors that define 
consumer acceptance. Consequently, these findings demonstrate that 
PMAs have a growing market and can be successfully integrated into 
coffee beverages, provided they maintain appealing sensory character­
istics. However, these studies primarily emphasize consumer perception 
and rarely link sensory outcomes with underlying physicochemical or 
functional mechanisms.
Plant-based milk alternatives (PMA) made from soy, almond, and 
coconut present significantly different nutritional and bioactive profiles. 
Soy stands out for its contribution of high-biological-value proteins and 
isoflavones, which are attributed a potential anticancer effect; more­
over, its formulation helps reduce the bitterness and astringency of 
coffee (Qie et al., 2022). Coconut, on the other hand, enriches beverages 
with lipids, vitamins, and minerals that improve beverage texture 
(Ariyaprakai et al., 2015; Hsieh et al., 2024). In contrast, almonds are 
notable for their vitamin E, unsaturated fatty acids, and protein content 
(Chen et al., 2006). Additionally, these plant matrices exhibit antioxi­
dant activity due to the presence of phenolic compounds, flavonoids, 
and vitamins A, C, and E (Wansutha et al., 2018; Yi˘git, 2019). Overall, 
incorporating PMA not only represents a viable alternative to conven­
tional milk but also provides nutritional and functional benefits to 
beverages (Jayachandran et al., 2019; Tonolo et al., 2019). Collectively, 
these compositional differences suggest that each plant-based matrix 
may differentially modulate coffee bioactive compounds and textural 
attributes depending on its molecular composition.
Given coffee's central role as the primary matrix in milk-based bev­
erages, understanding how different milk matrices interact with coffee 
systems is essential to preserving both functional quality and the sensory 
identity traditionally associated with coffee consumption.
Despite growing interest in plant-based milk alternatives for coffee 
beverage formulations, systematic quantitative studies comparing their 
effects on bioactive compound retention, antioxidant capacity, and 
sensory acceptance are scarce. Most available studies address these as­
pects in isolation, focusing either on nutritional composition, antioxi­
dant activity, or sensory perception. Furthermore, the optimal 
proportions of coffee and PMA (or whole milk) that maximize the bev­
erage's functional properties without compromising consumer accep­
tance have not been established across different plant matrices. This 
information gap represents an obstacle to the development of scientifi­
cally grounded formulations aimed at specific market segments.
Therefore, the main objective of this study was to compare the effects 
of dairy and plant-based milk addition on the physicochemical, func­
tional, and sensory properties of a coffee-based beverage. This inte­
grated approach seeks to clarify how matrix composition and 
formulation ratio influence coffee beverage quality, thereby contrib­
uting to the systematic design of milk-based coffee products.

2.2. Coffee procurement and processing
The experimental material consisted of green coffee beans (Coffea 
arabica L.), Casiopea variety, collected in the annex of Pisuquia, prov­
ince of Luya, Amazonas, Peru. The beans were certified with a score 
above 80 points under the Specialty Coffee Association (SCA) evaluation 
protocol (2022).
The initial processing of the coffee beans included hulling, classifi­
cation, and sieving using a No 15 mesh (1.19 mm opening) to ensure 
uniform particle size within the batch. The roasting process was carried 
out using an electric induction roaster (PROBAT SR 0116, Germany), 
with parameters adjusted to achieve a medium-dark roast (#75 on the 
AGTRON/SCAA scale). Immediately after roasting, the beans were 
cooled with forced air until they reached a temperature of 25 ◦C, in order 
to stop non-enzymatic browning reactions. Finally, the material was 
ground to obtain a fine granulometry (particles ≤500 μm), suitable for 
espresso preparation.
2.3. Extraction of the coffee beverage by the espresso method
Extractions were carried out using a Ruby Pro espresso machine 
(Quality Espresso, Spain). For each preparation, a coffee-to-beverage 
ratio of 1:3 (w/v) was used, with 10 g of ground coffee per extraction. 
The process was performed with water at 95 ◦C, 9 bar, and a percolation 
time of 30 s, maintaining a flow rate of 1 mL/s (Chavez et al., 2022).
2.4. Beverage formulation
For beverage preparation, both the PMA and the whole milk were 
preheated to 75 ◦C to minimize thermal shock when mixed with the 
freshly extracted espresso at 95 ◦C. The beverages were prepared ac­
cording to the methodology of Lydia and Surya (2023), using the coffee: 
PMA or coffee: whole milk proportions. Homogenization was carried out 
in two stages: first, the mixture was manually blended with a stainless 
steel spatula, and then mechanically agitated in a BenchMaker MHS-10 
L at 3000 rpm for 30 s until a uniform and stable dispersion was 
obtained.
The coffee-to-milk ratios evaluated (30:70, 50:50, and 70:30, v/v) 
were established through preliminary trials following the protocols of 
the Laboratory of Coffee Physiology, Postharvest, and Processing of the 
UNTRM, representing low, intermediate, and high levels of milk incor­
poration, respectively.
The formulation design followed a completely randomized two- 
factor experimental design, with milk matrix type and coffee-to-milk 
ratio as factors, each evaluated at three treatment levels, as summa­
rized in Table 2.
2.5. Characterization of the coffee beverage
2.5.1. Physicochemical analyses
To ensure the validity and traceability of the measurements, vali­
dated protocols were followed, and calibrated instruments were used. 
Titratable acidity (total acidity) was determined according to the AOAC 
method (“Books in Brief,”, 1997) and expressed as mL of NaOH 
consumed per 100 mL of sample. pH was measured using a pH meter 
(model PH1300, LAQUA), and soluble solids content (◦Brix) was 
measured using a digital refractometer (model MA871, Milwaukee).

2.5.3. Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR)
The prepared formulations were freeze-dried for 96 h at 0.003 mbar 
and −84 ◦C using a FreeZone 4.5 L lyophilizer (Labconco, USA). They 
were then pulverized in a disc mill to homogenize particle size. Fourier 
Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FTIR) analysis was conducted by 
weighing 1.0 ± 0.01 g of the freeze-dried sample. Spectra were acquired 
using a Nicolet Magna 550 spectrophotometer (USA) over the spectral 
range of 4000 to 400 cm⁻¹ at room temperature (25 ◦C), with a resolu­
tion of 8 cm⁻¹ and eight scans (Shafiei et al., 2024).
2.5.4. Antioxidant activity by the DPPH method
Antioxidant activity was determined using the spectrophotometric 
method based on the reduction of the 2,2-diphenyl-1-picrylhydrazyl 
(DPPH) radical, following the methodology of Lapiz-Culqui et al. 
(2024) with some modifications. In an amber flask, 3.9 mg of DPPH

adjusted to 0.660 ± 0.02 at 517 nm, then kept in the dark until use. To 
20 µL of the extract, 125 µL of DPPH (198 µM) were added, and the 
mixture was incubated for 30 min in the dark at room temperature. The 
absorbance of the sample was measured at 517 nm using a spectro­
photometer (Multiskan SkyHigh, China). Results were expressed as mg 
of Trolox equivalent/g of sample, and all analyses were performed in 
triplicate. The regression equation was y = 0.6719x + 44.181; R² = 0.99.

2.5.5. Total phenolic content (TPC)
The total phenolic content of the beverage was determined using the 
Folin–Ciocalteu method, following the procedure described by Singleton 
et al. (1999) with some modifications. A mixture was prepared by 
combining 20 μL of sample with 40 μL of 10 % Folin–Ciocalteu reagent 
(5 min) and 160 μL of 7.5 % (w/v) Na₂CO₃. The mixture was kept in 
darkness for 60 min at room temperature (18 ± 2.25 ◦C). After this 
period, absorbance was measured at 765 nm using a spectrophotometer 
(Multiskan SkyHigh, China). Quantification was performed using a 
calibration curve constructed from gallic acid equivalent (GAE) di­
lutions ranging from 0 to 200 mg L⁻¹. The equation obtained was: y =
0.005x + 0.0707; R² = 0.99. TPC was expressed as mg of gallic acid 
equivalent per g of sample (mg GAE/g). All analyses were performed in 
triplicate.
2.5.6. Quantification of the main phenols (UHPLC)
The identification and quantification of the main phenolic com­
pounds in coffee beverages formulated with different types of milk 
(plant-based and whole) were performed according to the method 
described by Balc´azar-Zumaeta et al. (2025). Analyses were performed 
using ultra-high-performance liquid chromatography (UHPLC; Agilent 
1290 Infinity II, Waldbronn, Germany) equipped with a diode-array 
detector (DAD). Filtered extracts (10 μL) were injected into a 
reversed-phase C18 column (100 mm × 4.6 mm, 2.3 μm). Los analisis se

The mobile phases consisted of (A) 2 % acetic acid in water and (B) 
acetonitrile/water/acetic acid (400:90:10, v/v/v), using a linear 
gradient elution at a flow rate of 0.75 mL min⁻¹, as previously described. 
Detection was performed at 280 nm, with the column temperature 
maintained at 40 ◦C.
Caffeic acid (CFA), 5-O-caffeoylquinic acid (5-CQA), and caffeine 
(CAF) were quantified using external calibration curves. Peak identifi­
cation was based on retention time and UV spectral matching with 
authentic standards. Under the chromatographic conditions used, the 
retention times were 13.82 min for 5-CQA, 14.88 min for caffeine, and 
16.24 min for caffeic acid.
All calibration curves showed excellent linearity (R² > 0.99). Data 
acquisition and processing were performed using ChemStation software 
(OpenLAB platform). All analyses were performed in triplicate, and re­
sults were expressed as mg of metabolite per mL of sample.
2.5.7. Determination of apparent viscosity and flow behavior
The flow properties of the beverages were determined using a 
modular compact rotational rheometer (Anton Paar, model MCR 302E, 
Austria) equipped with a double-gap concentric cylinder geometry 
(DG26.7). Samples were evaluated at 40 ◦C using a shear-rate sweep in 
controlled mode, with a linear ramp from 1 to 100 s⁻¹, 100 measurement 
points, and 2 s per point (200 s total). During the test, the instrument 
recorded the apparent viscosity as a function of the shear rate and fitted 
the data to the Herschel–Bulkley model. The apparent viscosity at 50 s⁻¹ 
and the flow behavior index (n) were treated as outcome variables and 
analyzed using RheoCompass v.1.30 software (Tang et al., 2024).
2.6. Sensory acceptance
Sensory evaluation of the coffee beverages was conducted following 
the methodology described by Diaz et al. (2025), with adaptations to the 
objectives of the present study. The evaluation was conducted in a 
controlled environment under natural lighting conditions, free of 
extraneous odors, to minimize external interference.
A total of 60 consumer panelists participated in the study. Panelists 
were undergraduate students from the Universidad Nacional Toribio 
Rodríguez de Mendoza de Amazonas (UNTRM) who reported regular 
coffee consumption habits. Most participants were familiar with coffee 
beverages prepared with milk and regularly consumed plant-based milk 
alternatives, reflecting current consumption trends within the target 
population.
Prior to the sensory evaluation, panelists received a brief explanation 
of the evaluation procedure, the sensory attributes to be assessed 
(appearance, aroma, flavor, and general acceptance), and the use of a 
structured 9-point hedonic scale, where 1 corresponded to ‘dislike 
extremely’ and 9 to ‘like extremely’ (Supplementary Material 1). No 
sensory training or calibration was performed, as the objective of the test 
was to assess consumer acceptance rather than descriptive sensory 
characteristics.
All panelists evaluated the complete set of 12 beverage formulations. 
To minimize sensory fatigue and carry-over effects, the evaluation was 
conducted over three sessions (blocks), with four samples evaluated per 
session. Samples were served in cups coded with random three-digit 
numbers and presented in randomized order within each session.
Between samples, panelists were instructed to rinse their mouths 
with water and consume unsalted crackers as palate cleansers. Sample 
volume and serving temperature were kept constant throughout the 
evaluation to ensure comparability of results. Each participant recorded 
their perceptions of appearance, aroma, flavor, and overall acceptance 
on printed evaluation forms.
All participants were informed in advance about the objectives of the 
sensory test and provided written informed consent prior to participa­
tion (Supplementary Material 2). The study protocol was reviewed and 
approved by the Institutional Research Ethics Committee of the Uni­
versidad Nacional Toribio Rodríguez de Mendoza de Amazonas 
(UNTRM) (CIEI-N◦00,022).
2.7. Data analysis
All physicochemical and chemical determinations were performed in 
triplicate (n = 3), and results are expressed as mean ± standard devia­
tion. Data were analyzed using a two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) 
to evaluate the main effects of milk type and coffee-milk mixture ratio, 
as well as their interaction.
Prior to ANOVA, the assumptions of normality and homoscedasticity 
were assessed using the Shapiro–Wilk and Levene’s tests, respectively. 
For variables that did not meet ANOVA assumptions, data were loga­
rithmically transformed prior to statistical analysis. Specifically, hue 
angle (h◦), total color difference (ΔE), and whiteness index (WI) were 
log-transformed to improve normality and homogeneity of variance. 
When significant differences were detected, mean comparisons were 
performed using Tukey's multiple-comparison test at the p < 0.05 sig­
nificance level. Regarding sensory analysis, the data were analyzed 
using the Kruskal–Wallis test. When significant effects were detected, 
multiple comparisons of medians were performed using Dunn's test. 
Statistical analyses were conducted using the Agricolae package (de 
Mendiburu, 2023) implemented in the R programming environment 
(RStudio, version 4.3.3, Boston, MA, USA). In addition, Pearson’s cor­
relation analysis was performed to evaluate the relationships between 
physicochemical parameters, bioactive compounds, and sensory attri­
butes, and the results were visualized using the ggplot2 package 
(Wickham, 2016).
3. Results and discussion
3.1. Physicochemical analyses
Table 3 presents the physicochemical and colorimetric properties of 
the beverages as a function of milk matrix type and coffee-to-milk ratio. 
The pH of the beverages showed significant variations (p < 0.05), 
influenced by both the type of matrix used and the proportion. Consis­
tent with coffee's intrinsic acidic composition, formulations with a 
higher coffee content (70:30) exhibited the lowest pH values (i.e., higher 
acidity). Conversely, increasing the proportion of the milk matrix 
(30:70) resulted in a progressive increase in the pH values of the final 
beverage. These findings are consistent with previous literature, which

reported that adding milk to coffee increased the pH from 5.1 to 6.2 
(Basheer et al., 2022).
Plant-based milk analogues (almond, soy, and coconut) also act as 
pH-buffering agents; however, the degree of buffering varies by matrix, 
with almond- and soy-based beverages commonly exhibiting higher pH 
values (Daszkiewicz et al., 2024). Consequently, increasing the pro­
portion of any milk matrix leads to a beverage with lower overall acidity.
Titratable acidity is considered a more accurate predictor of 
perceived acidity and is therefore a fundamental parameter in beverage 
formulation and consumer experience (Rao et al., 2018). In the present 
study, beverages with a higher coffee proportion (70:30) showed greater 
acidity, whereas increasing the milk proportion (30:70) led to a notable 
decrease in acidity. This response is attributable to the buffering effect of 
the proteins and minerals present in milk, which neutralize some of the 
acids in coffee; consequently, titratable acidity decreases as the pro­
portion of milk or plant-based matrix increases in the formulation 
(Hernandez et al., 2024).
The soluble solids content (◦Brix) showed significant variations 
among treatments (p < 0.05), increasing with the addition of the milk 
matrix. Whole milk showed the greatest effect, reaching a value of 10.13 
± 0.25 ◦Brix in the mixture with the lowest coffee proportion (30:70). 
This increase is due to the lactose and other solids present in whole milk, 
which not only raise ◦Brix values but also enhance mouthfeel 
(Martínez-Padilla et al., 2020; P´erez-Rodríguez et al., 2023). In contrast, 
the almond beverage presented the lowest values (1.97 ± 0.21 ◦Brix). 
This difference is due to the lower inherent sugar and solid content of 
this plant-based matrix, which explains its low ◦Brix values unless 
sweeteners or stabilizers are added to increase perceived sweetness 
(Maghsoudlou et al., 2016; Redan et al., 2024).
The colorimetric parameters of the beverages exhibited statistically 
significant variations (p < 0.05), underscoring the direct impact of the 
type and combination of milk matrix on the final appearance of the 
formulations. In general, increasing the matrix proportion (30:70) led to 
higher values of L*, C*, h◦, and WI, while a* values decreased. Whole 
milk stood out because, when added to the coffee beverage, it increased 
luminosity (L* = 55.34 ± 0.16) and whiteness index (WI = 50.08 ±
0.10). Conversely, plant-based milk alternatives—especially almond 
and coconut—showed the lowest L* and WI values at the 70:30 ratio, 
giving them a darker, more opaque appearance. Jeske et al. (2017)
demonstrated that bovine milk has a much higher whiteness index than 
plant substitutes, which may explain these results. Additionally, plant 
matrices tend to be darker (Tang et al., 2023).
In this study, the a* parameter indicated that the 50:50 mixtures 
better preserved the reddish tones of the coffee beverage, especially in 
the almond (8.30 ± 0.05) and coconut (8.13 ± 0.02) matrices. This 
suggests less optical interference between coffee pigments and the 
components of these matrices. The b* parameter increased with greater 
amounts of PMA and whole milk, reaching its highest value in almond 
30:70 (22.88 ± 0.09). Natural milk pigments influence the a* and b* 
values: riboflavin gives green-yellow tones (Lee et al., 1998), and ca­
rotenoids contribute to the yellow color (Stephenson et al., 2021).
Chromaticity (C*), which measures color saturation or intensity, was 
higher in mixtures containing almond PMA and whole milk, indicating 
greater visual stability and richer tones (Milovanovic et al., 2020). The 
hue angle (h◦) shifted toward more yellow tones with increasing milk 
matrix, with the highest values in whole milk and soy, resulting in a 
softer, more uniform appearance. This confirms that the combination of 
a* and b* through h◦provides a better description of the chromatic 
characteristics of the final product.
Finally, the total color difference (ΔE) confirmed the interaction 
between the coffee beverage and the matrices used. This parameter is 
widely applied to evaluate color changes in complex dairy systems, as it 
reflects 
variations 
in 
stability 
and 
product 
composition 
(Fernandez-Avila et al., 2017). Beverages with higher coffee content 
(70:30), particularly those with coconut and almond, showed the 
greatest color differences. In contrast, mixtures with whole milk (30:70) 
were more homogeneous, achieving a balance between the coffee's dark 
tone and the milk's clarity.

3.2. Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR)
The FTIR spectra (Fig. 1) showed characteristic bands reflecting the 
molecular composition of each system. The signals between 3286 and 
3921 cm⁻¹ correspond to O–H vibrations of water, hydroxyl groups, and 
phenolic compounds, which are characteristic of flavonoids and poly­
phenols commonly present in coffee and some plant matrices (Saragih 
et al., 2022; Wongsa et al., 2022).
The absorptions between 2922 and 2926 cm⁻¹ are attributed to 
aliphatic C–H vibrations, typical of lipids (Toledo-Alvarado et al., 2021). 
The bands at 1743 and 1744 cm⁻¹ indicate C = O vibrations of esters and 
fatty acids present in both animal milk and plant alternatives (Obeidat 
et al., 2018; Tain et al., 2021). The signals between 1019 and 1021 cm⁻¹ 
correspond to C–O–C bonds, characteristic of polysaccharides and 
structural carbohydrates (Ahmad, 2021; X. Liu et al., 2021).
The proportion of coffee and milk affected the intensity of the 
spectral bands. In the O–H region, mixtures with higher amounts of 
almond, soy, and whole milk showed greater absorption, while in the 
coconut matrix, the 50:50 mixture was the most prominent. This result 
agrees with the findings of Chen et al. (2023), who demonstrated that 
coffee polyphenols form hydrogen bonds with coconut proteins, altering 
the spectral response. Samborska et al. (2024) also confirmed that the 
O–H band (3650–3000 cm⁻¹) is sensitive to the macronutrient compo­
sition of PMA, which explains the variability observed among 
formulations.
In the C–H and C = O regions (1743–1744 cm⁻¹), mixtures with a 
higher coffee proportion showed higher spectral intensities. This is 
consistent with studies on coffee authenticity, which report that absor­
bance increases proportionally with coffee content in the mixture 
(Barrios-Rodriguez et al., 2022).
In the C–O–C region (1019–1021 cm⁻¹), the 70:30 coffee–almond 
mixture showed the highest intensity. In contrast, in the coconut 
(50:50), whole milk, and soy matrices, the highest intensities were 
observed at a 30:70 ratio. This variability reflects the relative abundance 
of functional groups and the molecular microenvironment of each sys­
tem (Ellerbrock & Gerke, 2021).
3.3. DPPH
The antioxidant capacity of the beverages varied moderately with 
milk matrix type and coffee proportion, ranging from 13.77 to 14.22 mg 
TE/g (Fig. 2). At a 50:50 ratio, beverages containing PMA showed the 
highest antioxidant activity. Soy showed the highest value (14.22 mg 
TE/g), followed by almond (14.16 mg TE/g), both marginally higher 
than whole milk (14.04 mg TE/g at 70:30) and coconut (14.04 mg TE/g 
at 50:50). These differences may be associated with the presence of 
phenolic compounds and isoflavones in soy, which coexist with coffee 
polyphenols in the beverage matrix (Baghbadorani et al., 2017; Sęczyk 
et al., 2017).
Whole milk formulations displayed relatively stable antioxidant 
values across all ratios (13.77–14.04 mg TE/g), indicating that coffee 
remains the primary contributor to antioxidant capacity. This behavior 
is consistent with reports of complexes forming between milk proteins 
and coffee polyphenols, which may partially limit the measurable 
antioxidant response without completely suppressing it (Ghafari et al., 
2017; Niseteo et al., 2012).
Overall, the results indicate that formulation balance plays a more 
relevant role than matrix type in determining antioxidant capacity. The

3.4. Total phenolic content (TPC)
Total phenolic content showed statistically significant variation 
across milk matrices and coffee: matrix ratios (Fig. 3). Contrary to the 
expectation that formulations with the highest coffee content would 
have the highest phenolic concentrations, TPC did not correlate directly 
with coffee content. This finding suggests that the matrices exert a sig­
nificant influence on the quantification and stability of phenolic com­
pounds, possibly through chemical interactions.
Blends with higher coffee proportions (70:30) and the balanced 
50:50 ratio exhibited among the highest phenolic concentrations across 
the evaluated matrices, with no statistically significant differences be­
tween them (p > 0.05). These results are consistent with those of W. Lee 
et al. (2025), who reported comparable phenolic contents and antioxi­
dant activity in coffee beverages formulated with plant-based milk and 
cow’s milk.
Almond milk formulations exhibited the highest numerical TPC 
value in the study (23.17 mg GAE/g at the 50:50 ratio), although this 
value was not statistically different from those obtained with the other 
matrices. Notably, almond-based beverages maintained relatively high 
phenolic concentrations even at the 30:70 ratio (22.67 mg GAE/g), 
which may be associated with the contribution of polyphenols from the 
plant matrix, as almond skin contains a substantial proportion of the 
fruit's phenolic compounds (La Torre et al., 2025; S¸ahin et al., 2025).
When the matrix proportion increased to 30:70, phenolic content 
generally decreased: whole milk (18.62 mg GAE/g), soy (18.81 mg 
GAE/g), and coconut (18.37 mg GAE/g), representing a reduction of 17 
to 21 % compared to the 50:50 mixtures. This is due to two reasons: the 
dilution of coffee’s phenolic compounds and the formation of complexes 
between proteins and polyphenols—particularly chlorogenic acid­
s—which reduces both the apparent phenolic content and the measur­
able antioxidant activity (LIczbi´nski & Bukowska, 2022).
3.5. Viscosity and flow behavior
Fig. 4 presents the rheological properties of the beverages, high­
lighting the influence of matrix type (dairy/plant-based) and the cof­
fee–matrix ratio. Apparent viscosity was significantly affected by both 
factors, showing matrix-dependent rheological behavior.
Whole milk exhibited the lowest viscosity across all evaluated pro­
portions (1.11–1.19 mPa⋅s), with only minor variations as milk content 
increased. In contrast, plant-based matrices showed a progressive in­
crease in viscosity with higher matrix proportions. For instance, almond- 
based beverages increased from 1.32 to 2.54 mPa⋅s and coconut-based 
beverages from 1.29 to 2.69 mPa⋅s when moving from 70:30 to 30:70 
formulations. These trends are consistent with previous studies report­
ing that viscosity increases with higher colloidal density and particle 
interactions in plant-based systems, and that this effect is influenced by 
particle size, shape, and dispersed-phase concentration (Düsenberg 
et al., 2023; Koca et al., 2018; Oh & Lee, 2024). The protein composi­
tion, lipid profile, and structural characteristics of the liquid matrix 
further modulate these rheological responses (Idrus et al., 2017; Liu 
et al., 2012).
Soy-based beverages consistently exhibited the highest viscosity at 
all ratios—3.74 mPa⋅s (70:30), 6.37 mPa⋅s (50:50), and 9.37 mPa⋅s 
(30:70)—representing an approximate 2.5-fold increase from the lowest 
to the highest matrix proportion. This pronounced effect is mainly 
attributed to the high protein content (~3.5 %) and the presence of 
soluble fibers and oligosaccharides, which enhance water binding, 
promote colloidal network formation, and increase resistance to flow, 
leading to a characteristic pseudoplastic behavior (Rana et al., 2024).
The flow behavior index (n) supported the viscosity results (Fig. 4). 
At the lowest matrix proportion (70:30), n values were close to unity 
(almond: 1.10; coconut: 1.14), indicating near-Newtonian behavior. As 
the matrix proportion increased, n progressively decreased, reaching 
0.70 in whole milk and 0.86 in coconut formulations at the 30:70 ratio,

3.6. Quantification of the main phenols by UHPLC
The coffee: matrix ratio and the type of matrix significantly affected 
the distribution of bioactive compounds in the beverages (Table 4). 5- 
CQA and CAF were dose-dependently diluted as the amount of milk 
matrix increased (p < 0.05). Beverages with a 70:30 ratio retained the 
highest concentrations (0.50–0.60 mg 5-CQA and 1.43–1.49 mg CAF), 
with only minor differences among matrices. In contrast, the 30:70 
formulations showed the greatest reductions (0.13–0.14 mg for 5-CQA 
and 0.60–0.67 mg for CAF), corresponding to approximately a 75 % 
decrease in 5-CQA. Overall, these results indicate a proportional rela­
tionship between coffee content and the retention of these compounds.
This trend is consistent with previous studies reporting lower 5-CQA 
levels and reduced polyphenol content in milk-containing coffee bev­
erages, which results in higher CAF/5-CQA ratios compared to milk-free 
products (Jeon et al., 2019; Wierzejska et al., 2024). The reduced 
bioaccessibility of polyphenols has been attributed to the formation of 
soluble or insoluble complexes with milk proteins via non-covalent in­
teractions, thereby altering their analytical availability (Jurema Soares 
et al., 2022).
In contrast to 5-CQA and CAF, CFA was more frequently detected in 
formulations with higher proportions of milk matrix. It was absent or 
present only at trace levels in the 70:30 beverages and increased slightly 
at the 50:50 ratio (0.06 ± 0.00 mg in whole milk, soy, and coconut). At 
the 30:70 ratio, the almond-based beverage showed the highest CFA 
concentration (0.15 ± 0.00 mg), approximately 2.5 times higher than 
that observed in soy-based formulations (0.06 ± 0.00 mg). This opposite 
behavior suggests matrix-dependent stabilization mechanisms.
Such behavior can be explained by the high affinity of CFA for milk 
proteins (β- and κ-caseins) and plant globulins, with which it forms 
relatively stable complexes via hydrophobic interactions and hydrogen 
bonding, enhancing its chemical stability and limiting degradation 
(Horita et al., 2025; Tagliazucchi et al., 2012; Xue et al., 2025). These 
interactions may also favor the analytical detectability of CFA under 
higher matrix dilution conditions.

flavor profile (Gupta et al., 2025). Consistently, previous studies have

thickness positively influence consumer acceptance of coffee beverages 
formulated with plant-based milks (Chung et al., 2022).

coffee predominated or was balanced (70:30 and 50:50). In contrast, 
soy- and coconut-based formulations benefited from higher matrix 
proportions (30:70). These compatibility profiles provide useful guid­
ance for designing coffee beverages tailored to different consumer 
preferences.
3.8. Correlation analysis
The Pearson correlation analysis, conducted separately for each type 
of milk-based coffee beverage, revealed that the relationships between 
physicochemical parameters, bioactive compounds, and sensory attri­
butes largely depended on the type of milk used (Fig. 5, Supplementary 
Material 3). Across all formulations, overall acceptance was positively 
associated with flavor, confirming that flavor is the primary factor 
influencing consumer liking. Coffee beverages containing whole milk (r 
= 0.95, p < 0.001) and coconut milk (r = 0.95, p < 0.001) exhibited the 
highest correlations between acceptance and flavor, followed by almond 
milk coffee (r = 0.98, p < 0.001) and soy milk coffee (r = 0.76, p < 0.05). 
These results indicate that flavor is a key determinant of acceptance, 
although its relative importance may vary depending on the type of 
milk. Sweetness was observed to enhance the overall flavor perception 
of milk-based coffee samples among consumers. Furthermore, con­
sumers who prefer plant-based products (and typically add plant-based 
milk to their coffee) exhibit different preferences compared to dairy 
consumers (Gorman et al., 2021). This suggests that attributes such as 
mouthfeel and texture also play a significant role in the acceptance of 
coffee beverages formulated with plant-based milks (McClements, 
2020).
Coffee beverages prepared with whole milk exhibited a sensory 
pattern mainly driven by aroma and flavor rather than by appearance. 
Overall acceptance was strongly associated with aroma (r = 0.77, p <
0.05) and flavor (r = 0.95, p < 0.001), whereas appearance did not show 
significant correlations with the other sensory attributes or with overall 
acceptance. In coconut milk beverages, overall acceptance was strongly 
associated with flavor (r = 0.95, p < 0.001), and also significantly 
related to appearance (r = 0.70, p < 0.05) and aroma (r = 0.67, p <
0.05). In the case of almond milk coffee beverages, overall acceptance 
was associated with sensory attributes such as appearance (r = 0.94, p <
0.001), aroma (r = 0.93, p < 0.001), and flavor (r = 0.98, p < 0.001), 
indicating a highly integrated sensory perception, while compositional 
parameters were not directly associated with consumer acceptance. 
Coffee beverages with soy milk showed that acceptance depended pri­
marily on flavor (r = 0.76, p < 0.05), while the remaining sensory de­
scriptors were not significantly associated with overall acceptance. The 
patterns observed for plant-based milks may be related to consumer 
familiarity with these products, as individuals who regularly consume 
them often have different motivations for doing so, which may influence 
sensory perception (Haas et al., 2019); familiarity has been associated 
with higher acceptability (Tan et al., 2016; Torrico et al., 2019), which 
may explain the liking scores of coffee beverages with plant-based milks 
among consumers accustomed to them.

4. Conclusion
The physicochemical profile of coffee beverages formulated with 
dairy and plant-based matrices showed that both matrix type and coffee- 
to-matrix ratio significantly affected pH, titratable acidity, and soluble 
solids (◦Brix). Formulations with a higher coffee proportion (70:30) 
exhibited lower pH values and higher titratable acidity. In contrast, 
increasing the proportion of the milk matrix (30:70) resulted in higher 
pH and reduced titratable acidity, due to the matrix components' buff­
ering effect. Soluble solids (◦Brix) increased with matrix addition, with 
whole milk formulations reaching the highest values (10.13 ◦Brix at the 
30:70 ratio), attributable to their lactose and total solids content.
5-Caffeoylquinic acid (5-CQA) and caffeine reached higher concen­
trations in formulations with a higher coffee proportion, decreasing as 
the milk matrix proportion increased. This reduction is consistent with a 
dilution effect and with protein–polyphenol interactions previously 
described and observed in the total phenolic content analysis. Caffeic 
acid (CFA) was mainly detected in formulations with a higher matrix 
proportion, particularly in beverages containing almond milk. Antioxi­
dant capacity varied moderately among formulations, with no statisti­
cally significant differences among matrices, indicating that formulation 
balance had a greater influence than matrix type.
From a rheological perspective, the beverages exhibited distinct flow 
behaviors depending on the matrix. The whole–milk–based beverage 
exhibited the lowest viscosity and nearly Newtonian behavior. Plant- 
based matrices progressively increased viscosity as their proportion 
increased. The soy milk–based beverage reached the highest viscosity 
and the most pronounced pseudoplastic behavior (flow behavior index n 
= 0.70), which can be attributed to its high protein content (~3.5 %) 
and the presence of soluble fibers.
Sensory acceptance showed statistically significant differences 
among formulations (p < 0.05), with matrix–formulation-dependent 
preferences. Beverages formulated with whole milk and almond milk 
showed higher acceptance values when coffee predominated or was 
balanced (70:30 and 50:50 ratios). In contrast, coffee beverages 
formulated with coconut and soy milk showed higher acceptance values 
at the 30:70 ratio, with mean values around 7.42 and 6.88, respectively. 
This response may be associated with the higher lipid content of coconut 
milk, which contributes to increased creaminess and a distinctive flavor 
profile. Correlation analysis confirmed that flavor was the main attri­
bute associated with overall acceptance across all matrices (r =
0.76–0.98, p < 0.05), particularly in beverages formulated with whole 
milk (r = 0.95) and coconut milk (r = 0.95).
From a product development perspective, these results provide pre­
liminary insights for the formulation of ready-to-drink coffee beverages 
and latte- or cappuccino-type preparations by adjusting the coffee-to- 
milk ratio to balance sensory acceptance and functional attributes. As 
future work, the evaluation of colloidal stability, shelf life, and system 
performance under industrial processing conditions is recommended.
