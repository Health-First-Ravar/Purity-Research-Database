a b s t r a c t 
Coffee and tea are amongst the most consumed beverages worldwide, and are the main source of caffeine 
in adults. In this review we present ﬁndings on the effects of habitual coffee and tea consumption on 
cardiovascular disease (CVD) prevention. Mild-moderate coffee/ caffeine consumption, at 2–3 cups/day, is 
associated with beneﬁcial effects on metabolic syndrome, including hypertension and diabetes mellitus, 
although may elevate lipid levels. Furthermore, coffee consumption reduces the risk of coronary heart 
disease, heart failure, arrhythmia, stroke, CVD and all cause mortality. Higher tea consumption, in partic- 
ular green tea, confers similar cardiovascular beneﬁts to coffee with 3 cups/day associated with improved 
survival in population based studies.

Introduction 
Coffee and tea are consumed worldwide and have been an im- 
portant aspect of societal norms and cultural traditions for cen- 
turies [ 1 , 2 ]. Coffee’s most well-known constituent, caffeine, is a 
psychostimulant that can result in physical dependence. There has 
been signiﬁcant interest in the health outcomes from habitual in- 
take of these beverages and their potential role as modiﬁable risk 
factors in cardiovascular disease (CVD). It is a common misconcep- 
tion that moderate coffee and tea consumption is not safe in pa- 
tients with CVD [1] . In fact, numerous studies have reported a ben- 
eﬁcial role of moderate coffee or tea intake in CVD prevention and 
management [ 1 , 2 ]. 
Research into the cardiovascular effects of coffee and tea are 
ongoing. Here, we aim to reappraise the current literature on the 
impact of coffee and tea in a wide range of cardiovascular condi- 
tions. 
Methods 
We performed a comprehensive literature search in July 2021 
of PubMed, EMBASE, and Medline, focusing on human studies 
published in English. The main search terms were ‘coffee’, ‘tea’, 
‘caffeine’, ‘diabetes’, ‘metabolic syndrome’, ‘lipid’, ‘coronary heart 
disease’, ‘heart failure’, ‘arrhythmias’, and ‘mortality’. The highest

Coffee and tea pharmacology 
Coffee and tea pharmacology have been discussed previously 
[ 1 , 2 ]. Coffee and tea are complex beverages that contain hundreds 
of active biochemical compounds beyond the most well-known 
constituent of caffeine. As such, health outcomes attributed to cof- 
fee and tea consumption are not necessarily conﬁned to the main 
component of caffeine itself [3] . 
Caffeine has a half-life of approximately 6 h, and near 100% 
bioavailability. One cup of coffee contains approximately 95 mg of 
caffeine, whereas black and green tea contain 55 mg and 35 mg 
of caffeine respectively [2] . Acute caffeine intake results in sympa- 
thetic activation, which is mediated by phosphodiesterase inhibi- 
tion, cytosolic calcium increase, and stimulation of noradrenaline/ 
adrenaline release [2] . An increase in intracellular calcium is po- 
tentially pro-arrhythmic through atrial pacemaker cell automatic- 
ity and after depolarization-induced triggered activity [4] . Indeed, 
animal studies have shown that high doses of caffeine (15 mg/kg 
per min) result in ventricular ﬁbrillation (VF) [5] with media re- 
ports of sudden death following the ingestion of caffeine pow- 
der. However, in modest doses caffeine intake can also be anti- 
arrhythmic through adenosine A 1 and A 2A receptor inhibition [2] . 
Other beneﬁts of caffeine include increased endothelial nitric ox- 
ide release leading to vasodilation, and antioxidant properties [2] 
( Fig. 1 ).

Fig. 1. Pharmacology of coffee and tea Coffee and tea contain numerous active biochemicals. Caffeine, as its main constituent, primarily results in sympathetic activation 
mediated through various processes. Polyphenols such as catechins, chlorogenic acid, and theaﬂavins have anti-oxidant and anti-inﬂammatory properties. 
Coffee also contains polyphenols such as chlorogenic acid, an- 
tioxidant ferulic acid, and microelements such as magnesium. 
These constituents have been shown to reduce oxidative stress, 
modulate metabolism, and improve gut microbiome [ 3 , 6 ]. Reg- 
ular coffee consumption resulted in an increase in the number 
and metabolic activity of the probiotic Biﬁdobacterium spp [7] .Tea 
contains numerous polyphenols including catechins – epigallo- 
catechin and its metabolite epigallocatechin gallate (EGCG); and 
theaﬂavin/ thearubigins. Green tea contains more EGCG, whereas 
black tea contains more theaﬂavins and thearubigins. Both EGCG 
and theaﬂavins have antioxidant and anti-inﬂammatory properties 
[1] . 
More recently, genome wide association studies (GWAS) have 
identiﬁed single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNP) which decrease 
caffeine metabolism, resulting in higher circulating caffeine levels. 
These include genes associated with CYP1A2, CYP2A6, AHR, and 
POR [8] . Those with slower caffeine metabolism tend to have lower 
habitual caffeine intake [3] . To date, the presence of these genetic 
variants do not have a signiﬁcant impact on the observed cardio- 
vascular outcomes in coffee drinkers [ 8 , 9 ]. 
Coffee, tea and the metabolic syndrome 
Hypertension 
Caffeine intake in the coffee naive can result in an acute in- 
crease in blood pressure (BP). However, tolerance develops in ha- 
bitual drinkers with no demonstrable increase in BP despite a sim- 
ilar increase in plasma caffeine levels when compared to non- 
habitual drinkers [3] . This would suggest that other biochemicals 
beyond caffeine are likely to contribute to the BP modulating ef- 
fects of coffee, particularly chlorogenic acid. 
Overall, current data would suggest that moderate levels of cof- 
fee intake decrease the risk of hypertension long term. Physiologi- 
cally, coffee and caffeine consum ption are linked to reduced aortic 
stiffness, with attendant reductions in both systolic and diastolic 
BP [10] . In a meta-analysis, a 9% reduction in risk of hypertension 
was observed with 7 cups of coffee per day, with a 1% reduction 
in risk for each additional cup of coffee per day [11] . A separate 
meta-analysis showed a dose-response inverse association between 
coffee consum ption and hypertension. Com pared with non-coffee 
drinkers, the risk of hypertension in those who consumed 2, 4, 6, 
and 8 cups/day was reduced by 3%, 5%, 8% and 10% respectively 
[12] . 
Other studies have shown a non-linear relationship between 
coffee consum ption and hypertension risk. A meta-analysis showed 
that a signiﬁcant protective effect from coffee was only seen start- 
ing from 3 cups of coffee per day, with a relative risk (RR) of 0.97; 
whereas one or two cups of coffee per day was not associated 
with hypertension [13] . A further meta-analysis had suggested an 
inverse ‘J-shaped’ relationship with hypertension risk initially in- 
creasing up to 3 cups/day and subsequently decreasing with higher 
intakes [14] . 
Studies on coffee consum ption and hypertension risk in popu- 
lation subgroups have been mixed. Women who consume higher 
levels of coffee had 26% lower risk of developing hypertension 
[15] . In elderly patients with hypertension, the risk of uncontrolled 
BP progressively increased in those who consume 1, 2, and ≥3 
cups/day, when compared to non-drinkers [16] . Recent genomic 
studies have shown interactions between genetic predisposition to 
hypertension and coffee consumption. Participants who consumed 
> 3 cups of coffee per day and a higher genetic risk score (GRS) for 
hypertension had signiﬁcantly higher blood pressures compared to 
those with a lower GRS. This ﬁnding would suggest that an indi- 
vidualized approach to coffee intake may be needed [17] .

A meta-analysis investigating tea consumption showed reduc- 
tions in both systolic and diastolic blood pressures, with postulated 
mechanisms including vasodilation from prostacyclin release, en- 
hancement of NO production and oxidative stress reduction [18] . 
Two separate meta-analyses on the effects of green tea consump- 
tion also report reductions in both systolic and diastolic BP [ 19 , 20 ], 
with mean effects sizes ranging from −1.94 to −2.08 mmHg for 
systolic, and −1.71 to −1.94 mmHg for diastolic BP respectively. 
Fig. 2 summarizes the effects of habitual coffee/ tea consumption 
on various cardiovascular outcomes. Habitual coffee and tea con- 
sumption is not associated with hypertension and may rather be 
associated with a negligible reduction in blood pressure. There is 
no substantial evidence to support omission of these beverages in 
the management of hypertension. 
Weight gain 
Coffee consum ption has a probable beneﬁcial effect on weight 
loss. Caffeine has a role in inhibiting gut absorption of fatty acids, 
modifying gut microbiome, downregulating lipogenesis, increas- 
ing food induced thermogenesis, and increasing the body’s basal 
metabolic rate [6] . 
Consumption of 1–4 cups of coffee per day, when compared 
with non-drinkers, was associated with signiﬁcant reductions in 
incident obesity and metabolic syndrome [21] . A recent study in- 
volving middle aged patients also showed that daily coffee con- 
sumption was inversely associated with body mass index (BMI), 
with mean effect size of −1.6 kg/m 2 (OR 0.14), as well as body fat 
percentage (BF%), with effect size of −3.2% (OR 0.33) [22] . Green 
coffee may have a greater effect on weight loss than roasted cof- 
fee and caffeine, likely due to a higher concentration of chlorogenic 
acid [6] . 
There is evidence that tea consumption results in weight loss 
through the effects of caffeine and tea polyphenols, in particular 
catechin compounds like epigallocatechin [ 2 , 6 ]. It is hypothesized 
that the tea polyphenols facilitate weight loss more through gut 
enzyme inhibition and gut microbiome interactions, rather than 
through anti-oxidant activities [23] . Evidence for the weight loss 
effects of tea is mostly based on green tea consumption, although 
black tea can be effective too [ 6 , 23 ]. Daily green tea intake resulted 
in lower BF% (effect size −2.8%, OR 0.36) in a middle aged popu- 
lation [22] . Whether the synergistic effects of caffeine and cate- 
chins are important to achieve weight loss, or whether green tea 
catechins by themselves are suﬃcient, remains unclear. A meta- 
analysis found that either catechin or catechin-caffeine mixtures 
have beneﬁcial effects on inducing and maintaining weight loss 
[24] . 
Hyperlipidemia 
Coffee is known to contain cholesterol-increasing diterpenes, 
which include cafestol and kahweol. Earlier studies had shown 
that the concentration of these diterpenes in coffee is dependent 
on the brewing method. Their concentrations are high in unﬁl- 
tered coffee such as boiled coffee, intermediate in espresso, and 
low / negligible in ﬁltered coffee [ 2 , 3 ]. Indeed current evidence 
would suggest that the coffee brewing method utilized has an 
effect on serum lipid levels. A meta-analysis showed that boiled 
coffee dose-dependently increased total cholesterol (TC) and low 
density lipoprotein (LDL) levels, whereas ﬁltered coffee resulted in 
very little change in TC levels [25] . A subsequent meta-analysis 
showed that coffee consum ption resulted in an increase in TC, 
LDL, triglycerides, although the increases were more pronounced 
in those with hyperlipidemia, unﬁltered coffee consumption, and 
high daily intake ( > 6 cups/day) [26] . In a UK Biobank study, 
each additional cup of coffee resulted in signiﬁcantly higher TC, 
LDL and high density lipoprotein (HDL) levels, when compared to 
non-drinkers. However triglyceride levels were signiﬁcantly lower. 
Espresso drinkers had higher LDL levels, which was not seen in in- 
stant coffee drinkers [9] . However, a large Italian cohort study re- 
ported that higher espresso consumption did not signiﬁcantly alter 
lipid levels [27] .

The effects of tea on serum lipids are more consistent, with the 
bulk of evidence based on green tea and /or catechin consumption. 
Separate meta-analyses had shown signiﬁcant reductions in TC and 
LDL levels [ 19 , 20 ]. The results were not inﬂuenced by the number 
of cups, study duration or individual health status. In a recent UK 
biobank study, higher tea consumption, when compared to non- 
tea drinker, resulted in signiﬁcantly lower TC and LDL levels, along 
with higher HDL levels. These associations were similar for both 
black and green tea [9] . 
Diabetes mellitus 
The effects of coffee on glucose homeostasis are complex. Caf- 
feine has been shown to reduce insulin sensitivity, possibly related 
to adrenaline release [28] . Adenosine A1 receptor antagonism from 
acute coffee intake (within 1–3 h) can reduced skeletal muscle glu- 
cose uptake [29] . Coffee intake can increase secretion of gastric in- 
hibitory polypeptide (GIP) and decrease secretion of glucagon-like 
peptide-1 (GLP-1), leading to reduced intestinal glucose absorption 
[30] . Furthermore, coffee polyphenols have important antioxidant 
and anti-inﬂammatory effects that can affect glucose metabolism 
and insulin sensitivity [30] . 
Several meta-analyses have demonstrated the beneﬁcial effect 
of coffee on reducing the incidence of type 2 diabetes mellitus 
(T2DM). In a meta-analysis, each additional coffee cup/day was 
associated with an incremental reduction in T2DM risk. This was 
seen in both caffeinated and de-caffeinated coffee, with the lowest 
risk seen in those consuming 6 cups/day (RR 0.67) [31] . A recent 
review showed a 6% risk reduction in T2DM for each cup/ day in- 
crease in coffee consumption; with the lowest risk seen in those 
at the highest category (5 cup/day) of coffee consum ption (RR 0.7) 
[32] . 
Amongst T2DM patients, regular caffeinated coffee consum ption 
of ≥4 cups/day did not increase the risk of CVD or mortality [33] . 
Elderly ( > 60 years) patients with pre-existing T2DM also demon- 
strated signiﬁcant improvement in fasting blood glucose with > 3 
cups of coffee/day [34] . 
Green tea consumption has also been shown to reduce the 
risk of T2DM, which is possibly mediated through the anti-oxidant 
and anti-inﬂammatory effects of catechins [35] . In a meta-analysis, 
green tea resulted in a signiﬁcant reduction in fasting blood glu- 
cose levels, particularly in those < 55 years and in Asian popu- 
lations [35] . A 2020 meta-analysis showed that green tea signiﬁ- 
cantly reduces the incidence of T2DM; however black tea did not. 
The authors concluded that different types of tea have varying pro- 
tective mechanisms on metabolic syndrome although the explana- 
tion for this observation is unclear [36] . 
Coronary heart disease 
Studies on coffee and the risk of coronary heart disease (CHD) 
have been mixed although the majority suggest reduced risk at 
moderate levels of consumption. Caffeine antagonizes the coronary 
vasodilatory effect of adenosine, but it has also been shown to im- 
prove endothelial function as assessed by brachial artery ﬂow me- 
diated dilation (FMD) [ 37 , 38 ]. 
A meta-analysis showed that moderate coffee consumption ( > 1 
cup/day in United States, or > 2 cups/ day in Europe) resulted in 
a signiﬁcant reduction in the risk of CHD, when compared with 
minimal coffee consumption ( < 1 cup/day in United States, or ≤
2 cups/ day in Europe) [39] . A separate meta-analysis showed a ‘U 
shaped’ inverse relationship between coffee consumption and CHD. 
All categories of coffee consum ption were associated with lower 
CHD risk, with the most signiﬁcant reduction seen in those con- 
suming a median of 3.5 cups/day [40] . In contrast, a meta-analysis 
of 17 studies showed that coffee consum ption > 4 cups/day sig- 
niﬁcantly increased the risk of CHD events (RR 1.48), whereas 2–3 
cups/day had no effect on risk [41] . Pathophysiologic correlations 
between coffee and CT coronary artery calcium (CAC] scores had 
also shown favourable outcomes with coffee consumption. Coffee 
intake at all levels protects against coronary artery calciﬁcation, 
with the greatest beneﬁt seen in those who consume > 3 cups/day 
(OR 0.33; CI 0.17–0.65). As expected, any potentially beneﬁcial ef- 
fects from coffee is negated by smoking [42] . 
A large prospective cohort study showed that tea consumption 
at all levels was protective against CHD events [43] . In the MESA 
study, regular tea drinkers ( ≥1 cup/day) had signiﬁcantly slower 
progression of CT CAC score when compared with never drinkers, 
which also correlated with a signiﬁcantly lower incidence of CVD 
events [44] . A recent study, however, found that those in the high- 
est tertile of tea and caffeine consumption had elevated risk of 
CVD. The authors postulated that the negative results could possi- 
bly be related to black tea being the predominant locally consumed 
tea, as well as the addition of sugar to tea [45] . 
In acute myocardial infarction (MI) survivors, a meta-analysis 
reported that habitual coffee consumption was associated with 
a reduced risk of mortality. Compared with non-drinkers, light 
drinkers (1–2 cups/day) had a 21% relative risk reduction, whereas 
heavy drinkers ( > 2 cups/day) had a 46% risk reduction [46] . In 
contrast, a prospective study of 2172 MI survivors from 6 Greek 
hospitals had shown that 1–2 cups of coffee per day, when com- 
pared to non-drinkers, signiﬁcantly increased the risk of acute 
coronary syndrome (ACS) events over a 10 year period (OR 1.35) 
[47] 
Cardiac arrhythmias 
Atrial arrhythmias 
Current evidence suggests that coffee consumption is safe and 
may even protect against atrial arrhythmias, in particular atrial 
ﬁbrillation (AF) [ 1 , 2 ]. The postulated mechanisms conferring pro- 
tection against arrhythmias are due to cardiomyocyte adenosine 
receptor antagonism by caffeine, and antioxidant effects [ 1 , 2 ]. A 
meta-analysis showed that AF incidence decreased by 6% for ev- 
ery 300 mg/day increment in caffeine intake [48] . A recent meta- 
analysis found that intermediate levels of coffee consumption (1–7 
cups/week) resulted in signiﬁcant reduction in AF incidence, when 
compared with non-consumers or those with higher levels of in- 
take ( > 1 cup/ day) [49] . A prospective study found that caffeine 
intake in excess of 320 mg/day decreases AF incidence compared 
to lower levels of consumption. Slow caffeine metabolizers from 
CYP1A2 gene polymorphisms had further AF reduction on univari- 
ate analysis, although this was not signiﬁcant on multivariate anal- 
ysis [50] . 
Green tea consumption has also been found to protect again in- 
cident AF in a dose dependent manner (OR 0.35) [51] . A cohort 
study of participants who underwent 24 hour Holter monitoring 
showed no correlation between tea intake with atrial or ventricu- 
lar ectopy [52] . 
Ventricular arrhythmias / sudden cardiac death 
Data examining the association between coffee/ tea and ven- 
tricular arrhythmia (VA), ventricular tachycardia (VT) and sudden 
cardiac death (SCD) are limited to small scale studies, with the ma- 
jority showing no increased risk [1] . A meta-analysis showed that 
caffeine consum ption had no impact on ventricular ectopy [53] . 
Patients with cardiomyopathy at high risk of VA who consumed 
500 mg caffeine did not experience any signiﬁcant difference in 
ventricular ectopy/ non sustained VT, when compared to those

Heart failure 
Coffee and tea consumption may be associated with a reduc- 
tion in the risk of incident heart failure (HF). In a meta-analysis, 
a J-shaped relationship between coffee and HF was observed, with 
the strongest beneﬁt of 11% risk reduction seen in those who con- 
sumed 4 cups/day of coffee [57] . A recent study on participants 
in the Framingham Heart Study (FHS) found that higher coffee in- 
take signiﬁcantly reduced the risk of HF, which was validated in 
the same study in participants from the large scale Cardiovascular 
Heart Study, and the Atherosclerosis Risk in Communities Study. 
Overall, the lowest HF incidence was seen in those who consumed 
2 cups/day. Increased caffeine intake (coffee/black tea) was also 
signiﬁcantly associated with reduced HF [58] . 
Coffee consum ption may im prove functional capacity in those 
with existing heart failure. Intravenous caffeine at 4 mg/kg (ap- 
proximately 2 cups of coffee) signiﬁcantly increased mean exercise 
time and peak minute ventilation, with no effect on peak oxygen 
consumption [59] . Recent studies have also examined the effect of 
coffee consum ption on left ventricular function, as assessed using 
multi-modality imaging. Low-to-moderate daily coffee consump- 
tion (1–4 cups/day) was associated with improvements in global 
longitudinal strain (GLS), and diastolic function on echocardiogra- 
phy, compared to non-drinkers. High daily coffee consumption of 
> 4 cups/day, however, was associated with worse left ventricular 
ejection fraction (EF) [60] . In a cardiac magnetic resonance imaging 
study, higher coffee consum ption was positively correlated with 
improved EF and late diastolic ﬁlling rates [61] . 
Cerebrovascular disease 
Acute physiological effect of caffeine include cerebral vasocon- 
striction through adenosine receptor blockade [2] . However, the 
majority of studies have thus far shown that tea/ coffee consump- 
tion reduces the incidence of TIAs and stroke. This is likely at- 
tributable to the polyphenols, which are associated with improved 
endothelial function, increased NO bioavailability, and favorable 
lipid and glucose proﬁles [62] . 
A meta-analysis of population-based studies showed that coffee 
intake of up to 6 cups/day was associated with a signiﬁcant re- 
duction in stroke risk. The lowest risk was seen in those who con- 
sumed 3–4 cups/day [63] . This ﬁnding was corroborated by a sep- 
arate meta-analysis, with the greatest risk reduction seen in 3–6 
cups/day (RR: 0.79) [64] . Moderate green and black tea consump- 
tion, at up to 3 cups/day, had also been shown to protect against 
stroke (RR 0.76) [65] . 
Cardiovascular disease (CVD) and all cause mortality 
Large scale population studies and meta-analyses have shown 
that moderate coffee consumption results in a reduced risk of car- 
diovascular and total mortality. The NIH–AARP Diet and Health 
Study demonstrated that both caffeinated and decaffeinated cof- 
fee consumption was inversely associated with all cause mortality, 
including mortality from CHD. The greatest beneﬁt was seen with 
4–5 cups of coffee/day [66] . A meta-analysis showed that intakes 
between 3 and 5 cups/day had the most signiﬁcant reduction in 
CVD mortality [67] . A recent meta-analysis of 3,852,651 subjects 
showed the greatest risk reduction in all-cause mortality with 3.5 
cups of coffee a day (RR 0.85), and in CVD mortality with 2.5 cups 
of coffee a day (RR 0.83). Subgroup analyses showed a more pro- 
nounced inverse association between coffee and mortality in Euro- 
pean and Asian populations [68] . 
A meta-analysis showed that higher green tea consumption sig- 
niﬁcantly reduced both CVD and all cause mortality. The relative 
risk reduction between the highest and lowest category of green 
tea intake for CVD and all cause mortality was 33% and 20% respec- 
tively. Furthermore, each cup per day increase in green tea was as- 
sociated with 5% lower CVD mortality, and 4% lower all cause mor- 
tality. A signiﬁcant inverse association was seen for black tea con- 
sumption and all cause mortality, but not for CVD mortality [69] . 
A separate meta-analysis showed that an increase in tea consump- 
tion by 3 cups per day signiﬁcantly reduced the risk of both CVD 
and all cause mortality [70] . 
Limitations 
Epidemiological studies on coffee and tea are potentially con- 
founded by other lifestyle factors, and if unaccounted for can lead 
to erroneous conclusions [3] . Beverage consumption may reﬂect 
other dietary or behavioral characteristics in geographic areas re- 
sponsible for the observed effect rather than a direct effect of the 
beverage itself. Studies may differ in cup sizes, type of coffee/ tea 
used, or amounts of sugar/ milk/ sweetener added. Generally, stud- 
ies rely on self reporting for beverage types and numbers which 
may vary over time. Self selection bias may be present where 
people would modify caffeine intake based on perceived cardio- 
vascular symptoms. In a UK Biobank study, those with angina or 
arrhythmia were less likely to have caffeinated coffee, and more 
likely to consume decaffeinated coffee [71] . Mendelian randomiza- 
tion studies have become increasingly popular where genetic vari- 
ants, which increase (or decrease) the propensity for people to 
consume caffeine, are used as proxy variables for actual caffeine 
intake [21] . However, these ﬁndings are potentially confounded by 
pleiotropic genetic variants [3] . 
Future directions 
There is a lack of evidence on alternative coffee/ tea types, in- 
cluding matcha/ oolong teas, on various cardiovascular outcomes. 
The interactions between genetic polymorphisms that affect caf- 
feine metabolism, polymorphisms that affect CVD risk (such as AF 
polygenic risk scores), and coffee and tea consumption, remains to 
be explored. Current evidence supports the safety of habitual cof- 
fee and tea consumption at 2–3 cups per day; however random- 
ized studies would be required before regular consumption is pre- 
scribed in non-drinkers. 
Conclusion 
Observational studies support mild- moderate habitual con- 
sumption of 2–3 cups of coffee per day, with beneﬁcial effects 
on metabolic syndrome, coronary heart disease, arrhythmia, heart 
failure, stroke and cardiovascular mortality. Whilst higher tea con- 
sumption confers similar cardiovascular beneﬁt, an optimal level 
of consumption is less well deﬁned. Habitual coffee and tea con- 
sumption may be considered part of a healthy lifestyle and need 
not be prohibited in patients with cardiovascular disease.
