ABSTRACT
In this research the physical properties of defective and nondefective coffee beans
were investigated. The study results show that there are highly signiﬁcant differ-
ences (P < 0.001) in the physical properties (true and bulk volume, true and bulk
density, mass) and (P < 0.05) in sphericity and porosity of green coffee beans,
respectively. Higher and lower values of mass and dimensional parameters were
obtained in full sour and full black coffee beans, respectively. On the other hand,
the physical properties of the beans investigated as the function of moisture
content (%db) were found to be a linear relation with volume, mass and equiva-
lent diameter, and an inverse relation with density of the beans. Generally, the
moisture content (% dry basis [d.b.]) of defective beans (full sour 12.87%, imma-
ture 11.34%, full black 10.41%) is higher than nondefective beans (10.22%) for
sample kept at oven temperature (105C) for 48 h. The results of the study indi-
cated that the physical properties alone can successfully separate defective and
nondefective beans prior and after roasting.
PRACTICAL APPLICATIONS
The physical properties of defective and nondefective coffee beans were investi-
gated. The determined physical properties of beans have useful applications in
coffee quality control. In addition, these properties have signiﬁcant importance for
machinery and process equipment design. The methods are simple, fast and inex-
pensive as compared with the chemical methods that need high-cost equipment
and solvents.
INTRODUCTION
Coffee is one of the most consumed beverages in the world
and an important commodity for many developing coun-
tries (ICO 2011). It creates an employment opportunity for
the vast majority of the rural populations, and the main
foreign currency earning and major contribution to gross
domestic product (Banks et al. 1999). The current world
coffee consumption was estimated to be 7,950 million kilo-
grams in 2010 (ICO 2011). However, it has been reported
that there will be lack of consistent supply of good quality
coffee beans in the market due to speciﬁc problems
occurring during the harvesting and postharvesting pro-
cesses. These defect beans are damped in the internal
market and consumed in the country (Craig et al. 2011,
2012).
The term defect used in commercial practice is in refer-
ence to the presence of defective beans (black, sour or foxy,
immature, insect damaged or bored beans and extraneous
matter) in a given sample (Franca et al. 2005). Particularly,
the presence of intrinsic defective beans (black, sour and
immature) highly depreciated the quality of coffee bever-
ages worldwide (Mendonca et al. 2008). Black beans result
from dead beans within the coffee cherries (Clarke and

beans can be associated over fermentation during coffee
processing (Clarke and Macrae 1987) and with improper

(Clarke and Macrae 1987; Oliveiral et al. 2006; Mendonca
et al. 2008). These beans represent 20% of the total coffee
produced in Brazil (Oliveiral et al. 2006; Mendonca et al.
2008) and India (Ramalakshmi et al. 2007). On the other
hand, no literature yet available indicate the percentage of
defect in coffee beans of Ethiopia.
The universal criteria commonly used to classify the
quality or grading of beans are the physical quality analysis
(bean size, color, shape, moisture content, the presence of
defective beans and roast potential processing method) and
organoleptic analysis, which relies on overall sensory evalu-
ation (body, acidity, ﬂavor and cup quality). The techniques
are more of subjective, time consuming and may not be reli-
able. Several studies have been carried out for the discrimi-
nation of defective and nondefective coffee beans using
chemical and physical properties of bean (Briandet et al.
1996; Mazzafera 1999; Franca et al. 2005; Farah et al. 2006;
Oliveiral et al. 2006; Ramalakshmi et al. 2007; Mendonca
et al. 2008, 2009; Craig et al. 2011, 2012; Rodriguez-Saona
and Allendorf 2011). The developed techniques for chemi-
cal analysis are electrospray ionization mass spectrometry
(Mendonca et al. 2008, 2009), high-performance liquid
chromatography (Franca et al. 2005; Farah et al. 2006;
Ramalakshmi et al. 2007), diffuse reﬂectance infrared
Fourier transform spectroscopy (Craig et al. 2011, 2012)
and attenuated total reﬂectance Fourier transform infrared
spectroscopy (Briandet et al. 1996; Rodriguez-Saona and
Allendorf 2011). The chemical techniques are powerful in
discriminating defective and nondefective beans; however,
these techniques need high-cost equipments and solvents,
and some of them required large initial capital cost. There-
fore, it is unusual to get such instruments in most coffee-
producing countries (especially in Africa and Asia); hence,
searching for an alternative technique is necessary.
On the other hand, utilization of physical properties of
coffee beans for the discrimination of defective and
nondefective beans is relatively simple, fast, economical and
reliable as compared with other techniques. Previous studies
conducted by Franca et al. (2005) and Ramalakshmi et al.
(2007) try to differentiate defective beans from nondefective
using some physical properties of coffee beans. Recently,
Ismail et al. (2013) also investigated the effects of storage
temperature and humidity on the physical properties of
Liberica green beans. Furthermore, Olukunle and Akinnuli
(2012) determined some engineering properties of coffee
seeds and beans. Apart from the above-mentioned studies,
as far as the authors’ knowledge concern, there is no report
on the physical properties of defective and nondefective
green and moisture-dependent beans. Therefore, the objec-
tives of the study are to determine the population of defec-
tive coffee beans of Ethiopia and investigate the physical

Statistical Analysis
All data were expressed as mean ± standard deviation of
triplet measurement and analyzed by Origin 6.1 software
(Origin Lab Co., Northampton, USA). One-way analysis of
variance performed in Origin 6.1 software was used to test
any signiﬁcant difference in the physical properties of defec-
tive and nondefective coffee beans. In addition, regression
analysis was also employed by this software to describe the
moisture-dependent physical properties of the beans.
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
Percentage of Total Defective Beans in
Selected Zones of Ethiopia
Table 1 presents the total percentage of defective beans from
four origins (12 different areas) in Ethiopia. The average
percentages of the total defective coffee beans are 37.49% of
the population of coffee produced. Defect due to immature
beans accounts for 36.14% of the total defect and 60%
resulting from intrinsic defect (full black, full sour and
immature), respectively. The highest percentage of total
defected beans (58%) was found in Sidamo (Kilenso
Mokenisa), whereas the lowest percentage of total defected
beans (24.86 %) was obtained from Harar (Hunde Lelisa).
High signiﬁcant difference (P < 0.001) in the percentage
of immature beans among different origins (Wollega,
Sidamo, Harar and Jimma) was also observed. The highest
(27.78%) and lowest (4.67%) immature bean contents were
found in Sidamo and Wollega zones, respectively. The total
percentage of defective beans reported in this research is
much higher than the result of Franca et al. (2005) and
Ramalakshmi et al. (2007) reported in Brazilian and Indian
coffee products. Therefore, in Ethiopia further work has to
be performed in order to decrease high percentage of defec-
tive beans as far as it has huge effects on the economic
development of the country.

The true volumes calculated for nondefective and immature
beans in this research are quite similar with the result
reported by Franca et al. (2005). However, the true volume
of full black and full sour is higher than the true volume
calculated by Franca et al. (2005) for black and sour beans.
The differences are the result of color level (full black and
black) of the beans. The average volume of the three defec-
tive beans is less than nondefective beans, which conﬁrms
the earlier reports of Franca et al. (2005) and Ramalakshmi
et al. (2007). The results of the study indicated that the
dimensions of the beans alone can successfully separate
defective and nondefective beans prior to roasting.
Bean shape is determined in terms of its sphericity and
aspect ratio (divide length of the bean to the width). The
average values of sphericity are 0.67, 0.65, 0.64 and 0.66,
and the aspect ratio for nondefective, full black, immature
and full sour are 0.68, 0.67, 0.65 and 0.66, respectively. As
per the investigation by Gamayak et al. (2008) and Bal and
Mishra (1988), it is reported that the grain is spherical when
the sphericity value is more than 0.70. From the present
study results, both the defective and nondefective beans are
not sphere; hence, the beans are more sliding than rolling
on the ﬂat surface. However, there are signiﬁcant difference
(P < 0.05) in the sphericity and aspect ratio of defective and
nondefective beans in which higher and lower values were
obtained in nondefective and immature beans, respectively.

CONCLUSION
The physical properties of green beans were determined for
defective and nondefective coffee beans. Highly signiﬁcant
differences were observed among some physical properties
of the beans. In general, the full sour and full black beans
have highest and lowest values of dimensional size (length,
width, thickness, equivalent diameter and volume) and
mass, respectively. Various moisture-dependent physical
properties were also investigated in which linear (equivalent
diameter, true and bulk volume), inverse (true and bulk
volume) and second-order polynomial function (sphericity)
equations were developed.
