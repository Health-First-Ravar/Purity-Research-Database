Coffee consumption and risk of type 2 diabetes,
cardiovascular diseases, and cancer
Rob M. van Dam
Abstract: Numerous epidemiological studies have evaluated the association between coffee consumption and risk of
type 2 diabetes, coronary heart disease, and various cancers. This paper briefly reviews the evidence for a relation between
coffee consumption and these conditions, with particular attention to methodological issues. Several early studies suggested
that coffee consumption could result in a marked increase in risk of coronary heart disease and several types of cancer.
However, more recent prospective cohort studies that are less prone to selection and information bias have not confirmed
these findings. High consumption of unfiltered types of coffee, such as French press and boiled coffee, has been shown to
increase low-density-lipoprotein-cholesterol concentrations. In addition, limiting caffeinated coffee intake during pregnancy
seems a prudent choice. However, evidence has been accumulating that frequent consumption of coffee may reduce risk of
type 2 diabetes and liver cancer. Further experimental studies are warranted to elucidate the underlying mechanisms and
possibly identify the components in coffee that are responsible for these putative effects. In sum, the currently available
evidence on coffee and risk of cardiovascular diseases and cancer is largely reassuring, and suggests that, for the general
population, addressing other health-related behaviors has priority for the prevention of chronic diseases.
Key words: coffee, caffeine, chlorogenic acid, cancer, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular diseases, blood pressure, serum
cholesterol, nutritional epidemiology.

Introduction
Coffee is among the most widely consumed beverages in
the world. It is the main source of caffeine in many popula-
tions (Table 1), but contains hundreds of other components,
including phenolic compounds, niacin (vitamin B3), and
moderate amounts of minerals (magnesium, potassium) and
fiber (U.S. Department of Agriculture Agricultural Research
Service 2007). Coffee also contains the diterpene cafestol,
which has been shown to increase serum low-density-
lipoprotein (LDL)-cholesterol concentrations (Weusten-Van
der Wouw et al. 1994). The amount of cafestol in brewed
coffee, however, depends greatly on the method of coffee
preparation, with high amounts in boiled, Turkish, Greek,
and French press coffee, intermediate amounts in espresso,

The potential effect of coffee on the risk of many diseases
has been studied, including type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular
diseases, and various types of cancer. This paper provides a
brief overview of the evidence for the effects of coffee on
risk of these diseases, mainly focusing on epidemiological
studies. In addition, some methodological issues that have
to be considered in the interpretation of epidemiological
studies on coffee and health are discussed.
Methodological considerations
The assessment of coffee consumption
Some measurement error is inevitable in the assessment
of the consumption of food and drinks, because people con-
sume a wide variety of foods and intake can vary widely
from day to day (Willett 1998). Assessments using food
diaries,
in
which
people
record
all
food
and
drinks
consumed, are not dependent on memory. In large-scale
epidemiological studies, self-administered food frequency
questionnaires are generally used. Validation studies com-
paring assessments of coffee consumption in food diaries
and food frequency questionnaires have shown good agree-
ment. For example, the correlations between coffee con-
sumption assessed by questionnaire and by diet records
were 0.75 for Japanese men (Yamaji et al. 2004), 0.78 for
American women (Salvini et al. 1989), and 0.93 for Ameri-
can men (Feskanich et al. 1993). These results indicate that
people can accurately estimate their usual coffee consump-
tion frequency.
In addition to frequency of consumption, coffee intake is
determined by the size of the coffee cup and the strength of
the brew. In some countries, cup size and brew strength
have traditionally been standardized. Nowadays, however,
in countries such as the United States, there are a wide vari-
ety in cup sizes and preferred brew strengths (Bracken et al.
2002). In addition, there can be important differences in the
concentration of coffee components, depending on the cof-
fee variety. For example, Robusta coffee beans contain
about twice the amount of caffeine as Arabica coffee beans
(Casal et al. 2000). Standard questionnaires do not typically
assess cup size, and brew strength is difficult to assess by
self-report. This could lead to substantial misclassification
of the amount of coffee consumed. Given the large differen-
ces in habitual coffee consumption in many populations
(e.g., 6 or more cups per day vs no consumption (Lopez-
Garcia et al. 2006)), it seems possible to determine the ef-
fects of coffee on disease risk. However, misclassification
of the amount of coffee consumed complicates the establish-
ment of health effects for specific amounts of coffee.
Biomarkers of coffee consumption
Blood concentrations of caffeine or caffeine metabolites
reflect caffeine intake in the previous hours (caffeine has a
plasma half life of ~4 h) (Blanchard and Sawers 1983). Be-
cause blood caffeine concentrations decrease rather rapidly,
the measured concentrations depend heavily on the timing
of coffee consumption during the day. In addition, caffeine
intake may not correlate strongly with coffee intake, de-
pending on the intake of other sources of caffeine (cola bev-
erages, tea, chocolate) and the intake of decaffeinated
coffee. An alternative biomarker for coffee consumption is
the urinary excretion of phenolic compounds. Ito et al.
(2005) found that consumption of 200 mL of instant coffee
significantly increased concentrations of chlorogenic acid in
urine collected in the subsequent 24 h. In line with this find-
ing, Mennen et al. (2006) reported that coffee consumption
assessed by diet records correlated significantly with chloro-
genic acid (r = 0.63) concentrations in spot morning urine
samples and, to a lesser extent, with these concentrations in
24 h urine samples in 53 French men and women. Although
coffee can be expected to be the main source of chlorogenic
acid in coffee drinkers, other foods, such as apples, contain
limited amounts of chlorogenic acid (Clifford 1999). Further
studies are needed to evaluate the validity of biomarkers of
coffee consumption in various populations.
Confounding
Heavy coffee consumption tends to be strongly associated
with cigarette smoking (Greenberg et al. 2005) and, in many
populations, with a generally less health conscious lifestyle
and lower socioeconomic status (van Dam and Feskens
2002). Therefore, it is important to take into account potential
confounding by smoking and other lifestyle factors when try-
ing to establish the independent health effects of coffee. Be-
cause the assessment of these potential confounders is also
affected by measurement error, adjustment for confounding
can be incomplete, even if the variable is adjusted for.

where the occurrence of disease may affect memory or re-
porting of past health-related behaviors. This is particularly
relevant for behaviors that the subject may associate with
his or her disease condition, such as smoking, alcohol con-

consumption are sparse. In 1 study, differences in coffee
and tea intake assessed before and after breast cancer diag-

Also, recall of past diet tends to be influenced by current
diet, and treatment or dietary advice can change the current
diet after participants develop a disease. In prospective stud-
ies, coffee consumption is assessed before the disease of in-
terest occurs, and therefore future disease is unlikely to be
related to the accuracy of the reporting of coffee consump-
tion.
Reverse causation
Among the general public and health professionals, be-
liefs about putative relations between coffee and various
health conditions are common. Because coffee consumption
is often perceived to be an unhealthy habit, quitting or re-
ducing the consumption of coffee may be one of the life-
style changes that people make in an attempt to improve
their health. These changes in coffee habits as a result of
awareness of health conditions can result in a lower coffee
consumption among people with these conditions than
among their healthy counterparts. Hence, cross-sectional as-
sociations between coffee consumption and health outcomes
that people are aware of are likely to be biased. Similarly,
cross-sectional associations between coffee and disease risk
factors may confound the association between coffee and
disease incidence. For example, diagnosed hypertension can
confound the association between coffee consumption and
risk of coronary heart disease, because it can be a reason to
reduce coffee consumption and it is a risk factor for coro-
nary heart disease.
Coffee and type 2 diabetes
Coffee and risk of type 2 diabetes
In 2002, a significant inverse association between coffee
consumption and risk of type 2 diabetes was reported in a
prospective cohort study in the Netherlands (van Dam and
Feskens 2002). To date, this association has been studied in
16 other prospective cohort studies, and all but 3 have sug-
gested a substantially lower risk of type 2 diabetes with fre-
quent
coffee
consumption
than
with
nonconsumption
(Carlsson et al. 2004; Greenberg et al. 2005; Hamer et al.
2008; Iso et al. 2006; Paynter et al. 2006; Pereira et al.
2006; Reunanen et al. 2003; Rosengren et al. 2004; Salazar-
Martinez et al. 2004; Saremi et al. 2003; Schulze et al.
2007; Smith et al. 2006; Tuomilehto et al. 2004; van Dam
et al. 2004a, 2006) (Table 3). The consistent association
across diverse populations in the United States, Europe, and
Japan reduces the likelihood that these findings are due to
confounding. Furthermore, the results tended to be strength-
ened by more complete adjustment for potential confound-
ers, and were consistent in subgroups according to diabetes
risk factors (van Dam and Hu 2005). Associations were sim-
ilar for men and women and for obese and nonobese partici-
pants. Findings of most studies suggest a dose–response
relation, with greater reductions in diabetes risk with higher
levels of coffee consumption. Because of misclassification
in coffee consumption, particularly related to cup size and
brew strength (see The assessment of coffee consumption),
it is difficult to precisely estimate reductions in risk by lev-
els of consumption. Consumption of 4 or more cups of cof-
fee
per
day
has
generally
been
associated
with
a
substantially lower risk of type 2 diabetes, whereas results
have been more variable for lower levels of consumption.
Because most cohort studies were based on clinical dia-
betes ascertained by self-report or disease registries, diag-
nostic bias could affect the results because a greater
proportion of diabetes cases may be undiagnosed in heavy
coffee consumers. However, multiple cross-sectional studies
have reported that higher coffee consumption is associated
with lower postload plasma glucose concentrations among
persons without diabetes (van Dam and Hu 2005). In addi-
tion, 2 prospective studies that included repeated oral glu-
cose tolerance tests in all participants found that coffee
consumption was associated with a lower incidence of im-
paired glucose tolerance and type 2 diabetes (Smith et al.
2006; van Dam et al. 2004a).
The effects of caffeine vs other coffee components
Five American studies have examined the association be-
tween decaffeinated coffee consumption and risk of type 2
diabetes (Greenberg et al. 2005; Pereira et al. 2006; Salazar-
Martinez et al. 2004; van Dam et al. 2006). In all these stud-
ies, except for the older subgroup of the NHANES cohort
(Greenberg et al. 2005), decaffeinated coffee consumption
was significantly associated with a lower risk of type 2
diabetes.
In
a
British
study
(Hamer
et
al.
2008),
decaffeinated coffee consumption also tended to be asso-

quent consumers (relative risk, 0.65; 95% CI, 0.36–1.16

Sprague–Dawley rats that were fed a high-fat diet (Shearer
et al. 2007). Beneficial effects of the coffee components
chlorogenic acid and quinides on glucose homeostasis have
also been found in animal models of diabetes (Andrade-
Cetto and Wiedenfeld 2001; Rodriguez de Sotillo and
Hadley 2002; Shearer et al. 2003). In humans, decaffeinated
coffee consumption acutely reduced the blood glucose re-
sponse after an oral glucose load, compared with placebo
(Battram et al. 2006). Hence, various lines of evidence indi-
cate that components of coffee other than caffeine may have
beneficial effects on glucose metabolism.

than increase the risk of type 2 diabetes. However, the pos-

study. For individuals with diabetes mellitus, evidence from

to exaggerated postprandial blood glucose responses (Lane
et al. 2008). Although longer-term data on glycemic control
are needed, patients could evaluate whether switching from
caffeinated to decaffeinated coffee has beneficial effects on
their glycemic control.
Coffee, risk markers, and cardiovascular
diseases
Coffee and cholesterol concentrations
Coffee consumption has been found to increase LDL-
cholesterol concentrations, but more recent studies have
shown that this effect depends on the type of coffee con-
sumed (Jee et al. 2001). Unfiltered types of coffee, such as
boiled coffee, can substantially increase LDL-cholesterol
levels, whereas paper-filtered and instant coffee do not have
substantial effects on serum lipoproteins (Jee et al. 2001).
Based on 5 trials (median coffee intake, 6 cupsday–1), Jee
et al. (2001) estimated that consumption of boiled coffee
increased serum LDL-cholesterol 17.8 mgdL–1 (95% CI,
11.1–24.5) over filtered coffee. The diterpene cafestol in the
lipid-soluble
fraction
of coffee
is responsible for this
cholesterol-raising effect (Zock et al. 1990; Weusten-Van
der Wouw et al. 1994). Thus, for the prevention of cardio-
vascular diseases, high consumption of unfiltered types of
coffee, such as boiled and French press coffee, should be
avoided.
Coffee and homocysteine concentrations
Coffee has been associated with higher blood homo-
cysteine concentrations in population-based studies (de Bree
et al. 2001). Consistent this observation, coffee consumption
increased homocysteine concentrations in randomized cross-
over trials in healthy volunteers (Urgert et al. 2000). It has
been shown that both caffeine and chlorogenic acid contrib-
ute this effect (Olthof et al. 2001; Verhoef et al. 2002).
Homocysteine has been associated with a higher risk of cor-
onary heart disease in many prospective studies (Wierzbicki
2007). It is unclear, however, if homocysteine is causally re-
lated to cardiovascular disease or whether it is only a marker
of disease risk. To date, intervention studies aimed at lower-
ing homocysteine concentrations have not found beneficial
effects on cardiovascular health (Wierzbicki 2007).
Coffee and blood pressure
Caffeine intake has been shown to lead to a marked in-
crease in blood pressure in people who do not habitually
use caffeine (Robertson et al. 1981). However, these find-
ings cannot be directly translated to long-term consumption
of caffeinated coffee. First, at least partial tolerance to the
effects of caffeine on blood pressure develops within a
week (Robertson et al. 1981). Second, trials comparing caf-
feinated with decaffeinated coffee seem to find weaker ef-
fects than trials comparing caffeine with placebo capsules.
A meta-analysis of randomized trials with intervention dura-
tions of at least 7 days separately estimated the effect of caf-
feine (7 trials; median dose, 410 mgday–1) and caffeinated
coffee (18 trials; median intake, 725 mLday–1) (Noordzij et
al. 2005). The pooled effects on systolic and diastolic blood
pressure were 4.2 (95% CI, 2.1–6.2) and 2.4 (95% CI, 1.0–
3.8) mm Hg, respectively, for caffeine, and 1.2 (95% CI,
0.5–1.9) mm Hg and 0.5 (95% CI, –0.1–+1.0) mm Hg, re-
spectively, for caffeinated coffee. It is possible that coffee
components other than caffeine reduce the effect of caffeine.
For example, quinides may counteract caffeine-induced ad-
enosine receptor antagonism by inhibiting the adenosine re-
ceptor transporter (de Paulis et al. 2002). Furthermore, green
coffee bean extracts, which are low in caffeine, reduced
blood pressure in a randomized trial in Japan (Watanabe et
al. 2006). In spontaneously hypertensive rats, the coffee
component chlorogenic acid also reduced blood pressure
(Suzuki et al. 2006). Results of a prospective cohort study
also suggested that the effect of coffee on blood pressure
may be different than would be expected from its caffeine
content: intake of caffeinated cola, but not caffeinated cof-
fee, was associated with a higher incidence of hypertension
(Winkelmayer et al. 2005). Other cohort studies reported no
significant association between coffee consumption and risk
of hypertension (Klag et al. 2002) or a lower risk of hyper-
tension in both abstainers or heavy consumers as compared
with those with a modest coffee consumption (Hu et al.
2007; Uiterwaal et al. 2007). Overall, caffeinated coffee
consumption does not seem to be a strong risk factor for hy-
pertension.
Coffee and risk of coronary heart disease
Many studies have looked at coffee consumption in
relation to risk of coronary heart disease. As described in 3
systematic reviews, the results of the case–control studies
suggest a substantial association between coffee consump-
tion and risk of coronary heart disease (Greenland 1993;
Kawachi et al. 1994; Sofi et al. 2007). In the most recent
meta-analysis, the summary of odds ratio of coronary heart
disease for 13 case–control studies was 1.83 (95% CI, 1.49–

15 cohort studies did not suggest any substantial association
between coffee consumption and risk of coronary heart dis-
ease (Greenland 1993; Kawachi et al. 1994; Sofi et al.
2007). Although the results from a systematic review by
Greenland (1993) suggested that results from the most re-
cently published cohort studies were consistent with a mod-
estly higher risk of coronary heart disease for heavy coffee
consumption, this was not substantiated by cohort studies
published after 1993. For example, Lopez-Garcia et al.
(2006) reported data from prospective US cohort studies of
44 005 men and 84 488 women without a history of cardio-
vascular disease or cancer at baseline. They documented
2173 incident cases of coronary heart disease among men
and 2254 cases among women during the 14–20 years of
follow-up. A strength of this study was the repeated assess-
ment of coffee consumption every 2 to 4 years. After adjust-
ment for age, smoking, and other risk factors, the relative
risks of coronary heart disease for 6 or more cups of caffein-

was 0.72 (0.49–1.07; p = 0.41 for trend) among men and
0.87 (0.68–1.11; p = 0.08 for trend) among women. Stratifi-
cation by smoking status, alcohol consumption, history of
type 2 diabetes mellitus, and body mass index gave similar
results. Also, a recently published cohort study of Swedish
women, aged 40–72 years, did not find any suggestion of a
direct association between coffee consumption and risk of
myocardial infarction (Rosner et al. 2007). After adjustment
for age, coronary heart disease risk factors, and dietary vari-
ables, the relative risk of myocardial infarction was 0.65
(95% CI, 0.37–1.12) for 6 or more cups per day, compared
with 0–4 cupsweek–1. In an observational analysis of the
GISSI Prevenzione trial of 11 231 Italian patients with re-
cent myocardial infarction, 1167 cardiovascular events oc-
curred (Silletta et al. 2007). After adjustment for potential
confounders, the relative risk of cardiovascular events was
0.88 (95% CI, 0.64–1.20) for more than 4 cups of coffee
per day, compared with nonconsumption. In 2 recent anal-
yses of US cohort studies, moderate consumption of coffee
or caffeine was associated with a reduced cardiovascular
mortality (Andersen et al. 2006; Greenberg et al. 2007).
Overall, results from prospective cohort studies do not indi-
cate that coffee consumption is associated with a substan-
tially increased risk of coronary heart disease. It should be
noted, however, that most of these studies did not include
participants with heavy consumption of unfiltered types of
coffee, such as boiled and French press coffee.
Potential explanation for heterogeneity in study results
There are several factors that may contribute to the differ-
ences in results between case–control and cohort studies of
coffee consumption and coronary heart disease. Methodo-
logical limitations that may have affected the results of
case–control studies include recall bias, selection bias, resid-
ual confounding, and publication bias. First, it is important
to consider the possibility of selection bias. If controls are
selected from other patient groups in hospitals, the condition
of these patients may have affected their coffee consump-
tion. This bias may even occur for hospital-based controls
who were admitted for illnesses not known to be related to
coffee intake. Rosenberg et al. (1981) studied coffee con-
sumption in potential hospital-based controls with different
reasons for hospital admission. The proportion of users of
caffeinated coffee was significantly lower in patients admit-
ted for chronic conditions (e.g., musculoskeletal conditions)
than in those admitted for acute conditions (e.g., fractures).
In addition, caffeinated coffee consumption decreased with
increasing contact with medical care in the year previous to
admission. Thus, inappropriate control selection could lead
to lower coffee consumption in controls than in the source
population of the cases, and consequently a biased associa-
tion between coffee consumption and coronary heart disease.
Selection bias can also occur if population-based controls
are selected: controls that are responders are likely to be
more health conscious than nonresponders, and health
consciousness
may
be
associated
with
lower
coffee
consumption. Because only nonfatal cases are included in
case–control studies, a relation between coffee consumption
and improved survival after myocardial infarction would
also result in selection bias. Although there are limited data
suggesting an inverse association between moderate coffee
consumption and cardiovascular death (Andersen et al.
2006), this type of selection bias seems less likely to explain
the findings of case–control studies. Second, recall bias may
affect results in case–control studies: patients may over-
estimate their past coffee consumption after having experi-
enced a coronary event. Third, residual confounding by risk
factors for coronary heart disease that are associated with
coffee consumption, such as smoking, may lead to an over-
estimation of the association between coffee consumption
and coronary heart disease. Residual confounding can affect
results of both case–control and cohort studies, but reporting
of potential confounders in the past, before disease occur-
rence, may be less accurate than reporting of current charac-
teristics in a disease-free state. Finally, publication bias may
affect the overall results of studies in the literature. Gener-
ally, studies reporting associations are more likely to get
published than studies reporting a lack of association (Egger
and Smith 1998). It is possible that ‘‘null results’’ from
case–control studies, which are generally considered to be
more prone to bias, are less likely to get published than null
results from prospective cohort studies.
We should also consider the possibility that cohort studies
have missed an existing association between coffee con-
sumption and coronary heart disease. One reason for this
could be that case–control studies better captured short-term
effects of coffee, because consumption in the period shortly
before the event is often assessed in case–control studies. To
address this issue, coffee consumption has been studied in
relation to coronary heart disease in only the 2 years after
the assessment of coffee consumption in prospective cohort
studies (Grobbee et al. 1990; Willett et al. 1996). No associ-
ation between recent coffee consumption and coronary heart
disease was observed in either men or women.
Another factor that may have led to the heterogeneity in
results of studies of coffee consumption and coronary heart
disease is the variation in genetic susceptibility to the effects
of coffee components. In a recent case–control study,
associations between coffee consumption and acute nonfatal
myocardial infarction differed by the genotype for the

Coffee consumption as a potential trigger of coronary
heart disease
Coffee consumption can acutely stimulate the sympathetic
nervous system and increase blood pressure, particularly in
nonhabitual coffee drinkers in whom no tolerance has devel-
oped (Robertson et al. 1981). It has been hypothesized that
coffee consumption may act as a trigger for myocardial
infarction as a result of this transient sympathetic nervous
system activation (Baylin et al. 2006). In a Croatian case–
crossover study of triggers of sudden cardiac death, the
relative risk of dying within 1 h of coffee consumption,
compared with other times of the day, was 1.73 (95% CI,
1.13–2.65) (Sˇelb Sˇemerl and Sˇelb 2004). In a cross-over
study
in

habits: the relative risk was 4.14 (95% CI, 2.03–8.42) for
occasional consumers (£1 cupday–1; n = 9), 1.60 (95% CI,
1.16–2.21) for intermediate consumers (2–3 cupsday–1; n =
44), and 1.06 (95% CI, 0.69–1.63) for frequent consumers
(‡4 cupsday–1; n = 27). These findings suggest that coffee
consumption may act as a trigger for myocardial infarction
in persons with infrequent coffee consumption. However, it
cannot be excluded that recall bias affected the results (i.e.,
that cases were more likely to remember coffee consumption
shortly before the occurrence of their myocardial infarction
when asked the question: When was the last time you had
coffee before your heart attack?). Also, confounding by
other triggers associated with the time of coffee consump-
tion (e.g., food consumption, physical exertion, psychologi-
cal stress) may have occurred (Cˇ ulic´ 2007). Confirmation in
other populations is therefore warranted. It has also been
pointed out that the observed relative risks should be inter-
preted in the context of the very low absolute risk of myo-
cardial infarction in any given hour (Poole 2007).
Coffee consumption and stroke
Few studies have examined the association between cof-
fee consumption and risk of stroke. In the Honolulu Heart
Program, coffee consumption was associated with a higher
risk of thromboembolic stroke in men with hypertension
(relative risk, 2.1; 95% CI, 1.2–3.7) (Hakim et al. 1998).
However, that analysis was based on only 55 cases. In stud-
ies of US male health professionals (Grobbee et al. 1990)
and Italian patients (Silletta et al. 2007) with myocardial in-
farction, no association between coffee consumption and
stroke was observed. In a recent analysis of a large cohort
study of male Finnish smokers, high coffee consumption
was associated with a lower risk of cerebral infarction (rela-
tive risk, 0.77; 95% CI, 0.66–0.90, p < 0.001 for trend)
(Larsson et al. 2008). No association was observed between
coffee consumption and risk of intracerebral or subarachnoid
hemorrhage. Overall, these findings do not suggest that cof-
fee consumption substantially increases risk of stroke.
Coffee and risk of cancer
Coffee consumption has been associated with a higher or
lower risk of various types of cancers, but many of these as-
sociations have not been confirmed in subsequent, better-
designed
studies.
For

have been due to an invalid selection of controls (Mac-

studies generally did not find an association between coffee
consumption and pancreatic cancer (Michaud et al. 2001).
Conversely, results from case–control studies suggested an
inverse association between coffee consumption and colon
cancer, but this has generally not been supported by results
from prospective cohort studies (Giovannucci 1998; Larsson
et al. 2006; Michels et al. 2005; Naganuma et al. 2007).
The overall evidence generally does not support a substan-
tial relation between coffee consumption and various types
of cancer, including bladder cancer (Zeegers et al. 2004),
gastric cancer (Botelho et al. 2006), renal cancer (Lee et al.
2007), and breast cancer (Ganmaa et al. 2008). Exceptions
are an inverse association between coffee and liver cancer
and a direct association between maternal coffee consump-
tion and childhood leukemia, both of which are discussed
below.

Coffee during pregnancy in relation to childhood
leukemia and birth outcomes
A few studies have reported an association between the
coffee
consumption
of
mothers
during
pregnancy
and
leukemia in their children (Menegaux et al. 2005; Ross
1998). This research was conducted using retrospective
case–control studies that relyied on the mothers’ recall of
coffee consumption habits during pregnancy. Studies of this
type are susceptible to recall and selection bias, and other
studies have not confirmed this association (Peters et al.
1994; Petridou et al. 1997).
High caffeine or caffeinated coffee intake during preg-
nancy has also been associated with modestly lower birth
weight and a higher risk of miscarriage and stillbirth (Xue
et al. 2008; Signorello and McLaughlin 2004). In a prospec-
tive cohort study in Denmark, consumption of 4 or more
cups of coffee per day was associated with a higher risk of
fetal death than no coffee consumption (Bech et al. 2005).
In a recent US cohort study, daily consumption of >200 mg
of caffeine was associated with an increased risk of miscar-
riage, compared with no caffeine intake (Weng et al. 2008).
Not all studies have observed associations between maternal
coffee consumption during pregnancy and fetal death, and
methodological limitations, such as confounding by other
lifestyle factors and pregnancy symptoms, may have biased
the results (Savitz et al. 2008; Signorello and McLaughlin
2004). For example, nausea during pregnancy is associated
with a substantially lower risk of miscarriage and can reduce
the preference for coffee (Signorello and McLaughlin 2004).
As a result, high coffee consumption during pregnancy can
be a marker of poor fetal development. Adjustment for nau-
sea did not explain the association between coffee consump-
tion and fetal death (Weng et al. 2008), but residual
confounding due to reporting errors or lack of consideration
of severity, frequency, and dynamic changes in symptoms
cannot be excluded. However, it is known that caffeine read-
ily crosses the placenta and, based on data from newborns, it
is likely that the fetus metabolizes caffeine very slowly
(Dlugosz and Bracken 1992). These data combined suggest
that it is prudent for pregnant women to limit their caffeine
intake.
Conclusions
Numerous epidemiological studies have evaluated the as-
sociation between coffee consumption and risk of type 2
diabetes, coronary heart disease, and various cancers. Sev-
eral early studies suggested that coffee consumption could
result in a marked increase in risk of coronary heart disease
and several types of cancer. However, more recent prospec-
tive cohort studies that are less prone to methodological
biases have not confirmed these findings. High consumption
of unfiltered types of coffee, such as French press and
boiled coffee, has been shown to increase LDL-cholesterol
concentrations. The use of paper-filtered or instant coffees,
which have very low concentrations of cholesterol-raising
diterpenes, can therefore be recommended. Caffeine in-
gested by the mother can be expected to lead to prolonged
caffeine exposure in the fetus, and there is suggestive evi-
dence linking high maternal caffeine intake during preg-
nancy with adverse effects on birth outcomes and child
health.
Limiting
caffeine
intake
during
pregnancy
to
<200 mgday–1 therefore seems a prudent choice. Although
it has not been established whether reducing caffeinated cof-
fee consumption has long-term benefits on blood pressure or
glycemic control, individuals with hypertension and type 2
diabetes could try switching from caffeinated to decaffei-
nated coffee to see if it improves their condition. Evidence
has been accumulating that coffee consumption may reduce
the risk of type 2 diabetes and liver cancer. Consumption of
decaffeinated coffee has also been associated with a lower
risk of type 2 diabetes, suggesting that coffee components
other than caffeine are responsible for this putative effect.
Further experimental studies are warranted to elucidate the
underlying mechanisms and, possibly, to identify coffee
components with beneficial health effects.
In sum, the currently available evidence on coffee and
risk of cardiovascular diseases and cancer is largely reassur-
ing, and suggests that addressing other health-related behav-
iors (e.g., quitting smoking, increasing physical activity) has
priority for the prevention of chronic diseases. Avoidance of
high intakes of unfiltered coffee and, for specific population
groups, caffeine seems wise. Further studies on coffee com-
ponents may lead to more insights on the health effects of
specific types of coffee.
