Summary
The relation between coffee, decaffeinated coffee, tea and oral/phar-
yngeal, and esophageal cancer risk is inadequately quantiﬁed. Data were derived from
hospital-based case-control studies conducted in Italy and Switzerland. The study on
oral/pharyngeal cancer included 749 cases and 1772 controls, and that of esophageal
cancer 395 cases and 1066 controls. Multivariate odds ratios (OR) and 95% conﬁdence
intervals (CI) were computed. The OR for >3 cups/day of coffee compared with 41
were 0.6 (95% CI 0.5—0.9) for oral/pharyngeal, and 0.6 (95% CI 0.4—0.9) for esophageal
cancer, consistent across strata of age, sex, education and alcohol. The inverse trends
in risk were signiﬁcant. No association emerged with decaffeinated coffee (OR 1.1 for
oral/pharyngeal and 0.6 for esophageal cancer) or tea (OR 0.9 for both cancers), con-
sumed in low amounts by these populations. Coffee may decrease the risk of oral/
pharyngeal and esophageal cancer.

1. Introduction
The relation between the intake of beverages
containing methylxanthines, particularly coffee,
and risk of cancer of the oral cavity, pharynx and
esophagus has not been extensively studied. Evi-
dence published before 1990 was reviewed in a
Monograph
of
the
International
Agency
for
Research on Cancer.1 It included six studies which
found little evidence of association with coffee
consumption, except for a possible association
with very high temperature.1 Since then, at least
one
cohort2
and
some
case-control
studies37
found no appreciable relation between coffee
intake and cancers of the upper digestive tract.

2.1. Data analysis
Odds ratios (OR) and the corresponding 95% con-
ﬁdence intervals (CI) for coffee, decaffeinated
coffee and tea intake were derived using uncondi-
tional multiple logistic regression, ﬁtted by the
method of maximum likelihood.18 All regression
models included terms for age, sex, study centre,
education, tobacco smoking, alcohol drinking and
intake of fruit and vegetables.

Table 2 shows the distribution and the corre-
sponding OR of cancers of the upper digestive tract
according to intake of coffee, decaffeinated coffee
and tea. Compared to drinkers of one or less cups
of coffee per day, those drinking more than three
cups per day had an OR of oral and pharyngeal
cancer of 0.6 (95% CI 0.5—0.9); the corresponding
value for esophageal cancer was 0.6 (95% CI 0.4—
0.9). Both trends in risk with number of cups per
day were signiﬁcant. The OR for an increment of
one cup of coffee per day was 0.9 (95% CI 0.9—1.0)
for the two cancer sites combined. Less than 5% of
cases and controls drank decaffeinated coffee and
the OR for regular drinkers of one or more cups per
day compared to occasional or non drinkers was 1.1
(95% CI 0.6—1.9) for oral and pharyngeal, and 0.6
(95% CI 0.2—1.5) for esophageal cancer. With
reference to tea, the OR for regular drinkers of one
or more cups per day compared to occasional or
non drinkers were 0.9 (95% CI 0.7—1.1) and 0.9
(95% CI 0.7—1.2), respectively for cancers of the
oral cavity and pharynx, and the esophagus.
The inverse association with either cancer sites
according to an intake of more than three cups of
coffee per day was consistent across strata of sex,
age, education and alcohol drinking, but appar-
ently stronger in current smokers compared to non
smokers (Table 3).
4. Discussion
These results support the presence of an inverse
association between coffee intake and risk of can-
cer of oral cavity, pharynx and esophagus. No
material modiﬁcation of risk was found in relation
to decaffeinated coffee and tea intake, although
consumption of these beverages was limited in
these populations, and consequently this study had
inadequate power to detect any possible relation.
The two studies combined in the present analysis
are hospital-based, but cases were identiﬁed in the
major teaching and general hospitals of the area
under surveillance and the participation of cases
and controls was similar, thus reducing the possi-
bility of selection bias. We excluded from the con-
trol
group
patients
admitted
to
hospital
for
smoking- and alcohol-related diseases, and for
chronic conditions and digestive tract diseases,
which could have affected lifestyle habits. A satis-
factory comparability of coffee information was
found for subjects interviewed at home and in the

ting for cases and controls also provides reassurance
against potential information bias,19 and there is no
reason to suggest different selective recall of coffee
and tea intake on the basis of disease status, as these
beverages are not known to affect oral, pharyngeal
and esophageal cancer risk. The results were similar
when the Italian and Swiss populations were con-
sidered separately, the OR being, respectively, 0.6
and 0.7 for more than three cups per day compared to
one or more cups for the two cancer sites combined.
Coffee and tea may substitute alcohol drinking
out of meals and this might explain the apparent
protective effect of high coffee intake on cancer at
these sites. In this analysis, accurate allowance
was made for alcohol drinking, tobacco smoking,
vegetable and fruit consumption and other major
covariates of interest. Moreover, an indirect sup-
port for a real inverse association between coffee
intake and oropharyngeal and esophageal cancer
comes from the consistent relation across strata of
age, sex, alcohol and the other considered vari-
ables. Indeed, the inverse association was appar-
ently stronger in heavy smokers, although the test
for heterogeneity was not signiﬁcant.
The inverse association between coffee intake
and cancer risk ﬁnds plausible biological support in
terms of the high antioxidant properties of coffee
beverages.20,21 Coffee beans contain several phe-
nolic compounds with antioxidant properties, such
as caffeic acid and chlorogenic acid, partly lost
during roasting.20 The roasting process, however,
has a complex effect on coffee bean pro-oxidant
and antioxidant properties, since during roasting,
although some pro-oxidant reactive radicals are
formed, the overall antioxidant properties of cof-
fee brews are maintained or enhanced by the
Maillard reaction,22 whose products have strong
reducing properties, increasing with the intensity
of the heating process.
