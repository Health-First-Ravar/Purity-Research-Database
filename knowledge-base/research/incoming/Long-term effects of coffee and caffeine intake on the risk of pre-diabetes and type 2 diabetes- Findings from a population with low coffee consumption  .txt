Abstract
Background and aim: Here, we examined the potential effect of coffee consumption
and total caffeine intake on the occurrence of pre-diabetes and T2D, in a population with low
coffee consumption.
Methods and Results: Adults men and women, aged 20e70 years, were followed for a median of
5.8 y. Dietary intakes of coffee and caffeine were estimated using a 168-food items validate semi-
quantitative food frequency questionnaire, at baseline. Cox proportional hazards regression
models, adjusted for potential cofounders, were used to estimate hazard ratios (HRs) and 95%
conﬁdence intervals (CIs) for the association between coffee and caffeine intakes and incidence
of pre-diabetes and T2D. The total population was 1878 adults (844 men, 1034 women) and 2139
adults (971 men, 1168 women) for analysis of pre-diabetes and T2D, respectively.
During the follow-up period the incidence of pre-diabetes and T2D was 30.8% and 6.6%,
respectively. Forty-three percent of our subjects were no coffee drinker whereas 51.4% consumed
1 cup of coffee/week and 6.0% consumed more than 1 cup of coffee/week. A lower risk of pre-
diabetes (HR Z 0.73, 95% CI Z 0.62e0.86) and T2D (HR Z 0.66, 95% CI Z 0.44e1.00) was
observed in coffee drinkers compared to non-drinkers, in the fully adjusted models. Higher di-
etary intake of caffeine (152 vs. <65 mg/d) was accompanied with a borderline (P Z 0.053)
reduced risk of pre-diabetes (HR Z 0.45, 95% CI Z 0.19e1.00).
Conclusion: Our ﬁndings indicated that coffee drinking may have favorable effect in prevention of
pre-diabetes and T2D.

approaches to prevent its development, and the role of coffee
consumption has gained a lot attention [1]. Regular con-
sumption ofcoffee and dietary intake of caffeine (from coffee,
tea, soft drinks and chocolates) have been suggested to be
linked to glucose and insulin metabolism, and development
of T2D, as well [2e4]. Accumulating data suggest that coffee-
mediated effects on metabolic functions can be linked to
thermogenic effects, reduction of oxidative stress, modula-
tion of immune cell function, and modulation of adenosine
signaling as well as the microbiome content and diversity [1].
Although some beneﬁcial effect of coffee on glucose and in-
sulin homeostasis has been attributed to its caffeine content,
various studies reported protective effects of decaffeinated
coffee on diabetes [4], suggesting that other components of
coffee like chlorogenic acid, lignans, trigonelline, N-methyl-
pyridinium, minerals and vitamins (e.g. magnesium, potas-
sium, niacin), proteins and lipids in special diterpenes (e.g.
cafestol and kahweol) maycontribute to the observed effects.
Recent systematic reviews with meta-analysis of pro-
spective studies conclude that regular consumption of coffee
and higher intake of caffeine is inversely associated with the
risk of T2D [1,4,5]. A vast majority of the studies documented
in these meta-analysis was derived from western and
developed countries where the confounding structures
related to coffee intake and diabetes may differ from devel-
opingorlow-middleincomecountries[6],includingdifferent
patterns and diverse range of coffee drinking and different
sources of caffeine intake (tea and coffee vs. caffeinated soft
drink). Despite current positive ﬁndings, recommending to
increased consumptionof coffee as a public health strategy to
prevent T2D is still premature [2], especially since the
mechanisms are not yet fully clear. Accordingly, more
population-based surveys seems to be required to clarify the
long-term effects of regular consumption of coffee and di-
etary intake of caffeine on the risk of T2D.
The potential inverse association between coffee and
caffeine intake and the risk of T2D is currently unknown in
Iranian adults, i.e. an Asian population with low rate of
coffee consumption. In this study, the aim was to deter-
mine whether regular consumption of coffee and total
caffeine intake from coffee, tea, soft drinks and chocolates
may modify the risk of pre-diabetes and T2D. To examine
these questions, we analyzed data from a large cohort
study of adult men and women participating in the Tehran
Lipid and Glucose Study (TLGS).

after

Written informed consent was obtained from all par-

Demographic and anthropometric measures
Trained interviewers collected information, including fa-
milial history of non-communicable disease, smoking
habits, and assessment of physical activity using standard
questionnaires. Detailed measurements of variables in
TLGS have been reported elsewhere [8]. Body weight was
measured to the nearest 100 g using digital scales, while
the subjects were minimally clothed, without shoes.
Height was measured to the nearest 0.5 cm, in a standing
position without shoes, using a tape meter. Body mass
index (BMI) was calculated as weight (kg) divided by
square of the height (m2). Waist circumference (WC) was
measured to the nearest 0.1 cm, midway between the
lower border of the ribs and the iliac crest at the widest
portion, over light clothing, using a soft measuring tape,
without any pressure to the body. Waist to height ratio
(WHtR) was calculated as WC divided by height (cm).
For measurements of systolic (SBP) and diastolic blood
pressure (DBP), after a 15-min rest in the sitting position,
two measurements of blood pressure were taken on the
right arm, during a standardized mercury sphygmoma-
nometer;
the
mean
of
the
two
measurements
was
considered as the participant’s blood pressure.
Physical activity was assessed using the Modiﬁable
Activity Questionnaire (MAQ); brieﬂy, the frequency and
time spent on light, moderate, hard and very hard in-
tensity activities were obtained according to the list of
common activities of daily life over the past year. The
reliability and convergent validity of the Persian version of
the MAQ had been investigated [9].

Dietary assessment
A validated 168-item food frequency questionnaire (FFQ)
was used to assess typical food intakes over the previous
year. Trained dietitians, with at least 5 years of experience
in the TLGS survey, asked participants to designate their
intake frequency for each food item consumed during the
past year on a daily, weekly, or monthly basis. Portion sizes
of consumed foods reported in household measures were
then converted to grams [10]. The validity of the FFQ has
previously been evaluated by comparing food groups and
nutrient values determined from the questionnaire with
values estimated from the average of twelve 24-h dietary
recall surveys and the reliability has been assessed by
comparing energy and nutrient intakes from two FFQ;
Pearson correlation coefﬁcients and intra-class correlation
for energy and nutrients showed acceptable agreements
between FFQ and twelve 24-h dietary recall surveys, and
FFQ1 and FFQ2 [11]. Participants were asked for frequency
of drinking coffee in a preceding year considering a given
portion size (cups per day or week or month). We did not
collect any information on the type of coffee and its
preparation methods in the current study.
However, since Iranian Food Composition Table has
limited data on nutrient content of raw foods and bever-
ages, the US Department of Agriculture Food Composition
Table was used to analyze foods and beverages for their
energy and nutrient content. Caffeine intake was calcu-
lated as mg/d from the coffee, tea, soft drinks and
chocolates.
Deﬁnition of terms and outcome
Participants who had impaired fasting glucose (IFG)
[fasting plasma glucose (FPG) levels 100 mg/dl (5.6 mmol/
l) to 125 mg/dl (6.9 mmol/l)], or impaired glucose toler-
ance (IGT) [2-h values in the OGTT of 140 mg/dl (7.8 mmol/
l) to 199 mg/dl (11.0 mmol/l)] were considered as pre-
diabetic subjects [12]. T2D was deﬁned using anti-diabetic
drugs, fasting plasma glucose (FPG)  126 mg/dL (7 mmol/
L) or 2 h plasma glucose (2-h PG)  200 mg/dL (11.1 mmol/
L) [13]. A positive family history of T2D was deﬁned as
having at least one parent or sibling with T2D. The dia-
betes risk score (DRS) was calculated as follows: SBP (mm
Hg) < 120 (0 point), 120 < SBP<140 (3 point), SBP 140 (7
point); family history of diabetes (5 point); waist to height
ratio (WHtR): <0.54 (0 point), 0.54e0.59 (6 point), 0.59
(11 point); TG/HDL-C: <3.5 (0 point), 3.5 (3 point); FPG
(mmol/L): <5 (0 point), 5.0e5.5 (12 point), 5.6e6.9 (33
point) [14]. Homeostatic model assessment of insulin
resistance
(HOMA-IR)
was

(mmol/L)/22.5 [15,16].
Statistical analysis
The incidence of T2D and pre-diabetes over the follow-up
period was considered as a dichotomous variable (yes/no)
in the models. The mean (SD) values and the frequency (%)
of baseline characteristics of the study participants ac-
cording to status of coffee drinking were compared using
independent t-test or chi-square test, respectively.
Cox
proportional
hazards
regression
models
with
person-year as the underlying time metric were used to
estimate hazard ratios (HRs) and 95% conﬁdence intervals
(CIs) for the association between coffee and caffeine in-
takes and incidence of pre-diabetes and T2D. HRs of the
outcomes were estimated across status of coffee drinking
(as “drinkers” and “non-drinkers”) and tertiles of caffeine
intakes. We also estimated hazards of pre-diabetes and
T2D per each 200 mg/d caffeine intakes. To assess the
overall trends of HRs across tertiles of caffeine intakes, the
median of each tertile was used as a continuous variable in
the Cox proportional hazard regression models.
The event date for cases was described as the middle-
time between the detection date of T2D/pre-diabetes for
the ﬁrst time, and the most recent follow-up visit pre-
ceding the diagnosis. Follow-up time was difference of the
mid-time date and the date of entrance. For the censored
and lost to follow-up subjects, the survival time was the
interval between the ﬁrst and the last observation dates.
Follow-up duration and person-years were calculated
using the measured survival time. Potential confounders
adjusted in the models were diabetes risk score (contin-
uous), physical activity (MET-h/week), smoking (yes/no),
total energy intake (kcal/d), dietary intake of ﬁber (g/d)
and carbohydrate (g/d).
To investigate the associations of caffeine and coffee
intakes in relation to serum insulin and insulin resistance
index, linear regression models with adjustment for age,

Discussion
In this prospective study we found that coffee drinking
may lower the risk of pre-diabetes and T2D among our
population. Coffee drinking was inversely related to insu-
lin levels and insulin resistance index. Compared with
participants in the lowest tertile of total caffeine intake
(<65 mg/d), those in the highest tertile (152 mg/d) had a
55% lower risk for pre-diabetes. Because drinking of tea
was main source of dietary caffeine (more than 90%) in our
population, the beneﬁcial effect of caffeine on the risk of
pre-diabetes must be related to the high intake of tea
rather than low intake of coffee.
Epidemiological and clinical studies have demonstrated
preventive or anti-diabetic effects of daily coffee con-
sumption. Findings from recent systematic reviews with
meta-analysis of prospective studies showed that coffee and
caffeine intake signiﬁcantly reduced risk of developing T2D
[1,4,5]. Doseeresponse analysis in the study by Jiang et al.

Possible association between coffee consumption and
total caffeine intake with long-term effects on glucose and
insulin homeostasis is less investigated. In a cross-sectional
study, coffee intake was inversely association with the 2-h
postload glucose and insulin responses, but not with fasting
glucose and insulin concentrations or glycated hemoglobin
levels [6]. The authors suggested that the favorable effects
of coffee intake on glucose and insulin homeostasis are
mainly coupled to postprandial modiﬁcations.
Compared to other populations investigated previously,
coffee drinking is much less common among Iranians di-
etary habits. The highest protective effect of coffee against
T2D has been mainly been observed with regular coffee
consumption of 3 cups per day [5,17]. In our study the
intake of coffee was much lower, with highest category of
coffee drinking being only one cup per week.
It is also should be noted that the genetic variants of the
liver enzyme Cytochrome P450 1A2 (CYP1A2), as the
strongest predictors of caffeine metabolism, may modify
the association coffee consumption and T2D [18]. In-
dividuals homozygous for the CYP1A2 *1 A/*1 A genotype
are fast caffeine metabolizers, whereas ‘slow metabolizers’
with *1 F allele have a higher circulating caffeine following
coffee consumption and may therefore be affected by a
greater impact of caffeine intake [18,19]. Coffee con-
sumption have been reported to contribute to develop-
ment of impaired fasting glucose in subjects who carries
the slow CYP1A2 *1F allele [19]; heavy coffee drinking (>3
cup/day) in subject who had the slow *1F allele led to
elevated risk of impaired fasting glucose (HR Z 2.8, 95%
CI Z 1.3e5.9) [19]. A genome-wide meta-analysis among
coffee consumers indicated six novel loci located in or near
genes potentially involved in pharmacokinetics (ABCG2,
AHR, POR and CYP1A2) and pharmacodynamics (BDNF and
SLC6A4) of caffeine [20]. Furthermore, the association
coffee consumption and T2D has been shown to be
affected by genetic polymorphisms related to T2D; in a
study by Lee at al., negative association of regular coffee
consumption with the risk of T2D and pre-diabetes was
limited to those with the GT/TT of IGF2BP2 rs4402960, GG/
GC of CDKAL1 rs7754840, or CC of KCNJ11 rs5215, which
are known to be related to T2D in East Asians [21]. In our
study we did not have the possibility to assess genome-
wide association to examine potential genetic variants in
relation to coffee and T2D. Therefore, we could not
perform a Mendelian randomization study to provide an
unbiased ‘causal’ estimate of the effect(s) of caffeine.
There are some strength in the current study. The
large, prospective population-based design, a high rate of
follow-up completeness, and use of a validated compre-
hensive FFQ to assess regular dietary intakes of the par-
ticipants

HDL-C ratio, and SBP, in multivariate models allowed us to
account for major pre-diabetes and T2D confounders
without adding many variables that would lead to insta-

In conclusion, the results of our investigation suggested
that higher intakes of caffeine from usual diet may have
favorable effect in prevention of pre-diabetes. Our ﬁndings
also implied that higher consumption of coffee and
caffeine may modify insulin levels and insulin resistance.
Moreover, one may speculate that the Iranian dietary
habit of low coffee consumption may contribute to the
relatively high prevalence of pre-diabetes and T2D in this
region. Of note, the results from this study cannot be
extended to coffee consumption and the risk of pre-
diabetes or T2D in Western countries or other regions.
