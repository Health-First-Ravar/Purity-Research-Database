Shelf-life Testing
Shelf-life assessment of foods has always represented an
exciting challenge for food scientists. During the last
decades, the prediction of food shelf life has been a topic of
continuous and extensive research. A lot of progress has
been made toward a scientiﬁc and generally accepted
approach to the study of the different deteriorative mech-
anisms occurring in food systems. Moreover, the correct
determination of the product shelf life is vital for the food
company to maintain its brand reputation on the market. In
fact, shelf-life testing may be an arduous process, but it is
certainly cheaper than a product recall.
To this purpose, the adoption of a systematic approach is
needed. Figure 1 schematically shows the steps for a cor-
rect shelf-life testing. The ﬁrst step identiﬁes the most
critical chemical, physical, or biological event leading to
the product quality depletion followed by the deﬁnition of
proper indicators. The next step is the evaluation of the
changes of the selected quality indicators as a function of
time under actual storage conditions to determine the
reaction rate by the application of the classic kinetic
approach. A number of articles dealing with this well-
recognized procedure have produced huge amount of data
on quality deterioration rate of several foods in different
environmental conditions [16, 29, 40, 44]. Although these
studies have supplied in-depth knowledge on food deteri-
oration kinetics, these kinetics are not equivalent to shelf-
life data. In order to convert kinetic data into shelf-life
data, knowing the acceptability limit is absolutely neces-
sary. Thus, shelf-life assessment requires the exact deﬁni-
tion of the criterion for assessing the end of product life.
Finally, once the shelf-life limit is available, such data can
be used to compute the shelf-life value by integrating it
into the classic kinetic models.
This methodology, which is based on the direct measure
of products’ shelf lives under their actual storage condi-
tions, can be easily used when quality decay occurs in
rather short times (i.e., perishable foods). Unfortunately, it
does not help when dealing with relatively stable products
with a medium to long life. In this case, the direct

An Insight on Coffee Quality Decay Reactions
The word coffee is a general term which comprises roasted
coffee (including decaffeinated coffee), derived beverages
and a wide variety of convenience and semi-manufactured
products such as instant coffee and coffee concentrates.
Although roasted whole and ground coffee are the main
coffee products present in the market, instant coffee and,
more recently, coffee concentrates and ready-to-drink
coffee brews represent interesting and dynamic products.
Depending on their physical and chemical properties,
coffee products undergo different deteriorative reactions,
have different sensitivities to environmental variables, and
thus have different shelf lives. For this reason, stability
problems relevant to roasted, instant, and liquid coffees
require a separate discussion. However, a common element

Roasted Coffee
Although generally recognized as a shelf-stable product,
roasted coffee undergoes important chemical and physical
changes which greatly affect quality and acceptability of
the coffee brew. The most important physical and chemical
events involved in roasted coffee staling during storage are
volatile and carbon dioxide release, surface oil migration,
and oxidation reactions.
Volatiles and CO2 are easily released into the vapor
phase due to diffusion mechanisms enhanced by the fact
that the pressure inside the bean pores is greater than
atmospheric pressure. The evolution of CO2 is of particular
importance because, although, it can be considered an
index of the aroma richness of roasted coffee, if released
after coffee packaging it produces over-pressure inside the
package with possible bursting and loss of package integ-
rity. The presence of a pressure gradient between bean
pores and exterior is also responsible for the migration of
lipids, together with their lipophilic solutes, onto the bean
surface. As a consequence of the increased exposed surface
area, these compounds become more prone to oxidation.
Volatile and CO2 release, together with oxidation reac-
tions, are considered the main causes of coffee staling. In
fact, the development of the typical stale ﬂavor of aged
coffee is the result of two different phenomena: the release
of volatiles in the vapor phase and the development of off-
ﬂavor as the result of oxidation of some of them [34].
Instant Coffee
Instant coffee has very long shelf stability if proper pack-
aging solutions are adopted. It is probably for this reason
that there are a limited number of studies on the stability
and shelf-life assessment of instant coffee. In contrast to
what happens to roasted and ground coffee, instant coffee
does not have the problem of a steady loss of CO2 during
storage. However, volatile release may be responsible for a
rapid quality loss of the product, because most of these
volatiles are susceptible to oxidation reactions. The extent
of oxidation can be enhanced when coffee oils are spread
over the granule surface to improve product aroma. Being
hygroscopic, instant coffee is also susceptible to the action
of moisture which affects volatile liquid–vapor partition-
ing. An increase in moisture content to 7–8% is responsible
for ‘caking’ where the powder or granules become a pasty
or solid mass [15]. Therefore, oxygen and moisture are the
environmental variables which can speedup quality decay.
Coffee Liquids
These products, generally pasteurized or sterilized and thus
stable from microbial spoilage, show a very low chemical
stability characterized by a change in the ﬂavor proﬁle and
an increase in perceived sourness. These changes are
accompanied by a pH decrease, corresponding to an
increase in titratable acidity. Quality depletion of liquid
coffee as well as of coffee concentrates starts immediately
after brewing and proceeds at a signiﬁcant rate, even at
sub-zero temperatures. At present, very little is known
about the mechanisms underlying liquid coffee instability.
It has been suggested that the decrease in pH could be the
consequence of complex reactions, probably related to
nonenzymatic browning pathways involving carbohydrates
and amino acids. Additional mechanisms involving lactone
hydrolysis could also contribute to pH decrease [7]. Similar
reactions have been reported for coffee brew aroma staling
which was mainly attributed to interactions between odor-
active thiols and melanoidins [22, 33].
Literature Data on Coffee Products Shelf life
The above-described methodologies for shelf-life predic-
tion should also be applied to coffee products. It is note-
worthy that the coffee world comprises a number of
different products including roasted whole and ground
coffee, but also a wide variety of convenient products such
as instant coffee, coffee concentrates, and ready-to-drink
coffee beverages. Unfortunately, very little shelf-life data
are available and most of them do not clearly report the
methodologies used for shelf-life assessment. Table 1 lists
literature data relevant to the shelf life of coffee products
packed under different conditions.
The shelf life of the roasted whole and ground coffee
packed in air either in metal cans or ﬂexible laminated
materials is very short, typically around 1 month. It can be
increased for up to 3 months by the adoption of one-way
safety valve allowing carbon dioxide to escape without
letting air in [26]. However, ﬂavors escape through the
valve together with CO2, reducing the coffee shelf life [34].
This problem is overcome by packing the coffee under
vacuum or modiﬁed atmosphere. The shelf life of roasted
coffee packed under vacuum in cans or ﬂexible laminated

further extended by replacing the air inside the package
with an inert gas such as N2 or CO2. The residual per-

The shelf life is extended up to 6–8 months. It should be
noted that such literature indications seem to contradict the
shelf-life dates actually reported on the labels of roasted
and ground coffee packed under vacuum in laminate foil
packages, which range from one to several years.
In combination with vacuum and inert gas packaging,
further atmosphere modiﬁcation inside the container can be
achieved by active packaging, such as oxygen or carbon
dioxide scavenger systems [45]. Although such systems are
reasonably expected to extend coffee shelf life, to our
knowledge, no shelf-life data are available in the literature
yet.
Longest shelf life of the roasted coffee is achieved by
creating a pressure inside the package which is greater than
in the coffee pores [34]. To this aim, a pressure higher than
the atmospheric one is generally adequate. By reducing the
pressure gradient between coffee and the environment,
pressurization prevents volatile and gas release from cof-
fee, allowing their solubilization into coffee oil and/or their
binding with melanoidins. According to the claims reported
by the producer on the labels, pressurization allows the
shelf life of coffee to be extended for up to 18 months.
However, no information is available on the criteria
adopted to reach such estimation.
Pods held in modiﬁed atmospheres are generally
claimed by the producers to have a shelf life higher than
18 months, not signiﬁcantly different from that of roasted
and ground multi-dose coffee. However, such indications
seem to contradict the shelf-life data reported for bulk
coffee packed under inert gas. It can be hypothesized that
shelf-life data reported for bulk coffee could be underes-
timated because it is based on actual product turnover and
not on the assessment of proper failure indices.
The few shelf-life data reported in the literature indicate
that instant coffee maintains its original quality for years,
provided that moisture content is less than 4–5% w/w to
keep the product below its glass transition point, and
oxygen present in the headspace is low enough to avoid
ﬂavor oxidation. The shelf life of un-opened instant coffee
varies from 18 to 36 months if stored in glass jars or tin-
plate cans, as indicated by producer claims.
Even less data are available on the shelf life of coffee
concentrates and beverages. By examining the products on
the market, shelf lives of coffee beverages are very dif-
ferent depending on the composition and especially the
soluble solids. Most beverages are claimed by the producer
to have a shelf life around 6 months but higher values can
also be found, especially for coffee concentrates intended
for coffee preparation by vending machines.
Main Criticisms in Shelf-Life Assessment of Coffee
Products
Despite the worldwide importance of coffee products, it is
evident that only limited and contradictory information is
available in the literature on coffee shelf life and relevant
assessment methodologies. Due to the lack of literature
data, the identiﬁcation of a reliable shelf life for the com-
plex range of coffee products must actually be performed
by applying the methodology reported in Fig. 1. It should
be kept in mind that the above methodology requires
adaptation to the speciﬁc product considered. Following
this approach, the application of the methodology is pro-
posed with reference to the different coffee derivatives
emphasizing pitfalls, uncertainties, and future perspectives.

Depletion and Identiﬁcation of Proper Indicators

identiﬁcation of the chemical, physical, or biological event

reactions occur in all coffee products but tend to be par-

water content, such as coffee concentrates and drinks. The
indicators of their development may be H3O? concentra-
tion, as assessed by a pH meter or by titration, as well as
assessment of sourness and overall acceptability by a
trained panel and a consumer panel, respectively.
Identiﬁcation of the Acceptability Limit
As shown in Fig. 1, shelf-life assessment requires the exact
deﬁnition of a criterion for the end of the product life.
Unfortunately, the considerations for the basis of deﬁning
the acceptance limit in the quality control programs of
coffee producers are often vague and inconsistent.
The procedure leading to the deﬁnition of the accep-
tance limit strictly depends on the nature of the deteriora-
tive reaction most limiting coffee product quality. Since
coffee products generally do not present safety risks, sen-
sory evaluation is the key factor for determining coffee
product acceptance limit, and hence shelf life. Neverthe-
less, the choice of scientists and companies for coffee
acceptance limits is nowadays still independent from the
actual attitude of the consumers to drink it or not. In fact, it
is evident that, at the end of shelf life, the product is cer-
tainly still acceptable by some consumers, despite being
rejected by others. To this regard, it has been recently
pointed out that shelf life is the result of the interaction of
foods with the consumers, since foods do not have sensory
shelf lives without the consumer [20, 24].
The evaluation of coffee acceptance limits, and hence
shelf life, need to be based on sensory studies involving
consumers [24, 38]. To this regard, the methodology pro-
viding comprehensive information to determine shelf life
using consumer data is based on survival analysis of food
acceptability [17, 18, 25]. According to the industrial
policy, the food company can choose more or less risk by
selecting, as the acceptance limit, the proper percentage of
consumers who reject the product.

Although this approach is very effective, it suffers the
basic criticism of the criterion used for deﬁning the end of
shelf life. In fact, according to their industrial policy, food
companies must choose the risk they are willing to accept,
by selecting the proper percentage of consumer rejecting
the product. Table 4 estimates the potential for unaccept-

In most shelf-life studies a medium risk level (50%
consumer rejection) is chosen as a reasonable acceptability
limit but it has been suggested that a 25% consumer
rejection could be much more reliable. Indeed, the choice
of this limit is arbitrary and shelf-life estimation is strongly
inﬂuenced by it. According to Guerra et al. [21], the choice
of the risk level can easily affect the ﬁnal shelf life value
from 20 to 100%. Food companies are generally not aware
of such uncertainty and tend to choose the acceptability
limit without statistically signiﬁcant marketing informa-
tion. It can be inferred that analysis of complaints collected
during product shelf life could allow companies to
rationally evaluate and justify their choices. However, at
the moment, no indications are reported in the literature
since consumer complaint data are not available.
In addition to this basic problem, it should be considered
that assembling consumer panels for shelf-life assessment
is expensive, not routinely performable by food companies,
and deﬁnitely not consistent with industrial needs. It would
be easier assessing instrumental (pH, headspace volatiles,
etc.) or sensory attributes (intensity of sourness, off-ﬂavor,
etc.) whose evolution could be correlated to consumer
acceptability [10, 19]. In order to follow this approach to
food shelf-life assessment, the issues to be resolved are: (i)
how consumer acceptability and analytical indices evolve
during food storage, (ii) which analytical indices best
correlate with consumer acceptability during food storage,
(iii) what value for these analytical indices cause con-
sumers to reject the product, and (iv) which mathematical
model of shelf life accounts for consumer acceptability.
The identiﬁcation of this relationship is a key step in
developing a simple and ready-to-use methodology for
shelf-life estimation skipping expensive and complex
consumer tests. It should be noted that any choice of a cut-
off value for a quality index, either instrumental or sen-
sorial, without clear knowledge of its relation with
consumer acceptability could induce mistakes in shelf-life
evaluation.
The relation between consumer rejection and easily
assessable quality indices may provide information to
assist companies in choosing acceptance limits. Figure 2
summarizes a possible methodology for the assessment of
the quality index limit corresponding to the consumer
acceptability limit.

As previously mentioned, ASLT could be exploited to
reduce the time span of shelf-life assessment. For example,
shelf-life testing of coffee derivatives can be sped up by
adopting temperature as the accelerating factor. ASLT
involving roasted coffee beans stored under air from 4 to
40C showed that the temperature dependence of head-
space volatile loss rate was well described by the Arrhenius
model [35]. In addition, Manzocco and Nicoli [31] studied
the chemical stability of coffee brew derivatives stored in
conventional atmosphere as a function of water activity and
storage temperature. As shown in Fig. 4, the temperature
dependence of hydrogen ion formation was well described
by the Arrhenius equation for coffee liquids (aw 0.99),
coffee concentrates (aw 0.33), and freeze dried coffee (aw
0.01). It is noteworthy that for coffee liquids and concen-
trates, the Arrhenius model correctly predicts the quality
decay rate in a wide range of storage temperatures, even
below 0C. Apparent activation energy and frequency
factor, calculated by regression analysis (Fig. 4), can be
used to estimate the apparent zero-order reaction rate of

The second and even more basic, working assumption
for temperature exploitation in ASLT is that temperature
really affects the reaction rate in reasonable times. Unfor-
tunately, if coffee derivatives are packed under modiﬁed
atmosphere conditions allowing the decrease of oxygen
concentration below a critical value, temperature may not
be a proper accelerating factor for ASLT. To this regard, it
has been reported that roasted and ground coffee having
less than 3% oxygen concentration in the headspace pre-
sented shelf-life values at 4, 22, and 35C much higher than
30 weeks [13]. When attempting ASLT in roasted coffee
powder packed in pods under modiﬁed atmosphere, similar

changes in the headspace presence of the volatile 2-ace-

accelerating factor. This means that this environmental
variable is not a proper accelerating factor for coffee
powders packed under modiﬁed atmosphere, since the
change in the temperature of the product does not allow
reducing their shelf-life testing times. On the basis of these
considerations, it is evident that accelerating factors other
than temperature should be identiﬁed and used to develop
adequate ASLT methodologies. In the case of coffee

Conclusions
Shelf-life prediction of products belonging to the coffee
world is a complex task due to the wide number of deteri-
orative mechanisms that could affect their quality decay.
For this reason, shelf-life testing must be carefully designed
depending on the product peculiarities. Actual literature
information provides limited help to solve the problem of
shelf-life determination, which can only be faced by
adopting rigorous, systematic, and well-designed shelf-life
assessment plans aiming to: (i) identify the acceptability
limit, (ii) mimic actual storage conditions, and (iii) reduce
testing time. However, much research work is needed to
provide experimental data which really allow efﬁcient
shelf-life testing for each different coffee derivative.
