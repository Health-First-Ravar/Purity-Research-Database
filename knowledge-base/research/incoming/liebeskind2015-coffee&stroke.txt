The coffee paradox in stroke: Increased
consumption linked with fewer strokes
David S. Liebeskind1, Nerses Sanossian2, Katherine A. Fu2, He-Jing Wang3,
Lenore Arab4
1Department of Neurology, UCLA Stroke Center, Los Angeles, CA, USA, 2Roxanna Todd Hodges Comprehensive
Stroke Clinic, University of Southern California, Los Angeles, CA, USA, 3Department of Biomathematics, UCLA,
Los Angeles, CA, USA, 4Department of Epidemiology, School of Public Health, UCLA, Los Angeles, CA, USA
Objective: To determine the association in amount of daily coffee consumption with incidence of stroke in a
broad cohort, considering other vascular risk factors.
Methods: We utilized the Third National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (1988–1994; NHANES III)
data on participants aged ≥17 years old to examine coffee consumption and stroke. Multivariate logistic
regression models related the amount of coffee use reported in a food frequency questionnaire with
stroke, controlling for other vascular risk factors.
Results: Of 33 994 NHANES III subjects, coffee consumption and stroke data in adults ≥17 years old were
available in 19 994. Daily coffee consumption ranged from 0 to 20 (median 1) cups and 644 (3.2%)
participants had a stroke diagnosed by a physician. Coffee intake varied with age, gender, and ethnicity
(P < 0.001). Interestingly, heart failure, diabetes, and hypertension were less frequent, and high
cholesterol more frequent in those consuming ≥3 cups per day (P < 0.001). Smoking was more frequent
in all coffee drinkers (P < 0.0001). Multivariate analyses revealed an independent effect of heavier coffee
consumption (≥3 cups/day) on reduced stroke (OR 0.44, 95% CI 0.22–0.87, P < 0.02) in healthy subjects
that was attenuated by vascular risk factors (OR 0.78, 95% CI 0.58–1.07, P ≈0.12).
Conclusion: Heavier daily coffee consumption is associated with decreased stroke prevalence, despite
smoking tendency in heavy coffee drinkers.
Keywords: Coffee, Caffeine, Stroke, Risk factors, Ischemia
Introduction
Coffee consumption in the USA exceeds 146 billion
cups per year by more than 50% of Americans.
Caffeine is also readily accessible in everyday life and
consumed through various drinks and foods, and is
widely
consumed
worldwide.
Such
ubiquitous
exposure of individuals to caffeine has spurred numer-
ous investigations on the potential health impact of
coffee, including caffeine and other constituents.
Despite concerns regarding potential adverse effects,
drinking
coffee has been associated with
many
health benefits, including decreased rates in numerous
types of cancer and cardiovascular disorders.1 Perhaps
most strikingly, these benefits have been noted not-
withstanding the association of coffee drinking with
known deleterious habits such as smoking.2
The vascular effects of caffeine, including adeno-
sine-receptor-mediated vasoconstriction and hyperten-
sion,
similarly
implicate
coffee
as
a
potential
modifiable risk factor in cardiovascular or cerebrovas-
cular
disorders.3
Routine
filtered
coffee
intake,
however, improves insulin metabolism and markedly
reduces
the
development
of
type
2
diabetes.4–7
Pathogenic arrhythmias, such as atrial fibrillation,
may actually be less common with increasing coffee
intake.8 Chronic neurological sequelae of coffee are
also unapparent, with studies suggesting decreased
rates of cognitive impairment, Parkinson’s disease,
and Alzheimer’s dementia in some coffee drinkers.9–11
Only a few studies have investigated the role of coffee
specifically in stroke, recently showing a protective
effect in women and middle-aged male, Finnish
smokers.2,12 Most studies have focused on cardiovas-
cular disease in general rather than stroke alone, and
the amount of daily coffee intake as a predictor of
stroke
has
not
been
completely
discerned.2,13–23
Despite these findings on coffee and stroke, a recent
study noted that naïve coffee drinkers more than
double their risk of stroke in the next hour compared
with
regular
coffee
drinkers.24

distinction
between the acute and chronic effects of coffee, or

The dose or amount of daily coffee intake was inves-

preliminary data suggesting reduced stroke rates and
a paradoxical increase in acute stroke events when
naïve coffee drinkers imbibe.24 We determined the

Methods
Study population
The
Third
National
Health
and
Nutrition
Examination Survey (NHANES III) was conducted
in the USA from 1988 through 1994 by the National
Center for Health Statistics of the Centers for
Disease Control and Prevention.26 This comprehen-
sive observational study consisted of an interview,
examination, and laboratory data collected from a
complex multistage, stratified, clustered probability
sample of the civilian, non-institutionalized popu-
lation,
with
oversampling
of
the
elderly,
non-
Hispanic
Blacks,
and
Mexican-Americans.
The
detailed methodology of this epidemiologic study
and information on coffee consumption have been
reported elsewhere.27 We limited our analysis to par-
ticipants aged ≥17 years who contributed complete
information on medical history and the food question-
naire that detailed coffee and other caffeinated bever-
age consumption. Complete data for our analyses
permitted inclusion of 19 994 participants (9374 men
and 10 620 women) to examine the relationship
between coffee consumption and stroke. Although
the dataset contained information on self-reported
potential TIA or stroke symptoms (weakness/paraly-
sis or numbness of the face, arm, or leg; loss of
vision; severe dizziness; problems with ability to
speak/understand), we elected to focus our analyses
on stroke diagnosed by a physician.
Assessment of coffee and caffeine intake
During the home interview, coffee and other caffei-
nated beverage intake were determined from responses
to the food frequency questionnaire to assess partici-
pants’ regular consumption over the past month.
Daily coffee consumption was categorized as 0, 1–2,
3–4, or ≥5 cups.
Statistical analysis
Descriptive variables were used to characterize the
study population and potential association of stroke
with
coffee
consumption.
Multivariate
logistic
regression models were used to relate the amount of
coffee use reported in a food frequency questionnaire,
controlling for other vascular risk factors and poten-
tial cofounders. We used linear regression modeling
to evaluate the relationship between caffeine intake
and history of stroke in the medical examination. We
dichotomized daily coffee consumption as 0–2 and
≥3 cups for the purposes of our principal objective.
Analyses were conducted for daily coffee consumption
and stroke in those subjects without established vascu-
lar risk factors and then including all variables. These
analyses were also repeated for only those subjects ≥50
years old. Baseline characteristics were analyzed with
respect to coffee consumption using the Wilcoxon
rank-sum test. For all different estimates, we calcu-
lated 95% confidence intervals (95% CIs), and all
P-values were two-sided. All statistical analyses were
computed using SAS version 8.2 (StataCorp, College
Station, TX, USA) incorporating sample weights and
adjusting for clusters and strata of the complex
sample design.
Assessment of covariates
The potential confounding effects of established vas-
cular risk factors, including demographic variables,
other
comorbidities,
and
habitual
risks
(e.g.
smoking) for stroke were considered. Univariate and
multivariate prediction algorithms and the use of
classification and regression tree (CART) analyses
were used to evaluate the degree and hierarchy of
such influences on the stroke outcome variable.28
Results
Population characteristics
Of 33 994 subjects participating in NHANES III, data
on coffee consumption and stroke in adults older than
17 years were available in 19 994 individuals, including
9374 men and 10 620 women. Within this subset of
NHANES III, mean age was 47.5 ± SD 20.8 years.
Detailed demographics of this cohort are listed in
Table 1. A history of stroke diagnosed by a physician
was noted in 644 (3.2%) of subjects, with the majority
of first strokes (63.6%) occurring after age 60. The
relationships of specific variables with stroke are
detailed in Table 2.
Coffee consumption ranged from 0 to 20 (median 1)
cups per day. More than half (50.5%) of subjects were
non-drinkers, and an additional 40.4% consumed 1–2
cups of coffee per day. Although missing data may
have limited our ability to detect a change in drinking
or eating habits owing to stroke, very few (n = 18)
reported such a behavioral change. Other sources of
caffeine that we considered included from 0 to 3 cola
beverages or cups of tea per day.

The amount of daily coffee intake was closely linked
with numerous demographic variables and comorbid-
ities (Table 3). Coffee intake increased with age, was
heavier in men and more common with White ethni-
city (P < 0.001). Interestingly, heart failure, diabetes,
and hypertension were less frequent and high choles-
terol more frequent in those consuming ≥3 cups/day
(P < 0.001). Smoking was more frequent in all coffee
drinkers (P < 0.0001).
Relationship of coffee consumption and stroke
Non-coffee drinkers had stroke prevalence of 2.9%,
with 1–2 cups/day 3.7%, 3–5 cups/day 3.0%, 6–9
cups/day 2.2%, and ≥10 cups/day 0.7%. Overall,
those with a history of stroke consumed mean 0.8 ±
SD 1.3 cups of coffee per day compared with 0.9 ±
SD 1.5 cups in those without a stroke. Subjects con-
suming more coffee per day had a reduced prevalence
of stroke (P ≈0.196), reflecting the inverted U-shaped
function of coffee consumption and stroke prevalence.
Multivariate logistic regression analyses revealed an
independent effect of heavier coffee consumption
(≥3 cups/day) on reduced stroke (OR 0.44, 95% CI
0.22–0.87, P < 0.020) among 12 959 healthy subjects
(Table 4). When established vascular risk factors
were included in the model, the association of
heavier coffee consumption with reduced stroke was
attenuated (OR 0.78, 95% CI 0.58–1.07, P ≈0.121).

with stroke in this cohort, we repeated our multivariate

Influence of other risk factors on coffee
relationship with stroke
The influence of established vascular risk factors on
our analyses with respect to stroke required further
investigation to delineate the hierarchy or relative
influence of daily coffee consumption. CART analyses

coffee consumption and reduced rates of stroke in both
men and women across a broad age range. These find-
ings represent the largest investigation on the relation-
ship between coffee and stroke in USA men and
women conducted to date. Prior evidence of potential
beneficial aspects of coffee on stroke emanate from a
large study restricted to U.S. women, analyses of
Finnish male smokers between the ages of 50 and 69
years old, and Japanese subjects older than 40.2,12,29
An earlier study of Finnish patients with diabetes
had shown only a non-significant reduction in stroke
mortality in heavier coffee drinkers.15 Many previous
analyses in small cohorts failed to detect an associ-
ation between heavier coffee intake and stroke follow-
ing an earlier suggestion that such consumption in
hypertensive, non-smoking men were at increased
risk.16,18,30 Our additional findings of a potential
stroke benefit in heavier coffee drinkers similarly add
important details on amounts of daily coffee intake.
We noted a U-shaped curve that defined a modest
increase in stroke rates among drinkers of one two
two cups per day and considerably reduced stroke
rates in daily consumers in excess of three cups. We
did not have the ability to distinguish ischemic and
hemorrhagic strokes that involve quite distinct patho-
physiology. Lopez-Garcia et al.12 demonstrated a ben-
eficial association of coffee and ischemic stroke in
women,
yet
no
relationship
with
hemorrhagic
strokes. Larsson et al.2 were unable to replicate the
beneficial relationship they noted with coffee on
ischemic stroke in intracerebral or subarachnoid
hemorrhage. Another recent study failed to detect an
association of coffee and tea consumption with
either
ischemic
stroke
or
hemorrhagic
stroke.14
Different results have been noted with respect to caf-
feine and intracerebral hemorrhage, with conflicting
evidence of potential harm and beneficial effects in
different
studies.17,31,32
No
association
between
coffee and aneurysmal subarachnoid hemorrhage
was noted recently, despite prior findings suggesting
potential harm.33 It should be noted that many
studies have had large numbers of undetermined
stroke subtypes. Disparate results on the association
of heavier coffee consumption with ischemic and
hemorrhagic stroke suggest mechanisms other than
hypertension and the need to distinguish coffee from
other sources of caffeine. The most remarkable
aspect of our findings that strengthen mounting evi-
dence on the potential benefit of heavier coffee
intake on stroke relates to the important distinction
between acute effects of coffee in naïve drinkers and
chronic, heavier use.24,25

drinkers has demonstrated similar odds ratio estimates

may exert a protective effect on stroke, such an associ-

simply be impossible for heavier coffee consumption
to overcome or negate potent stroke risk factors such
as diabetes or hypertension, once established. A
similar

Interestingly, the modifiable stroke risk factor of
smoking was less influential than chronic coffee
intake and heavier drinkers actually smoked more
than others in our study. The nature of the risk
reduction with increased daily coffee consumption
has also varied across studies, with some reports
noting our U-shaped curve with moderate drinkers
having relatively elevated risk compared with heavier
consumers. Such variability may reflect the influence
of numerous competing risk factors. For instances,
we noted that coffee intake varied with age, gender
(men drink more coffee), and ethnicity whereas,
cardiac disease, diabetes, and hypertension were all
less frequent with increasing number of cups of
coffee per day. The association of heavy coffee drink-
ing with reduced prevalence of stroke may be due to
longstanding coffee consumption. A prior analysis of
the same dataset showed that serum uric acid level
decreased with increasing coffee intake, yet the
effects may be due to constituents other than caf-
feine.27 Chronic, heavy coffee use may also decrease
risk of diabetes and heart failure.34,35
The coffee story is undoubtedly complex, showing
discrepant acute and chronic associations with stroke
that are clearly influenced by other risk factors. This
pattern of different acute (deleterious in naïve coffee
drinkers) and chronic (beneficial in chronic, heavy
drinkers) effects mirrors many other paradoxical
phenomena in stroke. Coffee may acutely induce
hypertension, vasoconstriction, and resultant hypo-
perfusion, yet repeated exposure may induce tolerance
through numerous mechanisms akin to precondition-
ing. Lunt et al.36 showed that caffeine in naïve cases
causes hypoperfusion, without evidence of hypoperfu-
sion with chronic ingestion. Perhaps the acute effects
of coffee do trigger a noxious response, yet allostatic
mechanisms
offset
hypoperfusion
with
chronic
exposure.24,25 The coffee paradox may therefore be
due to habitual consumption and other compensatory
mechanisms that avert diabetes or prolonged hyperten-
sion.7 The potentially antioxidant or vasoprotective
mechanisms of coffee, including amelioration of
inflammation and endothelial dysfunction, remain
elusive and may be due to more than 2000 constituents
other than caffeine.1 Ultimate effects of these protec-
tive mechanisms may also vary by amount or dose,
type of coffee, preparation method, chronic exposure,
and even individual genetic or enzymatic factors. It is
known that individual responses to coffee may have
specific genetic links, such as the CYP1A2 genotype
that influences metabolism of coffee.37 Coffee may
offset stroke risk even in smokers, whereas individual
susceptibility in other individuals may place them at
greater risk. The coffee paradox is similar to other
paradoxical features of stroke, such as smoking-
enhanced thrombolytic success and obesity-related
improved outcomes.38,39 During acute stroke, thera-
peutic interventions to normalize serum glucose,
blood pressure, and lipid levels may have paradoxical
effects despite established harmful effects of such
chronically elevated parameters. Overall, it is impera-
tive
to
delineate
differences
between
acute
and
chronic effects utilizing complete temporal infor-
mation on multifactorial risks.
Our multivariate and CART analyses demonstrate
how the potentially protective effects of coffee are
modified by multifactorial influences. Stroke preven-
tion has recently evolved to simultaneously target
numerous risks, rather than concentrating efforts on
one factor alone. It is therefore delusionary to ask
whether coffee is simply good or bad, as heterogeneous
influences and the temporal features of each risk
exposure must be considered. Many details of temporal
data are often missing even in the most comprehensive
epidemiological studies. Duration of hypertension,
diabetes, or heart disease may be lacking, and the
temporal features of habitual behavior such as coffee
drinking or smoking may also be limited.
The most salient implication of our study centers
around a clear distinction between acute versus
chronic coffee exposure effects on stroke and the
need to consider all risk exposures in a given individ-
ual. Coffee may be beneficial in some individuals,
yet it is solely one of innumerable factors that influence
stroke depending on chronicity. Large epidemiological
studies such as NHANES III provide no insight with
respect to pathophysiology and, without imaging,
much remains elusive about the temporal sequence
or
underlying
mechanisms.
Without
knowledge
regarding specific benefits of caffeine or other con-
stituents it remains difficult to make recommen-
dations.
Lifestyle
interventions
favor
a
comprehensive
approach
of
physical
activity,
smoking cessation, and weight management.

was lacking on the temporal sequence of exposures
or duration of comorbidities, coffee type, stroke
subtype, or underlying pathophysiology. Detailed
information on individual genetic predisposition to
coffee (e.g. CYP1A2 genotype) or coffee constituents

was also not available. Furthermore, as in any obser-
vational study, we noted associations yet causality
remains unclear. This study design may leave uncer-
tainty regarding the temporal sequence of exposur-
e–outcome relationships and is vulnerable to recall
bias in the self-reported data on coffee consumption.
Finally, dichotomization in the daily amount of
coffee consumption between 0–2 versus 3 or more
cups per day is an arbitrary cutpoint that may influ-
ence our results. Despite these limitations, our results
provide novel data on coffee and stroke in a nationally
representative sample of U.S. individuals of both
genders, across all age ranges. Future investigations
may include other variables that may have an impact
on the risk of stroke, including dietary variables,
BMI categories, and aspirin and antihypertensive
medication in their analyses to better elucidate the
association between coffee consumption and stroke.
In conclusion, we found evidence for a potential pro-
tective association of heavier daily coffee consumption
with stroke. Established risk factors for stroke eclipse
the influence of chronic coffee intake, underscoring
the multifactorial nature of effective stroke prevention
and the imperative to consider all risks, modifiable or
not. Acute coffee ingestion in naïve drinkers may
increase risk within the first 2 hours, yet heavier
chronic coffee consumption may paradoxically dimin-
ish stroke.24,25 Further studies are warranted to pursue
the underlying beneficial effects of coffee on stroke,
including potential vasoprotective properties.
