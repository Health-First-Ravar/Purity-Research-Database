Thermal process contaminants: acrylamide,
chloropropanols and furan
Burc¸e Atac¸ Mogol and Vural Go¨ kmen
Heat induces chemical changes that may lead to the formation
of toxic compounds, so-called thermal process contaminants.
Intensive scientiﬁc studies on these compounds have been
conducted over the past decade. Researchers have gained
increasing insight in understanding the presence, formation,
and potential risk to public health posed by the compounds
formed during heat processing of foods. From a food safety
point of view, occurrence of thermal process contaminants in
foods is still one of the major concerns for consumers, health
authorities and industry. Therefore, there is a strong need to
minimize their formation in different foods. This review
highlights the latest developments regarding the formation and
elimination of acrylamide, furan, chloropropanols and their
esters in foods.

Introduction
Thermal processing is an important treatment for food
preservation, especially in the manufacture of shelf stable
foods with speciﬁc nutritional properties. Also, it is indis-
pensable for determining the sensory properties, in par-
ticular color, ﬂavor and texture in fried, baked, and
roasted products. Heat treatments induces chemical
change and a plethora of new molecules are generated
in foods, some of which have been claimed to be health-
promoting antioxidants and antimutagens [1]. On the
other hand, it may lead to the formation of heat-induced
toxic compounds, so called thermal process contaminants,
that exert adverse toxicological effects, create potential
health risks to humans. Acrylamide, chloropropanols, and
furan are well-known as thermal process contaminants in
foods [2–4]. This review highlights the latest research
ﬁndings on these thermal process contaminants.
Acrylamide
In April 2002, a report by the Swedish Authorities on the
presence of acrylamide (2-propenamide) in a wide range
of fried and oven cooked foods [2] attracted worldwide
attention, due to the fact that acrylamide is classiﬁed as
probably carcinogenic to humans (Group 2A) by the
IARC [5]. Initial surveys have shown that relatively high
concentrations of acrylamide are found potato chips,
French fries, pan-fried potato products, crisp bread,
and coffee [1]. The main pathway of acrylamide forma-
tion in foods is linked to the Maillard reaction and, in
particular, the amino acid asparagine (Figure 1) [6].
Considering the contribution of coffee to daily acrylamide
intake, acrylamide content of coffee should be limited. A
recent study investigated the formation of acrylamide
concentrations and the degradation of Robusta coffee
bean polyphenols and its antioxidant activity during
different roasting conditions [7]. Increasing the air veloc-
ity regardless of the temperature resulted in the intensi-
ﬁcation of acrylamide formation, while increasing the
roasting air humidity lowers acrylamide formation, but
only at high roasting temperature. Low velocity and
humidity of roasting air at 203 8C provided relatively
low levels of acrylamide with moderate degradation of
polyphenols and antioxidant properties deterioration in
coffee beans having pleasant and full ﬂavor. Another study
reported yeast fermentation as a promising technique to
reduce acrylamide content of instant coffee [8]. Incuba-
tion the instant coffee with sucrose and Saccharomyces
cerevisiae reduces acrylamide concentration by about 70%.
French fries and potato chips seem to have the highest
levels of acrylamide, therefore, take special attention.
Truong et al. [9] reported that sweet potato French fries,
fried at 165 8C for 2, 3, and 5 min, contained acrylamide
124.9, 255.5, and 452.0 ng/g fresh weight, respectively.
Blanching the sweet potato strips and using processing
aids, additives, such as sodium acid pyrophosphate and
CaCl2, were found to be effective in reducing acrylamide
formation up to 7 times in those French fries.
The effect of different pretreatments, including chemical
reagents and air frying, on the formation of acrylamide in
fried potatoes was investigated [10]. Air-frying reduced
acrylamide content by about 90% compared with conven-
tional deep-oil-frying. Deep-oil fried potatoes pretreated

Vacuum roasting of coffee beans was proposed as alter-

of acrylamide. It was reported that acrylamide content of
medium roasted beans could be decreased approximately
50% by using vacuum roasting [13].
Radio-frequency (RF) heating was reported as alternative
technique to mitigate acrylamide formation in potato
chips [14]. In the conventional process, potato chips
were fried in oil at high temperatures in order to achieve
extremely low moisture (2% by weight), which led to form
acrylamide. To avoid this, potato slices were dried by RF
after partial frying in hot oil. This post-drying process,
which heats the remaining moisture selectively, sug-
gested as a viable approach to decrease acrylamide con-
tent up to 32% in potato chips while maintaining the
desired ﬁnal moisture content and quality attributes.
Owing to its potential reactivity, a recent study investi-
gated the fate of acrylamide in biscuits and fried potatoes
after ingestion [15]. The study revealed that at the end of
digestion, acrylamide was decreased by 49.2% and 73.4%
in biscuits, while increased signiﬁcantly during gastric
digestion of fried potatoes. The high-resolution mass
spectrometry analysis showed that the Schiff base formed
between reducing sugars and asparagine disappeared
rapidly, whereas the acrylamide level increased during
the gastric phase. It was suggested that intermediates,
such as the Schiff base, accumulating in potatoes during
frying are the potential precursors of acrylamide under
gastric conditions.
Chloropropanols and their esters
Chloropropanols, 
namely 
3-choloropropanediol 
(3-
MCPD) and 2-choloropropanediol (2-MCPD), and their
fatty acid esters (3-MCPDe and 2-MCPDe) are contami-
nants that are formed during the processing and manu-
facture of certain foods and ingredients, especially in acid-
hydrolyzed vegetable protein and soy sauce [16]. These
compounds are also found in noodles, meat, cakes as well
as cereal products, such as breads and biscuits [17].
Formation mechanism of 3-MCPD and 2-MCPD is
shown in Figure 2 [18].
Formation mechanism of 3-MCPDe was further eluci-
dated. Zhang et al. [19] investigated whether tristear-
oylglycerol may serve as a precursor for 3-MCPDe
formation. They proposed that either a cyclic acyloxo-
nium or a glycidol ester radical intermediate might be
formed from 3-MCPD monoesters and diesters from
triglycerides under a high-temperature and low-moisture
condition. Fe2+ and Fe3+ were found to catalyze the
formation of 3-MCPD’s and glycidol esters from triacyl-
glycerol. Finally, controlling the availability of Fe2+ and
Fe3+ through ingredients, equipment, processing water,
was suggested to reduce the levels of 3-MCPD esters and
glycidol esters in the reﬁned edible oils and food pro-
ducts. Acidity was reported as an important factor con-
tributing to the formation of MCPD-esters when the oil
was heated to high temperatures [20]. The researchers
recommend to reduce the acidity levels by optimizing
screening and clariﬁcation steps in palm oil mills, or,
alternatively, by introducing additional water wash step
into the process.
A recent study suggests lowering the thermal load or
limiting the chloride concentration in order to reduce
the formation of 3-MCPD, 2-MCPD and MCPDe in
biscuits [21]. Eliminating salt from the recipe decreased
the 3-MCPD and 2-MCPD formation rate constants in
biscuits by 57.5 and 85.4%, respectively. In addition,
bound-MCPDs were not formed in biscuits without salt
during baking. Similar to biscuits, NaCl addition, heating
temperature, heating time and especially water content
had signiﬁcant effects on the generation of 3-MCPD
esters in palm oil during deep fat frying [22]. The study
revealed that increasing the water dosage from 7 to 10%
increased 3-MCPDe content from 1260 to 2950 mg/kg.
Deodorization, an essential step in reﬁning process of
edible oils, was found to be responsible to the formation
of 3-MCPDe and possibly other processing by-product
due to high temperatures applied. The formations of 2-
MCPDe and 3-MCPDe during edible oil reﬁning were
clearly affected by the oil composition (i.e. type and origin
of the oil) and by the treatment to which the oil was
subjected prior to deodorization. Partial acylglycerols

Furan
Furan is a heterocyclic organic compound, formed in a
number of heated foods through thermal degradation of
natural food constituents. Different routes of formation,
available in the literature, were summarized in Figure 3
[27,28]. Recently, different model systems that are heated
at 90, 121, and 150 8C have been designed in order to
elucidate the factors affecting furan formation [29]. Among
amino acid and/or sugar model systems, ribose-serine
model contained the greatest amount of furan ranging
from 2.1 to 4931.9 ng/ml. Temperature showed increasing
effect on the furan formation in all Maillard binary reaction
models. Furoic acid, a precursor of furan, formed furan at
the greatest amount ranging from 1058.2 to 13,927.9 ng/ml.

animal carcinogen and classiﬁed as ‘possibly carcinogen
to humans’ (Group 2B) by the International Agency for
Research on Cancer (IARC) [30]. Due to its carcinoge-
nicity, ALARA ‘as low as reasonably achievable’ concept

should be applied to furan levels in foods. Microencap-
sulation was found to be an effective strategy to limit the
reactivity of ascorbic acid, precursor of furan. Encapsula-
tion with maltodextrin DE 19 and Arabic gum decreased
the furan formation rate ranging from 50% to 57% during
heating [31]. However, there were no statistically signiﬁ-
cant differences (P > 0.05) in the effectiveness of micro-
encapsulation using different coating materials. Another
study suggested reducing the baking pressure (i.e. vary-
ing from 101.33 to 0.15 kPa) to limit the formation of
furan in omega-3 enriched biscuits [32]. Baking at high
temperature and reduced pressure allowed to obtain
biscuits with acceptable water content and color, while
furan was found to be at negligible levels at certain
conditions, indicating that as soon as furan is formed, it
is almost quantitatively removed due to its higher vola-
tility. Similarly, vacuum post-treatment was suggested to
remove furan effectively (67%) from meat sauce without
affecting the food sensory properties and its overall qual-
ity [33]. However, the vacuum treatments were ineffec-
tive in removing furan from the biscuits, unless it was
preliminary hydrated at high water activity.
High-pressure thermal sterilization was reported as an
alternative process to conventional thermal processing
to make the baby food puree safer in terms of furan [34].
Applying short dwell times and temperatures between
107 and 121 8C reduced furan content by 81–96% in
comparison to retorting, even at conditions equal to the
thermal sterilization (F0 = 7 min). The authors added
that a scaling up is needed to validate the ﬁndings and
to implement this promising technology in the food
industry.
Blanching of potato slices has been suggested as a pre-
treatment in potato chip process in order to minimize
furan formation [35]. Blanching leached ascorbic acid and
reducing sugars out of the potato slices and led to de-
crease furan concentration 91% in potato chips.
Furan was formed from polyunsaturated fatty acids such
as linoleic and linolenic acid during thermal (120 8C,
25 min) and UV-C (11.5 J/cm2) treatments, whereas gam-
ma irradiation (up to 20 kGy) did not induce formation of
signiﬁcant amounts of furan from myristic, palmitic, stea-
ric, oleic, linoleic, and linolenic acids [36]. At pH 9, furan
was formed >3.5 times more than pHs 3 or 6 during
storage at 25 8C for 3 days. Antioxidants, e.g. Trolox,
BHA, and propyl gallate had no effect on furan formation
from linolenic acid.
The effects of different additives were investigated on
the formation of furan in canned-coffee model system
[37]. Chlorogenic acid was found to show the most
signiﬁcant inhibitory effect by 67.0% followed by epica-
techin (65.3%), sodium sulﬁte (64.1%), epigallocatechin
gallate (60.0%), and ferulic acid (57.6%), caffeic acid
(48.2%). Fat-soluble antioxidants, such as BHT and a-
tocopherol, showed less effect than water-soluble ones,
with an exception of caffeic acid.
In a soy sauce model, fermented up to 30 days, furan
content increased by 211% after sterilization compared to
without sterilization [38]. Highest furan level (86.21 ng/
ml) was obtained by fermentation at 30 8C for 30 days,
while furan concentration was reported lower at 20 8C
(45% less) and 40 8C (88% less). Magnesium sulfate,
calcium sulfate, BHT and BHA reduced furan concen-
tration by 36–90%, 27–91%, 84% and 56%, whereas iron
sulfate and ascorbic acid increased by 278%, 87%, respec-
tively.
Thermal process is the main factor in furan formation,
however, controlling the storage conditions has been
found more important for pasteurized orange and mango
juices [39]. The furan levels of orange and mango juices
were found below the limit of detection immediately
after pasteurization, but it signiﬁcantly increased after a
certain lag time as a function of the storage temperature
and time. The juice composition, i.e. sugars, ascorbic
acid and carotenoids, greatly affected the rate of furan
formation.
The furan levels in coffee beverages acquired from auto-
matic coffee vending machines in Madrid reported to
differ from 11 to 262 ng/ml (mean = 171  59.8) [40]. The
authors pointed out that the time to consume the coffee
brew should be taken into account for a realistic estima-
tion of the levels of furan exposure for humans. The
results revealed that furan content could be signiﬁcantly
decreased by stirring the coffee for 5 min (94%) or by
storing in sealed thermo for 8 h (97%). Daily intake
estimations indicated that exposure to furan from coffee
brew implied low risk to human health.
Conclusion
Nowadays there is a worldwide interest to the risks
associated with thermal process contaminants, because
they occur in varying concentrations in daily consumed
foods as a result of common cooking practices like frying,
baking and roasting. The ﬁnal concentrations of thermal
process contaminants in foods depend on the concentra-
tion of their corresponding precursors as well as the
severity of thermal processing conditions. Since safety
remains as a primary objective, one of the challenges
facing the food industry is to minimize these toxicants,
especially in baby foods without adversely affecting
desired attributes of thermal processing. Therefore,
any mitigation strategy should be practically viable at
industrial level, and should take into account both the
risks and the beneﬁts associated with thermal processing
of foods, and try to minimize the risk while keeping
beneﬁts.
