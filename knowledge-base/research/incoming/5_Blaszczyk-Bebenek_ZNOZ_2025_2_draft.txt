Abstract
Background Polyphenols are bioactive compounds found in plant-based foods and beverages, 
including coffee and tea. These compounds have been extensively studied for their potential
health benefits, particularly in the prevention and management of chronic diseases such as
cardiovascular diseases, diabetes, neurodegenerative disorders, and certain types of cancer. This
narrative review aims to summarize current literature on the health effects of polyphenols
derived from coffee and tea and their mechanisms of action in relation to chronic disease
prevention. Methods: Databases searching, included English-language publications, including
meta-analyses, systematic reviews, randomized controlled trials, and cross sectional study 
conducted in hu-man populations, between 2019-2024. Results: A moderate intake of
polyphenols from coffee and especially green tea has been shown to have a protective effect. In
the con-text of carcinogenesis, the ingestion of substantial quantities of green tea (≥5 cups/day)
seem to play an important role in the chemoprevention. Conclusions: Polyphenols from coffee
and tea provide substantial health benefits, also in the prevention and management of chronic
diseases. While observational studies suggest a protective role, more clinical trials are needed
to confirm causality and establish optimal consumption levels. 
Keywords: cardiovascular diseases, chronic disease, coffee, diabetes, flavonoids, polyphenols,
neurodegenerative disorders, tea

Polyphenols are a large group of naturally occurring secondary plant metabolites, [1]. 
They are compounds with a very diverse structure, with one or more aromatic rings and two or 
more hydroxyl groups and characterized by the presence of phenolic units [1–3]. Polyphenols 
can be classified into subgroups depending on their chemical structure into, mainly to: 
flavonoids, phenolic acids, lignans, stilbenes, and compounds with a variety of structures, such 
tannins [1]. Polyphenols affect growth and redox balance, enzyme function, and signaling 
pathways. They are abundant in fruits, vegetables, tea, coffee, red wine, cereals and related food 
products [1,4].  
Coffee and tea are among the most commonly consumed prepared beverages worldwide 
[5]. They are widely popular due to their cultural significance, unique flavors, and stimulating 
effects [6]. The study showed that the total content of phenolic compounds in tea and coffee 
infusions is similar, ranging from 16.7-37.1 mg/100 cm3 [7]. Polyphenols, abundant in coffee 
and tea (Table I), have been extensively studied for their potential health benefits, particularly 
in relation to chronic diseases.  
In coffee the polyphenol content vary based on factors such as the country of origin, 
cultivation methods, and processing techniques [8]. It was stated in research that instant coffee 
contains the highest concentration of polyphenols per gram of raw material (125.78 mg). 
However mild coffee from Java has the highest amount per portion (386.59 mg/100 ml). In 
decaffeinated coffee from Java, extending the brewing time to 30 minutes, increased the 
concentration of polyphenols compounds up to nearly 36% [8]. Coffee contains a complex mix 
of compounds that contribute to its flavor, aroma, and health effects. Primary ingredients found 
in coffee is caffeine as a natural stimulant. Varies by coffee type and brewing method, amount 
of caffeine is 40–180 mg per cup [9]. In coffee main antioxidants are chlorogenic acids, caffeine 
and melanoidins [10]. A group of Chlorogenic Acids may contribute to coffee’s health benefits, 
such as reducing inflammation and improving glucose metabolism. Coffee involves also lipids 
(coffee oil), founded in the coffee bean’s waxy outer layer (e.g., cafestol and kahweol). Coffee 
oils can raise cholesterol levels if consumed in unfiltered coffee. There are small amounts of 
sugars and fiber as well as small quantities of proteins and free amino acids. Coffee can be 
source of minerals like magnesium, potassium, manganese, and trace amounts of calcium and 
iron [11].

Non-communicable diseases (NCDs) are characterized by etiological variation related 
to a range of factors, including genetic, personal, physiological and environ-mental conditions. 
They are typically defined as chronic, given their protracted duration. According to the World 
Health Organization (WHO), the most prevalent chronic non-communicable diseases include 
cardiovascular diseases, diabetes, cancer, neuro-logical disorders, and chronic respiratory 
diseases (WHO). In European Union countries, they account for 80% of the disease burden and 
they are represent the primary causes of preventable premature death in Europe and globally 
[20,21]. The global overall prevalence of cardiovascular diseases increased from 0.061% to 
0.070% be-tween 2010 and 2019. The most common cardiovascular conditions included atrial 
fibrillation and flutter, peripheral artery disease, and stroke [22]. According to the latest 
estimates from the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) for 2022, the global 
incidence of cancer was approximately 20 million, with a death toll of 9.7 mil-lion. Lung cancer

cancers of the female breast (11.6%), colorectum (9.6%), prostate (7.3%) and stomach (4.9%)

disease, and others are a significant and growing public health concern. The prevalence is 
approximately 15% of the world's population, particularly in aging populations [24], vary by 
disease, age group, and region  [25]. In 2022, it was estimated that 828 million adults were 
living with diabetes. In the year 2022, the age-standardized prevalence of diabetes on a global 
scale was found to be 13.9% among female individuals and 14.3% among males. The lowest 
prevalence of diabetes in 2022 was observed in Western Europe and East Africa for both 
genders, with the lowest rates recorded among women and in Japan and Canada [26].

2. Materials and Methods 
This narrative review was conducted by searching databases such as PubMed, 
EMBASE, Cochrane Library and Google Scholar. The review included English language 
publications, including meta-analyses, systematic reviews, randomized controlled trials, and 
cross sectional study conducted in human populations, between 2019-2025. 
3. Chronic disease and polyphenols from coffee and tea   
3.1. Cardiovascular disease  
The mechanisms of polyphenols in cardiovascular disease (CVD) are associated with 
their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties, which enhance endothelial function by 
increasing nitric oxide (NO) bioavailability, impaired by oxidative stress and inflammation. 
Their antioxidant effects result from scavenging reactive oxygen and nitrogen species (ROS, 
RNS) and regulating endogenous antioxidant pathways. Additionally, polyphenols exert anti-
inflammatory effects by modulating transcription factors and inflammatory enzymes [29,30]. 
In population based study from Brazil, polyphenol intake from coffee was, shown  the lower 
odds among individuals who drank 1-3 cups of coffee/day to elevated systolic blood pressure 
(SBP), diastolic blood pressure (DBP) and hyperhomocysteinemia [31]. A review of the 
literature from 2020 Jee et. al revealed that the impact of coffee intake on the risk for of stroke 
has been demonstrated on numerous studies. These studies indicated that, in comparison to 
men, women have a reduced risk of stroke incidence with caffeine intake. As indirect evidence 
of the preventive effect of coffee consumption on stroke occurrence, there are papers reporting 
the preventive effect of coffee consumption on the onset of diabetes. It is related by maximizing

3.2. Neurodegenerative disorders  
A stated in the 2020 year review by Jee et. al., demonstrated association between the 
consumption of 3–4 cups of coffee per day and a reduced risk of various neurological disorders, 
including Alzheimer’s disease (AD), Parkinson's disease (PD), and de-pression [42].   
The Jee et. al. (2020) review , focused on gender differences and the associations 
between caffeine consumption and selected neurological disorders. Their findings indicated that 
in women, caffeine consumption exhibited a favourable impact, reducing the risk of stroke, 
dementia, and depression. Conversely, among male subjects, a reduced risk of PD was observed 
[42]. A nonlinear association was observed in Zhu et al. (2024) meta-analysis, between coffee 
consumption and AD risk. The strongest protective effect occurred at approximately 2.5 cups 
of coffee per day (RR: 0.74; 95% CI: 0.59–0.93). Conversely, a linear relationship was observed 
between tea consumption and cognitive disorders, with an 11% risk reduction for each 
additional cup consumed daily [43].

robust antioxidant activity, scavenging free radicals and chelating prooxidant metals, which 
mitigates oxidative stress (OS) caused by Aβ accumulation, redox imbalances, and 
mitochondrial dysfunction. Additionally, CA reduces intracellular ROS levels, lipid 
peroxidation, and inflammation by lowering TNF-⍺, IL-6, and NF-κB levels [45]. It enhances

through various mechanisms, including improving insulin sensitivity, reducing oxidative stress, 
and modulating the gut microbiome (Table III). Regular consumption of these beverages, along

managing diabetes [60,68–71]. Despite their benefits, the low bioavailability of polyphenols is

in these beverages, have the potential to enhance insulin sensitivity, glucose metabolism and 
vascular function. This, in turn, can contribute to reduction in the risk of type 2 diabetes.

3.4 Cancer   
The presence of malignant cells with the potential to disseminate to proximate tissues or 
metastasise to distant organs via the bloodstream has been identified as can-cerous. These cells 
are distinguished by uncontrolled proliferation and pose a significant threat to human health 
[73]. The process is frequently initiated by factors such as inflammation and oxidative stress, 
which have been demonstrated to inflict damage to cells and tissues, thus contributing to the 
initiation or progression of cancer. Sustained inflammation has been demonstrated to promote 
cancer development through in-creased cell division, genetic mutations and stimulation of 
blood vessel formation that delivers nutrients to tumours. It has been demonstrated that 
polyphenols have the capacity to impede the development of cancer at a number of distinct 
stages [73,74]. 
The Cochrane systematic review from 2020, included 142 studies with over 1.1 million 
participants to assess the association between green tea consumption and cancer risk. The 
evidence from this review was inconsistent, findings varied by cancer type and study design. 
Experimental studies linked green tea extract supplementation to a lower risk of prostate cancer 
but a higher risk of gynecological cancers, with no effect on non-melanoma skin cancer. While 
supplementation slightly improved quality of life, it was associated with adverse effects, 
including gastrointestinal issues, elevated liver enzymes, and, less frequently, insomnia, 
hypertension, and skin reactions. Non-experimental studies indicated a potential reduction in 
overall cancer incidence among high consumers, though no difference in cancer-related 
mortality was observed [75]. A review of human studies on the involvement of microRNA 
modification in the anticancer effects of the main polyphenols from green tea and coffee, 
modulate the expression of microRNAs, impacting the risk of developing cancer [76]. In 
epidemiological studies related to the consumption of foods containing these polyphenols, dif-
ferences in their effect on specific cancers have been observed. The ingestion of poly-phenols 
from coffee and tea has been linked to a reduced likelihood of developing certain types of 
cancer, including brain, glioma and breast [76]. In the context of the re-search conducted on the

4. Conclusions 
The present review focused on the natural sources of polyphenols found in bever-ages such as 
coffee and tea in terms of the prevention of civilisational diseases. A substantial body of 
research has identified a correlation between the regular consumption of coffee and tea and a 
reduced risk of developing various chronic conditions, including: cardiovascular diseases, type 
2 diabetes, neurodegenerative diseases (e.g., Alzhei-mer's and Parkinson's diseases), and 
specific types of cancer (e.g., colorectal and liver cancer). A moderate intake of polyphenols 
from coffee and especially green tea has been shown to have a protective effect. In the context 
of carcinogenesis, the ingestion of substantial quantities of green tea (equivalent to five or more 
cups per day) has been demonstrated to play a pivotal role in the chemoprevention of cancer. 
The outcomes of clinical studies are, on occasion, inconsistent. This inconsistency can be 
attributed to differences in dose, polyphenol type, and individual variability.  
Phenolic compounds face a number of challenges when used therapeutically. However,
