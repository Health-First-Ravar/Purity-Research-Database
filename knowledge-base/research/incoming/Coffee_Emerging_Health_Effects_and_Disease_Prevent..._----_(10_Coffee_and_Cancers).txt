Coffee and Cancers

10.1
INTRODUCTION
Coffee consumption appears to have originated in northeast Africa, spreading throughout
the Middle East in the ﬁfteenth century, and from there to Europe [1]. Coffee is among the
most widely consumed beverages in the world and may be the primary source of dietary an-
tioxidants, notably in Nordic countries [2]. Habitual coffee consumption has been associated
with a substantially lower risk of mortality [3] and a number of degenerative, progressive,
and chronic diseases including cancer [4–8].
The relationship between coffee and cancer holds great interest given a large array of
coffee constituents that could potentially confer protective effects through several biological
mechanisms. For example, reductions in cholesterol, bile acid, and neutral sterol secre-
tion in the colon is a direct effect of coffee consumption [9,10], as is increased colonic
motility, which can reduce epithelial exposure to carcinogens. Coffee is also the primary
source of caffeine. Results from animal studies and cell culture systems have indicated that
caffeine both stimulates and suppresses tumors, depending upon the species and phase of
administration [11].
Coffee contains the diterpenes cafestol and kahweol, which exert biological effects con-
sistent with anticarcinogenic properties, including inducing phase II enzymes involved in
carcinogen detoxiﬁcation [12,13], inhibiting the activity of the phase I enzyme responsi-
ble for carcinogen activation, and stimulating intracellular antioxidant defense mechanisms
[14]. Coffee is also an important source of polyphenols such as lignan phytoestrogens and
ﬂavonoids [15]. Polyphenols were found to exhibit anticarcinogenic properties in several
laboratory and epidemiological studies [16,17].
Coffee is also an important source of 5-caffeoylquinic acid, the main chlorogenic acid
in coffee, which contributes to its antioxidant effect [18]. In rats, chlorogenic acid reduces
glucose levels; and intake of quinides, the degradation products of chlorogenic acid, increases
insulin sensitivity [19]. Chronic hyperinsulinemia and insulin resistance are markers of high
risk for some cancers [20].
DNA hypermethylation is a common characteristic of tumor cells; it is an epigenetic mech-
anism for silencing genes including those encoding tumor-suppressor proteins, DNA-repair
enzymes, and receptors. Gene-speciﬁc hypermethylation inactivates pathways involved in
the tumorigenic process, including cell cycle regulation, inﬂammatory and stress response,

10.2
BREAST CANCER
Breast cancer is the second leading cause of cancer-associated deaths in women in most
industrialized countries. The incidence of this disease in Western countries is about ﬁve
times that of developing countries and Japan [22]. No consistent association has been reported
between coffee consumption and breast cancer among case-control studies, although some
studies have reported a signiﬁcant inverse association, particularly among premenopausal
women [11,17,23]. Preliminary results from the Nurse’s Health Study suggested a weak
inverse association between caffeine intake and the risk of breast cancer [relative risk (RR)
of 0.81; 95% conﬁdence interval [CI]: 0.71–1.00 for the ﬁfth versus the ﬁrst quintile] [24]. A
Norwegian cohort of 14,593 women who drank at least 5 cups of coffee daily had a signiﬁcant
50% lower breast cancer risk compared with those who drank 2 cups or less [25]. Among
premenopausal women, consumption of regular (nondecaffeinated) coffee was associated
with signiﬁcant linear declines in breast cancer risk. Consumers of 4 or more cups/day had
a 38% lower risk [odds ratio (OR) = 0.62, 95% CI: 0.39–0.98] [17].
In addition, an international investigation reported an inverse dose–response relation-
ship between coffee consumption and the risk of hereditary breast cancer. Premenopausal
women with a BRCA1 or BRCA2 mutation who habitually drank 6 or more cups of cof-
fee/day had a statistically signiﬁcant 70% reduction in breast cancer risk. This effect was
limited to the consumption of caffeinated coffee [8]. In a recent prospective study of 52,062
African American women enrolled in the Black Women’s Health Study, 1268 incident
breast cancers were identiﬁed through clinical examinations during the 12-year follow-up.
A nonsigniﬁcant inverse association was found between coffee consumption and breast
cancer among postmenopausal women, although half of these women rarely drank coffee
(4 cups/day vs. 0 cups; incident rate ratio = 0.85, 95% CI: 0.55–1.32) [26]. However,
a meta-analysis that included nine cohort studies reported no overall association between
coffee consumption and risk of breast cancer [27]. Similarly, a nonsigniﬁcant negative as-
sociation was reported for lean women [27] and women who were 18–35 years old [28].
The failure to establish a strong link between coffee consumption and breast cancer risk
data can be explained, at least in part, to different levels of coffee consumption, meth-
ods of coffee preparation, degree of roasting, serving size, and the coffee strength; how-
ever, biological mechanisms have been established and some evidence suggests that regular
coffee drinking at least 4 cups/day is related to modestly reduced premenopausal breast
cancer risk.

to subgroups of subjects that were inconsistent across studies and countries. In addition,
information on coffee consumption was collected only once and therefore did not take into
account change in consumption over time. Overall, the accumulated evidence suggests that
coffee consumption has no inﬂuence or only a moderately favorable inﬂuence on the risk of
colorectal cancer.
10.4
PROSTATE CANCER
Prostate cancer is the second most common cancer in the world and the sixth most common
cause of cancer death. Initial interest in the relationship between coffee consumption and
prostate cancer was triggered by ecologic analyses that suggested a positive correlation
between a country’s mean coffee consumption and its prostate cancer rate [39,40]. However,
these associations were not conﬁrmed in numerous subsequent studies.
A meta-analysis of six cohort studies investigating the relationship between coffee con-
sumption and the risk of prostate cancer reported a cumulative effect estimate of 1.00 (95%
CI: 0.94–1.07) per cup/day increase [27]. Another pooled analysis of ﬁve case-control studies
yielded a summary OR of 1.02 (95% CI: 0.99–1.04) per cup/day increase [27]. Similarly, a
study investigating the association between prostate cancer risk and cumulative lifetime daily
coffee consumption, duration of daily drinking, and age at which daily coffee consumption
began found no important relationship [41]. Overall, there appears to be a wealth of evi-
dence pointing to no coffee effect and no evidence of a dose–response effect. Thus, current
evidence excludes any substantial association between coffee consumption and the risk of
prostate cancer.

10.6
GASTRIC CANCER
Cancer of the stomach is the fourth most common type of cancer worldwide and the second
most common cause of death from cancer. A systematic review and meta-analysis pooled
effects from 23 studies [48], but showed no adverse association between coffee consumption
and gastric cancer. The OR for the overall association between coffee and gastric cancer
(highest vs. lowest category of exposure) was 0.97 (95% CI: 0.86–1.09). This was similar
for cohort studies (OR = 1.02, 95% CI: 0.76–1.37) and case-control studies, regardless
of the comparison population (population-based OR = 0.90, 95% CI: 0.70–1.15; hospital-
based OR = 0.97, 95% CI: 0.83–1.13). The OR was 1.26 (95% CI: 1.02–1.57) for the
ﬁve studies conducted in the United States, 0.97 (95% CI: 0.82–1.14) for the ﬁve Japanese
studies, 0.98 (95% CI: 0.81–1.17) for the six studies from Europe, and 0.64 (95% CI:
0.47–0.86) for the two studies from South America. A second analysis of data from two
large case-control studies in Italy found no consistent association between consumption of
regular coffee, decaffeinated coffee, or black tea and gastric cancer risk [49]. Since then,
a population-based cohort study [50] of 61,433 Swedish women with a mean follow-up of
15.7 years was published. Based on 160 incident cases of stomach cancer, the multivariate
hazard ratio (HR) for women who drank 2–3 cups/day was 1.49 (95% CI: 0.97–2.27),
and for those who drank 4 or more cups/day the HR was 1.86 (95% CI: 1.07–3.25; P for
trend = 0.01) compared with nondrinkers. In addition, a recent large prospective cohort study
[51] conducted in the United States with 481,563 subjects evaluated the relationship between
coffee drinking and gastric cancer. During a follow-up of 2,584,953 person-years, the authors
observed a signiﬁcant association between coffee drinking and risk of gastric cardia cancer;
compared with coffee consumption of 1 cup/day, the HR for drinking 3 cups/day was 1.57
(95% CI: 1.03–2.39). However, these ﬁndings appear to be outliers in the context of the other
25 studies. Overall, the ﬁndings indicate that regular and decaffeinated coffee does not appear
to have an important effect on gastric carcinogenesis.

10.7
OVARIAN CANCER
Ovarian cancer is the seventh most common cancer in women worldwide as well as the
seventh most common cause of cancer deaths. Relatively few studies have assessed the effects
of coffee on the risk of ovarian cancer. An early meta-analysis of crude data from seven case-
control studies published before 1990 investigated the effect of coffee consumption on ovarian
cancer and reported a combined OR of 1.3 (95% CI: 1.1–1.5) for coffee users versus nonusers
[52]. A meta-analysis of eight case-control studies reported a small borderline signiﬁcant
negative association, with a pooled OR of 0.98 (95% CI: 0.96–1.01) for additional daily
consumption of coffee [28]. After a mean 16.4-year follow-up of 49,613 Canadian women
enrolled in the National Breast Screening Study [53], 264 incidences of ovarian cancer
occurred. Ovarian cancer was not signiﬁcantly associated with coffee intake in this study. A
cohort study of 280 ovarian cancer cases [54] conducted in the Netherlands also reported no
signiﬁcant association between coffee and ovarian cancer, with a multivariable rate ratio of
1.04 (95% CI: 0.97–1.12) per cup/day. Similarly, no signiﬁcant association was apparent in
three prospective cohort studies [55–57]. Another study evaluating the association between
ovarian cancer risk and the combination of regular coffee consumption, alcohol drinking, and
smoking also detected no signiﬁcant association [58]. Evidence from these studies suggests
that coffee consumption does not strongly inﬂuence ovarian cancer risk.
10.8
PANCREATIC CANCER
Pancreatic cancer is among the leading causes of cancer-related deaths worldwide, with a
5-year survival rate lower than 5% and a case fatality proportion of 99% within 12 months
of diagnosis [59]. Many studies were conducted after an early warning in the early 1980s
suggesting that coffee consumption was related to pancreatic cancer risk [60]. The rela-
tionship between coffee consumption and the risk of pancreatic cancer was investigated by
ecologic [61,62], case-control [63,64], and cohort [65–67] studies carried out in the United
States, Canada, Europe, and Asia, which yielded inconsistent results. In a 2007 World Cancer
Research Fund (WCRF) report, 18 cohort studies, 37 case-control studies, and 11 ecologic
studies on human pancreatic cancer and coffee consumption were examined. The authors
reported a summary effect estimate of 1.04 (95% CI: 1.0–1.07) for case-control studies and
a summary effect estimate of 1.00 (95% CI: 0.94–1.07) per cup/day for cohort studies [27].
They concluded that no association existed. Since the WCRF report, a Japanese cohort study
[68] evaluated the association between the coffee and pancreatic cancer risk. The 102,137
participants were followed up for an average of 11 years, and 233 incident cases of pancreatic
cancer were identiﬁed; there was no increased risk of pancreatic cancer. However, a reduced
risk was apparent among men who drank at least 3 cups of coffee/day compared with those
who drank coffee rarely or not at all. Taken together, the accumulated evidence does not
support an association between coffee and pancreatic cancer.

coffee consumption and liver cancer in case-control and cohort studies. Therefore, coffee
consumption appears to reduce the risk of liver cancer.

HEAD AND NECK CANCERS
In 1990, the International Agency for Research on Cancer evaluated the possible association
between coffee consumption and head and neck cancers based on the results of six case-
control studies; however, the evidence was not adequate to reach a conclusion [52]. Since then,
an association between coffee intake and the risk of cancers of the oral cavity, pharynx, and
larynx (head and neck cancers) was assessed in at least two other prospective studies [57,79]
and several case-control studies [80–84]. Two cohort studies [57,79] and three case-control
studies [80–82] reported an inverse relationship, but in general, the results were inconsistent,
partly due to the limited number of cases in each study and the different groupings of various
head and neck cancers among studies. One cohort study [85] and ﬁve case-control studies
[86–90] considered the association between coffee intake and laryngeal cancer risk and
overall showed no association. At least two studies grouped upper aerodigestive tract cancers
together, including cancer of the esophagus as well as cancer of the head and neck. The
multicenter case-control study Alcohol-Related Cancers and Genetic Susceptibility in Europe
(ARCAGE), which was conducted in several European countries [91], and a prospective
study of Japanese men in Hawaii [92] both reported that no consistent association with
coffee drinking could be found. Another study evaluated the effect of decaffeinated coffee
on head and neck cancers and found no consistent association [81]. However, in a recent
pooled analysis of case-control studies [93], caffeinated coffee was inversely related with the
risk of cancer of the oral cavity and pharynx. The ORs were 0.96 (95% CI: 0.94–0.98) for an
increment of 1 cup/day and 0.61 (95% CI: 0.47–0.80) for those who consumed 4 cups/day

ENDOMETRIAL CANCER
Cancer of the endometrium is the eighth most common cancer and the 13th most common
cause of cancer deaths in women worldwide. Observational epidemiologic studies inves-
tigating the relationship between endometrial cancer occurrence and coffee consumption
have yielded homogeneous results. A hospital-based, case-control study [94] compared 229
endometrial cancer cases with 12,425 Japanese women as controls. In this population, in
which coffee consumption is rare and rather recent, a signiﬁcant inverse association between
endometrial cancer risk and coffee consumption was detected. The OR for daily consumption
of 1–2 cups was 0.64 (95% CI: 0.43–0.94) compared with nondrinkers, and for 3 or more
cups/day the OR was 0.41 (95% CI: 0.19–0.87), with a signiﬁcant linear trend (P  0.01).
A large, case-control study [95] of Swedish-born postmenopausal women aged 50–74 years
(709 cases and 2887 controls) with much higher coffee consumption also found coffee to be
associated with a reduced risk of endometrial cancer. A hospital-based case-control study
[96] from Italy (454 cases, 2:1 ratio control:case) found the risk at the upper quintiles of
intake to be half that of the lowest quintile. Two additional studies from Japan were published
in 2008. One was a population-based cohort study [97] with 53,724 women, 117 of which
developed endometrial cancer during the 15-year follow-up, and the other was a case-control
study [98] with a similar number of cases [32]; both showed relative risks of 0.4 for women
consuming 3 or more cups/day of coffee or those in the upper tertile of consumption.
Finally, a more recent population-based prospective cohort study [99] including 60,634
women with a 17.6-year follow-up reported that daily coffee consumption was associated
with a lower risk for endometrial cancer. Women drinking 2 or more cups of coffee a day
had a signiﬁcant risk reduction for endometrial cancer, and each additional cup of coffee
was associated with a 10% lower risk of endometrial cancer. However, the risk reduction was
largely conﬁned to overweight and obese women, who are at increased risk for endometrial
cancer. Among these women, each additional cup of coffee consumed daily decreased the risk
of endometrial cancer by 12% and 20%, respectively. Taken together, the evidence suggests
a decreased risk of endometrial cancer among women who consume coffee.

KIDNEY CANCER
Over the past three decades, kidney cancer incidence rates have increased steadily [100].
Since the primary functions of the kidneys are to regulate water and inorganic ion balance and
to excrete waste products and foreign chemicals, the risk of kidney cancer may be affected by
the quantity and type of beverages consumed [101]. Coffee consumption may reduce kidney
cancer risk through the diuretic effects of caffeine, which blocks secretion of an antidiuretic
hormone. Further, antioxidants in coffee alleviate oxidative damage to DNA, proteins, and
other molecules [101]. Coffee consumption also appears to reduce the risk of kidney cancer
by improving insulin sensitivity [102]. A possible link between insulin sensitivity and renal
cell cancer risk is suggested by the strong positive association between obesity and renal cell
cancer [103]. Studies have investigated the association between coffee consumption and risk
of kidney cancer; in particular, one ecologic study [104] reported a correlation of incidence of
0.62 for men and 0.4 for women. A total of 19 case-control studies have reported inconsistent
results, providing a summary estimate of 0.99 (95% CI: 0.96–1.01) [27]. A pooled analysis
of 13 cohort studies found that coffee consumption was associated with a lower risk of
kidney cancer overall, with a summary estimate of 0.95 (95% CI: 0.90–1.01) among women;
however, this effect was not signiﬁcant [105]. Thus, there is sufﬁcient evidence showing that
coffee consumption is associated with a slightly lower risk of kidney cancer.
10.13
BRAIN CANCER
Cancers of the brain and central nervous system are the 18th most common type of cancer
worldwide. Further, brain tumors are the 13th most common cause of cancer deaths. How-
ever, few studies have evaluated the relationship between coffee and brain tumors. Of the ﬁve
case-control studies [106–110] that have investigated coffee consumption and brain tumors,
results have been inconsistent. A meta-analysis [111] of three prospective cohorts from the
United States (335 incident cases of glioma) evaluated coffee, tea, and caffeine intake in
relation to glioma risk. A signiﬁcant inverse association was noted for combined coffee and
tea consumption (5 vs. 0–1 cups/day, HR = 0.60, 95% CI: 0.41–0.87) and a strong inverse
association for caffeine intake was observed among men (HR = 0.46, 95% CI: 0.26–0.81)
comparing top and bottom quintiles of total caffeine intake. One cohort study [112] of
133,811 subjects who were followed for up to 21 years found a signiﬁcant dose–response
effect of coffee consumption on primary adult-onset glioma risk. Those who consumed
7 cups of coffee/day had a 70% higher risk for glioma. The more recent European Prospec-
tive Investigation into Cancer and Nutrition cohort study [113] reported no signiﬁcant as-
sociation between coffee and the risk of glioma or meningioma, although ≥100 mL daily
coffee and tea consumption was associated with a lower risk of glioma.

CONCLUSIONS
Coffee is widely consumed worldwide, and substantial evidence from both laboratory and
animal studies suggests its ability to reduce the risk of cancer. The studies should be in-
terpreted cautiously because the method of coffee preparation and the type of coffee bean
vary considerably among studies. In addition, when drinking coffee, people incorporate in
various amount a number of food items such as milk, cream, sugar or sweeteners that could
modify cancer risk. However, a growing body of evidence from epidemiological studies
suggests that coffee drinking does not have a harmful effect, but is inversely associated with
the risk of liver, kidney, endometrial, head and neck cancers, and to a lesser extent, breast
and colorectal cancers. Data on decaffeinated coffee are too sparse for detailed analysis, but
indicate no increased risk. Additional well-designed studies, especially randomized clini-
cal studies among high-risk populations, are needed to elucidate the relationship between
coffee consumption and cancer risk and to analyze patterns of consumption with respect to
health outcomes.
