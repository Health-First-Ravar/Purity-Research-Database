Abstract
The abstract for your chapter is reproduced below for your reference. Please
note that this will not appear in the final printed version of your chapter.
Alzheimer's disease (AD) is the most prevalent form of dementia in the aged 
population. Definitive diagnosis of AD is based on the presence of senile 
plaques and neurofibrillary tangles that are identified in post-mortem brain 
specimens. The formation of AD specific lesions is attributed to the patholog-
ical accumulation of either extracellular amyloid beta peptide or intraneuro-
nal hyperphosphorylated Tau protein. Constituents of the lesions are prone 
to promote synaptic deficits leading to memory impairments. However, 
besides targeting lesions, other pathways may be of interest to control, such 
as inflammatory processes. In this review, we will emphasize the current data 
supporting the beneficial impact of coffee and constituents in Alzheimer's 
disease and presumable underlying mechanisms.

8.1ŌĆā Introduction: Alzheimer's Disease
Alzheimer's disease (AD) was first described more than a hundred years ago. 
With longer life expectancy, its incidence has increased dramatically and cur-
rent forecasts speak in terms of a doubling of the number of persons affected 
every 20 years.1 AD is the most frequently encountered form of dementia 
(about 70% of cases of dementia). The definitive diagnosis of AD is based 
on the observation of characteristic brain lesions, usually found during a 
post-mortem examination: senile plaques and neurofibrillary tangles.
Neurofibrillary degeneration consists of the intraneuronal accumulation 
of proteinaceous fibrils forming flame-shaped neurons into paired helical fil-
aments (PHF) (for reviews see Buee et al.,2 Sergeant et al.3). The major antigen 
of PHF was shown to correspond to Tau protein, which is phosphorylated. Tau 
is a neuronal protein essentially located within the axonal compartment. Its 
structure makes it essential for the organization, stabilization and dynamics 
of microtubules,2,3 but recent data also emphasize that Tau has other import-
ant neuronal functions at the dendritic and nuclear levels.4ŌĆō6 The physiologic

Amyloid pathology is characterized by the extracellular accumulation of 
the amyloid-beta peptide (A╬▓) that is normally present in low concentrations. 
A╬▓ peptides are derived from either a precursor called ╬▓APP (╬▓-Amyloid Pre-

notably involved in innate immunity regulation such as CR1, TREM2 or 
CD33 modulate AD risk in genome-wide association studies.14ŌĆō18 Microg-
lial cells seem to play an important role in AD-related central inflammatory 
processes. Whether microglial activation is protective or deleterious regard-

where.19,20 The picture seems more clear regarding Tau. It has been stressed

to play an important role in AD, notably regarding microglial activation.26,27

The widespread consumption of caffeine underlies the importance of 
studying the consequences of its chronic intake to the aging-associated 
cognitive decline, particularly in pathological conditions. Caffeine con-
sumption increases alertness and improves cognition in physiological 
conditions.30ŌĆō33 Caffeine also improves attention and information process-
ing.34 In rodents, evidence from the past few years supports the cognitive 
enhancer properties of caffeine in a variety of behavioral tasks that evaluate 
learning and memory.35,36 More recently, and in line with such experimen-
tal evaluations, a study pointed out that caffeine improves long-term mem-
ory in humans.37
However, only recently, the relationship between its consumption and 
the pathophysiology of many neurodegenerative diseases has been firmly 
established. Even if some controversy may exist,38 caffeine consumption 
has beneficial effects not only in aging, reducing the associated cognitive 
decline in healthy subjects,39ŌĆō41 but also in AD patients. Indeed, different 
epidemiological reports highlight the inverse correlation between caffeine 
consumption and the incidence or severity of AD. In the Canadian Study of 
Health and Aging (CSHA), daily coffee drinking decreased the risk of AD by 
31% during a 5 years follow-up.42 In line with this study, another study from 
the Cardiovascular Risk Factors, Aging and Dementia (CAIDE) study popu-
lation reports that coffee drinkers at midlife had lower risk of dementia and 
AD later in life compared with those drinking no or only little coffee. The 
lowest risk (65% decreased) was found in people who drank 3ŌĆō5 cups per 
day.43 Finally, a retrospective study has shown inverse correlation between 
coffee intake and the occurrence of AD later on in life. Indeed, while age-
matched controls had an average daily caffeine intake of 198.7 ┬▒ 135.7 mg 
during the corresponding 20 years of their lifetimes, patients with AD had a 
lower average daily caffeine intake of 73.9 ┬▒ 97.9 mg during the 20 years that 
preceded diagnosis of AD.44 Other retrospective data proposed that high 
plasma caffeine levels were associated with a reduced risk of dementia or a 
delayed onset in patients with mild cognitive impairment (MCI).45 It is also 
noteworthy that in a recent study from the Honolulu-Asian Aging Study, 
the authors did not find a significant association between caffeine intake 
and dementia risk.46 However, they interestingly reported that, at autopsy,

8.3ŌĆā Caffeine, Adenosine Receptor and AD Lesions
Caffeine has been shown to mitigate cognitive decline induced by A╬▓ as well 
as to reduce amyloid burden in transgenic mice overexpressing mutated 
APP (APPsw) in preventive but also therapeutic paradigms. Indeed, APPsw 
mice chronically treated from 4 to 9.5 months of age with caffeine (300 mg 
LŌłÆ1 by drinking water corresponding to 500 mg dŌłÆ1 in humans) were cogni-
tively improved in several behavioral tasks that evaluated working and spa-
tial memories and exhibited reduction of hippocampal A╬▓1-40 and A╬▓1-42.49 
Importantly, a similar treatment of APPsw mice at late pathological stages 
(18ŌĆō19 months) for 4ŌĆō5 weeks reversed memory deficits and reduced amy-
loid deposition as well as soluble A╬▓ levels in both entorhinal cortex and 
hippocampus.50 Such beneficial effects of caffeine against A╬▓ production 
has recently been confirmed by another group in an experimental model 
of sporadic AD based on feeding rabbits with a cholesterol-enriched diet 
that elevates both A╬▓ levels and Tau phosphorylation in the brain.51 In this 
study, rabbits fed with a cholesterol-enriched diet were treated with low 
doses of caffeine (0.5 to 30 mg dŌłÆ1) through drinking water, correspond-
ing to a maximal 60 mg dŌłÆ1 consumption in humans. In this paradigm, 
caffeine significantly decreased A╬▓ production in accordance with Aren-
dash's results.51 Interestingly, reduced production of A╬▓1-40 and A╬▓1-42 was 
also observed in a neuroblastoma model overexpressing mutated APP fol-
lowing treatment with concentrations of caffeine below 10 ┬ĄM,49 further 
supporting that caffeine impacts on mechanisms underlying amyloid pep-
tide production. In accordance, chronic caffeine treatment of APPsw mice 
has been associated with decreased presenilin (PS1) and ╬▓-amyloid cleaving 
enzyme (BACE1) protein expression as well as increased Insulin-Degrading 
Enzyme (IDE) levels, the latter presumably contributing to enhanced A╬▓ 
degradation.49,51 The effect of caffeine on BACE1 expression could relate 
to its ability to reduce c-Raf1 activity, possibly through PKA activation.50 
In addition, caffeine would reduce GSK3 expression and/or activity and 
thereby influence A╬▓ production.50 However, a direct effect of caffeine on 
╬│-secretase activity remains elusive and mechanisms linking caffeine and 
A╬▓ production/clearance deserve further evaluation. Recently, we evaluated 
effects of chronic caffeine intake (0.3 g LŌłÆ1 through drinking water), given 
at an early pathological stage, in the THY-Tau22 transgenic mouse model of 
progressive AD-like Tau pathology.52 We found that chronic caffeine intake

nearly two-fold higher compared to young rats.57,58 More importantly, the

cAMP-dependent effects.58,59 This is accompanied by clear behavioral defi-
cits in hippocampus-dependent tasks, such as spatial memory in rats.60 
Accordingly, rats overexpressing hippocampal A2A receptors also exhibit 
behavioral deficits including spatial memory defects as well as LTP impair-

impairment, such as hypoxia, diabetes or epilepsy, share similar A2AR over-

synaptic and cognitive dysfunction following A2AR blockade with the selec-

impairment within the context of aging.

appears to be a rational strategy to mitigate several detrimental compo-

8.5ŌĆā Conclusion
Caffeine consumption is prone to reduce cognitive decline and the risk to 
develop AD. Caffeine is not only a cognitive enhancer but is also able to 
counteract pathogenic processes underlying AD, notably those leading to the 
formation of both detrimental protein aggregates, namely amyloid plaques 
and neurofibrillary tangles. The qualities of caffeine as a safe, inexpensive 
and brain-penetrating agent deserves the translation of these findings into 
a pilot clinical trial in AD patients. So far, no clinical trials have been per-
formed to date to evaluate the extent to which caffeine can slow down dis-
ease progression in patients that have already developed AD. Mechanisms 
underlying caffeine effects remain unsolved but a great effort towards the 
understanding of one of its main druggable targets, A2A receptor, is also 
ongoing. This is of crucial importance since A2A receptor antagonists have 
a safe profile in humans and have been clinically tested in Parkinson's dis-
ease patients. Finally, we cannot neglect other coffee compounds that are 
of potential therapeutic interest. These have been much less studied, the 
scientific community focusing more on caffeine. A great effort needs to be 
made in this field since the few molecules already isolated from coffee share 
neuroprotective properties.
