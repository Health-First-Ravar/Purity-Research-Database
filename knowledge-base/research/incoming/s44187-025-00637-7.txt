Abstract
Polyphenols are a diverse and widely distributed group of plant-derived 
phytochemicals known for their broad spectrum of biological activities. Found 
abundantly in fruits, vegetables, grains, tea, coffee, and wine, these compounds 
are increasingly recognized for their significant contribution to human health. 
Their benefits are largely attributed to potent antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and 
bioactive properties, which support the prevention and management of chronic 
diseases. Among their many functions, polyphenols serve as powerful antioxidants, 
capable of neutralizing reactive oxygen species (ROS) and reducing oxidative stress 
a key contributor to the development of degenerative diseases such as cancer, 
cardiovascular conditions, and neurodegenerative disorders including Alzheimer’s 
and Parkinson’s disease. This review explores the classification of polyphenols based 
on their chemical structures, their major dietary sources, and their bioavailability and 
metabolism, which influence their biological efficacy. In addition to their antioxidant 
potential, polyphenols exhibit a wide range of therapeutic activities, including anti-
inflammatory, anti-cancer, cardioprotective, and neuroprotective effects. The review 
also discusses current challenges in optimizing the health benefits of polyphenols, 
including limited bioavailability, interactions with other dietary components, and 
variability in individual metabolism. Finally, future research directions are proposed, 
emphasizing advanced delivery systems, functional food applications, and integrated 
clinical studies using tools such as metabolomics and nutrigenomics.

Dietary polyphenols have garnered significant attention for their potential health ben­

disease, and neurodegenerative disorders [1, 2]. These compounds, abundant in plant-

exhibit a range of biological activities, notably inhibiting low-density lipoprotein oxida­
tion, a key factor in developing atherosclerotic lesions [3], a hallmark of cardiovascular 
disease. Research has further revealed their therapeutic potential in managing various 
conditions, including cancer, osteoporosis, neurodegenerative diseases, and diabetes 
mellitus [1]. Phenolic acids, a crucial subclass of polyphenols, are abundant in fruits 
and vegetables [3]. Extracts from fruits like grapes, tea, and pomegranate have shown 
protective effects against atherosclerotic lesions. Polyphenol-rich foods are crucial in 
maintaining health and preventing disease [3]. Despite the structural complexity of poly­
phenols posing challenges for research, their potential in preventing degenerative dis­
eases remains a prominent area of investigation [4].
Although polyphenols are well known for their antioxidant properties, they may also 
display pro-oxidant activity under certain conditions, modulating cellular metabolism 
and potentially enhancing their anti-cancer effects [5, 6]. This pro-oxidant activity can 
induce apoptosis and inhibit cell proliferation [6]. The additional biological activi­

tissue inflammation, various cancers, infections, and neurodegenerative diseases (Fig. 1)

2  Classification and chemistry of polyphenols
Dietary phenolics, commonly called polyphenols, are a vast and diverse class of natu­
ral products ubiquitous in the plant kingdom [12]. Over 8,000 phenolic structures are 
known, including more than 4,000 identified flavonoids [13]. Though unified by com­
mon phenolic structural motifs, polyphenols comprise a diverse array of subgroups (Fig. 
1). These compounds are abundant in various foods and beverages, including fruits, veg­
etables, whole grains, tea, wine, and chocolate [14]. Given their diversity and widespread 
presence in plants, numerous classification schemes exist for polyphenols, often based 
on origin, biological function, and chemical composition [15]. In plants, polyphenols

2.1  Phenolic acids
Phenolic acids are a subclass of non-flavonoid polyphenols, primarily categorized into 
benzoic acid derivatives (C1-C6 backbone) and cinnamic acid derivatives (C3-C6 back­
bone) [13, 18]. They exist in both free and bound forms. Bound phenolic acids are preva­
lent in grains and seeds and require hydrolysis (acidic, alkaline, or enzymatic) for release 
[19]. Free phenolic acids are more commonly found in fruits and vegetables, often con­
centrated in the bran and hull [20], providing further details on the classification and 
occurrence of phenolic compounds in various food sources (Fig. 2).
2.2  Flavonoids
Flavonoids, a large subclass of polyphenols, share a fundamental C6-C3-C6 skeleton 
composed of two aromatic rings (A and B) linked by a three-carbon chain that typically 
forms a heterocyclic ring (C) [21, 22]. Variations in the hydroxylation pattern and modi­
fications of the C ring give rise to various flavonoid subgroups, including anthocyanins, 
flavan-3-ols, flavones, flavanones, and flavonols (Fig. 3) [21]. Most flavonoids feature a 
B-ring linked to the C2 position of the central ring, whereas in isoflavones and neofla­
vonoids, the linkage occurs at C3 and C4, respectively [13]. Chalcones, lacking the het­
erocyclic C ring, are also considered part of the flavonoid family [13]. Although often 
represented by aglycones in structural depictions, flavonoids predominantly exist as gly­
cosides in plants [13], and this glycosylation pattern influences their biological activity, 
including antioxidant capacity [23]. For more information on flavonoid nomenclature 
and classification, please refer to [24].
2.3  Polyphenolic amides
Capsaicinoids, found in chili peppers, and avenanthramides, found in oats, are key 
examples of nitrogen-containing polyphenols [25, 26]. Capsaicinoids, like capsaicin, are 
known for their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties and ability to modulate 
cellular oxidative defense mechanisms [12]. Avenanthramides also exhibit antioxidant 
activity, including inhibiting LDL oxidation [12]. Although less prevalent than their oxy­
gen-containing counterparts, nitrogen-containing polyphenols are an emerging research

2.4  Stilbenes
Stilbenes are a relatively small but biologically significant class of polyphenolic com­
pounds characterized by a 1,2-diphenylethylene backbone. Among the stilbenes, resve­
ratrol (3,5,4’-trihydroxystilbene) is the most extensively studied due to its presence in 
grapes, red wine, peanuts, and several berries, and its broad spectrum of bioactivities. 
Resveratrol has been shown to possess antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, cardioprotective, 
neuroprotective, and anti-cancer properties. Mechanistically, stilbenes act by scaveng­
ing reactive oxygen species, modulating signaling pathways such as NF-κB, AMPK, and 
SIRT1, and regulating the expression of genes involved in apoptosis and cell survival. 
However, the bioavailability of stilbenes, particularly resveratrol, remains a challenge 
due to rapid metabolism and poor aqueous solubility. As a result, recent research has 
focused on enhancing their stability and bioefficacy through nanoencapsulation, struc­
tural analogs, and combination therapies. Although less abundant in the diet compared

2.5  Lignans
Lignans are a group of diphenolic compounds formed by the oxidative dimerization of 
two phenylpropanoid units (C6-C3), commonly found in seeds (especially flaxseeds), 
whole grains, legumes, fruits, and vegetables. Major dietary lignans include secoisolar­
iciresinol, matairesinol, pinoresinol, and lariciresinol. Once ingested, plant lignans are 
converted by gut microbiota into enterolignans, enterodiol and enterolactone which are 
believed to be the biologically active forms in humans. Lignans exhibit a wide range of 
biological activities, including antioxidant, estrogenic/anti-estrogenic, anti-inflamma­
tory, and anti-cancer effects. Their structural similarity to endogenous estrogens allows 
them to interact with estrogen receptors, which may be particularly beneficial in hor­
mone-related conditions such as breast and prostate cancers. Epidemiological studies 
have associated higher lignan intake with reduced risk of cardiovascular disease and 
hormone-dependent cancers, although findings remain inconsistent due to differences 
in dietary intake, microbiota composition, and individual metabolism. Current research 
is increasingly focused on understanding the interindividual variability in lignan metab­
olism and the implications of gut microbiota in modulating their health effects [15].
2.6  Other polyphenols
Ellagic acid and its derivatives in berries and nuts also exhibit antioxidant activity [1]. 
Curcumin, derived from turmeric, is a potent antioxidant with anti-inflammatory prop­
erties. Rosmarinic acid and ellagic acid are dimers of caffeic and gallic acid, respectively 
[13]. Although gallic and ellagic acids also occur in free forms, their presence as glucose 
esters in hydrolyzable tannins may exert anti-nutritive effects by interfering with mineral 
absorption [14, 28].
3  Bioavailability of polyphenols in relation to antioxidant efficacy
While polyphenols are widely recognized for their antioxidant and therapeutic potential, 
their biological effects in vivo are largely influenced by their bioavailability. This factor 
determines the extent to which polyphenols and their metabolites can reach target tis­
sues in active form and exert protective effects against oxidative stress. The chemical 
structure of polyphenols including the number and position of hydroxyl groups, degree 
of glycosylation, and molecular size plays a critical role in modulating their absorption 
and metabolic fate [29].
Flavanones found in citrus fruits and catechins present in teas differ not only in struc­
ture but also in how they are absorbed and metabolized, resulting in varying levels of 
biological activity. These differences directly impact their ability to neutralize reactive 
oxygen species (ROS) and modulate redox-sensitive signaling pathways involved in 
chronic disease development [30]. Assessing polyphenol absorption through plasma and 
urine concentrations after consuming polyphenol-rich foods is standard practice [31]. 
Plasma polyphenol levels typically decline after intestinal absorption, though some, like 
quercetin, exhibit longer half-lives due to interactions with plasma proteins [29]. The 
glycosylation of polyphenols, often involving one, two, or even three sugar molecules 
or sugar derivatives (including malonic acid substitutions), significantly impacts their

properties [32]. This glycosylation affects hydrophilicity, as exemplified by quercetin and 
quercetin-3-O-rhamnoglucoside, and other crucial factors like membrane permeability 
and interaction with transporters [33, 34]. This complexity underscores the need to con­
sider the forms of polyphenols in food when evaluating their bioavailability and poten­
tial health impacts.
The colon microflora plays a significant role in cleaving sugar moieties from polyphe­
nols [35, 36]. The mucosal lining of the gastrointestinal tract also contributes to this 
activity via enzymes like lactase-phlorizin hydrolase [34]. Although certain foods may 
contain glycosidases [37], their contribution is minor compared to the enzymatic activity 
of the gut microbiome and host. Human cells, particularly during development, express 
β-glucosidases [38]. Polyphenols bound to sugars often reach the colon, interacting with 
the microbiota (Fig. 1). This interaction leads to the biotransformation of polyphenols 
into more bioavailable metabolites [39] and the modulation of gut microbial composi­
tion, potentially favoring beneficial bacteria [40, 41].
4  Oxidative stress and its role in disease pathogenesis
Atoms are generally stable when their outermost electron shell is filled with electrons, 
minimizing their reactivity with other atoms [42]. However, if an atom loses or gains an 
electron in its outermost shell, it becomes a free radical with an unpaired electron, mak­
ing it highly reactive [43]. This increased reactivity can lead to interactions with other 
atoms and molecules, including crucial cellular components like lipids, proteins, and 
nucleic acids. These interactions can cause modifications to the structure of these mac­
romolecules, potentially disrupting their function and contributing to oxidative stress 
[44]. Oxidative stress is implicated in various degenerative diseases (Fig. 5) [45, 46].
Reactive oxygen species encompass all reactive molecules, including free radicals, 
where the reactivity is based on an oxygen atom [47]. Although ‘ROS’ highlights oxygen, 
reactive species can also include other elements such as nitrogen or chlorine, which con­
tribute to their chemical reactivity. These reactive species, whether oxygen-based or oth­
erwise, are generated through various cellular processes and exposure to environmental 
factors [48]. Oxidative stress results from an imbalance between the production of these 
reactive species and the body’s antioxidant defense mechanisms, leading to potential cel­
lular damage [49].
4.1  Antioxidant activity of polyphenols
Oxidative stress is a significant challenge in animal agriculture, negatively impacting 
livestock growth and productivity. The use of polyphenols as feed additives to mitigate 
this stress is a promising area of research [50, 51].
The basic antioxidant mechanism of polyphenols involves the transfer of a hydrogen 
atom from a hydroxyl group to a free radical [52]. The antioxidant activity of polyphe­
nols is multifaceted, involving direct radical scavenging, metal chelation, and enzymatic 
modulation [3, 52]. In addition to direct antioxidant effects, polyphenols can modu­
late cellular signaling pathways related to oxidative stress, further contributing to their 
protective effects [52, 53]. However, polyphenols’ bioavailability can vary significantly 
depending on factors like their chemical structure and the animal’s digestive system [34]. 
This bioavailability is a crucial factor to consider when evaluating the efficacy of poly­
phenols as feed additives [54].

Following mechanisms describe the antioxidant action of polyphenols:
4.1.1  Hydrogen atom transfer (HAT)
In HAT, an antioxidant donates a hydrogen atom to a free radical (R⋅), neutralizing the 
radical to form a stable molecule and generating an antioxidant radical (ArO⋅). This anti­
oxidant radical is typically less reactive than the initial free radical and can be further 
stabilized through resonance or other mechanisms [55, 56].
4.1.2  Single electron transfer-proton transfer
This mechanism involves a two-step process. First, an electron is transferred from the 
antioxidant to the free radical (R⋅), forming a radical cation (ArOH⋅+) and an anion. In 
the second step, a proton is transferred from the radical cation to the anion, resulting 
in a stable molecule and a neutral antioxidant radical (ArO⋅). It’s important to note that 
proton-coupled electron transfer is often considered a variant of HAT, particularly in 
biological systems, where proton transfer along a hydrogen bond can facilitate the elec­
tron transfer process [57, 58].
4.1.3  Sequential proton loss-electron transfer (SPLET)
This two-step process begins with the loss of a proton from the antioxidant, forming an 
anion. Subsequently, an electron is transferred from the anion to the free radical, neu­
tralizing it. SPLET is often favored in polar solvents, where the initial proton loss is sta­
bilized [59, 60].
4.1.4  Adduct formation
In this mechanism, a hydrogen bond forms between the hydroxyl group of the antioxi­
dant and the free radical. This interaction can lead to the formation of a stable adduct, 
effectively neutralizing the radical. In radiolytic solutions, the absorption of a hydrogen 
ion by the radical can create a double bond within an aromatic ring, further stabilizing 
the adduct [61].
These four mechanisms, along with other factors like metal chelation and modulation 
of antioxidant enzymes, contribute to the complex antioxidant activity of polyphenols. 
This antioxidant capacity plays a crucial role in protecting against oxidative stress and its 
associated diseases (Table 1).
4.2  Pro-oxidative activity of polyphenols
Seasonal type and weather during plant growth affect polyphenol concentrations, hence 
their link to pro-oxidant effects in animal feed requires further investigation. Environ­
mental factors like temperature and UV radiation indeed affect polyphenol biosynthesis 
in plants [31]. However, attributing a pro-oxidant effect solely to elevated polyphenols 
in feed from specific seasons oversimplifies a complex issue [51]. Many factors influence 
the overall redox balance in animals, and the pro-oxidant effects of polyphenols in vivo 
are still under investigation [50].
The flavonoid effects depend on various factors like the source and concentration of 
free radicals and the polyphenols [6, 62]. The potential pro-oxidant mechanisms, includ­
ing chemical instability, cellular glutathione depletion, the role of copper ions in autoxi­
dation, and the generation of cytotoxic semiquinone radicals [9, 63]. The interaction with

NADH exemplifies how these semiquinone radicals can contribute to ROS formation 
[64]. It is important, however, to remember that the pro-oxidant effects of polyphenols 
are often observed in in vitro studies at high concentrations [65], and their relevance 
to in vivo situations requires further investigation [66]. Additionally, the interaction of 
polyphenols with other dietary components and the gut microbiota can significantly 
influence their overall effects [67]. Polyphenols possess pro-oxidant potential, this does 
not diminish their health benefits [3, 63], especially at physiologically relevant concen­

Copper ions can contribute to oxidative stress by influencing the production of reac­
tive oxygen species like hydrogen peroxide and superoxide radicals [69]. However, the 
context of copper’s interaction with polyphenols is crucial. While copper can enhance 
the pro-oxidant activity of some polyphenols, particularly at high concentrations [6], 
polyphenols can also chelate metal ions like copper, reducing their ability to catalyze 
ROS formation. This metal-chelating activity contributes to the antioxidant properties 
of polyphenols [3].
The potential of polyphenols to induce a mild oxidative stress response that activates 
endogenous antioxidant systems and other protective mechanisms, resulting in a cyto­
protective effect [63]. This concept of hormesis, where low doses of stressors activate 
adaptive responses, is an emerging area of research in polyphenol biology [63]. However, 
it is important to distinguish between mild, adaptive oxidative stress and damaging oxi­
dative stress. The concentration of polyphenols, their chemical structure, and the spe­
cific cellular context play critical roles in determining the outcome [70].
Polyphenols’ primary function is protecting against free radicals and reactive oxygen 
species generated during oxygen metabolism [70]. The damaging effects of free radicals 
on cell structure and function, including alterations in net charge, osmotic pressure, and 
volume, potentially lead to cell death. The link between lipid peroxidation byproducts 
and cancer progression is well-established [69]. The accumulation of these byprod­
ucts can disrupt cellular signaling pathways, promote inflammation, and contribute to 
tumorigenesis [Figure 3]. It is important to remember that polyphenols, oxidative stress, 
and cellular health interplay are intricate [71]. Although antioxidant properties are

Flavonoids exert protective effects on cells by scavenging free radicals, which helps 
mitigate oxidative damage [73]. The following mechanism outlines how radicals (R) oxi­

The unpaired electron in the flavonoxy radical (O⋅) can be delocalized across the aro­
matic ring, stabilizing the radical through resonance. This resonance allows the fla­
vonoxy radical to engage in various reactions, such as dimerization, dismutation, 
recombination with other radicals, or oxidation to a quinone, depending on the inter­
actions with radicals, antioxidants, or biomolecules [74, 75]. When flavonoxy radicals 
interact with oxygen, they generate quinones and superoxide anions. While this reaction 
may confer benefits to tumor tissues, it can simultaneously provoke pro-oxidant effects 
in healthy tissue [76]. This underscores the idea that the antioxidant potential of flavo­
noids is influenced by both the reactivity of the flavonoxy radical and the cell’s redox 
status (i.e., the O/OH potential) [77].
Stabilizing the flavonoxy radical through resonance is key to the dual nature of fla­
vonoids as both antioxidants and pro-oxidants [78]. The balance between the reactivity 
of this radical and the cellular redox environment dictates the outcome [79]. This con­
text-dependent behavior enables therapeutic specificity by exploiting differential cellular 
environments, thereby facilitating targeted intervention—such as selectively inducing 
cytotoxicity in malignant cells—while simultaneously posing a risk of off-target effects in 
normal, healthy tissues [80, 81].
The structural features of phenolic compounds, particularly the presence of hydroxyl 
groups and a conjugated aromatic system are crucial for their antioxidant activity [82, 
83]. These structural elements enhance their ability to donate electrons and stabilize free 
radicals by forming stable phenoxyl radicals [84]. The influence of cofactors in modu­
lating the activity of phenolic compounds [85]. At higher concentrations, the increased 
availability of hydroxyl groups contributes to more pronounced radical scavenging activ­
ity [86]. However, it is essential to remember that the relationship between concentra­
tion and antioxidant activity is not always linear [78].
As discussed earlier, high concentrations of polyphenols can sometimes lead to pro-
oxidant effects [82]. Molecular weight, the number and position of hydroxyl groups, as 
well as other structural features like glycosylation and specific functional groups, all sig­
nificantly influence radical scavenging potential [75]. Therefore, evaluating the overall 
antioxidant capacity of polyphenols requires considering the interplay of these diverse 
factors, along with the specific cellular context [87].

powder, rich in epicatechin and other phenolic compounds, has indeed been shown to 
improve the oxidative stability of corn oil [88]. Its sustained antioxidant activity makes 
it a valuable natural ingredient in the functional food industry [89]. The antioxidant 
benefits of 6-gingerol and curcumin in soybean oil. These compounds from ginger and 
turmeric enhance oxidative stability and provide additional health benefits [90]. Freeze-

Strong evidence links the consumption of polyphenol-rich foods like fruits, vegetables, 
and whole grains to a reduced risk of chronic diseases, including cancer, cardiovascu­
lar disease, chronic inflammation, and neurodegenerative conditions. Although vitamin 
C contributes to the antioxidant capacity of fruits, polyphenols typically serve as the 
primary determinants of their overall antioxidant activity [99]. Polyphenols exert their 
antioxidant effects by neutralizing free radicals through donating electrons or hydrogen 
atoms. Their efficacy stems from highly conjugated structures and specific hydroxylation 
patterns, such as the 3-hydroxy group in flavanols, which are critical for scavenging free 
radicals [100].
Polyphenols mitigate oxidative stress and reduce the rate of oxidation by preventing 
the formation of active free radical species or deactivating their precursors [63]. They 
can act as “chain breakers,” interrupting lipid peroxidation chain reactions and halting 
the propagation of oxidative damage [82]. Research has demonstrated the effectiveness 
of dietary polyphenols in protecting against various diseases associated with oxidative 
stress. This protective effect highlights their importance in maintaining human health 
[101].
Beyond their radical scavenging activity, polyphenols demonstrate potent metal-che­
lating properties [102]. This ability to bind to transition metals, particularly Fe2+, is cru­
cial in inhibiting the Fenton reaction [75], a significant source of highly reactive hydroxyl 
radicals that contribute to oxidative damage [77]. Chelation effectively neutralizes the 
catalytic activity of these metal ions, preventing the generation of harmful free radi­
cals and protecting cellular components from oxidative damage [103]. This highlights 
another key mechanism through which polyphenols exert their antioxidant effects.

Polyphenols often work synergistically with other antioxidants, including vitamins, 
enhancing their protective effects [104]. They may participate in regenerating essential 
vitamins, ensuring their optimal function in maintaining cellular redox balance [105].
The limitations of in vitro models in evaluating the overall antioxidant capacity of 
polyphenols. Although these models offer valuable insights into specific antioxidant 
mechanisms, they fall short of fully capturing the complexity of interactions within bio­
logical systems [64]. In vitro models can offer useful information about how polyphenols 
scavenge free radicals and chelate metals, but extrapolating these findings to in vivo situ­
ations requires careful consideration [65]. The potential pro-oxidant effects of polyphe­
nols at high concentrations also need to be considered when using in vitro models [6], 
and further research is needed to understand these effects in vivo fully and their poten­
tial implications for human health [82].
5  Challenges and future perspectives
Besides helping to reduce oxidative stress by donating electrons or hydrogen, these 
compounds also affect how cells send signals, turn genes on or off, and control enzymes 
[106]. Emerging evidence suggests that polyphenols, especially their in vivo metabolites, 
interact with various intracellular signaling pathways, including protein and lipid kinase 
pathways, leading to modulatory effects on cellular processes [107].
The low bioavailability of polyphenols due to rapid metabolism raises valid questions 
about the extent of their direct antioxidant activity in vivo [107]. Plasma concentrations 
often remain low, typically below 1 µmol/L, which has led researchers to explore alter­
native mechanisms beyond traditional antioxidant functions. These alternative mecha­
nisms might involve interactions with cellular signaling pathways [12], modulation of 
gene expression, and other complex interactions within biological systems that contrib­
ute to their observed health benefits.
This shift in perspective has indeed fueled the growth of nutrigenomics. This field 
explores how phytochemicals, including polyphenols, influence gene expression and cel­
lular function. By modulating signaling pathways, polyphenols can induce changes in 
cellular processes, potentially offering significant health benefits. Understanding these 
complex mechanisms, especially the impact of metabolism on polyphenol activity, is 
crucial for evaluating their therapeutic potential in various areas, such as cancer pre­
vention, cardiovascular protection, and neuroprotection [108, 109]. This aligns perfectly 
with the direction of research focusing on the broader health benefits of polyphenols 
beyond their traditional antioxidant capacity.
6  Conclusion
Polyphenols, ubiquitous plant secondary metabolites, have long been recognized for 
their potential health benefits. Their role in preventing diseases associated with oxida­
tive stress hinges on their absorption, metabolism, distribution, and excretion within 
the body. Bioavailability, a key factor influencing their efficacy in vivo, is affected by 
complex interactions throughout the digestive process. Beyond their antioxidant capac­
ity, polyphenols exhibit remarkable nutritional properties, including antiglycation and 
anti-browning effects. These properties contribute to their potential role in prevent­
ing chronic diseases such as obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and cancer. Fur­
thermore, their antioxidant activity is valuable in food preservation as it reduces lipid

oxidation and enhances overall health benefits. Ongoing research continues to explore 
these natural compounds’ nutritional and therapeutic potential. This research contrib­
utes to consumer health and paves the way for developing novel functional foods incor­
porating these beneficial polyphenols. Understanding polyphenols’ diverse properties 
and mechanisms of action is crucial for maximizing their health benefits and developing 
effective disease prevention and management strategies.
Future research should focus on specific and translational approaches to maxi­
mize their efficacy and application. Promising directions include the development and 
evaluation of encapsulated polyphenols to enhance stability and targeted delivery, the 
incorporation of polyphenols into functional animal nutrition strategies, and the imple­
mentation of clinical trials integrated with metabolomics and nutrigenomics. These 
strategies will deepen our understanding of polyphenol metabolism, identify bioactive 
metabolites, and support the development of personalized nutrition and disease man­
agement frameworks.
