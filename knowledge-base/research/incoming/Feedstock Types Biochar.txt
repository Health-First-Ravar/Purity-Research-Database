The effects of feedstock types and their properties on
hydrothermal carbonisation and resulting hydrochar:
A review
Vigneshwaran Shanmugam1, Elif Kaynak1, Oisik Das1 and
Lokesh P. Padhye2,3,4,5
Hydrothermal carbonisation (HTC) technology benefits the
environment by lowering greenhouse gas emissions and pro-
moting sustainable waste management practices while support-
ing circular economy principles. Over the past two years,
research has focused on optimising HTC process parameters,
broadening the range of suitable feedstocks, and enhancing the
properties of hydrochar and byproducts. This review discusses
the characteristics of the hydrochar, including its calorific value,
surface area, and nutrient content, and the influence of these
parameters by feedstock type in the HTC. Furthermore, it reviews
the suitability of hydrochar derived from different feedstocks in
applications such as renewable solid fuel, soil amendment and
environmental remediation. By analysing the most recent
research advancements and identifying the associated chal-
lenges, this review underscores the importance of HTC in pro-
moting sustainable waste management practices and enhancing
resource utilisation in a circular economy framework.

Introduction
The production of primary crops has maintained its
upward trajectory, reaching 9.6 billion tonnes in 2022, a
56 % increase since 2000 and a 0.7 % rise from 2021 [1].
Cereals dominate the production landscape, accounting
for approximately one-third of the total output, followed
by sugar crops at 22 %, vegetables and oil crops at 12 %
each, fruits at 10 %, and roots and tubers at 9 % [2,3].
Sugarcane, maize, wheat and rice emerged as the top
contributors, making up half of the total primary crop
production. Their organic residues, such as sugarcane
bagasse, corn stover, wheat straw and rice husks, present
challenges in waste management [4].
Hydrothermal carbonisation (HTC) technology stands
out as an efﬁcient technique in the valorisation of waste
as it can convert high-moisture organic residues into
hydrochar, bypassing the need for energy-intensive
drying steps required by other methods and offering
shorter reaction times [5,6]. Decades of research in this
aspect have broadened the range of suitable feedstocks
for HTC [7]. During the HTC process, various reactions
occur either sequentially or simultaneously, including
hydrolysis, decarboxylation, deamination, dehydration,
aromatisation, and polymerisation, resulting in the pro-
duction of hydrochar [8e14]. It is important to note that
the selection of biomass feedstock signiﬁcantly in-
ﬂuences the quality and characteristics of the produced
hydrochar [15,16]. Figure 1 illustrates the HTC process,
showcasing various feedstocks such as agricultural,

Hydrothermal Carbonisation Process:
Feedstock Characteristics and Selection
Ash content: impact on HTC process and hydrochar
The ash content in the feedstock is a critical factor that
can signiﬁcantly affect the HTC process and hydrochar
characteristics. Feedstocks with low ash content can
promote uniform carbonisation and produce hydrochar
with high energy density and minimal inorganic com-
ponents [17]. These characteristics make them suitable
for energy applications, reducing the operational chal-
lenges associated with reactor maintenance. Low ash
content feedstocks include wood-based materials like
pine wood with an ash content (wt.%) of 2.8 %, pine-
wood meal at 1.2 %, pinewood chips at 0.7 %, Eucalyptus
wood at 0.3 %, and Pinus sylvestris sawdust at 0.3 %

the reactor fouling and scaling and reduce the caloriﬁc

feedstocks are particularly effective as soil amendments
[19], leveraging their mineral content to improve soil
structure and pH. Nevertheless, careful consideration is
required to avoid detrimental effects on nutrient avail-
ability, such as reduced iron and phosphorus uptake.
Carbon content: Determinant of energy efficiency and
yield
The degree of carbonisation and energy density in the
hydrochar is governed by its carbon content. Although
agricultural residues contain higher anhydrosugar con-
tent, leading to more signiﬁcant degradation of carbon
compounds, woody residues generally have a higher
carbon content, contributing to superior carbon assimi-
lation during HTC [12]. For instance, the HTC of wood
waste, such as sawdust, can produce hydrochar with high
carbon content and yield [20,21]. Improving the carbon
content of hydrochar can critically enhance the thermal
stability and caloriﬁc value, which are necessary for its
fuel applications [22]. However, a traditional HTC
process generally produces hydrochar with low carbon
content, necessitating further treatment. In this regard,
Ipiales et al. [23] explored various approaches to
enhance the carbon content of hydrochar derived from
the HTC of swine manure, with particular emphasis on
hydrochloric acid (HCl)-assisted HTC and subsequent
washing treatments using HCl and acetone. Their
research indicated that incorporating 0.5 M HCl in the
HTC process at 230 C signiﬁcantly increased the
carbon content of hydrochar from 38 % to 58 %. Post-
treatment washing with acetone reduced nitrogen and
sulfur contents, enabling the production of solid bio-
fuels that meet the ISO/TS 17225e8 standard.

Nitrogen content
Nitrogen content inﬂuences both the HTC process
conditions and yield, as well as the functional properties
of the resulting hydrochar. The higher nitrogen content
in the feedstock containing a high protein content and
fewer lignin leads to lower heating values and C% [25].
In solid fuel applications, it is crucial to minimise ni-
trogen content to prevent the formation of nitrogen
oxides (NOx) during combustion, which are environ-
mentally harmful. On the other hand, high nitrogen
levels in hydrochar are advantageous when used for
manufacturing
supercapacitor
electrodes
and
as
a
fertiliser [26]. Thus, the nitrogen content is a key factor
in deciding the appropriate use of hydrochar, high-
lighting the importance of understanding nitrogen
migration during the HTC process. Xie et al. [27]
investigated the nitrogen content and transformation
during HTC of sewage sludge, focussing on the inﬂu-
ence of different organic components (protein, lipid,
cellulose, hemicellulose, humic acid and lignin) on ni-
trogen retention and hydrochar quality. It was high-
lighted that proteins and lipids are not determinants of
nitrogen content in hydrochar. At the same time, cel-
lulose, hemicellulose, lignin, and humic acid play crucial
roles in nitrogen retention and the degree of aromati-
sation. Although proteins provide nitrogen, they un-
dergo transformations that reduce the nitrogen content.
During the HTC process, proteins decompose into
ammonia, amines and small organic molecules, leading
to a synchronous decrease in nitrogen and carbon con-
tents. Humus was identiﬁed as having the most
comprehensive performance in reducing nitrogen con-
tent and enhancing aromaticity, which is essential for
improving hydrochar as a cleaner solid fuel.
It is understood that ash, carbon and nitrogen content
signiﬁcantly inﬂuence HTC efﬁciency and perfor-
mance, with their effects varying depending on the
application. Carbon content is the most important
among these as it increases the energy density of
hydrochar, making it ideal for energy applications and
carbon sequestration. While ash content negatively af-
fects hydrochar performance by lowering caloriﬁc value
and energy efﬁciency, it does not contribute to carbon-
isation. Nitrogen content has a less pronounced effect
on energy efﬁciency, but it is critical for agricultural
applications because it increases hydrochar’s potential
as a slow-release nitrogen fertiliser. However, high ni-
trogen levels may be detrimental to combustion due to
the formation of NOx [28]. Thus, carbon content is the
most critical factor in overall HTC performance, nitro-
gen content is application-speciﬁc and ash content
limits efﬁciency. The properties of various feedstocks

Mixed feedstocks
Co-hydrothermal carbonisation (Co-HTC) has become
a popular method for converting mixed biomass into
hydrochar due to its efﬁciency and versatility. Co-HTC
is the process of treating two or more types of biomass or
waste materials simultaneously at controlled tempera-
tures and pressures, typically ranging from 150 C to
250 C. This method takes advantage of the comple-
mentary properties of various biomass types, such as
combining nitrogen-rich materials like sewage sludge
with carbon-rich materials like agricultural residues. Cui
et al. [29] provided valuable insights into hydrochar
production via Co-HTC by combining sewage sludge
(SS) with various lignocellulosic feedstocks such as pine
sawdust (PS), bagasse (BS), sunﬂower straw (SFS), and
water hyacinth (WH). The study reported hydrochar
yields from the individual HTC process as 69.40 %,
54.92 %, 47.80 %, 44.45 % and 41.41 % for SS, PS, BS,
SFS and WH, respectively. However, when Co-HTC was
employed, the yields from the combined feedstocks
(SS&PS, SS&BS, SS&SFS, and SS&WH) increased to
65.72 %, 60.81 %, 58.50 %, and 57.09 %, respectively.
These results demonstrate the efﬁciency of Co-HTC in
improving hydrochar yields and provide a clear under-
standing of how different lignocellulosic materials
interact with sewage sludge in the carbonisation process,
making it a promising approach for waste valorisation
and solid fuel production. The ﬁndings emphasise the
importance of feedstock selection and suggest that
combining different biomass types can enhance hydro-
char quality. Future research should build on these
ﬁndings to optimise Co-HTC processes and explore
new feedstock combinations, further advancing the
state-of-the-art of HTC research.

Characteristics of Hydrochar
Fuel properties
The quantity and nature of aromatic structures have a
signiﬁcant impact on hydrochar quality, speciﬁcally its
fuel properties and stability. Hydrochar with a high
content of aromatic structures was found to have better
fuel properties, such as higher carbon content and
caloriﬁc value, making it a viable alternative to tradi-
tional fuels such as bituminous coal [41]. The stability
of aromatic structures was found to contribute to the
long-term carbon sequestration potential of hydrochar
[42]. Understanding the formation of aromatic struc-
tures in hydrochar was deemed critical in optimising the
HTC process.
Wu et al.’s [43] investigation of HTC of agricultural
wastes underscored the importance of temperature
control on fuel properties. It was reported that by
favouring the formation of aromatic structures, the ma-
terial could be tailored for fuel production. In another
work, Lang et al. [44] found that increasing the HTC
temperature from 180 to 220 C slightly increased the
release of aromatic substances in cow manure hydrochar,
from ca.83 to ca.87 %, but this was opposite to the re-
sults of corn stalk produced hydrochar (reduced from
ca.67 to 65 %), and Myriophyllum aquaticum (wetland
plant) produced hydrochar (reduced from ca.68 to
58 %). The principal component analysis results showed
that feedstock type and HTC temperature signiﬁcantly
impacted the content, compositions and characteristics
of dissolved organic matter released from hydrochar.
The caloriﬁc value of hydrochar is a crucial parameter
that determines its energy content and efﬁciency as a
fuel. A higher caloriﬁc value indicates that hydrochar can
release more energy per unit mass when burned, making
it an efﬁcient fuel source. Thus, it is critical to under-
stand the hydrochar’s caloriﬁc value to enable effective
blending with other fuels, determine its potential for
various applications and maximise energy derived from
feedstocks. Oumabady et al. [45] demonstrated that
blending 20 % agricultural waste with 80 % coal pro-
duced hydrochar with a caloriﬁc value of 27.65 MJ/kg,
showing its potential as a supplementary fuel. Liang
et al. [46] and Parnthong et al. [47] highlighted the

Adsorption capacity
Hydrocarbons’ porous nature, combined with their large
surface area, signiﬁcantly improve their adsorption ca-
pabilities, allowing them to effectively adsorb a wide
range of substances [49e51]. This feature is especially
useful in environmental remediation, where they can
absorb pollutants, oil spills and contaminants from water
and soil. In agriculture, they adsorb excess fertilisers and
pesticides, lowering runoff and its environmental impact
[52e54], while also capturing airborne pollutants and
VOCs, improving air quality [55]. Cavali et al. [56]
investigated the inﬂuence of temperature (180, 215, and
250 C), residence time (1, 2, and 3 h) and the mass
ratio of pine residual sawdust to non-dewatered sewage
sludge (1/30, 1/20 and 1/10) on the yield and properties
of hydrochar produced through co-HTC. It was found
that hydrochar produced at 250 C with a residence time
of 1 h exhibited the highest BrunauereEmmetteTeller
surface area and pore volume, achieving values of
30.94 m2/g and 0.143 cm3/g, respectively. The increase
in surface area and pore volume of hydrochar is linked to
the loss of volatile compounds during HTC and Co-
HTC processes.
Lower volatile content generally leads to a larger speciﬁc
surface area [56]. However, hydrochar can be activated
to increase surface area and porosity, improving its efﬁ-
ciency in applications that rely on these properties. Wen
et al. [57] conﬁrmed this in their study of the hydro-
thermal technique in combination with potassium hy-
droxide (KOH) activation. KOH activation on tobacco
stem feedstock resulted in hydrochar with higher sur-
face areas (up to 2875 m2/g). Deepak et al. [58] inves-
tigated KOH-activated orange peel hydrochar prepared
at three different temperatures of 180, 200, and 220 C.
It was found that increasing the HTC temperature led
to a greater surface area in the resulting hydrochar, with
the maximum surface area (ca.457 m2/g) achieved at
220 C. The activation conditions primarily inﬂuenced
the pore structure of the carbons and their carbon di-
oxide (CO2) uptake capacity. Hydrochar produced at
220 C exhibited the highest CO2 uptake, reaching
3.045 mmol/g at 25 C and 1 bar. This was attributed to
its high microporosity and moderate surface area of
457.22 m2/g. In comparison, hydrochar produced at 180
and 200 C displayed less developed micropores, with
surface areas of 50.60 m2/g and 222.04 m2/g and CO2
uptakes of 2.459 mmol/g and 2.892 mmol/g, respectively.

and properties of substances that are released when
hydrochar is exposed to environmental factors such as
water, temperature, and pH. These characteristics
include the quantity, composition, molecular weight,
and diversity of dissolved organic matter (DOM)
released by the hydrochar. Release characteristics are
critical for understanding hydrochar’s environmental
impact, optimising its production, and maximising its
beneﬁts in a variety of applications, including carbon
sequestration, soil improvement and pollution manage-

Conclusions and Future Perspectives
Hydrochar shows signiﬁcant promise not only as a sus-
tainable solid fuel due to its high energy density,
reduced emissions and compatibility with existing coal
infrastructure but also for a range of other applications,
including carbon sequestration, wastewater treatment,
bioremediation and soil enhancement. Its high surface
area and adsorption capacity make it a versatile material
that can contribute to circular economy strategies and
environmental sustainability. Improving HTC parame-
ters over a range of feedstocks remains a major challenge
in scaling the process. It is important to build sound
predictive models for improving hydrochar quality and
process efﬁciency. For example, Hwang et al. [73] used
random forest regression to model near-infrared spectra
for the prediction of hydrochar properties from kraft
lignin HTC with good prediction accuracy (R2 > 0.98)
for carbon content, atomic O/C ratio and H/C ratio.
Their work proved the capability of selective spectral
data in enhancing hydrochar characterisation. Similarly,
Shaﬁzadeh et al. [74] used decision tree and support
vector regression models to forecast hydrochar yield and
quality, where decision trees were more accurate than
support vector models. These machine learning-based
methods considerably minimise experimental time and
costs, making them useful tools for optimising HTC
reaction parameters.
The commercial viability of hydrochar as a solid fuel is
promising due to its high energy density, reduced
emissions, and compatibility with existing coal infra-
structure [6,75]. Large-scale implementation, however,
must overcome economic and technological challenges,
given the production costs of hydrochar range from V50
to V500 per tonne, depending on feedstock and process
conditions [76], compared with subbituminous coal at
about V110  10 per tonne [77]. Future investigations
must focus on reducing costs through reactor design
optimisation,
feedstock
preprocessing
and
process
upscaling. Hydrochar quality optimisation to meet in-
