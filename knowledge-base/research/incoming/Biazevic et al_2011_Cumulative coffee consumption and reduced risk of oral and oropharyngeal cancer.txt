We examined the association between coffee consumption and
oral cancer in a hospital-based case-control study comprising 143
patients with oral and oropharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma
attended at 3 major hospitals in S˜ao Paulo, Brazil, and 240 con-
trols without cancer, recruited from outpatient units of the same
hospitals and matched with cases by sex and age. Associations
were assessed by multivariate logistic regression conditioned on
sociodemographic and behavioral characteristics. Tobacco smok-
ing, alcohol drinking, and higher intake of bacon and deep-fried
foods were directly related to disease; the inverse was observed to
family income and salad intake. Coffee consumption and tobacco
smoking were partially correlated (Spearman correlation coefﬁ-
cient 0.14 among cases, 0.31 among controls). When adjusted for
all covariates, a cumulative coffee consumption higher than 18.0
daily liters × year during lifetime was indicated to be protective
against disease (adjusted odds ratio 0.39, 95% conﬁdence interval
0.16–0.94, P = 0.037). This observation may have pharmacological
implications for clinical medication of these cancers and is relevant
to programs aimed at reducing the burden of disease.

INTRODUCTION
Controversial ﬁndings have been reported on the potential
participation of coffee consumption in oral carcinogenesis. The
hypothesis that roasted coffee might enhance the effect of car-
cinogenic substances contained in tobacco was raised (1). Other
studies singled out the antioxidant and anticarcinogenic activity
of coffee constituents (2,3).
Epidemiological studies assessing dietary habits have also
produced conﬂicting evidence on the nexus between cancer and
coffee consumption. The review of several studies assessing cof-
fee consumption and oral and pharyngeal cancer indicated that
some supported the hypothesis of a protective effect, whereas
others have not found signiﬁcant associations (4). A recent ex-
pert report (5) on the importance of food, nutrition, and physical
activity to cancer prevention also stated that evidence on coffee
and head and neck carcinogenesis is limited and inconclusive.
The hypothesis that hot beverages, mainly coffee and tea,
would increase the risk of oral cancer due to recurrent heat
damages produced in the oral cavity has also been assessed.
Some studies have conﬁrmed this hypothesis (6), while others
have rejected it (7). A larger number of studies assessed hot bev-
erages as a factor associated with esophageal cancer; evidence
on this issue is also controversial, with reports of positive (8)
and absent association (9).
This study tried to clarify this matter by adding evidence on
the association between coffee consumption and oral cancer.

The objective was to assess the association between cumulative
coffee consumption during lifetime in a country that is one of
the largest producers of coffee worldwide, and in which con-
sumption has been historically high (10).
MATERIALS AND METHODS
Participants
This study used a hospital-based case-control design (11).
Patients undergoing treatment for oral and oropharyngeal cancer
in 3 major Brazilian hospitals (Hospital Heli´opolis, Hospital
do Cˆancer “A.C. Camargo,” and Instituto do Cˆancer “Arnaldo
Vieira de Carvalho”) from January to December 2008 were
eligible for the case group. These hospitals comprise reference
units for head and neck surgery in Brazil, and mostly offer free-
of-charge treatment to patients insured by the national health
care system.
Speciﬁcally trained examiners interviewed 143 individuals
(27 women and 116 men) presenting squamous cell carcinoma in
the mouth (C01 to C06; International Classiﬁcation of Diseases,
10th rev.) or oropharynx (C09 and C10). The distribution of
patients by age had quartiles at 35 to 50, 51 to 55, 56 to 62,
and 63 to 85 yr old. The control group comprised 240 patients
assisted in outpatient units of the same hospitals, without prior or
current experience of cancer, and with treatment needs unrelated
to the upper digestive tract.
All patients were interviewed immediately after their clini-
cal consultation in a separate room of the ambulatory. Hospital
ﬁles were consulted to register information comprised in the
medical record of patients. This study observed Brazilian and
international statutes on ethics in research involving human be-
ings; all participants signed a written consent, and ethical clear-
ance was given by the research committees of the participating
institutions.
Data Gathering and Variables
Interviews used a 30-min structured questionnaire on socioe-
conomic status (SES), lifestyle exposures (smoking and alcohol
drinking), and dietary habits. SES was assessed in terms of
family income (number of Brazilian minimum wages, a stan-
dard measurement of monthly income, roughly equivalent to
US$200 during the study period). The assessment of diet used
an extensive food frequency questionnaire speciﬁcally devised
for a previous study (12), which comprised the most usual com-
ponents of Brazilian dietary patterns. Subjects were instructed to
answer taking into account their usual diet during the year that
preceded the diagnosis. To avoid misinterpretation of respon-
dents in the case group, separate annotations were made for
current restrictions of food intake associated with the disease or
the onset of treatment. Similarly, modiﬁcations of diet related to
the most recent year were not considered for the control group.
The hypothesis of dietary habits potentially associating with
oral and oropharyngeal cancer was assessed by the weekly fre-
quency of intake of 3 food items: lettuce, bacon, and deep-fried
foods. Probably the most prevalent salad in the Brazilian usual
diet, lettuce was considered a marker of raw vegetable intake.
Usually added to beans (one of the most common food item in
the Brazilian diet) for ﬂavoring, bacon was assessed as a sur-
rogate for animal fat consumption. Deep-fried foods (saturated
fat) referred to different items that are common in the Brazilian
diet, such as French fries, manioc or cassava, deep-fried pas-
tries, and stuffed tidbits, a high-fat hors d’oeuvres containing
animal protein.
Patients reporting having not smoked at least 1 daily cigarette
during a whole year were considered nonsmokers. The assess-
ment of tobacco smoking considered sequential patterns of fre-
quency, duration, and type of products consumed during life-
time. A cigar was considered equivalent to 4 cigarettes, and each
pipe served equivalent to 3 cigarettes. Cumulative doses of to-
bacco exposure were calculated in terms of pack-years (number
of packs with 20 cigarettes smoked per day multiplied by the
number of smoking years) (13).
Patients reporting never having consumed at least 1 drink
on a regular monthly basis were considered nondrinkers. The
assessment of alcohol consumption also considered sequential
patterns of frequency, volume, duration, and type of beverages
consumed during the life. Alcohol drinking was measured by
grams of ethanol, considering that 1 L of ethanol weighs 798
g, and that beer contains 5% ethanol by volume; wine, 12%;
liqueurs, 30%; and distilled spirits, 40%. Cumulative exposure
to alcohol was expressed in grams-year (grams of ethanol per
day multiplied by the number of drinking years) (14).
Patients were asked to report whether they drank coffee reg-
ularly and the frequency and duration of the habit. Types of the
beverage (percolated, espresso, or decaffeinated) were gathered
for a joint analysis. The cumulative intake of coffee (daily liters
× year) was assessed by summing up results related to sequen-
tial patterns of frequency and duration. The correspondence of
volume used the following standards: cup: 50 ml (disposable),
70 ml (small), 170 ml (large); glass: 190 ml (small), 240 ml
(large); thermal bottle and coffeepot: 700 ml. The assessment
of volume was adjusted for one-third, one-half, or two-thirds
for those consuming coffee with milk, according to the type
of mixture reported by individuals. Aiming to perform a joint
assessment of espresso (more concentrated) and percolated cof-
fee, we multiplied by 3 the speciﬁc volume of espresso reported
by participants.
Data Analysis
Cumulative coffee consumption was stratiﬁed into quartiles
of the distribution of controls; the remaining explanatory vari-
ables were classiﬁed into 3 to 5 categories. This strategy aimed
at ordering the gradient of exposure to protective and deleterious
factors, and thus to assess dose-response effects in the distribu-
tion of cases and controls. For each explanatory variable, the
lowest level of exposure was considered the reference cate-
gory, whereas the remaining categories were classiﬁed into an

ordinal scale expressing comparison groups with cumulative
higher exposure to socioeconomic and behavioral factors (11).
Data analyses used logistic regression. Associations were as-
sessed by odds ratio (OR) point and interval estimates for oral
and oropharyngeal cancer, as adjusted for sex and age distribu-
tion. The ﬁtting of the multivariate model observed a conceptual
framework organizing covariates into hierarchical levels (15).
Therefore, the effect of cumulative coffee consumption on oral
and oropharyngeal cancer was assessed as adjusted for condi-
tions already known to associate with the risk of disease.
All associations were conditioned on sex and age. SES was
included as a distal determinant of disease; i.e., family income
was considered to inﬂuence patterns of smoking, alcohol drink-
ing, and diet. Smoking and alcohol drinking were classiﬁed as
mesial determinants; i.e., these behavioral factors were consid-
ered to be inﬂuenced by SES and to inﬂuence dietary habits.
At last, dietary habits and cumulative coffee consumption were
assessed as proximal determinants of disease; i.e., the inﬂu-
ence of these covariates on socioeconomic conditions, tobacco
smoking, and alcohol drinking was not considered in this study.
Complementarily, the hypothesis of a partial correlation be-
tween behavioral exposures (tobacco smoking, alcohol drink-
ing, and coffee consumption) was independently assessed for
cases and controls by the Spearman correlation coefﬁcient.
Two-sided tested with P values lower than 0.05 were consid-
ered indicative of signiﬁcant association between covariates and
the outcome. The statistical analysis used the Stata 10.0 2007
software (Stata Corporation, College Station, TX).
RESULTS
All patients in the case group presented with squamous cell
carcinoma, no other histological type was included. Eleven pa-
tients were diagnosed with tumors affecting more than one
anatomic site of the mouth; the remaining patients had tumors
in the oropharynx (28), tongue (46), ﬂoor of the mouth (25),
palate (19), gum (7), retromolar area (5), and cheek mucosa (2).
Among controls, 59 had no health complaints: 32 were under-
going preventive checkups and 27 were accompanying patients
without cancer. The remaining participants of the control group
were undergoing treatment for dermatological conditions (79),
disorders of the circulatory system (26), genitourinary tract (25),
endocrine disorders (15), respiratory diseases (9), orthopedic
conditions (8), infectious diseases (8), rheumatoid arthritis (6),
and plastic surgery (5).
Six cases and 9 controls reported never having consumed
coffee on a regular daily basis. The remaining participants (ex-
cept for 1 control) drank percolated coffee, the most usual type
of the beverage in the Brazilian context. Twelve cases and 27
controls reported consuming espresso regularly during at least
1 period in lifetime. Decaffeinated coffee (2 cases, 3 controls)
and soluble coffee (2 controls) were also reported as items reg-
ularly consumed. Table 1 describes the distribution of cases and
controls by all covariates of the study.
The multivariate model conﬁrmed the statistical association
between oral and oropharyngeal cancer and all covariates of the
study (Table 2). Family income and frequent intake of lettuce
were inversely related to disease; whereas tobacco smoking,
alcohol drinking, and frequent intake of bacon and deep-fried
foods was associated with an increased risk.
When exclusively adjusted for sex and age, coffee consump-
tion was not associated with oral and oropharyngeal cancer (P
for trend = 0.532; Table 2). Indeed, analogous proportions re-
ported the highest cumulative consumption among cases and
controls: 25.9% and 25.0%, respectively (Table 1). However,
when the full conceptual model was considered, and the highest
exposure to coffee was adjusted for the remaining covariates of
the model, its inverse relationship with disease was observed:
adjusted odds ratio (OR) 0.39; 95% conﬁdence interval (95% CI)
from 0.16 to 0.94 (P for trend = 0.013; Table 2). The frequency
of usual intake of hot beverages (coffee and tea) was assessed
using different cutoffs for classiﬁcation; none of them resulted
in signiﬁcant association with the risk of oral and oropharyngeal
cancer (nontabled result).
The third quartile of the distribution of cases (18.0 daily
liters × year) was the cutoff for the highest cumulative expo-
sure to coffee, the unique category of consumption signiﬁcantly
associated with the protection against the disease. This ﬁgure
is relatively high, and corresponds to an average daily intake of
half a liter of coffee for almost 36 yr.
The stratiﬁed assessment of association between behavioral
exposures showed signiﬁcant positive correlations between cof-
fee consumption, alcohol drinking, and tobacco smoking among
controls. Among cases, alcohol drinking and tobacco smoking
were signiﬁcantly correlated; coffee consumption was not sig-
niﬁcantly associated with the remaining exposures at the P <
0.05 level (Table 3).
DISCUSSION
This study supports the hypothesis that a high cumulative
consumption of coffee during lifetime has a protective effect
on the risk of oral and oropharyngeal cancer. The observation
of an inverse association between the highest pattern of coffee
intake and the risk of disease in an assessment that considered a
conceptual framework adjusting for sociodemographic factors,
the exposure to alcoholic beverages and tobacco smoking, and
dietary patterns is the most important ﬁnding of this study.
Anticarcinogenic effects of coffee are supported by stud-
ies assessing several biological compounds such as caffeine,
diterpenes, caffeic acid, and polyphenols, as well as volatile
aroma and heterocyclic substances. While reviewing these stud-
ies, Nkondjock (16) reported evidence on the ability of caf-
feine to both stimulate and suppress tumors, depending upon
the species and the phase of administration. Cafestol and kah-
weal, diterpenes contained in coffee, produce biological effects
compatible with anticarcinogenic properties. Coffee is also an
important source of chlorogenic acid, an antioxidant compound,

Head and neck cancer incidence is known to differ by sex
and age (17); the current study controlled for these factors by
including sex and age as adjusting factors in the assessment of
associations. The burden of oral and pharyngeal cancer has al-
ready been reported to be inﬂuenced by socioeconomic position
in the Brazilian context (18); thus the importance of adjusting
for socioeconomic covariates. Tobacco smoking and alcohol
drinking are well-known risk factors for cancer (17) and have to
be controlled for when assessing the association between coffee
consumption and oral and oropharyngeal cancer.
Oral carcinogenesis is also inﬂuenced by systemic and topical
effects of diet on oral mucosa. Although no general biological
mechanism has been fully established to explain the modulating
effect of fat on carcinogenesis, there are convincing indications
that dietary lipids can signiﬁcantly alter the membrane fatty acid
composition of cells, thus affecting cell membrane integrity and
inﬂuencing intracellular (endocytosis) and intercellular (perme-
ability) pathways to the absorption of carcinogenic constituents
of some foods and tobacco (19,20). This argument is in agree-
ment with the current indication of deleterious effects for the
usual intake of fatty foods and the protective effect of lettuce,
because the frequent intake of salads and raw vegetables may
cleanse the oral mucosa from residual fat.
The inverse relationship between coffee consumption and
oral and pharyngeal cancer has also been reported by case-
control studies applied to other geographic contexts (21,22).
The assessment of an historical cohort in Japan also reached
an analogous conclusion (23). Other studies appraised the bio-
logical mechanism by which such association can be explained
(2,3,24). However, systematic reviews (4,5) appraised this as-
sociation as inconclusive, because some studies did not observe
any signiﬁcant association, whereas others concluded that there
is a risk of consuming hot beverages.
Overall, patients were collaborative and willing to discuss
personal issues with the hospital staff. The gathering of data on
coffee consumption was encompassing and considered modi-
ﬁcations over time. The questionnaire demanded a structured
answer; participants were invited to consider their entire life
in their responses. Interviewers ﬁlled out the form, register-
ing information on sequential patterns of type of beverage and
frequency, volume, and duration of the habit. This method al-
lowed estimating the cumulative exposure to coffee by sum-
ming information related to sequential patterns, which may be
preferable than exclusively assessing the frequency of current
consumption. In addition to optimizing patient recollection of
information, this aspect is expected to have reduced measure-
ment error that could distort results.
The association between coffee intake and cigarette smok-
ing (few smokers do not drink coffee) is well known, and was
explored didactically by a handbook of epidemiology (25) to ex-
plain confounding and causation. The current study conﬁrmed
the positive correlation between the 2 exposures among con-
trols and controlled the interaction between tobacco and coffee
consumption by considering smoking a more distal covariate in
the multivariate model. This association (coffee and tobacco)
explains the change between unadjusted and adjusted ORs for
coffee drinking. The unadjusted assessment failed to indicate
a protective effect for coffee consumption due to confusion of
smoking as an intervening factor. When the multivariate model
was adjusted for tobacco smoking, the inverse association be-
tween coffee consumption and oral and oropharyngeal cancer
could be effectively stated.
This study also reported the absence of negative correlation
between alcohol drinking and coffee consumption. Therefore,
the hypothesis that the custom of drinking coffee instead of
alcoholic beverages outside meals would explain part of the
protective effect of coffee is not supported by our ﬁndings.
Having observed a case-control design, this study is subject
to criticism regarding the selection bias (11). The sample is
not representative of Brazilian society; this limitation affects
most clinical studies. The control group was not constituted by
plainly healthy individuals; however, the exclusive enrollment
of participants in units of outpatient care, without cancer, and
presenting treatment needs unrelated to smoking, alcohol intake,
and diseases of the mouth and oropharynx are reassuring against
selection bias.

Recall bias is also a relevant limitation of case-control studies
(11). Patients diagnosed with oral or oropharyngeal cancer may
have spent some time pondering which habits or events would
have contributed to the disease. Therefore, cases would be more
likely to recall actual patterns of smoking and alcohol intake
than the controls.
However, the exposure to tobacco and alcoholic beverages,
as well as dietary patterns and coffee consumption, does not
generate effective registers at population level, and a structured
interview may be a unique opportunity to assess the current
hypothesis, in spite of the risk of imprecise reporting of partic-
ipants. Recall bias is a limitation difﬁcult to overcome in the
scope of case-control studies; notwithstanding, the perspective
of assessing coffee consumption by food frequency question-
naires was appraised as satisfactorily reliable and valid (26). A
study speciﬁcally assessing data provided by hospital controls
on cigarette smoking and alcohol and coffee intake concluded
that this information was in agreement with the at-home repeated
interview (27).
This retrospective study mostly relied on the self-report of
dietary habits during adulthood. We hypothesized that espresso
could be considered 3 times more concentrated than percolated
coffee; but no additional information on coffee concentration
was gathered because the concentration of percolated coffee
may vary considerably in the short and in the long term. Absent
information on coffee concentration is also acknowledged as a
study limitation.
In conclusion, this study conﬁrmed the inverse association
between a high level of cumulative consumption of coffee during
lifetime and oral and oropharyngeal cancer, in an assessment
adjusted for the most relevant covariates. Antioxidant properties
of coffee constituents provide plausible biological mechanisms
to support this result. The hypothesis that coffee consumption
yields a protective effect represents a non-negligible scope for
primary prevention and may have pharmacologic implications
for clinical medication of these cancers.
