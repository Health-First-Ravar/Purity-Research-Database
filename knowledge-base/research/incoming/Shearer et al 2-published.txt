Effects of chronic coffee consumption on glucose
kinetics in the conscious rat
J. Shearer, E.A. Sellars, A. Farah, T.E. Graham, and D.H. Wasserman
Abstract: Epidemiological studies indicate that regular coffee consumption reduces the risk of developing type 2 diabetes.
Despite these findings, the biological mechanisms by which coffee consumption exerts these effects are unknown. The aim
of this study was twofold: to develop a rat model that would further delineate the effects of regular coffee consumption on
glucose kinetics, and to determine whether coffee, with or without caffeine, alters the actions of insulin on glucose kinetics
in vivo. Male Sprague–Dawley rats were fed a high-fat diet for 4 weeks in combination with one of the following:
(i) drinking water as placebo (PL), (ii) decaffeinated coffee (2 g/100 mL) (DC), or (iii) alkaloid caffeine (20 mg/100 mL)
added to decaffeinated coffee (2 g/100 mL) (CAF). Catheters were chronically implanted in a carotid artery and jugular
vein for sampling and infusions, respectively. Recovered animals (5 days postoperative) were fasted for 5 h before hyper-
insulinemic-euglycemic clamps (2 mUkg–1min–1). Glucose was clamped at 6 mmol/L and isotopes (2-deoxy-[14C]glucose
and [3-3H]glucose) were administered to obtain indices of whole-body and tissue-specific glucose kinetics. Glucose infu-
sion rates and measures of whole-body metabolic clearance were greater in DC than in PL or CAF, indicating increased
whole-body insulin sensitivity. As the only difference between DC and CAF was the addition of alkaloid caffeine, it can
be concluded that caffeine antagonizes the beneficial effects of DC. Given these findings, decaffeinated coffee may repre-
sent a nutritional means of combating insulin resistance.
Key words: insulin action, diabetes, coffee, animal model, tracer.

Introduction
Chronic coffee consumption is correlated with large risk
reductions in type 2 diabetes (Salazar-Martinez et al. 2004;
Soriguer et al. 2004; van Dam and Hu 2005). Being plant-
derived, coffee contains high amounts of many beneficial
compounds found in fruits, vegetables, and medicinal plants,
including chlorogenic acids and quinides, to which its blood-
glucose-normalizing effects have been attributed (Andrade-
Cetto and Wiedenfeld 2001; Shearer et al. 2003; Farah et
al. 2006). A comprehensive review of studies examining the
effects of coffee consumption on type 2 diabetes in 193 437
participants and 8 394 incident cases reveals multivariate-
adjusted relative risks of 0.65 and 0.72 for the consumption
of >6 and 4–6 cups of coffee per day, respectively, com-
pared with those with low or absent coffee intakes (van
Dam and Hu 2005). Such findings are consistent and exist
regardless of sex, type of coffee (caffeinated and (or) decaf-
feinated), method of brewing (pressed and (or) filtered), or
geography (Salazar-Martinez et al. 2004; Soriguer et al.
2004; van Dam and Hu 2005). These results also persist de-
spite clear evidence showing that acute caffeine consump-
tion has negative effects on glucose tolerance. Caffeine
lowers glucose disposal and insulin sensitivity in lean
(Thong et al. 2002; Thong and Graham 2002; Battram et al.
2006; Dekker et al. 2007), obese (Petrie et al. 2004), and
type 2 diabetic individuals (Robinson et al. 2004; Lee et al.
2005).
Overall aims of this study were the following: (i) develop
a rat model to further delineate the effects of regular coffee
consumption on glucose kinetics, and (ii) determine whether
coffee, with or without caffeine, alters the actions of insulin
on glucose kinetics in vivo. It was hypothesized that coffee
would have positive effects on insulin action and glucose
utilization in a model of dietary-induced insulin resistance
and these effects would persist despite the presence of caf-
feine. Results show decaffeinated, but not caffeinated, coffee
improves insulin action, suggesting it is of value for those at
risk of developing type 2 diabetes.
Materials and methods
Experimental model
Procedures were approved by the Vanderbilt University
Animal Care Committee. Experiments were performed in
vivo on chronically catheterized, unrestrained conscious
rats. This model allows metabolic assessment in the absence
of stress and strict control over diet and exercise. Male
Sprague–Dawley rats (Harlan Industries, Indianapolis, Ind.)
were housed individually and maintained at 23 8C on a
0600–1800 light cycle.
Rats were fed a diet consisting of 60% fat, 24% carbohy-
drates, and 16% protein (custom high-fat, high-sucrose diet,
Bio-Serv, Frenchtown, N.J.) for 4 weeks prior to the experi-
mental protocol on day 28. In addition, rats were maintained
with
either
placebo
(PL,
drinking
water),
instant
decaffeinated coffee (DC, 2 g/100 mL) (Taster’s Choice,
Nescafe´, Nestle´, Vevey, Switzerland), or caffeine (CAF,
20 mg/100 mL) (Sigma Aldrich, St. Louis, Mo.) added to
instant decaffeinated coffee (2 g/100 mL). These doses
were determined on the basis of the approximate caffeine
content of brewed coffee and the manufacturers’ recom-
mended preparation of instant coffee. Each beverage was
supplemented with aspartame (0.4 g/100 mL, Equal Brand,
Chicago, Ill.) to sweeten the coffee and promote fluid con-
sumption by the animals. Body mass and food and fluid
consumption were measured twice per week.
The effects of high-fat feeding on tissue-specific and
whole-body insulin resistance are well documented (Halseth
et al. 2000). Given this, only high-fat-fed animals were used
in the experimental protocol so that improvements in insulin
sensitivity with coffee could more readily be detected. This
model would also be expected to exaggerate any insulin re-
sistance resulting from caffeine administration, should any
exist (Petrie et al. 2004). Moreover, previous studies have
documented that acute infusion of decaffeinated coffee has
no effect on glucose homeostasis or insulin action in chow-
fed animals (Shearer et al. 2003). The 4-week time frame of
dietary manipulation was chosen as it has been previously
shown to induce whole-body and tissue-specific insulin re-
sistance in the Sprague–Dawley rat (Halseth et al. 2000).
Surgical procedures
Surgical procedures were performed as previously de-
scribed (Halseth et al. 2000). Briefly, animals were anesthe-
tized with a mixture of ketamine–xylazine–acepromazine
(50:5:1 by mass), and the left common carotid artery and
right jugular vein were catheterized with PE50 under sterile
conditions. Catheters were exteriorized and secured at the
back of the neck, filled with heparinized saline (150 U/mL),
and sealed with stainless steel plugs. Immediately after sur-
gery, each animal received 75 mg/kg of ampicillin sub-
cutaneously to prevent infection. After surgery, animal mass
and food intake were monitored daily, and only animals in
which presurgery mass was restored were used for experi-
ments. The postoperative period was approximately 5 days.
Animals were fasted for 5 h before the onset of experimen-
tal blood sampling. Catheters were flushed with heparinized
saline (10 U/mL) and connected to PE50 and silastic tubing
for infusions and sampling, respectively.
Hemodynamic measures
On the morning of experiments, blood pressures were
monitored continuously from the carotid artery catheter in
unrestrained animals with a pressure transducer coupled to a
Digi-Med BP analyzer (Micromed, Louisville, Ken.). After a
30-minute acclimatization period, measurements were made
for 30 min.

Plasma analyses
Plasma glucose, NEFA, and insulin were calculated using
established methods (Halseth et al. 2000). [3-3H]glucose and
[14C]DG were measured in 20 mL plasma samples deprotei-
nized in 100 mL of Ba(OH)2 (0.06 N) and 100 mL of ZnSO4
(0.06 N) and centrifuged. Radioactivity in supernatants was
determined after addition of fluor using a liquid scintillation
counter. Plasma methylxanthines were quantified by high-
performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) (Aldridge et al.
1979).
Tissue analyses
Tissues were analyzed for accumulation of free and phos-
phorylated ([14C]DGP) [14C]DG as previously described
(Halseth et al. 2000). Tissue was homogenized in 2 mL of
0.5%
perchloric
acid
and
centrifuged.
Supernatants
(1.5 mL) were neutralized and radioactivity in 250 mL was
determined after the addition of fluor. This fraction provided
total radioactivity of [14C]DG free + [14C]DGP. In addition,
500 mL of supernatant was treated with 250 mL of Ba(OH)2
and 250 mL of ZnSO4 and centrifuged. Radioactivity was
then determined in 500 mL of supernatant. Treatment with
Ba(OH)2 (0.3 N) and ZnSO4 (0.3 N) removes all but free
[14C]DG. Therefore, [14C]DGP was calculated as the differ-
ence in radioactivity with (free [14C]DG) and without (total)
Ba(OH)2 and ZnSO4. This analytic approach allows the sep-
aration of [14C]DG from [14C]DGP, including that incorpo-
rated into glycogen (Meszaros et al. 1989).
Coffee analysis
Decaffeinated instant coffee used in this study was
analyzed for phenolic compounds including chlorogenic
acids and quinides by HPLC (Farah et al. 2006). The same
batch of decaffeinated coffee was used to make the caffei-
nated coffee preparation by addition of alkaloid caffeine
(20 mg/100 mL).
Calculations
Glucose Ra, Rd (Steele et al. 1956), and Rg were calcu-
lated as previously described (Shearer et al. 2004). A one-
way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was performed to
compare differences between groups, and a one-way re-
peated measures ANOVA was used to compare differences
between groups over time for plasma measures. Tukey’s
post hoc test was used to identify time points that were sig-
nificantly (p £ 0.05) different between groups. Data are re-
ported as means ± SE.

Discussion
Epidemiological studies show that chronic coffee con-
sumption is associated with a significant reduction in the
prevalence of type 2 diabetes. Although this relationship
has been well documented, the physiological bases of cof-
fee’s effects remain unclear. In the present study, we em-
ployed a model of dietary-induced insulin resistance in
combination with the chronic administration of placebo, de-
caffeinated coffee, or caffeinated coffee. A high-fat diet was
used because we hypothesized it would be more apt to pre-
cipitate any insulin-sensitizing effect of decaffeinated coffee
as well as any negative effects of caffeine on glucose uti-
lization. Isotopes were utilized to obtain information on
whole-body and tissue-specific glucose flux under both basal
and insulin-stimulated conditions. Novel findings from this
study are the following: (i) the consumption of decaffeinated
coffee improves insulin-stimulated Rd and muscle Rg, (ii) the
addition of alkaloid caffeine to this preparation negates these
beneficial effects but does not result in any further impair-
ment in Rd and skeletal muscle Rg compared with placebo,
(iii) alkaloid caffeine sharply attenuates cardiac Rg during
an insulin clamp below rates in either placebo and decaffei-
nated coffee, and (iv) coffee consumption (either plus or mi-
nus caffeine) does not significantly affect Ra.
Animals treated with decaffeinated coffee disposed of
glucose at twice the rate of that of animals treated with ei-
ther placebo or caffeinated coffee. This finding is consistent
with reports in humans as early as 1970 showing lower glu-
cose and insulin responses with decaffeinated coffee admin-
istered before a glucose load than with caffeinated coffee
(Wachman et al. 1970). It also supports the results of
Battram and colleagues (2006) who have shown a 50%
lower plasma glucose in response to the consumption of de-
caffeinated coffee versus placebo (water) before an oral glu-
cose tolerance test. Together, these findings clearly show
that decaffeinated coffee consumption improves whole-body
glucose disposal, a factor that is directly related to the risk
of developing type 2 diabetes.
Pinpointing the compounds mediating the positive effects
of decaffeinated coffee on reducing the risk of diabetes is
not simple, considering coffee contains numerous biologi-
cally active substances. Coffee is the greatest source of diet-
ary antioxidants in the typical North American diet (Svilaas
et al. 2004), and oxidant stress has been implicated in the
development of insulin resistance, a hallmark feature of
type 2 diabetes (Scott and King 2004). Antioxidants found
in roasted coffee include chlorogenic acids and their quinide
derivatives (Farah et al. 2006). Analysis of the coffee prepa-
ration administered in this study shows a considerable
amount of both of these. In addition, coffee contains a num-
ber of micronutrients in small quantities, including magne-
sium, potassium, niacin, and vitamin E (Higdon and Frei
2006).
Analysis of plasma methylxanthines revealed circulating
levels of both caffeine and dimethylxanthines during the in-
sulin clamp. This occurred despite a 5-hour fast, which is
sufficient time to allow for the degradation of caffeine by
one half-life. As the only difference between decaffeinated
coffee and caffeinated coffee was the addition of alkaloid
caffeine, the observed differences between these 2 groups
can be directly attributed to the presence of caffeine. As
whole-body glucose requirements during the insulin clamp
in caffeinated coffee were less than that of decaffeinated
coffee, it can be concluded that caffeine opposes the benefi-
cial actions of decaffeinated coffee on insulin-stimulated
glucose metabolism. This finding is in line with those of
Graham and colleagues (Graham et al. 1998; Battram et al.
2006) who have demonstrated differential responses to caf-
feinated coffee compared with an equivalent dose of alka-
loid
caffeine.
Specifically,
they
have
shown
that
the
consumption of alkaloid caffeine results in glucose intoler-
ance, whereas the same dose of caffeine, as a component of
coffee, does not. Given this, data showing caffeine to cause
glucose intolerance cannot be equated to the identical dose
of caffeine if it is consumed as a component of coffee
(Battram et al. 2006).
The mechanisms by which caffeine acts to reduce the ef-
fects of decaffeinated coffee were explored using radioactive
tracers. Our results in this rat model show caffeine acts at
heart and skeletal muscle. Caffeine is a nonspecific adeno-
sine antagonist. Adenosine binding and its downstream sig-
nals
are
involved
in
numerous
intracellular
signaling
cascades including those associated with insulin action and
glucose uptake (Law and McLane 1991; Vergauwen et al.
1994; Linden 2001). When consumed as a component of
the diet, the highest concentration of caffeine in blood is
measured in the portal venous blood perfusing the liver. Ad-
ministration of caffeine directly in the portal vein increases
insulin-stimulated net hepatic glucose uptake by more than
2-fold in the conscious dog (Pencek et al. 2004). This is
consistent with studies in the dog showing the infusion of
adenosine into the portal circulation reverses the suppressive
effects of insulin on glucose production (McLane et al.
1990). Caffeine in the present study did not significantly al-
ter insulin action at the liver, presumably because of the
chronic nature of the treatment or possibly because of the
high fat content in the diet. Rg in skeletal muscle was in-
creased in decaffeinated coffee in relation to placebo. This
effect was completely countered by the inclusion of caf-

and the activity of glycogen synthase, the enzyme responsi-
ble for storing incoming glucose in glycogen (Thong et al.
2002). The mechanisms by which caffeine acts to reduce glu-
cose utilization may be attributed in part to the effects of caf-
feine on the sympathetic nervous system. Abolishing the
sympathetic response to caffeine has been shown to eliminate
some of its negative effects on glucose tolerance (Thong and
Graham 2002; Battram et al. 2007). However, in the model
used in the present study, neither norepinephrine nor epi-
nephrine was increased in the arterial blood of rats ingesting
caffeinated coffee. In contrast to skeletal muscle, decaffei-
nated coffee had no effect on cardiac Rg during the insulin
clamp compared with placebo. Caffeine, on the other hand,
showed a marked inhibitory effect on cardiac Rg compared
with either placebo or decaffeinated coffee.
In both human and other animal models, responses to caf-
feine differ depending on the frequency of use. In the early
stages of treatment, there was a delay in mass gain in the
animals treated with caffeine. This may have been due to
sympathetic nervous system activation, increased thermo-
genesis (Astrup et al. 1990), and (or) caffeine-induced
diuresis (Neuha¨user-Berthold et al. 1997). However, with
prolonged caffeine exposure, tolerance develops and the
sympathetic nervous system is activated to a lesser degree
or, as in the present study, not at all (Astrup et al. 1992;
Bangsbo et al. 1992; Van Soeren et al. 1993). After 12 days
of treatment, caffeine-treated rats gained more mass so that
there was no difference in mass on the day of the experi-
ment. It appears that other animals, like humans, rapidly ha-
bituate to the drug. A prospective study in humans over a
12-year period investigated the effects of prolonged caffeine
consumption on mass change (Lopez-Garcia et al. 2006).
Results showed caffeine to have a small, but significant ef-
fect on mass with the highest consumers being 0.43 kg
lighter compared with those who rarely consumed caffeine.
A recent study conducted in human subjects showed that
the combination of caffeine and ephedrine for 16 weeks re-
sulted in a 6% reduction in body mass due to associated re-
ductions in visceral and subcutaneous fat stores (Bogacka et
al. 2007). The reduction in body mass was also associated
with downregulation of the gene expression of stearoyl-CoA
desaturatase 1, an enzyme associated with fat accumulation.

Here, we used a rat model to investigate the effects of
28 days of decaffeinated coffee and caffeine consumption
on insulin action in vivo. Results are consistent with epide-
miological studies in human populations showing that decaf-
feinated coffee consumption improves insulin action. Our
results show that, in the high-fat-fed, insulin-resistant rat, in-
gestion of decaffeinated coffee, but not caffeinated coffee,
improves insulin-stimulated glucose disposal by its effects
on skeletal muscle. Caffeine opposes the beneficial effects
of decaffeinated coffee on insulin action on skeletal muscle
and inhibits cardiac glucose uptake. These studies provide a
scientific basis for the recommendation that those at risk of
developing type 2 diabetes consume decaffeinated coffee or
a supplement containing its constituents.
