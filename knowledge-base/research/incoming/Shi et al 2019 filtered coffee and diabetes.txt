Background. Habitual coffee intake has been associ-
ated with a lower risk of developing type 2 diabetes
(T2D), but few studies used biomarkers to reﬂect
intake and investigated different coffee brews, that
is boiled and ﬁltered, separately.
Objectives. To identify plasma metabolites associated
with boiled or ﬁltered coffee intake and to examine
their association with T2D risk in Swedish adults.
Methods. In a case–control study nested within the
V€asterbotten
Intervention
Programme,
baseline
plasma samples from 421 case–control pairs and
samples from a subset of 149 pairs at a 10-year
follow-up were analysed using untargeted LC-MS
metabolomics.
We
identiﬁed
metabolites
associated
with
food
frequency
questionnaires
(FFQ)-estimated coffee intake and assessed odds
ratios of T2D.
Results. In total, 24 and 32 metabolites were asso-
ciated with boiled or ﬁltered coffee intake. We
determined robust metabolite panels for highly
speciﬁc prediction of boiled or ﬁltered coffee. We
observed
an
inverse
association
between
the
metabolite panel of ﬁltered coffee and T2D risk.
No association with T2D was observed for the panel
of
boiled
coffee
intake.
Similar
results
were
observed for FFQ-estimated coffee intake.
Conclusions. We identiﬁed plasma metabolites specif-
ically associated
with boiled or ﬁltered coffee
intake, which might be used as selective biomark-
ers. Our study supports a protective role of habit-
ual intake of ﬁltered coffee on T2D development.
The lack of association for boiled coffee intake
might be due to the lack of a protective effect of
boiled coffee or due to the limited number of boiled
coffee consumers in this population, but it war-
rants further investigation.
Keywords: coffee intake, dietary biomarker, epidemi-
ology, metabolomics, type 2 diabetes.
Introduction
Observational
studies
have
shown
an
inverse
association between habitual coffee intake and
risk of several noncommunicable diseases, includ-
ing type 2 diabetes (T2D) [1–4]. Existing evidence
on the role of coffee intake and T2D is mostly
derived from studies where total coffee, and/or
caffeinated versus decaffeinated coffee have been
investigated. Although the underlying mechanisms
remain
unclear,
coffee
contains
various
com-
pounds,
for
example,
caffeine,
phenolic
com-
pounds, diterpenes and phytosterols which have
shown
different
effects
on
multiple
pathways
involved in T2D development, such as glucose
and lipid metabolism, fatty acid metabolism and
inﬂammatory activities [2, 5–7].
Coffee prepared by different brewing methods, that
is
ﬁltered
and
unﬁltered
(boiled),
results
in

different chemical compositions [8–10]. Boiled cof-
fee is typically prepared by boiling a mixture of
coarsely ground coffee beans and water [11] and
contains considerably higher amount of diterpenes
(i.e. cafestol and kahweol) than ﬁltered coffee, of
which cafestol was previously shown to increase
total
and
low-density
lipoprotein
cholesterol,
triglycerides and blood pressure [12, 13]. Differ-
ences in chemical properties between ﬁltered and
boiled coffee are, therefore, likely relevant for the
risk of developing T2D, with potential implication
for public health. However, only two prospective
studies have investigated the roles of boiled and
ﬁltered coffee separately for the risk of developing
T2D [14, 15]. One study [15] reported a moderate
inverse association between both boiled and ﬁl-
tered coffee and T2D risk in a Norwegian popula-
tion. Another study from Finland [14] found a
borderline inverse association for boiled coffee
intake, however, twice as high T2D risk for boiled
compared to ﬁltered coffee. There is insufﬁcient
epidemiological
evidence
on
the
association
between types of coffee brews and the risk of
developing T2D.
Observational studies investigating associations
between coffee intake and T2D often rely on food
frequency
questionnaires
(FFQs),
which
suffer
from systematic and random measurement errors
[16]. Although the amount of coffee intake appears
to be well-captured by FFQs, the lack of well-
documented information on coffee brewing meth-
ods in most cohorts have limited investigations of
different coffee brews in relation with disease risk
[17]. Metabolomics has emerged as a powerful
approach to discover biomarkers of dietary intake
that may provide a complement to self-reported
dietary assessments with the potential to accu-
rately
reﬂect
the
relationship
between
dietary
intake and disease risk [18, 19]. Several metabo-
lites measured in urine and/or blood samples from
both intervention studies and observational stud-
ies have been proposed as potential biomarker
candidates of coffee intake [20–23], but they have
in most cases been associated with ﬁltered coffee
intake, which is the dominant form of consumption
globally [1, 4]. No study has investigated whether
metabolites could speciﬁcally reﬂect boiled and
ﬁltered coffee intake, and whether such metabo-
lites are associated with risk of developing T2D.
Sweden is one of the countries with the highest
ﬁltered coffee intake worldwide, also with a high
consumption of boiled coffee especially in the large,
rural areas of northern Sweden [11, 24]. We have
previously conducted a nested case–control study
in the Swedish prospective cohort V€asterbotten
Intervention Programme (VIP), of which well-docu-
mented information on the type of coffee con-
sumed, that is ﬁltered and boiled is available [11,
24–26]. Here, we aimed to identify plasma metabo-
lites associated with ﬁltered or boiled coffee intake,
using
untargeted
liquid
chromatography–mass
spectrometry
(LC-MS)
metabolomics.
We
also
investigated the association of ﬁltered or boiled
coffee, either reﬂected by the identiﬁed metabolites
or measured by FFQ, with incident T2D.
Materials and methods
Study population
A nested case–control study was set up within the
V€asterbotten
Intervention
Programme
[25,
27]
(Fig. 1). Detailed information on the VIP and
selection of participants has been reported else-
where
[25–28].
In
brief,
the
inhabitants
from
V€asterbotten County at ages 40, 50 and 60 years
(until 1996 also 30 years) were invited to partici-
pate in a health examination with blood sampling
and to complete a questionnaire capturing infor-
mation on diet, socioeconomic conditions, self-
rated health, personal health history and family
history of diabetes, tobacco use and physical
activity. VIP was initiated in 1985, and participants
included in the present study were recruited from
1991 to 2005 (baseline). T2D cases were diagnosed
according to the Diabetes Register in Northern
Sweden (DiabNorth) and were ascertained by dia-
betes specialists [29]. We selected 421 participants
(male = 196, female = 225) at baseline (median
7 years prior to diagnosis) amongst eligible dia-
betes cases, who had an unthawed fasting plasma
sample in the biobank. Each case was individually
matched
to
one
nondiabetic
VIP
participant
(healthy controls). Matching criteria included gen-
der, age (2 years), sample date (90 days) and
type of questionnaire at baseline examination.
Amongst the 421 case–control pairs, 149 pairs
had a follow-up FFQ and blood sample drawn
10 years after baseline, and the follow-up samples
were included in the present study. In the present
study, the samples at baseline (n = 421 pairs) were
used to identify biomarkers of coffee intake and to
investigate associations between coffee intake and
T2D risk. The follow-up samples (n = 149 pairs)
were included to evaluate and validate whether the
prediction performance of identiﬁed biomarkers of
coffee
intake
at
baseline
differed
from
their

Coffee consumption
Coffee intake was determined based on two mod-
iﬁed and validated versions of the Northern Sweden
Diet Database FFQ: one with 84 food items and one
with 64 items [30–32]. Both questionnaires had
identical questions for coffee intake, that is one for
ﬁltered and one for boiled coffee [11, 24, 30, 32]. In
the investigated population at baseline, the intake
of both boiled and ﬁltered coffee estimated by FFQ,
scored from 0 to 8 according to consumption
frequencies, reﬂecting never (0), a few times/year
(1), 1–3 times/month (2), 1 time/ week (3), 2–3
times/week (4), 4–6 times/week (5), 1 occasion/
day (6), 2–3 occasions/day (7) and 4 occasions/day
(8). The categorical scores were transformed into
occasions per day and were subjected to further
analysis (Table S1). Amongst participants, 2.7%
never consumed coffee (FFQ score = 0 for both
coffee), 8.4% only had intake of boiled coffee (FFQ
score > 0 for boiled and =0 for ﬁltered coffee),
19.5% only had intake of ﬁltered coffee (FFQ
score > 0 for ﬁltered and =0 for boiled coffee), and
74% consumed both boiled and ﬁltered coffee (FFQ
score > 0 for both types of coffee intake).
Untargeted metabolomics analysis
A detailed description of the untargeted LC-MS
metabolomics analysis has been published else-
where [26, 33]. In short, deproteinized fasting
heparin plasma samples were analysed by an
UHPLC-qTOF-MS
system
(Agilent
Technologies)
that consisted of a 1290 LC system, a Jetstream
electrospray ionization (ESI) source and a 6540
UHD accurate-mass qTOF spectrometer. The sam-
ples were analysed using reverse phase (RP) and
hydrophilic interaction chromatography (HILIC) in
positive (ESI+) and negative (ESI-) polarity. Raw
data acquired in each analytical batch were con-
verted to mzXML format, and deconvolution was
performed with the open-source R package ‘XCMS’
[34]. Optimized parameters applied for untargeted
LC-MS data processing are presented in Table S2.
Plasma samples were analysed in eight batches,
and a constrained randomization was applied to
keep sample pairs and follow-up samples within
the same batch. Instrumental analyses were per-
formed
with
approximately
250
injections
per
batch, including study samples and quality control
samples constituted approximately 16 % of study
samples to monitor the stability and functionality
of the system [26, 33]. Two types of quality control

Statistical analysis
All statistical analysis was conducted in R version
3.5.1. Differences between cases and controls in
baseline characteristics, including coffee intake,
were compared by two-sided Student’s t-test for
continuous variables and Chi-squared test for
categorical variables, considering P < 0.05 as sta-
tistically signiﬁcant.
Discovery of coffee-related plasma metabolites
First, we applied a repeated double cross-valida-
tion framework with unbiased variable selection (R
package ‘MUVR’[35]) on metabolomics data to
optimally select metabolites that could best dis-
criminate three strata amongst healthy controls:
the three strata amongst healthy controls were
deﬁned as low coffee consumers (n = 40, <1 occa-
sion per day for both types of coffee), boiled coffee
consumers (n = 43, ≥2–3 occasions per day for
boiled coffee and <1 occasion per week for ﬁltered
coffee) and ﬁltered coffee consumers (n = 54, ≥4
occasions per day for ﬁltered coffee and <1 occa-
sion per week for boiled coffee). Cut-off threshold of
daily coffee intakes were set to achieve roughly
balanced strata [36]. Signiﬁcance of modelling
performance was assessed by permutation analy-
sis [37]. In total, 52 metabolite features were
selected as best discriminators to classify the three
strata of participants. These features were then
subjected to metabolite identiﬁcation utilizing pure
chemical standards, online databases and earlier
publications, resulting in 43 metabolites annotated
at level 1–4 according to the Metabolomics Stan-
dard Initiative (MSI) [38] (see details in ‘Metabolite
identiﬁcation’, Table S3).
To speciﬁcally reﬂect boiled or ﬁltered coffee intake
at baseline, two different metabolite panel scores
were generated using least absolute shrinkage and
selection operator (LASSO) regression with 10-fold
cross-validation from the boiled or ﬁltered coffee-
associated metabolites, respectively (R package
‘glmnet’ [39]). Metabolite panels were also applied
to generate scores for the subset at 10-y follow-up.
Associations of each type of coffee intake with
individual metabolites as well as metabolite panels
were assessed by partial Spearman correlation
analysis, adjusting for case–control status, age at
blood draw, gender, daily energy intake (kcal), BMI
(kg m2), smoking status (smoker, former smoker,
occasional smoker and nonsmoker), education
(elementary school, vocational school, secondary
school and university education/college) and phys-
ical activity (inactive, moderately inactive, moder-
ately active and active), across all participants
(n = 421 pairs).
Predictive performance of coffee-related metabolites for reported
coffee intake
The area under the receiver operating characteris-
tic curves (AROC) was used to evaluate the predic-
tive
accuracy
of
individual
metabolites
and
metabolite panel scores for discrimination of low
total coffee consumption from boiled or ﬁltered
coffee consumption using the R package ‘pROC’
[40]. Predictors with AROC value above 0.7 were
considered good for discrimination between groups
[41, 42].
Associations of coffee-related metabolites with T2D risk
Associations of individual metabolites or metabo-
lite panel scores with the likelihood of developing
T2D were assessed by conditional logistic regres-
sion (R package ‘survival’ [43]). Odds ratios (OR)
were calculated across quartiles and per standard
deviation (SD) of metabolites levels, adjusted for
potential confounders that have been reported to
be associated with T2D risk, that is BMI, fasting
plasma glucose, smoking status, education, phys-
ical activity, as well as total daily energy and a
priori
determined
Baltic
Sea
Diet
Score
that
reﬂected a healthy Nordic dietary pattern [27, 44].

Metabolite identiﬁcation
Each metabolite feature related to coffee intake
was identiﬁed based on accurate mass (ppm ≤10)
and tandem mass spectrometry (MS/MS) fragmen-
tation (ppm ≤15) by matching of retention time
and MS/MS spectra with those of authentic chem-
ical standards wherever possible. Otherwise, iden-
tiﬁcation was performed using MS-DIAL [45], CSI:
FingerID [46] and MyCompoundID [47], and man-
ually matched against online spectral databases,
that is the Human Metabolome Database (HMDB
[48]) and the METLIN metabolite database, or
previously
published
literature
[17,
49–51].
Unknown compounds were presented as ‘analyti-
cal mode m/z_retention time (min)’ only (Table S3).

Discussion
We have, for the ﬁrst time, identiﬁed plasma
metabolites speciﬁcally associated with boiled or
ﬁltered coffee intake in a free-living Swedish pop-
ulation and found that they were differently asso-
ciated
with
risk
of
developing
T2D.
In
the
investigated study population, an inverse associa-
tion was observed between the metabolite panel
reﬂective of self-reported ﬁltered coffee intake and
T2D risk, whereas no apparent association was
observed for boiled coffee, either estimated by FFQ
or reﬂected by metabolites. The lack of association
for boiled coffee might be due to the limited number
of boiled coffee consumers or a lack of protective
effect of boiled coffee intake, which warrants fur-
ther investigation. Our study demonstrates a great
potential of using plasma metabolites as speciﬁc
biomarkers for different coffee brews as a comple-
ment to traditional dietary assessment. This is
useful for investigations of the role of different
coffee brews in disease development, especially in
populations with heterogeneous habits of coffee
brews consumption and/or in cohorts with no
information on coffee brewing methods available.

Associations of coffee-related metabolites with T2D risk
Previous observational studies in which ﬁltered
coffee was the dominantly consumed coffee brew
have shown beneﬁts of ﬁltered coffee intake on T2D
prevention and found that people with reported
consumption of 4 cups ﬁltered coffee per day had
lower risk of developing T2D, compared to those
who reported < 1 cups per day [1, 4]. Our results
are well in line with previous studies (Fig. 3c).
Although biological mechanisms underpinning the
role of coffee in T2D are not completely resolved,
many studies have suggested that chlorogenic acid
and polyphenols are the main factors behind the
observations. This might be attributed to their
beneﬁcial effects on glucose and lipid metabolisms
as well as inﬂammation [2, 7]. However, no obser-
vational
study
has
investigated
associations
between chlorogenic acid metabolites and T2D risk.
We found that 4 out of 9 reported chlorogenic acid
metabolites [5] were associated with ﬁltered coffee
intake, including 3-hydroxyhippurate (r = 0.11), 3-
(3-hydroxyphenyl)propionate (r = 0.14) and cate-
chol sulphate (r = 0.09), even though they were not
selected as optimal predictors of coffee intake from
multivariate modelling. We also found a borderline
inverse association between 3-hydroxyhippurate
with T2D risk, after adjustment for daily energy
intake and lifestyle-related factors (ORperSD = 0.82
[95%CI 0.65, 1.02]), but not for the other metabo-
lites. In line with previous studies, two tentatively
annotated phenolic compounds, that is 7-hydroxy-
4-(methoxymethyl)coumarin
and
dihydroferulic
acid that were positively correlated with ﬁltered
coffee intake were also inversely associated with
T2D risk, supporting the preventive role of phenolic
compounds in T2D development.
The beneﬁt of coffee intake on T2D prevention is
unlikely attributed to caffeine. Observational stud-
ies have shown inverse associations for both caf-
feinated and decaffeinated coffee with risk of T2D
[52,
53].
Evidence
from
intervention
studies
regarding the role of caffeine on insulin sensitivity
and glucose metabolism are conﬂicting [54, 55],
and in the present study, we in fact found a positive
association between plasma caffeine and T2D risk
after adjusting for covariates (Table S5).
Several boiled coffee-related metabolites as well as
the metabolite panel score reﬂective of boiled coffee
intake
correlated
with
triglycerides
and
total
cholesterol, similar to previous studies [1]. How-
ever, no apparent association with T2D risk was
observed, in contrast to the previous study where
an inverse association of boiled coffee intake with
T2D risk was found amongst 68 788 Norwegian
people who reported drinking boiled coffee only
(scale ranged from 0–9 cups per day). In this
Swedish study population, only 71 participants
reportedly consumed boiled coffee, and amongst
them, 40 consumed boiled coffee at least once per
day. OR of T2D was numerically lower for the 4th
compared with 1st quartile of the metabolite panel
score, indicating that the lack of association for
boiled coffee might be attributed to the small
number of participants.

Conclusion
In conclusion, using comprehensive metabolomics
data and materials from a case–control study
nested within the Swedish population-based VIP
cohort, we identiﬁed plasma metabolites speciﬁ-
cally associated with boiled or ﬁltered coffee intake.
Our results support the beneﬁts of habitual ﬁltered
coffee intake for T2D prevention. However, no
association between boiled coffee intake and T2D
risk was observed which may be due to the fact
that the study was limited by the low number of
boiled coffee consumers in this population or a lack
of protective effect of boiled coffee intake. The
present study demonstrates the potential of using
metabolite biomarkers speciﬁc for coffee brews as a
complement to traditional dietary assessment in
investigating their role in T2D development, espe-
cially in existing cohorts where consumption data
of different coffee brews are lacking. Identiﬁed
metabolites should be further investigated as can-
didate biomarkers of speciﬁc intakes of ﬁltered and
boiled coffee in future studies.
