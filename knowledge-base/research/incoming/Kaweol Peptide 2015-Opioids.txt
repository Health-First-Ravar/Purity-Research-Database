A B S T R A C T
Background: Kahweol is a diterpene present in the oil derived from coffee beans. Although several
pharmacological activities of kahweol are already well described in the literature, no study was done in
order to assess the analgesic activity of this substance. Thus, the aim of this study was to investigate the
possible peripheral antinociceptive effect of kahweol. Considering that the opioid peptides have been
implicated in peripheral antinociception induced by non-opioidergic compounds, the present study also
evaluated the endogenous opioids involvement in this effect.
Methods: The rat paw pressure test was used, and hyperalgesia was induced by intraplantar injection of
prostaglandin E2 (2 mg/paw). All drugs were administered subcutaneously in the hindpaws of male
Wistar rats. The expression of b-endorphin was examined by immunohistochemistry in the skin tissue
samples of the plantar surface of rat right hindpaws.
Results: Intraplantar injection of kahweol (40 and 80 mg) induced signiﬁcant peripheral antinociception.
The antinociceptive effect of kahweol was due to a local peripheral action because the higher dose
(80 mg/paw) did not produce any effect in the contralateral paw. The opioid receptor antagonist
naloxone (50 and 100 mg/paw) prevented action of kahweol (80 mg/paw) and the aminopeptidases
inhibitor bestatin (400 mg/paw) potentiated the antinociceptive effect of kahweol (40 mg/paw).
Furthermore, kahweol treatment increased the intensity of b-endorphin immunoreactivity in the
epithelium of rat paws.
Conclusions: The results discussed here provide evidence that kahweol treatment has peripheral
antinociceptive effect and suggest that this effect is mediated by the release of endogenous opioids.

Previous studies have reported the involvement of opioid
233 
peptides in peripheral antinociception induced by non-opioidergic
234 
compounds [23–29]. The endogenous opioids comprising Met-
235 
enkephalin, Leu-enkephalin, b-endorphin, dynorphin, endomor-
236 
phin, and numerous others all belong to the family of opioid
237 
peptides that work as ‘‘natural pain relievers’’ by decreasing the
238 
perception of pain and act in the brain, spinal cord and peripheral
239 
sensory neurons [36–38]. After a ligand binds at the opioid
240 
receptors, conformational changes allow coupling of inhibitory G-
241 
proteins (Gi/o) to the C-terminus of opioid receptors. Subsequent-
242 
ly, 3050-cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP) production is
243 
inhibited and G-proteins subunits can interact directly with ion
244 
channels in the membrane. Peripherally, these molecular events
245 
may lead to attenuation of the excitability of nociceptive neurons
246 
and inhibition of the release of excitatory neurotransmitters and
247 
pronociceptive 
neuropeptides 
(e.g., 
substance 
P, 
glutamate,
248 
calcitonin gene-related peptide) from nociceptors terminals,
249 
thereby leading to antinociceptive effect [38,39].
250 
In order to test the involvement of endogenous opioids in the
251 
peripheral antinociceptive effect of kahweol, the opioid receptor
252 
antagonist naloxone was used as a pretreatment to kahweol and
253 
prevented in a dose-dependent manner the antinociceptive effect of
254 
kahweol. In addition to the naloxone, bestatin was used to inhibit the
255 
action of aminopeptidases, which are enzymes involved in the
256 
degradation of endogenous opioid peptides [40]. Bestatin potenti-
257 
ated the peripheral antinociceptive effect of kahweol, suggesting
258 
the mobilization of endogenous opioid peptides in this effect.
259 
To conﬁrm the hypothesis of endogenous opioid participation
260 
in the antinociceptive effect of kahweol, immunohistochemistry
261 
analysis of b-endorphin in the epithelial region of the plantar
262

263 
intraplantar injection of kahweol increased b-endorphin immu-
264 
noreactive activity in nonhyperalgesic paws. This increase in
265 
b-endorphin expression may occur in keratinocytes because they
266 
are the major source of b-endorphin in the skin [41]. The
267 
keratinocytes contain opioid peptides [42–44] and constitutively
268 
express proopiomelanocortin [45,46], which is the precursor for a
269 
variety of peptides including the endogenous opioid peptide
270 
b-endorphin. The mechanism by which CB2 cannabinoid receptor
271 
agonists [41] and a selective endothelin-B receptor agonist [43]
272 
induce peripheral antinociception is dependent on keratinocyte
273 
action, which releases the opioid peptide b-endorphin. Resident
274 
immune cells may contribute to the release of b-endorphin. Opioid
275 
peptides [47,48] and proopiomelanocortin [49,50] have been
276 
reported in cells of the immune system. In studies involving
277 
hybridization, the presence of mRNAs encoding the opioid
278 
peptides was observed in T and B lymphocytes, monocytes and
279 
macrophages [51].
280 
Interestingly, the animal group that received PGE2 as a
281 
pretreatment to kahweol did not show increased levels of
282 
b-endorphin, indicating that in our study, b-endorphin is released
283 
by the action of PGE2. Thus, kahweol increases levels of b-endorphin
284 
that will only be released in the presence of a noxious agent. It is
285 
known that PGE2, by acting on EP3 receptors, increases the
286 
concentration of cAMP [22] by activating protein kinase A (PK-A)
287 
and consequently increasing the inﬂux of cellular calcium [52]. Ritt-
288 
ner et al. [53] reported that chemokine-induced opioid release from
289 
polymorphonuclear cells requires elevation of intracellular calcium.
290 
This increase in intracellular calcium is probably the mechanism by
291 
which PGE2 leads to the release of b-endorphin. We have not
292 
excluded the possibility that other endogenous opioids may
293 
contribute to the antinociceptive effect of kahweol.
294 
Knowing that opioid receptors are widely expressed in primary
295 
afferent neurons [54] and that opioid peptides can activate this
296
receptors and inhibit nociception [55], it is likely that kahweol
297
produces peripheral antinociception indirectly through the stimu-
298
lation of keratinocytes and/or resident immune cells that release
299
opioid peptides.
300
The present study is the ﬁrst to provide evidence suggesting
301
that kahweol exerts antinociceptive activity, and moreover, we
302
propose that kahweol could also indirectly induce peripheral
303
antinociception by releasing endogenous opioid peptides that
304
leads to the activation of opioid receptors on the terminals of
305
primary afferent neurons. The results suggest that kahweol may be
306
a substance with potential applicability in pain therapy, which
307
broadens prospects for future research regarding the antinocicep-
308
tive mechanism of this diterpene.
