Immunomodulatory effects of caffeine: Friend or foe?
Louise A. Horrigan a,⁎, John P. Kelly a, Thomas J. Connor b
a Department of Pharmacology, National University of Ireland, Galway, University Road, Galway, Ireland
b Department of Physiology and Trinity College Institute of Neuroscience, University of Dublin, Trinity College, Dublin 2, Ireland
Abstract
Caffeine is a member of the methylxanthine family of drugs, and is the most widely consumed behaviourally active substance in the western
world. This article is focused on the impact of caffeine on immune function. In this regard, a number of in vitro and in vivo studies have
demonstrated that caffeine modulates both innate and adaptive immune responses. For instance studies indicate that caffeine and its major
metabolite paraxanthine suppress neutrophil and monocyte chemotaxis, and also suppress production of the pro-inflammatory cytokine tumour
necrosis factor (TNF)-α from human blood. Caffeine has also been reported to suppress human lymphocyte function as indicated by reduced T-cell
proliferation and impaired production of Th1 (interleukin [IL]-2 and interferon [IFN]-γ), Th2 (IL-4, IL-5) and Th3 (IL-10) cytokines. Studies also
indicate that caffeine suppresses antibody production. The evidence suggests that at least some of the immunomodulatory actions of caffeine are
mediated via inhibition of cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP)–phosphodiesterase (PDE), and consequential increase in intracellular cAMP
concentrations. Overall, these studies indicate that caffeine, like other members of the methylxanthine family, is largely anti-inflammatory in
nature, and based on the pharmacokinetics of caffeine, we suggest that many of its immunomodulatory effects occur at concentrations that are
relevant to normal human consumption. Finally, the potential of caffeine-induced immunomodulation to significantly impact upon health and
well-being are discussed.

1. Introduction
Caffeine (1,3,7-trimethylxanthine) is a member of the
methylxanthine family of drugs, and due to its presence in
coffee, tea and medicinal products it is probably the most
widely consumed psychoactive substance known to man
(reviewed by Fredholm et al., 1999). Historically, caffeine
has been the subject of extensive research, and studies have
been conducted in various species to determine the impact of
caffeine on various biochemical and physiological processes.
Based on the studies conducted to date it is clear that in
addition to its well-established psychoactive effects, caffeine
also impacts upon the endocrine, cardiovascular, respiratory,
renal and gastrointestinal systems (reviewed by Aruna, 1997).
Regarding the implications of these studies, there is often
intense debate and controversy over the general impact of
caffeine on human well-being. This controversy has probably
been greatest in the field of cardiology (Reville, 2000;
Anonymous, 2002; James, 2004; Ziegelstein, 2004). In
addition, evidence is now accumulating to indicate that
caffeine and its methylxanthine metabolites can impact upon
functioning of the immune system, but hitherto caffeine has
escaped the scrutiny of a consolidated review dealing with its
ability to impact upon the immune system. The objective of
this article is to present the studies that have been published in
this area, and to provide a critical review of the immunomod-
ulatory effects of this drug, as well as the implications of these
effects for the caffeine consumer (Fig. 1).
2. Should we expect the
immune system to be a target for caffeine?
It is generally believed that at concentrations relevant to
normal patterns of consumption, caffeine exerts most if not all
of its effects via antagonism of adenosine receptors (reviewed
by Fredholm et al., 1999). Caffeine is also a well-known
inhibitor of cyclic adenosine monophosphate–phosphodiester-
ase (cAMP–PDE) (Wells et al., 1975; Fredholm et al., 1978),
and some effects of caffeine have been shown to be mediated
by increased concentrations of intracellular cAMP and
subsequent activation of protein kinase A (PKA) (Rosenthal
et al., 1992; Horrigan et al., 2004). Thus, there are 2 likely
mechanisms by which caffeine may modulate immune
function.
Firstly, cAMP is a very potent immunomodulator, exerting
generally suppressive effects on the functions of inflammatory
and immunocompetent cells (reviewed by Kammer, 1988).
For example, activation of the cAMP/PKA pathway inhibits
lipopolysaccharide (LPS)-stimulated production of pro-inflam-
matory cytokines such as tumour necrosis factor (TNF)-α and
interleukin (IL)-12, and promotes production of the anti-
inflammatory cytokine IL-10 in murine macrophages (Procó-
pio et al., 1999) and human peripheral blood mononuclear
cells (PBMC) (Eigler et al., 1998). mRNA expression of IL-4,
IL-3 and granulocyte-monocyte colony-stimulating factor
(GM-CSF) are also down-regulated by activation of this
pathway (Borger et al., 1996a, 1996b), as is secretion of IL-5
by human T cells (Kaminuma et al., 1999; Staples et al.,
2001). cAMP also inhibits lymphocyte proliferation (Aandahl
et al., 2002). Likewise, PDE inhibitors such as rolipram and
the xanthines have extensive effects on immune function
including suppression of pro-inflammatory cytokine release
(including TNF-α, IL-2 and interferon [IFN]-γ), stimulation
of anti-inflammatory cytokine (IL-10), and suppression of
neutrophil and eosinophil chemotaxis and superoxide gener-
ation (reviewed by Dent & Rabe, 1996; Souness et al., 2000).
These findings suggest that the ability of caffeine to inhibit
PDE (albeit weakly) could result in immunomodulatory
effects.
As mentioned above, dietary caffeine is believed to exert
most, if not all, of its effects via antagonism of adenosine
receptors. Adenosine is a potent immunomodulator, exerting
generally (but not exclusively) suppressive effects on immune
cells. Suppression of TNF-α production by adenosine and
agonists of adenosine A1, A2 and A3 receptors has been
described under both in vivo and in vitro conditions in
various cell types, including human PBMC (Eigler et al.,
1997) and monocytes (Le Vraux et al., 1993; Bouma et al.,
1994; Le Moine et al., 1996), the human macrophage cell line
U937 (Sajjadi et al., 1996), rat Kupffer cells (Reinstein et al.,
1994), and J774.1 (McWhinney et al., 1996) and RAW 264.7
(Haskó et al., 1996) macrophage cell lines. IL-12 is also
suppressed by adenosine agonists, whilst the production of
the anti-inflammatory IL-10 is increased (Haskó et al., 1998;
Link et al., 2000). Adenosine also inhibits the respiratory

burst of human monocytes via the A3 receptor (Broussas et
al., 1999). There is less evidence for an effect of adenosine on
T cell-derived cytokines. However, IFN-γ production has
been found to be reduced in endotoxemic mice treated with
the A3 agonist IB-MECA (Haskó et al., 1998). Of the 4
subtypes of adenosine receptors, the A2A receptor is generally
regarded as the most important subtype in mediating the
immunomodulatory actions of adenosine (Ohta & Sitkovsky,
2001; Zhang et al., 2005; Kreckler et al., 2006). In this
regard, Ohta and Sitkovsky (2001) demonstrated that the anti-
inflammatory role of the adenosine A2A receptor may be vital
in protecting the body against life-threatening inflammatory
stimuli. In this study, concanavalin A (Con A)-induced pro-
inflammatory cytokine production, liver damage and lethality
was greatly exacerbated in A2A receptor-deficient mice, in
comparison to wild-type controls. Similarly, a more recent
study demonstrated that A2A receptor knockout mice
displayed enhanced pro-inflammatory cytokine and chemo-
kine expression in neutrophils and mononuclear cells in an in
vivo LPS airpouch model (McColl et al., 2006). Taken
together these studies clearly demonstrate that the A2A
adenosine receptor has a non-redundant role in controlling
inflammation and tissue damage in vivo.
It is known that excessive accumulation of extracellular
and intracellular adenosine is toxic to lymphocytes and
results in severe combined immunodeficiency (SCID) (Hersh-
field & Mitchell, 1995; Buckley et al., 1997). For instance,
adenosine deaminase deficiency in mice causes a reduction in
the size of the thymus and spleen, depletion of T and B
lymphocytes, and a decrease in the functional responsiveness
of T and B lymphocytes (Blackburn et al., 1996; Apasov et
al., 2001). It has also been shown that elevated levels of
adenosine are responsible for the abnormal T cell receptor
signaling (Apasov et al., 2001).
There is also some evidence that adenosine may activate
immune cells, and potentiate the immune response under
certain conditions. For instance, adenosine promotes chemo-
taxis via the A1 and A2 receptors (Rose et al., 1988; Cronstein
et al., 1990), although A3 receptor stimulation produces a dose-
dependent reduction in the chemotaxis of human eosinophils
(Knight et al., 1997; Walker et al., 1997). In addition,
adenosine has been shown to cause activation of mast cells
in asthmatics (though not in non-asthmatics) via the adenosine
A2B receptor (reviewed by Feoktistov & Biaggioni, 1997). It
was also reported that inhaled adenosine increased levels of
histamine, prostaglandin D2, and tryptase in bronchoalveolar
fluid from asthmatics (Polosa et al., 1995), indicating mast cell
activation (Schwartz, 1990). Similarly, adenosine potentiated
histamine release induced by anti-immunoglobulin E (IgE),
Con A, compound 48/80, or the calcium ionophore A23187 in
isolated rat mast cells (Marquardt et al., 1978). Feoktistov and
Biaggioni (1995, 1997) reported that A2B receptors activated
the human mast cell line HMC-1, and also potentiated phorbol
myristate acetate (PMA)-stimulated secretion of IL-8. As an
antagonist of adenosine receptors, it is reasonable to expect that
caffeine may modulate the aforementioned effects of endog-
enous adenosine on immune function.

that have investigated the effects of caffeine on immune

3.1. Concentrations or doses of caffeine employed
Many studies have been criticised for their use of
extraordinarily high concentrations/doses of caffeine. Howev-
er, as regards a definition of a ‘high’ concentration or dose of
caffeine, there is also some controversy! Intake of caffeine
varies widely from person to person and from country to
country. For example, it is estimated that approximately
200 mg caffeine per person per day is consumed in Ireland
(213 mg/person/day), the UK (202 mg/person/day), the US
(168 mg/person/day), and Canada (210 mg/person/day),
whilst over 400 mg per person per day is consumed in the
Netherlands (414 mg/person/day), Sweden (407 mg/person/
day) and Norway (400 mg/person/day) (reviewed by
Fredholm et al., 1999). In addition, because of biological

of caffeine in this study, it is reasonable to expect that plasma

individuals taking cold and flu remedies containing caffeine,
plasma levels could be much higher. In this regard, a recent
study by Varani et al. (2005) reported a mean peak plasma
caffeine concentration of 63 μM in 11 subjects following
ingestion of 600 mg of caffeine per day for 1 week. Also, higher
plasma caffeine concentrations may also occur in women taking
oral contraceptives (Patwardhan et al., 1980; Abernethy &
Todd, 1985). As in vivo and in vitro studies have shown that
caffeine is poorly bound to plasma albumin over a wide range of
concentrations (1–1000 μg/mL), ages, and species examined
(Albary et al., 1981; Blanchard, 1982), it is assumed that
concentrations of caffeine measured in plasma represent the
active concentrations of caffeine in the plasma.
3.1.1. In vitro studies
Based on the above estimates of plasma caffeine concentra-
tions, the in vitro studies outlined in Tables 1–11 can be
separated into studies that are relevant to normal levels of
caffeine consumption, and those that have used excessively
high concentrations of the drug. Although 100 μM caffeine is
probably at the higher end of the range of relevant concentra-
tions, in this review in vitro studies employing 100 μM caffeine
or lower will be considered relevant to human caffeine
consumers. While studies employing higher concentrations
are valuable from a biochemical perspective, they are probably
not relevant to typical consumers of the drug.
3.1.1.1. Measures of innate immunity.
Based on the catego-
risation outlined above, caffeine concentrations relevant to
human consumption had no effect on NK cell cytotoxicity
(Table 8) (Kantamala et al., 1990). Similarly, Meiners et al.
(2004) found that concentrations of caffeine relevant to human
consumption had no effect on production of the pro-
inflammatory cytokine TNF-α in monocytes stimulated with
chicken egg albumin-advanced glycation products (CEA-
AGE). In contrast, we consistently observed that lower doses
of caffeine suppress TNF-α production in LPS-stimulated
diluted human whole blood (Table 1; Fig. 2) (Horrigan et al.,
2004).
Whilst total chemiluminescence was increased by caffeine in
LPS-primed mixed leukocytes (neutrophils and PBMC)
(Sullivan et al., 1995), and also in
L-formyl-met-leu-phe
(fMLP)-stimulated PBMC incubated with amphotericin B
(Sullivan et al., 1992), superoxide production was not altered
by concentrations of caffeine relevant to normal consumption
(Sullivan et al., 1995; Jafari & Rabbani, 2000) (Table 2). As
luminol-enhanced chemiluminescence is dependent upon both
superoxide production and release of myeloperoxidase from
neutrophilic granules (Rosen & Klebenoff, 1976), Sullivan et al.
(1995) investigated the possibility that the increased chemilu-
minescence was due to an increase in the release of
myeloperoxidase. Indeed, the authors observed that caffeine
significantly enhanced myeloperoxidase release from the mixed
leukocyte population (Table 9).
Although Sullivan et al. (1995) failed to observe any effect of
caffeine on migration of fMLP-stimulated neutrophils, or
neutrophils with PBMC, in the presence of amphotericin B,
we observed that caffeine suppressed chemotaxis in both fMLP-
stimulated monocytes and fMLP-stimulated neutrophils (Horri-
gan et al., 2003) (Table 7).

3.1.2. In vivo studies
With regard to the in vivo situation, many studies
investigating the physiological and biochemical effects of
caffeine have been performed in rodents. As when extrapolat-
ing any pharmacological study from animal to human, issues
such as dose equivalence between species are essential to
consider. The difficulties associated with establishing a dose
equivalent relationship between humans and rodents are
compounded by the widely varying human patterns of
consumption discussed above. Also, as mentioned by Fred-
holm et al. (1999), in most experiments on rodents, 1 single
high dose of caffeine is administered, whereas human
consumption of caffeine is usually divided up during the day
(see Section 3.3). However, according to Fredholm et al.
(1999), a 10 mg/kg dose in a rat represents about 250 mg of
caffeine in a human weighing 70 kg (3.5 mg/kg), and this
corresponds to about 2–3 cups of coffee. Cardinali (1980)
made a similar extrapolation, and estimated that a dose of
20 mg/kg of caffeine given to a rat is equivalent to about
600 mg of caffeine to a human. Based on these extrapola-
tions between rodents and humans, and the measurements of
plasma caffeine in humans cited above, a 10 mg/kg dose of
caffeine in a rat would be expected to yield a plasma caffeine
concentration of 20–50 μM. This estimate correlates well
with data presented by Wang and Lau (1998). In their study,
serum caffeine concentrations were measured in rats every
hour for a 4-hr period. Peak caffeine concentrations resulting
from 10 mg/kg of the drug administered intraperitoneally or
orally were approximately 9 μg/mL (approximately 45 μM)
and 5 μg/mL (approximately 25 μM), respectively. On the
basis of these data, many of the studies investigating the
effects of in vivo caffeine on immune parameters (Tables 1–
11) have found significant effects using relatively low doses
of caffeine that are relevant to human consumption.

3.1.2.1. Measures of innate immunity.
In vivo administration
of caffeine has been shown to decrease mast cell-mediated
hypersensitivity (Table 10) (Saxena et al., 1984; Shin et al.,

activity was also suppressed by concentrations of caffeine as
low as 2 mg/kg in the mouse (Table 9). Kantamala et al. (1990)

(Table 8), depending on the dose employed. Whilst 2 and
18 mg/kg caffeine failed to alter NK cell function, 6 mg/kg
inhibited NK cell cytotoxicty.
3.1.2.2. Measures of adaptive immunity.
Similar to what we
have already outlined for NK cell cytotoxicity, Kantamala et al.
(1990) found differential effects of caffeine on leukocyte
proliferation (Table 3), depending on the dose employed.
Whilst 2 and 18 mg/kg caffeine had no effect on PWM-
stimulated proliferation of splenic mononuclear cells, 6 mg/kg
caffeine inhibited PWM-stimulated proliferation. On the other
hand, 2 and 6 mg/kg had no effect on PHA-P-stimulated
proliferation, whilst 18 mg/kg caffeine caused an increase in
PHA-P-stimulated proliferation. According to in vivo studies, a
concentration of caffeine that is likely to be relevant to human
consumption (15 mg/kg) caused an increase in antibody
production, whilst higher doses (60 mg/kg and 200 mg/kg)
suppressed antibody production (Table 4) (Laux & Klesius,
1973; Saxena et al., 1984).
The only study that investigated the in vivo effects of
caffeine in the human assessed the effects of caffeine on
immune function before and after strenuous cycling (Table 5)
(Bishop et al., 2005). One hour following consumption and
immediately prior to cycling, the investigators observed that
6 mg/kg caffeine, given as a bolus oral dose, caused an increase
in the total lymphocyte count, and in the CD8+ count compared
to placebo. Following cycling, there was no significant
difference between the total lymphocyte, CD4+ or CD8+ counts
of cyclists who had consumed caffeine and those who had
consumed placebo. On the other hand, the percentage of
CD4+CD69+ cells were significantly increased before and
immediately following exercise, but not 1 hr post-exercise in
caffeine consumers. CD69 is an early activation marker,
expressed rapidly following engagement of the T cell receptor
(Testi et al., 1994). Expression of this marker is associated with
T cell proliferation and cytotoxic activity (Mueller et al., 2002).
According to the authors, the main finding was an increase in
the natural state of activation of CD4+ and CD8+ cells both
before and after exercise with pre-exercise caffeine ingestion
only. Similar to the in vitro and rodent in vivo studies, this study
indicates that caffeine consumption alters immune function.
However, this study demonstrates an immunostimulatory role
for caffeine in T-cells, whereas much of the in vitro and rodent
in vivo data indicate that caffeine is generally anti-inflamma-
tory. It is noteworthy that a 6 mg/kg bolus dose of caffeine is
probably higher than most coffee consumers would encounter in
a single sitting. For instance in a 70-kg human, this would
correspond to 420 mg caffeine, which represents the products of
approximately 3–6 cups of coffee. Clearly, more human in vivo
studies are necessary to determine whether lymphocyte
responsiveness to antigenic challenge (or indeed other measures
of immune function) will be altered in caffeine consumers.
3.2. Metabolism of caffeine, and its impact on immune function
In order to establish a complete pharmacological profile of
any drug, the effects of the metabolites must also be considered.
In the human, the majority of a caffeine dose is excreted in the
urine in the form of metabolites (70–89%) with little unchanged.
Caffeine is efficiently eliminated through liver biotransforma-
tion to paraxanthine (1,7-dimethylxanthine), theobromine (3,7-
dimethylxanthine), and theophylline (1,3-dimethylxanthine).
These caffeine derivatives account for 80%, 11% and 4%,
respectively, of the metabolites produced by caffeine in the
human (Lelo et al., 1986b). Each primary metabolite is further
biotransformed, either by N-demethylation or C-8 oxidation, and
the metabolites excreted in the urine mostly represent these
secondary reactions (reviewed by Walton et al., 2001).
The metabolic profile of caffeine is species-dependent
(Labedzki et al., 2002). This has very important consequences,
considering that most in vivo assessments of the immunological
effects of caffeine have been performed in rodents. In the rat,
57–68% of caffeine is excreted in the urine as metabolites. As
well as 1-, 3- and 7-N-demethylation, in the rat, caffeine is
extensively metabolised by C-8-oxidation. In the rat, 40% of the
caffeine metabolites are trimethyl derivatives as compared with
less than 6% in humans (Arnaud, 1985; Arnaud, 1993).
Caffeine half-lives range from 0.7 to 1.2 hr in the rat and
mouse (for doses lower than 10 mg/kg) and from 2.5 to 4.5 hr in
humans (Arnaud, 1987). Thus, the metabolic profile and rate of
clearance of caffeine from the bloodstream varies considerably
between humans and rodents, and this is a factor that ought to be
taken into consideration when comparing results from rodent
and human studies.
Despite the plethora of studies investigating various
activities of caffeine, very few studies have investigated the
effects of its major metabolite paraxanthine. This is surprising
considering that at any one time two−thirds of the caffeine
consumed exists in the body as paraxanthine (Lelo et al.,
1986b). From the relatively small number of studies that have
been performed using paraxanthine, this metabolite appears to
have similar sympathomimetic and ergogenic effects to caffeine
(Hetzler et al., 1990; Dulloo et al., 1994; Benowitz et al., 1995;
Hawke et al., 2000). We investigated the effects of paraxanthine
on monocyte and neutrophil chemotaxis (Horrigan et al., 2003),
LPS-stimulated TNF-α production (Horrigan et al., 2004), Con
A-stimulated T-cell proliferation, IFN-γ production and IL-5
production (Horrigan et al., 2005), and in all cases, paraxanthine
elicited qualitatively similar immunomodulatory effects to those
observed with caffeine.
Whilst there is little information available on the pharma-
cological actions of theobromine, a small number of studies
have demonstrated that it is pharmacologically less potent than
other methylxanthines (Kosazuma & Kawauchi, 1994; Sven-
ningsson et al., 1995; Lentini et al., 1998). With regard to
immune function, Chorostowska-Wynimko et al. (2004)
investigated the status of the immune system in 2-month-old

Balb/c mice fed theobromine during pregnancy and lactation.
They observed that the mitogen-induced proliferative activity of

The immunomodulatory effects of theophylline have been
well established, as it is well-known for its immunosuppressive
and anti-inflammatory properties. Theophylline has been shown
to suppress cytokine production (Scordamaglia et al., 1988;
Spatafora et al., 1994; Yoshimura et al., 1995; Mascali et al.,
1996; Shute et al., 1998; Culpitt et al., 2002), cell proliferation
(Scordamaglia et al., 1988; Yoshimura et al., 1995; Mascali et
al., 1996), superoxide production (Yasui et al., 2000), and
chemotaxis (Condino-Neto et al., 1991; Tenor et al., 1996;
Yasui et al., 2000; Culpitt et al., 2002). Immune cell apoptosis
has also been shown to increase in the presence of theophylline
(Yasui et al., 1997). Theophylline has been used for many years
for the treatment of asthma. Although bronchodilator actions of
theophylline may partially account for its beneficial effects in
asthma, it has become clear in recent years that anti-
inflammatory effects of theophylline also play a role in
mediating its therapeutic effects (reviewed by Barnes, 2003).
Theophylline is recommended as one of the central drugs in the
management and treatment of symptomatic chronic obstructive
pulmonary disease (Global Initiative for Chronic Obstructive
Lung Disease, 2005), and its therapeutic efficacy is most
probably due to its ability to attenuate airway inflammation
(Culpitt et al., 2002; Kobayashi et al., 2004). In addition, due to
its ability to induce leukocyte apoptosis, it has beneficial effects
in treating chronic lymphocytic leukaemia (Basu & Mitra Basu,
2000).
3.3. Acute versus chronic
effects of caffeine on immune function
When assessing the effects of caffeine on human body
function, it is important to distinguish between the effects of a
single bolus dose of caffeine and the effects of caffeine when it
is repeatedly administered. Most caffeine consumers are
exposed to the drug on a regular basis, and so studies which
examine the effects of a single dose may not bear relevance to
the real-life situation. It has been demonstrated that tolerance to
some effects of caffeine can develop quite quickly in habitual
caffeine consumers (reviewed by Fredholm et al., 1999). For
example, a number of studies have demonstrated that tolerance
develops to the effects of caffeine on blood pressure and heart
rate within a couple of days (Robertson et al., 1981; Denaro et
al., 1991; Shi et al., 1993). Also, tolerance to the behavioural
effects of caffeine has been documented (Finn & Holtzman,
1986). Therefore, studies which present the effects of a single
dose of caffeine may demonstrate drug-induced effects that are
not relevant to habitual coffee consumers. In addition, with
regard to a number of parameters, caffeine has been shown to
have opposite effects when administered acutely versus chronic
dosing. Chronic caffeine dosing causes a decrease in locomotor
activity, whereas acute treatment with caffeine causes increased
locomotor activity (Nikodijeviç et al., 1993). Also, whilst single
high doses of caffeine can cause seizures, long-term caffeine
treatment decreases seizure susceptibility (Georgiev et al., 1993;
Johansson et al., 1996). Therefore, it is important to consider the
various dosing strategies employed in studies investigating the
effects of caffeine on immune function, and to bear in mind that
adaptive changes in immune function may occur with repeated
caffeine exposure. As can be seen from Tables 1–11, most
studies investigating the immunological effects of caffeine have
employed a single drug dose. However, studies by Kantamala et
al. (1990) and Saxena et al. (1984) observed the effects of
caffeine following repeated dosing for up to 120 days and
30 days, respectively. When caffeine was administered daily to
mice for 7 days, only a very high dose caused suppression of
hypersensitivity (Saxena et al., 1984), whilst a low dose
administered acutely to rats caused suppression of hypersensi-
tivity (Shin et al., 2000) (Table 10). Although these studies
differed from each other in a number of ways, it is possible that
the different effects observed were due to the development of
tolerance in the chronic study. Also, Saxena et al. (1984)
observed variable effects of caffeine on antibody production by
murine splenocytes (depending on the dose involved), when
mice were treated with caffeine for 7 days (Table 4). On the
other hand, in vitro studies (Laux & Klesius, 1973; Rosenthal et
al., 1992) demonstrated that caffeine decreased antibody
production over a wide concentration range (Table 4). Again,
these studies differed in a number of ways, but the varying
effects of caffeine observed between studies may be due to
differential effects of acute versus chronic treatment. Kantamala
et al. (1990) observed differential effects of caffeine (following
daily dosing for 120 consecutive days) on rat splenic
mononuclear cell proliferation (increase, decrease or no effect),
depending on the dose of caffeine and the mitogen employed
(Table 3). On the other hand, regardless of which mitogen or
which concentration of caffeine was employed, the same
investigators reported that in vitro incubation of rat splenic
mononuclear cells with a single bolus caffeine concentration
caused a decrease in mitogen-stimulated cell proliferation
(Table 3). Similarly, Kantamala et al. (1990) observed
differential effects of caffeine on NK cell cytotoxicity
(following in vivo dosing for 120 days) depending on the
dose of caffeine employed (Table 8). Regardless of the
concentration of caffeine used, the same investigators observed
that a single in vitro bolus concentration of caffeine had no
effect on NK cell cytotoxicity (Table 8). Further work would be
necessary to compare the effects of a single in vivo dose of
caffeine with the in vivo effects following chronic dosing.
Interestingly, according to Lau and Falk (1995), tolerance may
be more marked to high doses than to low doses of caffeine.
This could explain the variable dose-dependent effects observed
in the studies described, and indicates a need for further studies
in this area.
4. A biochemical basis for the
immunomodulatory effects of caffeine
As discussed above, dietary caffeine is believed to mediate
most, if not all, of its effects via antagonism of adenosine
886
L.A. Horrigan et al. / Pharmacology & Therapeutics 111 (2006) 877–892

receptors. Table 12, taken from Fredholm et al. (1999),
demonstrates that low levels of caffeine have the potential to
interact with any of the four adenosine receptor subtypes in the
human as well as the rat. In contrast, it is often suggested that
caffeine only inhibits PDE activity at concentrations far in
excess of those occurring in the plasma following caffeine
consumption (reviewed by Fredholm et al., 1999; Mandel,
2002). Nonetheless, with regard to the anti-inflammatory effects
of caffeine, the published evidence indicates that PDE inhibition
may be a more important mechanism of action than adenosine
receptor antagonism.
Firstly, as adenosine mediates many anti-inflammatory
effects (see Section 2), antagonism of adenosine receptors by
caffeine would be expected to inhibit the anti-inflammatory
effects of endogenous adenosine, and possibly elicit a pro-
inflammatory effect. However, as the evidence clearly indicates
that caffeine is generally anti-inflammatory, attention has been
directed towards other possible mechanisms of action. In this
regard, we recently demonstrated that caffeine (100 μM) caused
a significant increase in cAMP in LPS-stimulated human
monocytes, which paralleled the ability of caffeine to suppress
production of the pro-inflammatory cytokine TNF-α (Horrigan
et al., 2004; see Fig. 2). Furthermore, the suppressive effect of
caffeine on TNF-α production was blocked by the highly
specific cAMP antagonist Rp-8-Br-cAMPS (see Fig. 2).
Similarly, Rosenthal et al. (1992) demonstrated that HA1004,
an inhibitor of cAMP- and cyclic guanosine monophosphate
(cGMP)-dependent protein kinases, blocked caffeine-mediated
inhibition of sheep red blood cells (SRBC)-specific antibody
responses by murine spleen cells in vitro. These data suggest
that the aforementioned immunomodulatory actions of caffeine
are mediated by inhibition of cAMP–PDE and a resultant
increase in intracellular cAMP concentrations.
To date there has been little investigation of the ability of
caffeine to inhibit PDE enzymes in cells of the immune system.
Without such data, it is difficult to conclusively determine the
role of PDE inhibition in mediating the immunomodulatory
effects of caffeine. However, caffeine has been shown to inhibit
cAMP–PDE from rabbit renal cortex and from the supernatant
fraction of homogenates of pig coronary arteries with IC50 of
140 μM (Fredholm et al., 1978) and 500 μM (Wells et al.,
1975), respectively. In comparison, theophylline inhibits
cAMP–PDE from rabbit renal cortex and from the supernatant
fraction of homogenates of pig coronary arteries with IC50 of
600 μM (Fredholm et al., 1978) and 140 μM (Wells et al.,
1975), respectively. It is noteworthy that caffeine inhibits
cAMP–PDE in rabbit renal cortex with a much lower IC50 than
theophylline, which is well known for its immunomodulatory
actions. Since the aforementioned studies were conducted, 44
distinct human PDE proteins have been identified (see Castro et
al., 2005). As a result of the huge diversity in structure and
function of the various PDE isoforms, further studies are
required in order to determine the potency of caffeine in
inhibiting PDE isoforms in immune cells, and to compare the
ability of caffeine to inhibit PDE in immune cells with other
known immunomodulatory PDE inhibitors.
Other mechanisms of action may be at least partially
responsible for the actions of caffeine in immune cells. In this
regard, a number of novel mechanisms have been proposed for
mediating the actions of low concentrations of theophylline, a
structurally related metabolite of caffeine (Barnes, 2003). These
mechanisms include histone deacetylation (Ito et al., 2002) and
activation of adenosine A3 receptors (Ezeamuzie, 2001). It
remains to be determined whether these mechanisms of action
may be contributing to the immunomodulatory effects of
caffeine. It is also noteworthy that in a recent study,
administration of caffeine (400–600 mg/day) for 1–2 weeks
up-regulated A2A receptor expression and responsiveness on
human neutrophils, resulting in an enhanced anti-inflammatory
action of the adenosine receptor agonist NECA (Varani et al.,
2005). These data indicate that long term exposure to caffeine
can increase expression of the A2A receptor, and thereby
facilitate anti-inflammatory actions that occur via A2A receptor
activation.
5. Evidence for anti-inflammatory actions
of caffeine in animals models or human disease states
There is both preclinical and clinical evidence to suggest that
caffeine has protective effects in some inflammatory disorders.
5.1. Preclinical studies
Caffeine has been shown to reduce pro-inflammatory
cytokine production in a rat model of endotoxic shock (Tofovic
et al., 2001), and also to suppress TNF-α-induced hepatitis and
apoptosis in mice (Sugiyama et al., 2001). However, another
study demonstrated that ingestion of coffee provided protection
against LPS-induced liver injury in rats, indicated by a blunted
increase in serum liver enzymes, without significantly altering
production of the pro-inflammatory cytokines IL-6 and TNF-α
(Sakamoto et al., 2000). Of course in this study it is not certain
that the beneficial effects of coffee ingestion were due to
caffeine per se.
A recent study also demonstrated that caffeine had beneficial
effects in experimental autoimmune encephalitis, an animal
model of multiple sclerosis (MS) (Tsutsui et al., 2004). In this
study, the onset and severity of experimental autoimmune
encephalitis was significantly reduced and delayed in animals
receiving chronic caffeine treatment. It was suggested that
caffeine mediated its effects in this model by increasing
expression of adenosine A1 receptors on microglia/macro-
phages in the central nervous system.
Despite the aforementioned evidence to support an anti-
inflammatory action of caffeine, it has also been reported that

The benefits of caffeine in animal models of disease are often
investigated in combination with other pharmacological agents.
As well as the studies mentioned above, there is specific
evidence that caffeine can augment the anti-inflammatory
actions of other drugs. For instance, caffeine was shown to
augment the anti-inflammatory actions of aspirin, indometha-
cin, and phenylbutazone in the carrageenan pleurisy or hindlimb
models of inflammation in the rat (Vinegar et al., 1976).
Similarly, Seegers et al. (1981) observed that caffeine dose-
dependently (12.5–100 mg/kg) reduced rat paw oedema
significantly when used alone, and also when used in
combination with aspirin, paracetemol, and phenacetin. Marek
(1985) also observed that caffeine augmented the anti-
inflammatory actions of aspirin and paracetamol.
5.2. Clinical studies
There is epidemiological evidence suggesting that caffeine
intake is a protective factor for asthma (Pagano et al., 1988).
Specifically, this Italian study surveyed 72,284 individuals aged
over 15 years, and demonstrated that the prevalence of
bronchial asthma was inversely related to the level of coffee
intake. However, whether such a beneficial effect of coffee
consumption on the incidence of asthma is due to an
immunomodulatory effect of caffeine, or a direct bronchodila-
tory action of caffeine, remains to be established.
In terms of neurological disease, a recent report indicated
that there were significant improvements in fatigue experienced
by MS sufferers receiving a proprietary blend of histamine and
caffeine (Prokarin) for 12 weeks (Gillson et al., 2002); of course
the relative contribution of caffeine and histamine in alleviating
symptoms in MS sufferers remains to be determined. In
addition, whether any beneficial effect of caffeine in MS
patients is due to an immunomodulatory effect, or simply due to
its stimulant properties is not known. There is epidemiological
evidence suggesting that caffeine intake is a protective factor for
Alzheimer's disease (Maia & De Mendonça, 2002) and
Parkinson's disease (Schwartz & Weiss, 1992). It is noteworthy
that inappropriate microglial activation and the neuroinflamma-
tion that ensues plays an important contributory role in both of
these neurodegenerative disease states (see Liu & Hong, 2003;
Blasko et al., 2004; Rozemuller et al., 2005). Thus, it is possible
that a neuroprotective effect of caffeine could be due (at least in
part) to its anti-inflammatory actions. In support of this
hypothesis, we recently observed that caffeine (10 μM and
upwards) suppresses LPS-induced TNF-α production and
induction of iNOS and nitric oxide production in cultured
mixed glial cells (McNamee and Connor, unpublished obser-
vation), indicating that caffeine can elicit anti-inflammatory
actions in the brain. Further studies are required in order to
determine if caffeine can elicit neuroprotective actions via its
immunomodulatory effects.
6. Immunomodulatory actions
of related methylxanhtine compounds
As well as the direct evidence that caffeine itself may
modulate outcomes in inflammatory conditions, there is
considerable evidence to indicate that closely related methyl-
xanthines, such as theophylline and pentoxifylline, are of
benefit in illnesses characterised by disordered immune
responses. As discussed above, theophylline has been used in
the treatment of asthma and chronic obstructive pulmonary
disease for many years. In these conditions, the therapeutic
activity of theophylline is attributed, at least in part, to its ability
to suppress airway inflammation (Culpitt et al., 2002; reviewed
by Barnes, 2003). Similarly, pentoxifylline has been shown to
have potential in the treatment of various inflammatory
conditions. This drug modulated immune function in patients
suffering from MS (Rieckmann et al., 1996), and in patients
with dilated cardiomyopathy (severe heart failure characterised
by elevated cytokine levels) (Sliwa et al., 2002). Pentoxifylline
has also been shown to be effective in the treatment of
experimental autoimmune myocarditis, a rat model of the
autoimmune mechanism involved in dilated cardiomyopathy
(Takehana et al., 2002). In addition, Abdel-Salam et al. (2003)
suggested that pentoxifylline has potential in the treatment of
inflammatory disorders of the gastric mucosa, either alone or in
conjunction with cyclooxygenase inhibitors or with steroids.
This conclusion was based on their observations that pentoxifyl-
line decreased carrageenan-induced paw oedema formation. In
light of the therapeutic potential of theophylline and pentoxifyl-
line, it is of interest that caffeine may have a similar
immunomodulatory profile to these methylxanthines. In this
regard, Van Furth et al. (1995) demonstrated that caffeine
inhibited the release of TNF-α in whole blood stimulated with
S. pneumoniae for 48 hr to a similar extent as pentoxifylline or
theophylline. Rosenthal et al. (1992) also observed that
pentoxifylline and caffeine had similar immunosuppressive
effects on murine spleen cell proliferation, antibody production
by murine spleen cells, proliferation and lymphokine (IL-4 and
IL-2) secretion by murine T cell clones, and proliferation of
human T cells. Based on this evidence, further investigations of
the potential of caffeine in inflammatory or autoimmune
conditions are certainly warranted.
7. Conclusion
A number of in vitro and in vivo studies have demon-
strated that caffeine modulates various indices of immune
function. These studies indicate that caffeine, like other
members of the methylxanthine family, is largely anti-
inflammatory in nature. Based on known pharmacokinetics
of caffeine, we have demonstrated that many of these im-
munomodulatory effects occur with levels of caffeine that are
relevant to coffee consumers. However, as it is unknown what
degree of functional reserve exists within the immune system,

important that the effects of caffeine, and its primary metabolite
paraxanthine, are investigated in models of infection, and in
models of autoimmune and inflammatory disorders. Also,
further in vivo human studies, as well as studies comparing
the effects of acute and chronic caffeine treatment, are greatly
warranted. Only then will we be able to determine with certainty
the true impact of the cherished cup of coffee on immune
function and susceptibility to infectious/inflammatory disease.
