Scope: This study intended to clarify whether two coffee brews, a market blend (MB) and a study blend (SB), con-
taining equal amounts of caffeine, but differing in their contents of N-methylpyridinium, trigonelline and
chlorogenic acids, differentially affect blood lipid proﬁles and glucose concentrations as well as blood platelet phos-
phodiesterase and lymphocyte energy charge potential in healthy volunteers.
Methods and results: In this double-blinded, randomized, controlled cross-over intervention study, 84 healthy
normal-weight female and male volunteers consumed 750 mL of medium roast MB and dark roast SB coffee per
day for 4 weeks. Following MB and SB coffee intervention, plasma free fatty acid concentrations equally increased
(p b 0.001) by 140 ± 187 μM and 178 ± 160 μM, respectively. Plasma glucose remained largely unchanged.
Lymphocyte adenosine nucleotide analysis revealed a comparable rise in the energy charge potential, as calculated
from ADP/ATP concentrations, adjusted to total adenosine nucleotides. Blood platelet phosphodiesterase activity
was found decreased to about the same extent (p b 0.001). Levels of HDL-cholesterol, adiponectin, leptin, insulin
and osteopontin were found to some extent differentially inﬂuenced by MB, respectively SB coffee.
Conclusion: The results of this intervention study indicate that MB and SB coffees, although differing in contents of
N-methylpyridinium, trigonelline and chlorogenic acids, largely exert similar biological effects as monitored by
the biomarkers tested.

1. Introduction
Coffee beverages are mostly appreciated for their tasty ﬂavor and
their stimulating effects on the central nervous system. Furthermore,
certain beneﬁcial health effects are discussed that may contribute to
the consumers' choice of coffee. Although it is still a matter of debate
whether the overall effects to human health of regular coffee
consumption are positive or negative, epidemiological as well as
clinical data indicate that moderate coffee consumption is inversely
associated with the risk of certain human cancers (Giri et al., 2011;
Radoi et al., 2013; Sang, Chang, Li, & Jiang, 2013), Parkinson's disease
(Popat et al., 2011) and diabetes mellitus type II (Bhupathiraju et al.,
2013). Epidemiological data also suggest a potential effect of coffee
consumption for promoting body weight maintenance (Greenberg,
Axen, Schnoll, & Boozer, 2005; Lopez-Garcia et al., 2006). Since
body weight maintenance reﬂects energy intake and energy expen-
diture, increased physical activity, enhanced thermogenesis or utili-
zation of endogenous substrate result in a reduction of body weight
at constant energy intake. The coffee constituent most widely stud-
ied for its stimulating effects on energy expenditure is caffeine.
Controlled clinical studies demonstrated an increased resting energy
expenditure in healthy, normal weight or overweight subjects after
consumption of regular coffee or intake of caffeine by means of

seen for caffeine metabolites. Some authors suggest that the main
mechanism of action of caffeine is to antagonize adenosine recep-
tors, especially the A2A subpopulation. Inhibition of intracellular
cyclonucleotide phosphodiesterases (PDEs) and in consequence
enhanced 3′,5′-cAMP levels may also be of relevance, for instance
by enhanced response to catecholamines (Randall, Burggren, &
French, 1997). It is still under debate whether and to what extent
PDE inhibition may contribute to the actions of caffeine. Evidence
is growing, though, that most pharmacological effects of this xanthine
result from adenosine receptor antagonism (Tunnicliffe, Eller, Reimer,
Hittel, & Shearer, 2011).
Despite strong evidence for caffeine being the prime coffee constitu-
ent responsible for increased total energy expenditure, it is still not clear
whether this effect is longer lasting. Previous studies focused primarily
on short-term effects with intervention periods up to 24 h. Moreover,
since coffee contains a multitude of compounds, other coffee constitu-
ents may as well affect energy metabolism. Chlorogenic acids (CGAs),
the most abundant group of polyphenols in coffee, are also supposed
to have an impact on body weight gain. In a controlled human interven-
tion study, a 12-week consumption of a CGA-enriched coffee resulted in
a signiﬁcant body weight reduction in overweight subjects (Thom,
2007). More recently, a cross-over study in pre-obese subjects showed
that a 6-week supplementation of a green coffee extract (rich in CGA)
was effective in reducing body weight and body fat (Vinson, Burnham,
& Nagendran, 2012). CGA is hypothesized to be primarily responsive
for modifying glucose homeostasis. During an oral glucose tolerance
test, concomitant administration of 1 g CGA reduced glucose response
after 15 min, compared to water control (van Dijk et al., 2009). The
authors hypothesized that this effect might have resulted from delayed
intestinal absorption or enhanced transport into peripheral tissues. Fur-
thermore, peritoneal injection of 80 mg CGA per kg body weight daily
for 8 weeks to hamsters on a high fat diet decreased fasting serum levels
of free fatty acids, glucose and insulin, compared to non-CGA-treated
controls (Li, Chang, Ma, & Yu, 2009). In cultured L6 skeletal muscle
cells as model for peripheral tissue, incubation with 2 mM CGA
increased glucose uptake up to 24 h via activation of AMP-activated
protein kinase and translocation of glucose transporter type 4 (Ong,
Hsu, & Tan, 2012). Concentrations of 0.1–100 μM CGA were also report-
ed to stimulate 2-[N-(7-nitrobenz-2-oxa-1,3-diaxol-4-yl)amino]-2-
deoxyglucose uptake in 3T3-L1 adipocytes (Alonso-Castro, Miranda-
Torres, Gonzalez-Chavez, & Salazar-Olivo, 2008). Thus, the above
studies in muscle cells and adipocytes show CGA to increase glucose
uptake in peripheral tissues resulting in an elevated cellular energy
supply. However, an increased cellular glucose uptake does not provide
a mechanism for CGA to promote body weight loss.
In humans, CGA is effectively metabolized resulting in peak plasma
concentrations of CGAs and their conjugates within the nanomolar
range after coffee consumption (Erk et al., 2012; Lang et al., 2013;
Stalmach et al., 2009). However, it is still unclear to which extent CGA
may contribute to the overall effect of coffee on body weight
maintenance. Apart from caffeine and chlorogenic acid, the alka-
loid trigonelline and its degradation product formed upon coffee
roasting, N-methylpyridinium (NMP) (Stadler, Varga, Hau, Vera,
& Welti, 2002), might also have an impact on energy metabolism
in humans and contribute to the overall effect of coffee on body
weight maintenance. For trigonelline, it was shown, that a 500 mg
bolus intervention lowered peak plasma glucose concentrations
compared to water control in healthy subjects 15 min after administra-
tion of 75 g glucose in an oral glucose tolerance test (van Dijk et al.,
2009). Moreover, reduction in body weight in overweight subjects
after a 4-week consumption of dark roasted coffee high in NMP has
been reported (Kotyczka et al., 2011). The molecular mechanisms by
which trigonelline or NMP may help to maintain a healthy body weight
is unknown yet.
Taken together, evidence for beneﬁcial effects of coffee consumption
is suggestive, although it is still unknown whether moderate coffee con-
sumption as part of a regular diet affects resting energy expenditure.
Likewise, mechanisms of thermogenesis or utilization of endogenous
energy substrates like glucose or fatty acids are unknown, as are the cof-
fee constituents that may contribute to these effects. To address the
question whether regular coffee consumption affects mechanisms of
energy metabolism, we conducted a randomized, cross-over interven-
tion study. In this study, healthy volunteers were administered daily
750 mL of two different coffee beverages for 4 weeks. Both coffees
contained comparable amounts of caffeine, but differed in their contents
of CGA, trigonelline and NMP due to their different roast characters.
Accordingly, a CGA- and trigonelline-rich medium roast market blend
coffee and a NMP-rich study blend coffee were studied for effects on
parameters of lipid, glucose and energy metabolism.
2. Material and methods
2.1. Study design and base-line characteristics of volunteers
This randomized, double-blinded, cross-over human intervention
study was approved by the Local Ethics Committee of Rhineland-
Palatinate, no. 837.414.10 (7423). The study design, the base-line char-
acteristics of the 84 volunteers participating in this study and the com-
position of the two coffees administered in this study are explained in
detail in the publication of Bakuradze et al. (2014). In brief, volunteers
who matched the following inclusion criteria were recruited to partici-
pate in the study: age 20–44 years, BMI 19–26 kg/m2, healthy, coffee
drinkers, non-smokers, no intake of pharmaceutical drugs and/or food
supplements, and no pregnancy. In total, 84 volunteers (mean age
25.6 ± 5.8 years, BMI 22.9 ± 1.9 kg/m2) with regular coffee consump-
tion participated in the 20 week cross-over study. Volunteers were
randomly assigned into two groups (group A: n = 43, 24 males, 19
females; group B: n = 41, 22 males, 19 females) with two wash-
out and two coffee intervention periods, respectively, each lasting
4 weeks. During wash-out periods, volunteers consumed 750 mL
water per day. During coffee intervention periods, 750 mL freshly
brewed regular coffee (6 coffee pads at 7.5 g/day), without milk
and ad libitum sugar addition, were taken in three portions each of
250 mL per day. The volunteers were instructed to keep their dietary
habits during the study, except for additional intake of coffee, caf-
feine containing products, dietary supplements and foods rich in
polyphenols, such as dark chocolate and red berries, which should
have been avoided. Daily ﬂuid intake during the study ranged from
1.5 to 2.5 L, which was constant throughout the study and was not
different among the intervention groups (Bakuradze et al., 2014).
The study design was as follows: weeks 1–4 ﬁrst wash-out period,
weeks 5–8 ﬁrst coffee intervention period (group A: market blend
coffee; group B: study blend coffee), weeks 9–12 second wash-out peri-
od, weeks 13–16 second coffee intervention period (group A: study
blend coffee; group B: market blend coffee) and weeks 17–20 third
wash-out period.
Body weight as well as body composition of the study subjects were
assessed at the beginning and the end of each intervention period and
nutrient intake was recalled from 7-day dietary protocols recorded in
the last week of each intervention period (Bakuradze et al., 2014).
2.2. Coffee characterization
Details on the administered coffees and their preparation are pub-
lished in this issue by Bakuradze et al. (2014). Brieﬂy, a medium roast
market blend (MB) coffee and a dark roast study blend (SB) coffee
were administered in one of the two coffee intervention periods. MB

2.3. Blood sample collection
Blood samples were drawn from the study subjects after an over-
night fast from 8 p.m. until 8 a.m. at the beginning and the end of
each intervention period, as described elsewhere (Bakuradze et al.,
2014). Blood was collected in EDTA-coated tubes (Sarstedt, Germany)
and centrifuged at 1800 ×g at 4 °C for 15 min. The resulting plasma
was used for the quantiﬁcation of leptin, adiponectin, osteopontin,
free fatty acids as well as caffeine and its metabolites. For quantitative
glucose analyses, plasma samples were obtained by centrifugation at
1800 ×g for 15 min at 4°C of blood samples withdrawn in ﬂuoride-
and EDTA-coated tubes (Sarstedt, Germany). Insulin, triglycerides,
total cholesterol, HDL-cholesterol and LDL-cholesterol were determined
in serum samples. Blood samples were drawn into EDTA-coated serum
tubes (Sarstedt, Germany) and centrifuged at 2000 ×g at 20 °C for
10 min. All plasma and serum samples were stored in aliquots at
−80 °C until analysis of biochemical parameters. For cAMP and PDE as-
says, platelets were isolated from platelet-rich plasma, divided in two
different tubes, and centrifuged (800 ×g, 15 min, RT). Supernatants
were aspirated and stored for adenosine deaminase (ADA) assays. Pel-
lets were gently re-suspended in appropriate buffer.
2.4. Quantiﬁcation of methylxanthines and 1,7-dimethyluric acid
Methylxanthines and 1,7-dimethyluric acid were quantiﬁed
according to the method published previously by Lang et al. (2013).
2.5. Leptin, adiponectin, glucose, insulin, osteopontin and free fatty acid
assays
Plasma leptin and serum insulin levels were determined using
commercially available sandwich ELISAs (DRG Diagnostics GmbH,
Germany). The ELISA kits for the quantiﬁcation of plasma adiponectin
and osteopontin were purchased from Ray Biotech, Inc., USA. Plasma
glucose was measured photometrically and free fatty acids (FFA) were
quantiﬁed ﬂuorometrically, both by means of commercially available
kits (Cayman Europe, Estonia). All analyses were carried out according
to the manufacturer's protocols. Homeostasis model assessment of in-
sulin resistance (HOMA-IR) was calculated according to the equation
HOMA-IR = fasting insulin [μU/mL] × fasting glucose [mg/dL]/405.
2.6. Triglycerides, total cholesterol, HDL-cholesterol and LDL-cholesterol
Total cholesterol, triglycerides, HDL-cholesterol and LDL-cholesterol
levels were quantiﬁed by commercially available enzymatic assay kits
(Abbott Diagnostics, Germany).
2.7. Leptin receptor expression
Leptin receptor expression was determined in isolate peripheral
blood mononuclear cells. Cell isolation from whole blood and RNA
extraction were conducted as described previously (Volz et al., 2012).
The quantitect reverse transcription kit (QIAGEN, Germany) was used
to obtain cDNA out of 1 μg RNA and quantitative real-time PCR was
performed according to the manufacturer's manuals (Quantitect SYBR
Green PCR and Quantitect Primer Assays, QIAGEN, Germany). The
applied primer assays were Hs_LEPR_1_SG QT00006524 (leptin
receptor), Hs_GAPDH_1_SG QT00079247 (GAPDH), Hs_RPL13A_1_SG
QT00089915 (RPL13a). All samples were determined at least in dupli-
cates and controls without templates were included. PCR reaction pro-
tocol: 15 min at 95 °C, 40 cycles of 15 s at 94 °C, 30 s at 55 °C, and 30 s at
72 °C. Relative transcription was calculated using the 2−ΔΔCt method
with Ct-values normalized on the endogenous control genes GAPDH
(glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate dehydrogenase) and RPL13a (ribosomale
protein L13a).
2.8. Quantiﬁcation of AMP, ADP and ATP
The cellular concentration of AMP, ADP and ATP was quantiﬁed in
isolated lymphocytes. Anti-coagulated EDTA-blood was diluted with
the same amount of PBS and transferred into Ficoll-Paque ﬁlled
Leucosep®-tubes (Greiner Bio-One, Germany). After centrifugation
(1000 ×g, 10 min, RT), the lymphocyte fraction was aspirated and trans-
ferred into a washing medium (RPMI-1640 medium supplemented
with 10% FBS, 2 mM L-glutamine and 1% penicillin/streptomycin). Sam-
ples were re-centrifuged (250 ×g, RT, 10 min), the supernatant was
discarded and the washing process was repeated twice. Immediately
after isolation, ice-cold 0.3 M perchloric acid was added to the lympho-
cytes and cells were re-suspended. The cell lysate was centrifuged
(16,000 ×g, 10 min, 4 °C) and the supernatant was adjusted to pH 6
with sodium carbonate (1 M). Quantiﬁcation of AMP, ADP and ATP in
the lymphocyte extracts was carried out by HPLC–DAD according to a
method described previously (Riedel et al., 2012). The total adenosine
nucleotides (TAN) were calculated from the cellular AMP, ADP
and ATP concentrations by TAN [μmol/L extract] = AMP [μmol/L
extract] + ADP [μmol/L extract] + ATP [μmol/L extract]. Data of AMP,
ADP and ATP are given as a percentage of TAN. To avoid confounding
by high inter-individual variations in lymphocyte counts, AMP, ADP
and ATP concentrations analyzed in the lymphocyte extract of each
study subject were adjusted to the total concentration of adenosine nu-
cleotides. The energy charge potential (ECP) was estimated by ECP =
(ATP [μmol/L extract] + 0.5 ADP [μmol/L extract])/TAN [μmol/L
extract] (Pradet & Raymond, 1983).
2.9. Uncoupling protein-2 assay
Uncoupling protein-2 (UCP-2) concentrations were determined
in lymphocytes isolated as described in the previous section
(Quantiﬁcation of AMP, ADP and ATP). Lymphocytes were re-
suspended in ice-cold PBS and frozen immediately. For analysis, lym-
phocyte extracts were treated with ultrasoniﬁcation, and sonicates
were centrifuged (16,000 ×g, 10 min, 4 °C). Supernatants were used
for the quantiﬁcation of UCP-2 by means of a commercial sandwich
ELISA Kit (USCN Life Sciences, China). UCP-2 contents were normalized
for protein contents of the supernatants applying the bicinchoninic acid
assay using bovine serum albumin as standard (Pierce® BCA Protein
Assay Kit, Thermo Scientiﬁc, Austria).

2.10. Adenosine deaminase activity and adenosine concentrations
Plasma adenosine deaminase (ADA) activity was measured using a
commercially available assay kit (Diazyme Laboratories, USA). Quantiﬁca-
tion of adenosine in blood samples was performed by HPLC–ESI-MS/MS
with slight modiﬁcations of the method described by Dolezalova, Krijt,
Chladek, Nemcova, & Hoza (2005). Blood samples were deproteinized
immediately after collection with an equal volume of 1 M perchloric
acid, to prevent potential enzymatic degradation or production of adeno-
sine. Samples were cooled in ice-water and centrifuged (4000 ×g, 5 min,
4 °C). The clear supernatant was transferred to a test tube and the inter-
nal standard containing 1.6 μM [1′,2′,3′,4′,5′-13C5]-adenosine (Omicron,
USA) was added. The solution was neutralized by the addition of a buffer
solution (2.5 M K3PO4, 1.3 M KOH). After a short centrifugation step
(4000 ×g, 5 s) the potassium perchlorate precipitate was removed
and the supernatant was diluted with distilled water and loaded on a
preconditioned solid phase extraction column (StrataX 33u Polymeric
Reversed Phase, 30 mg/mL, Phenomenex, Germany). After a washing
step with distilled water, adenosine was eluted with methanol. The eluate
was concentrated to a volume of about 100 μL using a vacuum centrifuge
and diluted with 0.02% acetic acid (1:4). Aliquots were analyzed using
HPLC–ESI-MS/MS. The chromatographic separation of adenosine was ac-
complished with an Aqua C18 column (250 × 4.6 mm, 5 μ, Phenomenex,
Germany) using an isocratic solvent system of 0.1% formic acid in water
(v/v) and methanol (85:15; v/v) at a ﬂow rate of 0.5 mL/min.
Analyte and internal standard were detected by means of a triple–
quadrupole tandem mass spectrometer API 3200™(Applied Biosystems,
Germany) with positive electrospray ionization. Adenosine was
quantiﬁed by determining the ratio of the adenosine peak area and
the isotope-labeled standard peak area.
2.11. cAMP concentration and PDE activity in platelets
Intracellular cAMP was determined with the Perkin Elmer's LANCE®
kit (Massachusetts, USA.) following the manufacturer's protocol.
For PDE-activity assays, platelets were re-suspended in Tris buffer
(50 mM Tris/HCl, pH 7.4, 10 mM MgCl2, 0.1 mM EDTA, 5 mM
benzamidine, 0.5 mM β-mercaptoethanol and a protease inhibitor
mix) and lysed in a Wheaton glass tissue grinder (“tight” Pistill, 40
strokes). After centrifugation (100,000 ×g, 4 °C, 40 min) the superna-
tant was removed and directly subjected to the PDE assay. PDE activity
was determined according to a modiﬁed method by Poch (1971). Brief-
ly, the PDE-containing preparations were incubated at 37 °C with the
cAMP[3H] in a buffer solution (30 mM Tris/HCl, pH 7.4, 9 mM MgCl2,
3 mM 5′-AMP and 3 μM cAMP). The incubation period was adjusted
by performing a pre-test applying different incubation times since the
cAMP turnover should not exceed about 20% ensuring steady state
enzyme kinetics. The enzyme reaction was stopped with ZnSO4 and 5′
AMP[3H] was precipitated by adding Ba(OH)2. Tubes were centrifuged
(20,000 ×g, 4 °C, 5 min) and the supernatant containing the non-
hydrolyzed cAMP[3H] subjected to liquid scintillation counting. PDE3
and PDE4 isoenzyme activity were typed by using speciﬁc inhibitors
(10 μM milrinone for PDE3 and 50 μM rolipram for PDE4).
2.12. Statistical analysis
Statistical analyses were carried out using the software R 2.13.1
(GNU General Public License) and SigmaPlot software (Systat Software,
Inc., USA). All statistical hypotheses were tested against two-sided
alternatives at the 5% level of signiﬁcance.
The statistical model that corresponds to the chosen cross-over de-
sign includes effects of group, treatment and time, as well as the interac-
tion of treatment and time and carry-over effect. Details of the statistical
model and the appropriate statistical analysis methods are described by
Jones and Kenward (1988). Prior application of t-tests, the assumption
of normality was veriﬁed by the Shapiro–Wilk test. In case of rejection
of the normality assumption, data were transformed logarithmically. If
normality could not be achieved by the transformation, data were
analyzed by Wilcoxon tests.
In the presence of signiﬁcant interactions or carry over effects, the
data of the second intervention period were excluded from the calcula-
tion of treatment effects. Effectiveness of both coffees was compared by
two sample t-tests (or Wilcoxon's rank sum test). To compare data be-
fore and after consumption of either MB or SB coffee one-sample t-test
(or Wilcoxon's signed rank test) on the basis of intra-individual differ-
ences between measurements after and before coffee consumption
were performed. The statistical model allows the estimation of product
effect differences for the comparison of the two coffee blends, although
the effects of single coffee blends cannot be distinguished from time ef-
fects in the cross-over design. Nevertheless, time effects which can be
calculated between wash-out periods and intervention periods, respec-
tively, may serve as indicators for seasonal inﬂuences.
The number of volunteers was n = 43 in group A and n = 41 in
group B, unless stated otherwise. All biochemical parameters were ana-
lyzed on the basis of the mean of 2–3 technical replicates. Data are either
presented as means ± standard deviation (SD) for normally distributed
data, or otherwise as medians (interquartile range).
Spearman correlation coefﬁcients were calculated based on the
changes of the ﬁrst wash-out period to the ﬁrst intervention period.
Signiﬁcant correlations were determined by means of t-tests.
3. Results
3.1. Plasma concentrations of methylxanthines and 1,7-dimethyluric acid
The results of quantiﬁed methylxanthines and 1,7-dimethyluric acid
in the plasma of healthy volunteers are represented in Table 2. The sta-
tistical analyses include both intervention periods of the measured
methylxanthines, except for caffeine for which a signiﬁcant carry-over
effect was calculated (p b 0.05). Caffeine, theophylline, paraxanthine,
2-methylxanthine, 7-methylxanthine and 1,7-dimethyluric acid were
below the limit of detection in the plasma of all volunteers after the
wash-out periods (Lang et al., 2013). However, theobromine was de-
tectable in plasma samples after the wash-out periods. Following each
of the 4-week intervention with MB and SB coffees, plasma concentra-
tions of all methylxanthines increased as compared to the preceding
wash-out period (p b 0.001).
3.2. Blood lipid proﬁle, plasma adiponectin and leptin concentrations
Plasma free fatty acid concentrations of the study subjects are
shown in Fig. 1A. Due to signiﬁcant interaction effects (p b 0.05), the
second cross-over period was excluded from statistical analyses. During
the ﬁrst intervention period, plasma free fatty acid levels were increased
after the administration of MB coffee and SB coffee in comparison to the
preceding wash-out period by 140 ± 187 μM (p b 0.001) and 178 ±
160 μM (p b 0.001), respectively. No difference in the effectiveness
was observed since both coffee interventions increased free fatty
acid levels to a similar extent. However, as a signiﬁcant time effect
(p b 0.05) within the treatment periods was calculated, the increase
in plasma free fatty acids cannot exclusively be deduced to the ad-
ministration of either MB or SB coffee.
The statistical analysis of triglycerides, HDL-cholesterol and LDL-
cholesterol included data from both intervention periods of the cross-
over study, whereas data from the second intervention period were
excluded for total cholesterol due to a signiﬁcant carry-over effect
(p b 0.05). Triglyceride levels of the volunteers remained unchanged
during both interventions (Fig. 1B) and total cholesterol increased
only after consumption of the MB coffee (Fig. 1C). In contrast, after
the 4-week consumption of both coffees serum HDL-cholesterol
levels were found enhanced when compared to the preceding
wash-out periods (Fig. 1D). Administration of SB coffee increased

3.6. Correlation analyses
Spearman correlation coefﬁcients were calculated between parame-
ters related to energy metabolism and body fat. A signiﬁcant correlation

well as body fat and ECP (ρ = −0.31, p b 0.05) were ascertained.
4. Discussion
This human intervention study investigated the effect of a 4-week
coffee consumption on parameters of lipid, glucose and energy metabo-
lism in healthy subjects. The effects of two regular coffee beverages dif-
fering in their concentrations of chlorogenic acid (CGA), trigonelline
and N-methylpyridinium (NMP) were studied. So far, no controlled
cross-over human intervention study on 4-week effects of coffee con-
sumption with discriminative analysis of two administered coffees has
been conducted. The compositional differences of the coffee beverages
were obtained by either blending ﬁve commercially available regular
ground coffees (market blend) or blending mainly dark roast coffee
with signiﬁcant proportions of light roast coffee (study blend). Both cof-
fees contained comparable amounts of caffeine. Hence, concentrations
of caffeine metabolites (methylxanthines) equally increased in the plas-
ma of the volunteers after both coffee interventions. At the end of the
wash-out periods, however, theobromine was detected in the plasma
of the study subjects, in spite of the caffeine-abstinent diet in the pre-
ceding wash-out period. This result might reﬂect the consumption of
theobromine-containing foods. Study subjects were asked to abstain
from caffeine-containing beverages like cola, tea (black, green and
white), malt coffee and dark chocolate during the study, whereas milk
chocolate was allowed to be consumed. Thus, milk chocolate intake
might explain baseline theobromine concentrations demonstrated
after the wash-out periods. Coffee consumption during the wash-out
periods was excluded since no increase in urine concentrations of
NMP, as speciﬁc indicator of coffee consumption, was seen (Bakuradze
et al., 2014; Lang, Wahl, Stark, & Hofmann, 2011). Following the coffee
intervention periods, not only methylxanthines, but also NMP concen-
trations increased, clearly indicating compliance of the study subjects
(Bakuradze et al., 2014). In the present study, coffee intake induced al-
terations in body composition and nutrient intake as described in detail
by Bakuradze et al. (2014). In brief, body weight was not changed after
4-weeks of MB consumption, but decreased slightly by SB intervention.
No difference between the effectiveness of both coffees was observed. In
contrast, body fat was reduced after the consumption of both coffees
compared to the preceding wash-out periods, however it was more

According to our hypothesis, that a 4-week consumption of regular
coffee promotes body weight maintenance in humans by mobilization
of endogenous energy substrates, plasma glucose and free fatty acid
concentrations in the study subjects were analyzed.
At the end of each intervention period, plasma free fatty acid con-
centrations of overnight-fasted volunteers were elevated compared to
the preceding wash-out period, independent of the type of coffee con-
sumed. This result is in accordance with data on acute effects of coffee
consumption on plasma free fatty acids published by Bellet et al. who
reported that plasma free fatty acids increased by 94% in healthy
volunteers 4 h after the consumption of 500 mL caffeinated coffee
containing 250 mg caffeine whereas free fatty acid concentrations
after the consumption of decaffeinated coffee were not affected
(Bellet, Kershbaum, & Finck, 1968). These observations in combination
with our results support the assumption that caffeine might be one of
the coffee constituents that stimulates the utilization of free fatty
acids as endogenous energy substrates. We suggest that caffeine may
increase glycerol degradation and lipolysis, as reported for 3T3-L1
adipocytes in culture (Murosaki et al., 2007).
We also observed changes in the cholesterol proﬁle of healthy
volunteers after the consumption of the SB and the MB coffee. Serum
total cholesterol and HDL-cholesterol concentrations increased after
the 4-week consumption of both coffees. In contrast, LDL-cholesterol
remained unaffected. We here demonstrate for the ﬁrst time a positive,
enhancing long-term effect of coffee consumption on HDL-cholesterol.
The SB coffee, containing higher amounts of NMP but lower concentra-
tions of CGA and trigonelline, was more effective in increasing serum
HDL-cholesterol than the MB coffee, suggesting that the beneﬁcial effect
on serum lipid proﬁle depends on the coffee constituent proﬁle.
Cheung, Gupta, and Ito (2005) described a short-term increase, 1 h
after coffee consumption (without milk or sugar), in total cholesterol
and HDL-cholesterol in humans. In contrast, Superko, Bortz, Williams,
Albers, and Wood (1991) found no effect on total cholesterol and
HDL-cholesterol after an 8-week coffee consumption period. In that
study, plasma LDL-cholesterol concentrations were raised, but only
after the consumption of decaffeinated coffee. Moreover, a cross-
sectional study demonstrated a positive association between coffee
consumption and total cholesterol as well as LDL-cholesterol, and an in-
verse association with triglycerides by recall of dietary habits over one
month (Yamashita et al., 2012). Taken together, most studies report
an increase in total cholesterol after coffee consumption and suggest
the diterpenes kahweol and cafestol to be the cholesterol-elevating
factors in coffee (Urgert & Katan, 1997). Filtering coffee removes these
diterpenes which may be of relevance for the varying effects observed
in the studies mentioned above (Urgert et al., 1996).
Regarding the impact of coffee consumption on the regulation of
lipid metabolism, the adipocyte-derived hormones adiponectin and
leptin play a central role in glucose and lipid metabolism (Rosen &
Spiegelman, 2006). In the present study, the administration of MB
coffee resulted in higher plasma adiponectin levels compared to the
administration of SB coffee. A positive association between coffee
consumption and adiponectin concentrations has also been reported
in cross-sectional studies (Imatoh et al., 2011; Yamashita et al., 2012).
In an animal model, CGA has been shown to increase adiponectin levels
when administered as a supplement with a high-fat diet (Cho et al.,
2010). Whether the higher contents of CGA of the MB coffee studied
in this work contributed to its more pronounced effect on plasma
adiponectin levels cannot be answered unequivocally. Although an as-
sociation might be possible, plasma concentrations of CGA metabolites
did not differ among the volunteers following MB vs. SB treatments
(Winkler et al., 2014).
Moreover, in the present study the consumption of the MB coffee
elevated leptin concentrations, while the consumption of the SB coffee
did not. Leptin release from the adipose-tissue is known to stimulate
catabolic pathways by activating the leptin receptor in the hypo-
thalamus, especially in the arcuate nucleus, inducing the repres-
sion of orexigenic and concomitantly stimulating anorexigenic
pathways (Rosen & Spiegelman, 2006). In peripheral tissues, adiponectin
and leptin alter the metabolic state by activating the AMP-activated
protein kinase thereby stimulating mitochondrial activity and fatty acid

The parameters of glucose metabolism were hardly affected by a 4-

after the consumption of the MB coffee, while no changes were observed
after SB coffee intake. This suggests that SB coffee may have provided
compounds counteracting the reducing effect on insulin-sensitivity
reported for caffeinated coffee (van Dam, Pasman, & Verhoef, 2004).
However, fasting plasma glucose levels remained unchanged after the
consumption of both coffees. We further estimated plasma concentra-
tions of the pro-inﬂammatory cytokine osteopontin that is also associated
with insulin resistance. Osteopontin concentrations were found to in-
crease in obese humans (Gomez-Ambrosi et al., 2007) and neutralization
of its action led to improved insulin sensitivity in obese mice (Kiefer et al.,
2010). While no investigations have focused on the impact of coffee con-
sumption on osteopontin concentrations in humans, we provide a ﬁrst
hint to coffee-derived modiﬁcations in its plasma levels. While the 4-
week consumption of the MB coffee reduced osteopontin concentrations,
they were elevated after intervention with the dark roast SB coffee sug-
gesting that coffee constituents highly present in one or the other coffee
exert opposite effects on osteopontin concentrations.
Regarding these ﬁndings in healthy fasted humans, coffee consump-
tion promotes the utilization of free fatty acids and not of glucose as
energy substrate. Correlation analyses support this hypothesis, as a
signiﬁcant positive correlation was found between plasma FFAs and
the ATP/TAN ratio, as marker of the cellular energy status. In contrast,
there was no association between glucose and ATP/TAN ratio. More-
over, the negative correlations between body fat and ATP/TAN as well
as body fat and ECP in male volunteers indicate the usage of endogenous
body fat for the maintenance of energy supply.
In comparison to the wash-out periods, in a fasting state, regular
coffee consumption increased cellular energy supply. ECP values rose
in lymphocytes after the consumption of both coffees, and a shift from
ADP/TAN to ATP/TAN ratio was observed. In previously published
short-term studies, caffeinated coffee was shown to stimulate energy
metabolism and energy expenditure compared to decaffeinated coffee
(Acheson et al., 1980; Hollands et al., 1981). Therefore, caffeine is sup-
posed to be one of the active coffee constituents that was also shown
to increase the ECP and ATP levels in HepG2 cells in culture (Riedel
et al., 2012). In the present study, we were able to demonstrate a shift
in the adenosine nucleotide proﬁle in human lymphocytes, which may
be used as a surrogate biomarker for the cellular energy status after
long-term consumption of caffeinated coffee. As both coffees contained
comparable amounts of caffeine, and increased the cellular energy
charge potential to a similar extent, the increase in energy supply
might be attributed primarily to caffeine. The stimulatory effect of caf-
feinated coffee on ATP production might result from the induction of
the cAMP signaling pathway. Methylxanthines, such as caffeine, theo-
bromine and theophylline at concentrations higher than 0.1 mM inhibit
3′,5′-cyclic nucleotide phosphodiesterase which results in elevated
cAMP levels (Butcher & Sutherland, 1962). Enhanced cAMP levels may
mediate a continuous energy demand, with the potential consequence
of stimulated energy producing pathways. We observed a strong inhib-
itory effect of caffeinated coffee on PDE activity in platelets. Additional-
ly, adenosine plasma concentrations were increased after SB coffee
consumption, potentially stimulating cAMP production in platelets.
Since platelet, cAMP levels increased only in the ﬁrst intervention peri-
od, not in the second, signiﬁcant time effects precluded the further
consideration of the second cross-over period, where elevated cAMP
levels were not observed.
In addition, we demonstrated an induction of UCP-2 protein expres-
sion in human lymphocytes after SB coffee consumption. Caffeine is
commonly accepted as the thermogenic agent in coffee (Astrup et al.,
1990). A stimulating effect of caffeine on UCP-2 protein contents
(Riedel et al., 2012) and UCP-2 gene expression (Kogure et al., 2002)
was found in cell culture (1.5 mM caffeine) and animal models (60 mg
caffeine/kg body weight s.c.) after treatment with concentrations above
the physiological range. In healthy subjects, peak plasma concentrations
of 40–50 μM are reached after the consumption of 3–4 cups of coffee
(Verhoef, Pasman, Van Vliet, Urgert, & Katan, 2002). Therefore, we
assume that other coffee constituents may promote uncoupling protein
expression after 4-week consumption of 750 mL coffee in our study.
Recently, it was demonstrated that NMP treatment increased UCP-2
protein expression in HepG2 cells in culture (Riedel et al., 2014). Since
the SB coffee contained higher amounts of NMP than the MB coffee, our
ﬁndings support this in vitro result and suggest a thermogenic action of
NMP-rich coffee in humans.
However, the data interpretation of this study is limited due to some
confounding factors. The cross-over study was designed to compare the
effectiveness of two coffee beverages, differing in their constituent pro-
ﬁle, on biomarkers of energy status. Coffee interventions were lacking a
parallel water control group. Thus, effects caused by individual coffee
brews could only be estimated by comparing data with those of the pre-
ceding wash-out period. Here, time effects within wash-out periods and
within treatment periods as well as interaction and carry-over effects
had to be considered that might have affected the changes observed in
some biomarkers. Moreover, the consumption of hot coffee during in-
tervention periods, instead of cold water as done during wash-out pe-
riods, might have affected outcome measures, since drinking warm or
hot drinks inﬂuences physiological responses (Quinlan, Lane, &
Aspinall, 1997). In addition, adenosine was quantiﬁed only in the plas-
ma of a subpopulation.
In conclusion, 4-week coffee consumption was shown to alter indi-
cators of energy metabolism in healthy normal-weight subjects. Coffee
consumption increased free fatty acid utilization and reduced body
fat content (Bakuradze et al., 2014). An increased lymphocyte energy
charge potential, possibly resulting, at least in part, from reduced PDE
activity might have been resulted in an augmented free fatty acid
oxidation. Since both coffee beverages largely evoked similar effects,
caffeine is hypothesized to play a pivotal role for those coffee-induced
changes in energy metabolism that promote body weight maintenance.
However, some biochemical parameters, e.g. HDL-cholesterol, leptin,
adiponectin, insulin and osteopontin, were differentially affected
by MB and SB coffee consumption. Therefore, we suggest to likewise
consider further coffee constituents when evaluating health-beneﬁcial
effects of coffee.
