The major risk factors and processing steps that can
lead to contamination of green co ee with ochratoxin A
(OTA) have been identi®ed. Surveys of the green co ee
production chain indicate that Aspergillus ochraceus
and A. carbonarius are the most potent OTA producers
on co ee. Both have been successfully grown in vitro on
green co ee and co ee cherries, respectively, producing
high amounts of OTA (5±13 mg kg 1). The so-called
dry processing of co ee, which is cherry drying, was
identi®ed as one of the steps during which OTA
formation can take place, particularly under humid
tropical conditions. Cherries contain su
cient amounts

during the initial 3±5 days of drying on the outer part of
the cherries. Not surprisingly, after dehulling, husks
can be highly contaminated with OTA, as also indicated
by its enhanced concentration in soluble co ees adult-

activity of 0.80 (about 14% MC) is required for in
vitro OTA production on green co ee, a fact that does
not rule out the possibility of OTA contamination due
to improper transportation and storage of green co ee.
However, this appears not to be a major route for OTA
contamination
of
co ee. OTA
contamination can
clearly be minimized by following good agricultural
practice and a subsequent post-harvest handling con-
sisting of appropriate techniques for drying, grading,
transportation and storage of green co ee; these pro-
cedures are well established.
Introduction
Ochratoxin A (OTA), which is essentially considered
a nephrotoxic mycotoxin (it is also carcinogenic,
teratogenic and immunosuppressive) (WHO 2001),
is found in many plant raw materials and food prod-
ucts (Pohland et al. 1992). Most commonly it is found
in cereals, but it is also found in a wide range of other
commodities and products including beer (Guldborg
1997), cocoa and cocoa products (Matissek and
Raters 2000), co ee (van der Stegen et al. 1997), ®gs
(Steiner et al. 1993), chilli peppers (Thirumala et al.
2000), liquorice (Majerus et al. 2000), grape juice
(Majerus et al. 2001), dried vine fruit (MacDonald
et al. 1999), and wine (Otteneder and Majerus 2000).
The European Community (EC) prohibits the sale of
foodstu s with excessive amounts of contaminants
from the public health viewpoint, and in particular at
a toxicological level (EC 1999). Since the European
Commission’s Scienti®c Committee for Food (SCF)
has been considering OTA to be such a contaminant
(European Commission SCF, Working Group on
Contaminants 1995), EC Member States like the
UK (MAFF 1996, Patel et al. 1997) and Germany
(Bresch et al. 2000) have undertaken surveys on the
presence of OTA in co ee products. Other states have
introduced national limits on green co ee (Italy,
8 mg kg 1; Finland, 10 mg kg 1; Greece, 20 mg kg 1)
or co ee products (Switzerland, 5 mg kg
1). With a
view to legal harmonization, the EC has set limits to
control OTA concentrations in cereals, cereal prod-
ucts and dried raisins at the beginning of October

Fungi capable of OTA production
Ochratoxin A derives its name from A. ochraceus, the
mould from which it was ®rst isolated (van der Merwe
et al. 1965). In A. section Circumdati, Ciegler (1972)
reported six species of ochratoxin producers: A.
ochraceus, A. alliaceus, A. sclerotiorum, A. ostianus,
A. melleus and A. sulphureus. Hesseltine et al. (1972)
reported the same species plus A. petrakii. Varga et al.
(1996) reported for the ®rst time the production of
OTA by A. albertensis and A. auricomus. Other
Aspergillus sections have been reported to produce
OTA such as A. fumigatus (section Fumigati) and A.
versicolor (section Versicolores) although only on
natural substrate such as moistened corn. In yeast
extract sucrose (YES) broth its production was not
detected (Abarca et al. 1997).
The production of OTA by members of section Nigri
has received considerable attention since the ®rst
description of OTA production by A. niger var. niger
(Abarca et al. 1994). Horie (1995) ®rst reported the
production of OTA by A. carbonarius.
OTA is also produced by the genus Penicillium. It was
®rst described as a metabolite of P. viridicatum and
some other Penicillium spp. However, because of mis-
identi®ed fungal strains and changes in Penicillium
taxonomy, it is now widely accepted that P. verruco-
sum is the only known and con®rmed Penicillium spp.
that can produce OTA (Pitt 1987).
Growth conditions of A. ochraceus, A. carbonarius,
A. niger and P. verrucosum
A. ochraceus grows between 8 and 37°C, with an
optimum at 24±31°C. Optimal conditions for growth
are reached at a water activity (aw) of 0.95±0.99, while
the lower limit for growth is 0.79 on media containing
sugars, and 0.81 on media based on NaCl (Pitt and
Hocking 1997). A. ochraceus is widespread in foods,
but not common.
A. niger grows in the range 6±47°C with an optimum
at 35±37°C and is a xerophile with germination
reported at aw 0.77 at 35°C (Pitt and Hocking
1997). A. niger is very common in warmer climates,
both in ®elds and in stored foods. The black spores
apparently give protection from sunlight and UV

A. carbonarius and A. niger capable of producing
OTA have been found on co ee by several authors
(Nakajima et al. 1997, TeÂ ren et al. 1997, Taniwaki
et al. 1999b, Joosten et al. 2001, Urbano et al. 2001).
A. niger had been frequently found on OTA-contami-

Joosten et al. (2001) demonstrated that it was most
likely A. carbonarius causing OTA formation during
co ee cherry drying. These ®ndings led to the con-
sensus that the three aspergilli: A. ochraceus, A. niger
and A. carbonarius are responsible for OTA on co ee
(Taniwaki et al. 1999b, Frank 2001). Pitt et al. (2001)
reported a survey on the distribution of these three

cing regions of Brazil (table 1). The most common
species found was A. niger (550 isolates), but only 3%
of the isolates produced OTA. A. ochraceus (270
isolates) was also commonly found, with 75% of the
isolates capable of OTA production, and a very high
presence in beans from the drying yard or in storage.
A. carbonarius (54 isolates) was found during the
same stages of co ee processing, but only in a very
hot region, with 77% of the isolates capable of
producing OTA.

Sun drying of co ee cherries
In 1996, a small survey on nine farms in the south of
Thailand revealed an extremely high incidence of
OTA in dried co ee cherries, with up to 1206 mg kg 1
OTA detected in the husks of one farm. This result
initiated a series of experiments aimed at determining
the fate of OTA under di erent conditions and steps
of post-harvest processing (Bucheli et al. 2000, 2001).
For 4 consecutive seasons (1996±99), OTA was con-
sistently found to be formed during sun-drying ex-
periments in the pulp and parchment fraction (husks)
of drying co ee cherries (table 3). In replicated drying
experiments, covering the main 2-month harvesting
period, dried green co ee was shown to contain on
average OTA concentrations approximately 100 times
lower than those found in husks. How could such a
strong accumulation of OTA in the husks be ex-
plained? At the beginning of drying, cherries contain
plenty of water (59±63%), with an easily accessible
carbon source in the form of free sugars. That makes
them, together with the ambient humid drying envir-
onment in the south of Thailand (26±27°C, 77±82%
RH), an ideal substrate for the development of
moulds. In most of the drying experiments, OTA
contamination occurred within 5 days of drying,
and often continued to increase until the end of 15
or 20 days of drying (table 3). That such conditions
are very favourable to the formation of signi®cant
amounts of OTA was recently shown also under
laboratory conditions with ®-irradiated co ee cherries
(Joosten et al. 2001). In addition, re-wetting experi-
ments between 5 and 10 days of drying promoted a
strong OTA formation in cherries, underlining the
importance of occasional rain interfering with correct
drying. OTA contamination of green co ee depended
on cherry maturity, with green cherries being the least
and overripe cherries the most susceptible. Defects,

Wet-processing of co ee cherries
Published data on the impact of wet-processing on the
presence of OTA are scarce. However, indirect evi-
dence from a survey (MAFF 1996) indicates that the
depulping process must reduce signi®cantly the risk of
OTA contamination during the subsequent ferment-
ation and drying steps. Wet-processing appears less
susceptible to infection by Aspergillus spp. and OTA
contamination (Frank 1999). Since the fruit pulp is an
excellent substrate for the growth of OTA-producing
A. carbonarius strains (Joosten et al. 2001), its re-
moval eliminates a very suitable substrate. In the
above-mentioned survey of 291 samples of green
Arabica and Robusta beans, all imported to the
UK, 73% of Robusta, but only 20% of Arabica,
beans were contaminated with OTA. Robusta being
entirely dry-processed, these data point to the crucial
role the drying process of cherries could play in the
high incidence of OTA contamination. However, the
initial quality of harvested co ee cherries, the pres-
ence of OTA-producing moulds, and the local pro-
cessing conditions will certainly also contribute to
whether OTA formation can occur during wet-pro-
cessing.

Recommendations for OTA reduction in co ee
Co ee beans go through basically four stages from
their origin on the co ee tree to their use in co ee
manufacturing: cherry maturation, cherry processing,
transport and storage of green co ee. The available
data on the distribution of OTA-producing fungi and
the presence of OTA at these stages of the green
co ee production chain (Bucheli et al. 1998, 2000,
Frank 1999, Taniwaki et al. 1999b) indicate that OTA
is likely to be formed between the time of co ee
cherry harvest and its arrival in the factory (table
4). High moisture content (>20%) represents a par-
ticular risk of growth of OTA-producing moulds. The
following critical points of risk have been identi®ed
along the green co ee production chain.
Cherry maturation
Co ee cherry maturation takes 8±10 months, during
which time the co ee bean is securely developing
within the seed coat, called the parchment. With a
normal use of water, fertilizers and maintenance,
good-quality co ee cherries can be produced that
are mostly free of OTA despite the presence of
OTA-producing moulds.
Cherry processing
Only ripe cherries should be harvested and further
processed. Overripe, fermented and damaged cherries
or those fallen onto the soil should be discarded from
the process because they are likely to have increased
levels of OTA and might harbour OTA-producing
moulds which could rapidly be propagated in the
drying yard. This is a very crucial step for the dry
processing of Robusta, and of Arabica in some
producing countries. Cherries should not be stored
in plastic bags before drying because the practice can
induce fermentation and mould growth, making the
co ee organoleptically unacceptable. Cherry drying
has to be carried out in a clean place, and any re-
wetting by rain must be avoided at any time during
the procedure. The dehulling process should be per-
formed under proper conditions.
Co ee grading
Incentives for the reduction of defects at purchasing
will reduce the OTA load and carryover of OTA-
producing moulds into transport and storage facil-
ities. Existing data indicate that the better the quality,
the lesser the risk for OTA contamination. The recent
®nding in a survey on co ee products that one of the
leading German co ee manufacturers was consis-
tently producing co ee without detectable OTA in-
dicates clearly the importance of green co ee quality
(Bresch et al. 2000).
Transport
Only well-dried green co ee (10±12% MC) should be
used for transportation overseas. This will reduce the
risk of condensation at the top of the containers, thus
avoiding mould spoilage during transportation.
Storage
Successful storage of green or parchment co ee can
be achieved provided the initial quality of beans is
good, beans are well dried to 10±12% MC and the
storage atmosphere is at 50±70% RH to prevent
excessive moisture uptake (Stirling 1980). Maintain-
