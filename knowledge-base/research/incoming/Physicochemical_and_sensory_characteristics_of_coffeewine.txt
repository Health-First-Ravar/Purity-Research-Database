Abstract. Wine coffee is coffee products which is produced through a natural coffee processing 
process which is modified with a longer fermentation process. Currently, the coffee wine 
processing in the Gayo highlands of Aceh is very popular because it has a fairly high selling 
price, reaching two to three times higher than regular coffee. This research aims to analyze the 
physicochemical and sensory properties of three types of wine coffees consisting of soft, hard 
and black wine coffee which are distinguished by their process based on the length of 
fermentation. Wine coffee with longer fermentation time has a darker color, sour smell and lower 
pH. The colour of wine coffee is range from yellowish to brownish. These wine coffees has water 
content ranges from 10.33-11.67% (meets the standard) and a slightly sour to sour smell with a 
pH range of 4.65-4.87. The wine coffee flavor scale ranges from 6.5 (good)-7.75 (very good) 
with a cupping score below the specialty coffee score. The fermented taste attributes detected in 
wine coffee were winey, overripe, vinegar, citrusy and sourly. The winey character that is often 
associated with wine coffee is found with moderate to high intensity. In particular, overripe, 
vinegar and sourly characters have a higher intensity in soft and strong wine coffee than black 
wine coffee. 
 
1. Introduction 
Coffee is one of potential export raw materials in world trade. Indonesia is the fourth largest coffee 
exporting country in the world beside Brazil, Vietnam, and Colombia [1]. Aceh is one of provinces that 
contributes to the large amount of coffee production in Indonesia, especially Arabica coffee which is 
spread in three regency, namely Aceh Tengah, Bener Meriah and Gayo Lues districts. Especially in 
Bener Meriah Regency, coffee plantations reach 45,316.15 ha [2]. Since the 2000s, Gayo Arabica coffee 
has become one of the best coffee in the world. 
The coffee processing method generally consists of three types, namely wet processing, dry 
processing and semi-wet processing (a combination of wet and dry methods). The difference between 
these methods lies on the process of peeling the skin of the coffee fruit and the carried out fermentation 
process. This fermentation process is intended to form the distinctive flavor elements of coffee [3]. 
Arabica coffee processing carried out by farmers in the Gayo Highlands, including in Bener Meriah 
Regency, is quite diverse. Most producers do coffee processing in a semi-wet process and also do natural 
processing (dry) according to consumer demand. In addition, producers try to keep up with market

fully washed coffee, honey coffee (natural honey), farming civet coffee and natural wine coffee [4]. The

product in Indonesia is called wine coffee [5,6]. 
Wine coffee is a coffee product that is produced through a natural coffee processing process which 
is modified with a longer fermentation process than regular coffee [7]. Literally this coffee is not coffee 
made by adding or soaking coffee beans in real wine as studied by Arai [8]. Currently, the processing of 
coffee wine in the Gayo Highlands of Aceh is very popular because it has a fairly high selling price, 
reaching two to three times compare to ordinary coffee. This condition occurs due to the relatively 
complex manufacturing process with selected coffee raw materials, special fermentation methods, and 
the relatively long process resulting in a very special coffee taste. However, so far, wine coffee does not 
have quality standards. Therefore, as a first step, it is necessary to characterize commercial wine coffee 
both physicochemical and sensory properties. 
“JR Coffee” is one of the main wine coffee producers in Bener Meriah Regency which produces 
wine coffee continuously. There are 3 types of wine coffee commercialized by this producer, namely 
soft, hard and black wine coffee. These three types wine coffee are produced from the similar wine 
coffee processing method but with different fermentation times: 14-15 days (soft), 25 days (strong), and 
more than 30 days (black) [8]. The aim of this study was to analyze the quality of the three types of wine 
coffee, both physicochemically and sensory. 
 
2. Materials and methods 
 
2.1. Samples Collection 
Three types of wine coffee samples (soft, strong and black wine coffee) were obtained from the wine 
coffee producer “JR Coffee” which is located at Pondok Gajah Village, Bener Meriah Regency, Aceh- 
Indonesia. Coffee wine processing is done by putting freshly harvested coffee cherries into airtight 
plastic and allowed to ferment naturally. The fermentation process is carried out for 15 days (soft coffee 
wine), 25 days (strong coffee wine) and more than 30 days (black coffee wine). Furthermore, the 
fermented coffee cherries are repeatedly dried in the sun to dry (blackish brown). These dried coffee 
cherries are ground with a huller to separate the skin and coffee beans. The resulting coffee beans are 
taken for each sample as much as 2 kg for analysis, put into a plastic bag that has been labeled. 
 
2.2. Physicochemical analysis of wine coffee 
Wine coffee beans were analyzed based on several parameters of SNI 01-2907-2008 [9] including color, 
odor, bean size and moisture content. In addition, additional analysis was carried out in the form of pH 
analysis using a pH meter. 
 
2.3. Sensory analysis of wine coffee 
The sensory assessment of brewed wine coffee refers to the Specialty Coffee Association of America 
(SCAA standard) [10]. The sensory attributes assessed include fragrance, flavor, body, acidity, 
aftertaste, sweetness, balance and overall. The sensory scores were divided into four groups: 6.00–6.75 
= good; 7.00–7.75 = very good; 8.00–8.75 = excellent; 9.00–9.75 = outstanding. In relation to the 
fermentation process, special observation was made on the intensity of the sour/fermented aroma 
including winey, overripe, vinegar, citrusy and sourly.

The difference in the color of wine coffee beans with regular Arabica coffee is thought due to the 
processing process that involves a long fermentation. During the fermentation process, heat is generated 
from the oxidation of sugar components in the mucilage spread over the surface of the coffee skin. These 
sugar components will be utilized by microbes as a growth medium which causes the breaking down of 
the mucilage compounds so that they become more watery [13]. This dilution of mucilage causes the 
coffee cherries to be submerged more, thereby lowering the brightness level. In addition, an oxidation 
process occurs which results in a change in the color of the beans and if this process occurs continuously 
there will be a decrease in the brightness level and as the result the color of coffee beans will be darker 
[14]. This condition affected the color of black and hard wine coffee to be darker than soft wine coffee. 
The difference duration of fermentation process of wine coffee is thought to also affect the smell of 
the wine coffee produced. Wine coffee tends to smell sour. According to SNI 2907-2008 [9], green beans 
must be free from foul and mold odors. Based on the results of this study, wine coffee samples have a 
slightly sour to sour smell. This odor comes from the metabolites produced in the wine coffee 
fermentation process. It is well-known that the fermentation process in coffee beans occurs due to the 
help of enzymes and microorganisms that are naturally found in coffee beans and the microorganisms 
around them [15] producing a number of organic acids such as lactic acid, acetic acid, butyric acid and 
other carboxylic acids [16]. The longer the fermentation, the more acid is produced. This causes the sour 
smell to get stronger and the pH to decrease. In this study, it was proved that wine coffee that was 
fermented longer (hard coffee wine) had a lower pH of 4.65 than hard and soft wine coffee, which were 
4.76 and 4.87, respectively. The length of fermentation in general causes the pH value of wine coffee 
lower than the pH of Gayo Arabica coffee beans which are generally above 5 [17]. 
Moisture content is the important factors related to the storage of coffee beans. Water content can 
affect the quality of coffee, related to the shelf life to prevent discoloration, the growth of fungi and other 
microorganisms. Products with low moisture content are more resistant to damage by microorganisms 
during storage. According to SNI 01-2907-2008, the maximum water content in coffee beans is 12.5% 
and according to SCAA is 12%. The water content of the three types of wine coffee in this study ranged 
from 10.33-11.67%, was meeting these standards. This result is slightly higher than the water content of 
wine coffee from Central Aceh studied by Dairobbi et al [18] ranging from 7.7 to 9.8%.

3.2 Bean size 
In the coffee trade, coffee quality can be related to bean size [26]. Bean size is an important factor in 
determining the quality of green beans that can affect the selling value. Larger coffee beans tend to get 
relatively higher price. In addition, large and uniform beans will produce quality uniformity in roasting 
results and do not burn quickly [20]. The measurement results of wine coffee beans can be seen in Table

Table 2 shows that the percentage of coffee beans retained with a 6.5 mm diameter sieve was more 
dominant. However, the size of the wine coffee beans are all classified into medium size. Based on the 
quality standard set by SNI 1-2907-2008 [9], large green beans are green beans that are retained on a 6.5 
mm diameter sieve (sieve number 16) with a maximum pass requirement of 5%. Medium size green 
beans are green beans that survive on a 6 mm diameter sieve (sieve number 15) with a maximum amount 
of 5% but pass through a 6.5 mm sieve. In this study, green beans that were able to pass through a 6.5 
mm diameter sieve ranged from 26.5%-31.9%, this value exceeded the maximum limit of 5%, so that 
wine coffee green beans were classified as having medium-sized beans. These results are in accordance 
with the research of Abubakar et al (2022) [21] which states that the green beans size of Gayo Arabica 
coffee is medium. The size of the green beans is mostly influenced by the coffee variety compared to 
the processing method. The size of green beans will affect weight loss during the roasting process, total 
acidity, pH, and body but does not affect optical density, juice content, flavor, odor and level of 
preference [22]. 
 
3.3 Sensory Profile 
The wine coffee that was evaluated for sensory characteristics was roasted wine coffee that had been 
brewed and tested by the cupping test method referring to the SCAA [10]. The parameters tested were 
fragrance (smell of dry ground coffee), aroma (good smell), flavor (typical smell of coffee), body 
(thickness), acidity (sour taste), bitterness (bitter taste), and sweetness (sweet taste). In addition, the taste 
balance, taste cleanliness (clean cup), uniformity of taste (uniformity) and overall aspects of all 
parameters (overall) were also assessed. The sensory profile of wine coffee can be seen in Figure 1.

Based on Figure 1, it can be described that the three types of wine coffee as a whole have similar 
sensory characteristics. However, it can be seen that strong wine coffee has slightly lower aroma, 
aftertaste and overall than other wine coffee. The rating scale for aroma/fragrance is 7-7.5 (very good), 
flavor 7 (very good), aftertaste 6.5-6.75 (good), acidity 6.5 (good), body 7.75 (very good), balance 6.5 
(good), overall 6.5-6.75 (good). The sweetness and uniformity score scale reached 10, but the clean cup 
score was very low (0). This result certainly gave a low cupping score overall, which ranged from 67.50 
- 69.25 and of course did not reach a specialty score (above 80), even though it is well-known that regular 
Gayo Arabica coffee from the Gayo highlands has a perfect value (10) for these three attributes [4]. The 
wine coffee processing process with a long fermentation time is thought to affect the appearance of the 
cleanliness of the taste characters that appear which it contributes to the low value of the clean cup. It is 
suspected that excessive fermentation can result in the formation of propionic acid or butyric acid which 
is responsible for poor coffee taste [23]. Therefore, these unwanted chemical compounds can cause taint 
(flavor defects) in the coffee taste which can reduce the total cupping test score [24]. 
Clean cup refers to the taste of coffee that is free from defects (assess the level of cleanliness of the 
coffee flavor character). This does not mean sanitary hygiene or unclean coffee but refers to taste, 
specifically the absence of other annoying flavors. Based on the perspective of the Specialty Coffee 
Association (SCA), several determinants of specialty status are the attributes of sweetness, uniformity 
and clean cup. If the coffee is judged to be unsweetened, non-uniform, or has a low score for clean cup, 
the total score will be low and the market value or selling price will also be low [25]. The interesting 
thing is that although cupping the total score of this wine coffee is lower, it turns out that the survey 
results of Muzaifa et al [6] show that the selling price of coffee wine is still higher than the price of 
regular Gayo coffee and can even reach two times. 
In connection with the wine coffee taste, a special flavor evaluation of wine coffee was also carried 
out on the fermented attributes that appeared, referring to the SCAA coffee flavor wheel [10]. The 
sour/fermented taste of coffee wine and its intensity was detected with can be seen in Figure 2.

Figure 2 shows that the sour/fermented flavors detected in wine coffee are winey, overripe, vinegar, 
citrusy and sour with varying intensity, from low to high. Winey, overripe, vinegar and sourly characters 
were detected in all samples. In particular, overripe, vinegar and sourly characters have a higher intensity 
in soft and strong wine coffees than black wine coffee. This difference is thought to be due to the loss 
of several volatile components to the air due to excessive fermentation and lasts too long causing the 
aroma to decrease [26]. In this study, it turned out that the winey flavor was indeed detected in all 
samples of wine coffee with moderate to high intensity. Winey is a term to describe the sharp, aromatic 
sensory attributes of coffee such as fruit alcohol associated with red wine [6]. The presence of alcohol 
aroma indicates the presence of alcohol compounds formed due to the work of microorganisms during 
wine coffee fermentation, especially yeast, in addition to the work of bacteria that contribute to the 
formation of certain organic acids. More than 50 species of yeast and bacteria are present during coffee 
fermentation, contributing to the production of ethanol, lactic, butyric, acetic, and other high carboxylic 
acids during fermentation [15]. In particular, this fermented metabolite in wine coffee has never been 
done. It is hoped that further research will carry out more complex metabolite and sensory analyzes so 
that the overall taste of wine coffee can be understood. 
 
 
4. Conclusions 
“JR Coffee” produces three types of wine coffee, consisting of soft, hard and black wine coffees, which 
differ in their process based on the length of fermentation. Wine coffee that is fermented longer has a 
darker color, sour smell and lower pH. The color of the commercial wine coffee is varied from yellowish 
to brownish. The water content of wine coffee meets the standard and the odor is a slightly sour to sour 
smell. The wine coffee cupping score is below the specialty coffee with the fermented flavors detected 
in wine coffee are winey, overripe, vinegar, citrusy and sourly. The winey character that is often 
associated with wine coffee is found from moderate to high intensity and detected in all samples.
