Introduction
Diets rich in antioxidant compounds have been associated 
with benefits to human health, including protection against 
chronic degenerative diseases [1]. Plant foods are major 
sources of antioxidants in diet [2, 3]. It is suggested that 
the antioxidant compounds in foods with different chemical 
characteristics act synergistically integrated to protect the 
human body against oxidative damage [4]. The antioxidant 
capacity (AC) is a comprehensive evaluation of this com-
bination of individual antioxidants that occurs naturally in 
foods [3]. Several assays have been developed to evalu-
ate AC in foods. These assays are based on the two main 
mechanisms of antioxidant action, proton transfer (HAT—
hydrogen atom transfer) and electron transfer (SET—
single electron transfer). Because different antioxidant

Purpose  The aim of the present study was to evaluate 
the relative contribution of the most commonly consumed 
plant foods in Brazil to the total antioxidant capacity (AC) 
of Brazilian’s diet. The importance of regional consuming 
habits and income for dietary AC was also approached.
Methods  The annual per capita consumption database 
from the Brazilian Institute of Geography and Statistics 
(IBGE) was used for identification of the most consumed 
plant foods in Brazil. Out of 124 key plant foods, 42 top 
AC contributing candidates were selected for AC deter-
mination based on both the frequency of consumption, 
and AC results reported in the literature, and in our pre-
liminary assays. The selected food products were prepared 
according to the Brazilian Food Guide, and their AC was 
measured by TEAC and FRAP assays. Dietary AC was 
determined by combining these AC results with IBGE con-
sumption data, and the relative contribution of each plant 
food was calculated.

and green maté tea presented the highest AC, followed 
by toasted maté tea, red wine, açaí—a native Amazo-

In order to assess the importance of AC intake, both the 
frequency of consumption and the amount consumed have 
to be considered. Therefore, the concept of dietary AC has 
been created to consider these aspects and can be defined as 
the total AC from plant foods and beverages (alcoholic and 
non-alcoholic) consumed daily in a diet [6]. Even though 
the bioavailability and effectiveness of antioxidant com-
pounds in the human body have to be considered when esti-
mating their bioactive potential, the in vitro assessment of 
dietary AC through plant foods consumption is considered 
as an important tool to evaluate the association between the 
antioxidant intake and possible health benefits reported in 
epidemiological studies [7].
Studies evaluating the contribution of foods to dietary 
AC (undertaken in Norway [3], Italy [6], Spain [7], Japan 
[8, 9] and Czech Republic [10]) or estimating the intake of 
polyphenols in foods (undertaken in Poland [11] and [12] 
France) have elected coffee, wine, Camelia sinensis teas 
and cocoa products as major contributors to antioxidants 
intake. Brazil is currently the seventh world’s largest econ-
omy, and also the fifth largest country, with 8,514,877 km2 
of territory extension inhabited by 192 million people [13], 
but to date, there are no estimates on Brazilian dietary AC. 
Due to the country’s vast extension, the Brazilian tradi-
tional diet varies considerably according to geographic 
regions. However, the country as a whole is the second 
largest coffee consumer (preceded by USA) [14]. C. sin-
ensis tea and wine are not as consumed as in Europe, being 
replaced by typical beverages such as Ilex paraguariensis 
tea (maté tea), especially in the South, and cachaça—a 
distilled alcoholic beverage obtained from fermentation of 
sugarcane, rice and different types of beans that are major 
ingredients of large meals in almost all regions of the coun-
try, although corn and derivatives are also considerably 
consumed, especially in the Northeast and/or by lower-
income groups. Despite the large production of fruits and 
vegetables, the contemporary lifestyle has decreased the 
consumption of these food groups, especially in the most 
developed regions, being replaced by processed juices and 
ready-to-eat foods. However, the consumption of native 
Amazonian fruits, especially açaí, a small red–purple fruit 
that grows in Euterpe oleracea palm tree, is high in the 
North region and has been increasing in almost all regions 
of the country due to its exotic taste as well as to the dis-
semination of the knowledge of its health properties [13].
Considering the aforementioned singularities in the Bra-
zilian diet, the purpose of this study was to evaluate the rel-
ative contribution of the most commonly consumed plant 
foods in Brazil to the AC of the population’s diet, focusing 
not only on the country as a whole, but also on its geo-
graphic regions. The impact of household income was also 
investigated.
Materials and methods
Selection of food products and sampling
Because of the classical variation in the chemical compo-
sition and AC values of plant foods according to genetics 
(e.g., species and variety), soil, climate, post-harvesting 
methods, processing and so forth, in order to evaluate the 
relative AC contribution of different plant foods to the Bra-
zilian diet, foods grown in Brazil as well as typical Brazil-
ian methods of preparation were used.
For plant foods selection, the dietary intake data pre-
sented in the Brazilian national survey (confidence level 
95 %), performed by the Brazilian Institute of Geography 
and Statistics (IBGE), were used [13]. Estimates based 
on national dietary intake data have been used in similar 
studies [3, 6, 7]. Data were acquired on the basis of inter-
views performed in 56,000 households in urban and rural 
areas throughout the 27 federal units of the country. For 
data validation, 13,569 of these interviews included 24-h 
dietary recalls for two non-consecutive days, consider-
ing all foods consumed at home or elsewhere, as well as 
purchasing data. Estimates of the average amount of foods 
consumption are presented in the database for total popula-
tion, totaling 124 key plant foods. Data are also presented 
and divided by geographic region and income class, among 
other subsections. In the present study, foods were selected 
for antioxidant evaluation considering both the frequency 
of consumption and antioxidant capacity reported in the lit-
erature (6, 7, 15) or on results from preliminary AC assays,

AC measurements in Brazilian foods
Considering the different environmental conditions that 
characterize the major regions of Brazil, as well as the 
availability of different cultivars of fruits and vegetables, 
three samples of each evaluated food item, including the 
cultivars or brands most commonly consumed in Brazil, 
were acquired from different regions of the country. Fruits 
and vegetables were kindly donated by the “Supply Center 
of Rio de Janeiro” (CEASA) that commercializes fruits and 
vegetables from the three main producing regions of Bra-
zil: Northeast, Southeast and South. Other products were 
acquired in supermarkets. The annual and daily per capita 
intakes of the plant foods and beverages selected from Bra-
zilian diet for antioxidant analysis are also listed in Table 1 
[13].
Food products were prepared (raw or cooked) as com-
monly consumed by Brazilians, according to the Brazilian 
Food Guide [16], except for wheat flour alone, which was 
evaluated in the raw form, since it may be consumed in dif-
ferent ways not specified by the national survey database. 
Cooked foods were prepared using conventional methods 
(boiling, steaming or boiling under pressure). For the prep-
aration of coffee and maté tea drinks at 10 % (v/v), 100 mL 
of hot water (90–95ºC) was percolated through 10  g of 
ground roasted coffee and filtered in paper filter (Whatman 
No. 1). For maté preparation, dried green leaves or toasted 
leaves were soaked in water at 80 °C for 5 min and filtered 
in paper filter. Solid and semisolid food samples were 
mixed and homogenized under nitrogen in a high-speed 
blender (T-25 digital ULTRA-TURRAX, IKA, Germany) 
and kept frozen in nitrogen atmosphere at −80 °C, for up to 
24 h, until extraction and analysis. Beverages were directly 
filtered and analyzed.

Antioxidant capacity measurement
The AC was measured by two different in  vitro assays: 
Trolox equivalent antioxidant capacity—TEAC [17]—and 
ferric reducing antioxidant power—FRAP [18]. Results 
from TEAC assay were expressed in mmol Trolox/L of 
drink or mmol Trolox/kg of solid food. Results from FRAP 
assay were expressed in mmols Fe2+/L of drink or mmols 
Fe2+/kg of solid food. The absorption spectra of diluted 
beverages and food extracts were obtained by spectropho-
tometer UV-1800 (Shimadzu, Japan), in the region between 
593 and 734 nm.
Estimation of dietary antioxidant capacity
The estimation of % contribution to dietary AC was per-
formed by multiplying the AC values by the annual per 
capita consumption values from the national survey data-
base [13]. The % contribution of dietary antioxidant capac-
ity of food was performed using the same equation as for 
food selection. Values were expressed by groups of foods 
(beverages, cereals and derivatives, beans, fruits and vege-
tables), considering the country as a whole, and breaking it 
into major geographic regions (South, Southeast, Midwest, 
North, Northeast) and household income.
Statistical analysis
AC results were presented as mean  ±  SD. Dietary AC 
results were presented as percentage. The evaluated food 
products were classified according to the AC in very high, 
high, medium or low, considering the percentiles’ distri-
bution of AC values. ANOVA was used for comparison 
of foods antioxidant results, and the correlation between 
TEAC and FRAP assays was assessed by Pearson’s cor-
relation test. Dietary AC values based on TEAC and 
FRAP assay results were compared by non-paired T test. 
Chi-square test followed by post hoc analyses was used to 
evaluate the influence of region and household income on 
contribution to total dietary AC. Prisma software (version 
5.0, USA) was used, and differences were considered at 
5 % significance level (p < 0.05).
Results
Antioxidant capacity of foods and beverages
The AC results for the selected foods evaluated by TEAC 
and FRAP are shown in Table 2. For the sake of clarity and 
simplification, after ranking, the food products were organ-
ized in categories by average percentile values of mean AC 
results from TEAC and FRAP assays.
Although values obtained by TEAC and FRAP assays 
differed slightly for a few food items, the results from both 
assays were highly correlated (r = 0.97, p < 0.0001). Small 
differences among AC results were also observed for the 
same food items and assay. However, these differences did 
not affect the ranking position of such food products.
Among all evaluated food products, green maté tea and 
coffee presented the highest AC, followed by toasted maté 
tea, red wine and açaí. Despite the wide range of AC val-
ues observed in fruits, vegetables, cereals and derivatives, 
beans showed consistently high AC values, regardless of 
the variety evaluated. Soft drinks, refined wheat flour and 
cachaça—a distilled alcoholic beverage obtained from fer-
mentation of sugarcane juice, presented the lowest AC.
Dietary antioxidant capacity
The average percent contribution of each food group to 
Brazilians’ dietary AC, based on the AC data obtained by 
TEAC and FRAP assays associated with the consumption 
database, is presented in Fig. 1.
No significant difference was observed in dietary AC 
when TEAC and FRAP assay results were considered. Cof-
fee gave the highest average contribution to the dietary AC 
(65.9 %), followed by other beverages (maté tea, wine, fruit 
juices, among others) which together contributed to 12.7 % 
of the dietary AC. Beans alone were responsible for 8.9 %. 
Low contributions to the Brazilians’ dietary AC were 
observed for cereals and derivatives (3.9 %), in natura fruits 
(2.8 %) and vegetables (1.9 %).

in the Midwest to 2.0 % in the Southeast region. The high 
dietary AC in the North region is derived from high die-
tary AC of açaí (13.8 %, p < 0.0001). The contribution of 
beans was important in all regions of the country, from 
6.1 % in the South to 11.1 % in the Northeast region. Dif-
ferent types of beans gained more importance according 
to the regions. Overall, black-eyed, pinto and black beans 
were important contributors to dietary AC in most regions, 
except for in the Northeast, where black-eyed beans 
gained special importance (p = 0.005). The contribution of 
cereals and derivatives to the dietary AC was similarly low

Dietary antioxidant capacity by household income
Stratifying the population according to the monthly house-
hold income classes (Table 4), we observed differences in 
patterns of consumption and, consequently, in the contribu-
tions of different food sources of antioxidants to the dietary 
AC. The household income did not affect the non-alcoholic 
beverages contribution to total dietary AC, due to the high 
coffee consumption in all classes. The contribution of alco-
holic beverages to dietary AC increased with household 
income (p < 0.0001) due to the increase in wine consump-
tion (p < 0.0001), its high AC and higher cost comparing to 
other evaluated beverages.
Beans’ contribution tended to decrease with increasing 
household income (p = 0.056). Among cereals and deriva-
tives, as household income increased, there was a tendency 
of increase in the consumption and contribution of ready-
to-eat foods such as bread and cookies in detriment of 
polished rice, for example. In natura fruits and vegetables 
also tended to increase their contribution to dietary AC as 
income increased. However, no statistical difference was 
observed in the contribution of these groups to dietary AC 
when household income was considered.
Discussion
Antioxidant capacity of foods and beverages
In the present study, the AC of the main plant foods com-
monly consumed in Brazil was evaluated by two in vitro 
assays, TEAC and FRAP. The choice of both methods 
allowed comparison with literature data [6, 7, 15, 19]. 
Additionally, we were careful in using assays based on 
different mechanisms of antioxidant action and choosing 
at least one method that would be sensitive to lipophilic 
in addition to hydrophilic antioxidant compounds in plant 
foods, in this case TEAC, while FRAP seems to be more 
sensitive to hydrophilic compounds [5].
The occasional variation observed between the values 
obtained by TEAC and FRAP assays for the same food 
products was expected, since it has been also observed in 
previous studies [6, 7, 15]. This variation has been attrib-
uted to differences in degree of complexity of both assays, 
in their sensitivity, and in mechanisms and reactive species 
involved [5]. Variations observed in the values for the same 
food product and assay can be explained by genetic differ-
ences (e.g., species and varietals) as well as variability in 
growing conditions such as soil and climate, inherent to the 
major regions of Brazil from where the different samples of 
each food product originated.
The high AC of coffee and its potential beneficial effects 
to human health has been well documented in epidemiolog-
ical, clinical and in vitro studies. Its consumption and anti-
oxidant effects have been associated with lower incidence 
of chronic degenerative diseases such as diabetes type 2 
[20], coronary diseases [21] and various types of cancers, 
including colon and liver cancers [22]. The higher AC of 
coffee comparing to other foods with typically high AC, 
including red wine, was not surprising, considering previ-
ous results by Pellegrini et al. [6] who using TEAC, TRAP 
and FRAP assays obtained average AC value for coffee 
3 times higher than that for red wine and 5 times higher 
than that for green C. sinensis tea. Saura-Calixto & Goni 
[7], Halvorsen et al. [15] and Wu et al. [23], using TEAC, 
FRAP and ORAC assays, respectively, also obtained

average AC value for coffee 2 times higher than that for 
red wine. Fushikoma et al. [8, 9], using the Folin–Ciocal-
teu assay, obtained AC value for coffee 2 times higher than 
for green C. sinensis tea. Small differences in the results 
obtained in the various studies comparing coffee and other 
antioxidants sources may probably derive from combined 
factors such as coffee roasting degrees, methods used for 
the beverages preparation, variations in AC analytical 
methods, and genetic and edaphoclimatic factors.
The higher AC values obtained for green and toasted 
maté teas comparing to most food items have not been 
previously reported, and the fact that green maté AC was 
similar to coffee and 2 ½ times higher than that of red wine 
was quite surprising. As in coffee, the very high AC of 
green and toasted maté tea, red wine, açaí and—in a lower 
extent—beans may be mainly attributed to their phenolic 
composition and enhanced by other compounds with anti-
oxidant activity. Green coffee and green maté tea leaves 
are the highest known edible sources of chlorogenic acids 
in nature. Chlorogenic acids’ content may commonly vary 
from 5 to 9  g/100  g in green coffee [24] and may reach 
more than 9 g/100 g in green maté tea leaves [25]. Despite 
their thermolability, a light-roasted coffee blend may com-
monly contain up to 4 g/100 g of these compounds along 
with antioxidant derivatives, which can give result in about 
500 mg/100 mL brew [26]. Although the commercial cof-
fee samples evaluated in the present study were roasted to 
moderately dark to dark degrees, they still contained from 
1 to 2 g/100 g (about 150–200 mg/100 mL brew) of chloro-
genic acids and derivatives, which is a considerable amount 
comparing to other food sources of phenolic compounds 
in nature [24]. Additionally, the chlorogenic acids incor-
porated into melanoidins during roasting and other minor 
natural and Maillard compounds help in maintaining coffee 
AC [27]. As with coffee, despite losses during processing, 
toasted maté tea still contains high amounts of chlorogenic 
acids (e.g., 1.9/100 g), in addition to probable incorpora-
tion of melanoidins during toasting [25].
The phenolic compounds of red wine such as resveratrol 
and anthocyanins derive primarily from grape skin and are 
transferred to the juice in the first stage of wine fermen-
tation. Thus, the longer the contact between the juice and 
the rind, the higher will be the content of polyphenols in 
red wine [6]. The values of AC obtained for red wine by 
TEAC assay were similar to those described by Pellegrini 
et al. [6]; Saura-Calixto & Goni [7] have found TEAC val-
ues slightly lower, possibly due to differences in process-
ing, grape variety and or/climatic conditions involved in 
the wine production. Açaí’s high AC is mostly related to its 
high content of anthocyanins [28] which are also respon-
sible for the known high AC of “berries” from temperate 
countries [6, 7, 15, 23]. It is known in the literature that 
sources of flavonoids are among the foods with highest AC 
[7]. The high AC observed in beets is probably due to the 
betalains, phenolic water-soluble pigments whose struc-
tures are derived from flavan nucleus, like those of antho-
cyanins [15, 29].
Regarding beans, their high AC is mainly derived from 
proanthocyanidins, in addition to anthocyanins [30]. 
The variability observed among the results obtained for 
black-eyed, black and pinto beans may be due to differ-
ent amounts of polyphenols and additional antioxidants in 
them. Halvorsen et al. [15] also found varying AC values 
for legume seeds although all of them were high.
Among the cereals and derivatives evaluated, corn and 
manufactured cookies showed the highest AC, being prob-
ably the first mainly associated with high carotenoid con-
tents and the later probably due to the synthetic antioxidant 
compound (tert-butylhydroquinone—TBHQ) added to their 
formulation for conservation purposes.
The high AC values observed for pineapple and citrus 
fruits like orange and mandarin are consistent with results 
from Manach et al. [31] and derive from their high content 
of vitamin C as well as from different types of flavonols 
[15].
Surprisingly, fresh tomatoes which are known sources 
of lycopene (a carotenoid pigment that can quench various 
reactive species including singlet oxygen) [32] presented 
a medium–high AC. This could derive from the fact that 
the AC assays used in the present study as well as most 
of the assays used to evaluate foods AC do not use singlet 
oxygen in their oxidation system. Other additional expla-
nation would be the incomplete extraction of lycopene 
from tomato, since it is present inside the chromoplasts 
in crystalline form [15], and this could reduce the extrac-
tion efficiency even by acetone, used in the present study 
for extraction of fat-soluble antioxidants. Accordingly, a 
high AC was observed in the tomato extract, probably justi-
fied by the heating process, which breaks the cell wall and 
allows the extraction of lycopene from chromoplasts [32]. 
Pumpkin, potatoes and carrot also exhibited a medium–
high AC when measured by TEAC assay, but when meas-
ured by FRAP assay, they showed low AC. This is not 
surprising, since these foods contain high amounts of carot-
enoid pigments, which are lipophilic antioxidants most effi-
ciently detected by TEAC assay [5].
As expected, the refined cereals and derivatives such as 
wheat flour, cracker, polished rice and spaghetti showed 
the lowest AC, justified by the refining process to which 
they are subjected, resulting in substantial loss of antioxi-
dant components. Finally, soft drinks and cachaça—a dis-
tilled alcoholic beverage—showed the lowest AC values, 
explained by high-refined sugar or alcohol and low plant 
extract contents.

Dietary antioxidant capacity
Speaking of the country as a whole, although other bever-
ages (red wine, maté tea, fruit juices, among others), beans, 
cereals and derivatives, fruits and vegetables also contrib-
uted to the dietary AC, their percentage contributions were 
considerably lower than that of coffee. The importance of 
coffee to the dietary AC in Brazil was expected, given its 
high consumption in the country. According to the Brazil-
ian Coffee Industry Association (ABIC) [14], in 2014, the 
per capita consumption of roasted coffee in Brazil was 
4.9 kg/year. This is comparable to Germany’s consumption 
(5.2 kg/year) and higher than in Italy (3.4 kg/year), France 
(3.2 kg/year), USA (3.1 kg/year) and Spain (3.0 kg/year), 
known as important coffee consumer countries. The high-
est per capita consumption is, however, still observed in the 
Nordic countries—Finland and Norway (9.6 and 7.2  kg/
year, respectively). The high contribution of coffee to die-
tary AC in Brazil, therefore, was expected, since high con-
tributions have previously been reported by similar studies 
performed in other countries where coffee is highly con-
sumed such as Norway (64 % contribution) [3], Italy (38 % 
for women and 27 % for men) [6], Spain (45 %) [7], Japan 
(56 %) [8, 9] and Czech Republic (54.6 % for women and 
43.1 % for men) [10].
Regarding the regional differences, due to the large ter-
ritorial extension of Brazil, diverse food consumption pat-
terns characterize the five major geographic regions (North, 
Northeast, Midwest, Southeast and South, responsible for 
7.1, 28.9, 6.5, 42.6 and 14.9 % of the country’s population, 
respectively). Considering the percent contribution of dif-
ferent food groups to the dietary AC of the regions, the fact 
that in all regions of the country the non-alcoholic bever-
ages contributed the most to dietary AC was mainly derived 
from the high consumption of coffee in all regions of the 
country and of maté tea in the South. Originated in the sub-
tropical region of South America, yerba maté (Ilex para-
guariensis) is a tree from the Aquifoliaceae family. The 
indigenous Quechua and Guarani nations kept the habit of 
drinking unfiltered infusions from its leaves, and still nowa-
days, this habit is popular in the South of Brazil, Uruguay 
and Argentina. Only in the South region, the contribution of 
coffee and maté to dietary AC was similar.
The importance of other food groups varied among the 
geographic regions. In the North, fruits were more impor-
tant than in other regions, mostly due to the high consump-
tion of açaí, used almost daily as a component of large 
meals, in the form of a puree which is often mixed with 
manioc flour. The contribution of a variety of beans was 
considerable in the whole country, where they are also con-
sumed mainly in large meals, in the form of salads, soups, 
stews with or without meet (accompanied with rice), fried 
bean flour dumplings, among other types of preparations, 
which vary with regions. In Northeast region, black-eyed 
beans contribution stood out due to its high consumption.
In the South, producer region of grapes and wine, the 
alcoholic beverages stood out after non-alcoholic bever-
ages basically due to the high consumption of red wine, 
combined with its high AC. In the Southeast and Midwest 
regions, all food groups presented similar percent contri-
bution to dietary AC. Although the consumption of cere-
als and derivatives to dietary AC tended to decrease from 
North to South, with higher consumption of corn in the 
North and Northeast regions and higher consumption of 
polished rice and refined wheat products in the Southeast, 
Midwest and South, probably because of relatively low AC 
of these foods, no statistical difference was observed in 
their contribution to the dietary AC according to household 
income.
Regarding the influence of household income on the 
contribution of foods to the Brazilian dietary AC, low-
income groups do not usually meet government recommen-
dations for consumption of fruits, vegetables, whole grains 
and low-fat dairy products, and they consume fewer serv-
ings of these nutritious foods than other households due to 
their high costs. Higher incomes expand food choices and 
are associated with other factors that tend to improve diet 
quality, including higher education, better access to well-
stocked grocery stores, greater diet and health knowledge 
[33, 34]. On the other hand, higher income can sometimes 
reduce the consumption of healthy foods like rice and 
beans in favor of fast or industrialized foods. This was the 
case with beans and rice contribution to dietary AC in Bra-
zil, which tended to decrease with increasing household 
income, associated with increase in the contribution of 
ready-to-eat foods such as bread and cookies. At the same 
time, the contribution of wine, fruits and vegetables tended 
to increase with household income.
Based on the present AC results associated with annual 
consumption database and income data from IBGE, it is 
possible to say that coffee is the most important contribu-
tor to dietary AC in all income classes. Coffee consump-
tion is in the heart of the Brazilian culture, and due to its 
high production, it is accessible to all. On the other hand, 
higher-quality coffee blends are only accessible to higher-
income classes.
Conclusions
In conclusion, in the present study, for the first time, coffee, 
maté tea, açaí and beans were indicated as important con-
tributors to the dietary antioxidant capacity in Brazil. Our 
results show that coffee is the main contributor, regardless 
of household income or diversified food patterns in most 
regions of the country. An exception is the South region,

contributions, differently from previous similar studies. 
Açaí fruit and beans also played important roles in the 
antioxidant foods intake in Brazil, being açaí especially 
important in the North region of the country and beans in 
the Northeast region. The contribution of food groups to 
total dietary antioxidant capacity differed with household 
income, highlighting the contribution of wine in higher 
incomes and of beans in lower incomes.
Antioxidant capacity measurements may provide basis 
to explore the potential health beneficial effects of foods 
rich in antioxidants. However, the bioavailability of the 
main compounds responsible for such in  vitro activity 
should be considered when comparing foods.
